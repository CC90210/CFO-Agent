## PART 1: TRUST TAXATION — FUNDAMENTALS

### 1.1 What Is a Trust (Legal vs. Tax Definition)

**Legal Definition:**
A trust is a legal relationship in which one person (the **settlor** or **grantor**) transfers legal ownership of property to another person (the **trustee**) to hold and manage for the benefit of one or more persons (the **beneficiaries**). Three certainties are required under common law:
1. Certainty of intention — clear intent to create a trust
2. Certainty of subject matter — identifiable trust property
3. Certainty of objects — identifiable beneficiaries or purpose

**Tax Definition (ITA):**
The ITA does not define "trust" explicitly but treats a trust as a **separate taxpayer** under s.104(1)–(2). A trust is deemed to be an individual for income tax purposes. The ITA distinguishes between:
- **Testamentary trust** — arising on and as a consequence of the death of an individual (s.108(1))
- **Inter vivos trust** — any other trust (created during lifetime)

**Key Parties:**

| Party | Role | Tax Significance |
|-------|------|-----------------|
| Settlor | Creates the trust, transfers initial property | Attribution rules may apply (s.74.1, 75(2)) |
| Trustee | Holds legal title, manages assets, files T3 | Fiduciary duty, signing authority |
| Protector | Optional oversight role | No standard ITA treatment |
| Beneficiary | Receives income/capital distributions | Reports amounts on personal T1 |

**Ontario Trustee Act:** Governs trustee powers and duties in Ontario. Trustees have statutory powers to invest, sell, and manage trust property. Trust deed can expand or restrict these powers.

---

### 1.2 Trust Taxation Overview — The Core Mechanics

**The Conduit Principle:**
Trusts act as conduits — income allocated to beneficiaries is deducted from the trust's income (s.104(6)) and reported on the beneficiary's T1. Income retains its **character** when flowed to beneficiaries (capital gains stay capital gains, eligible dividends stay eligible dividends).

**Tax Rates — The Critical Difference:**

| Trust Type | Federal + Ontario Rate |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Inter vivos trust (non-GRE/QDT) | 53.53% (top marginal) |
| Graduated Rate Estate (GRE) | Graduated 20.05%–53.53% |
| Qualified Disability Trust (QDT) | Graduated 20.05%–53.53% |
| Testamentary trust (post-2015, non-GRE/QDT) | 53.53% (top marginal) |

**Why This Matters:** Before 2016, all testamentary trusts had graduated rates — a massive income-splitting tool. The 2014 Federal Budget (effective 2016) eliminated this, leaving only GREs and QDTs with graduated rates. This fundamentally changed estate planning in Canada.

**Trust Income Calculation:**
```
Trust Income = All income earned on trust property
Less: s.104(6) deduction for amounts paid/payable to beneficiaries
= Income retained in trust (taxed at trust level)
Plus: Income allocated to beneficiaries (taxed in beneficiary's hands)
```

**T3 Slip:** Issued to each beneficiary showing their allocated income by type (interest, dividends, capital gains, other income).

**By Creation:**

**By Discretion:**

## PART 2: SPECIFIC TRUST TYPES — DEEP DIVES

### 2.1 Inter Vivos Trust (Living Trust)

**Definition:** A trust created by a living person, effective immediately upon execution of the trust deed.

**ITA Tax Rate:** Top marginal rate — **53.53% in Ontario** (33% federal + 20.53% Ontario). Every dollar of income retained in the trust is taxed at the highest possible rate. This makes distributing income to lower-bracket beneficiaries essential.

**Creation Requirements:**
- Written trust deed (not legally required but essential for tax purposes)
- Transfer of property to trustee (formal deed of settlement)
- Minimum nominal consideration (e.g., $100 from settlor to trustee)
- Trustee must be a separate person from the sole beneficiary (exceptions: bare trust, alter ego)

**Key Uses:**
1. **Income splitting** — Allocate trust income to lower-bracket family members (subject to TOSI s.120.4)
2. **Creditor protection** — Assets in properly constituted trust are generally beyond reach of settlor's creditors (if not a fraudulent conveyance under Fraudulent Conveyances Act)
3. **Privacy** — Trusts do not go through probate; assets and beneficiaries remain private
4. **Succession planning** — Bypass probate fees (Ontario: 1.5% of estate over $50K)
5. **Business succession** — Hold shares of operating company

**Minimum Requirements to Maintain Trust Status:**
- Annual trustee meetings and minutes
- Separate trust bank account
- Annual T3 filing (even if nil income)
- Trustee must actually exercise discretion (not just rubber-stamp settlor's instructions)
- Do NOT commingle trust assets with personal assets

**Attribution Rules (Critical Warning):**
When a settlor transfers property to an inter vivos trust for the benefit of their spouse or minor children, attribution rules can pull income back to the settlor:
- s.74.1(1): Spouse/common-law partner attribution — interest and dividends attributed back
- s.74.1(2): Minor child attribution — interest and dividends attributed back to age 18
- s.74.2: Capital gains on spouse's property attributed back
- s.75(2): Reversionary trust — if property can revert to settlor, ALL income attributed back

**TOSI Interaction (s.120.4):**
Post-2017, income from a family trust to an adult family member in a private corporation context may be subject to Tax on Split Income. See Part 5 for TOSI navigation. For income from a non-corporate trust to adult beneficiaries not engaged in the business, TOSI does not apply — but this area has CRA attention.

**Costs:**
- Trust deed preparation: $1,500–$5,000 (tax lawyer)
- Annual accounting/T3: $800–$2,500
- Ongoing legal: variable

### 2.2 Testamentary Trust

**Definition:** A trust that arises on and as a consequence of the death of an individual, typically created by a will (s.108(1) ITA).

**Historical Context:** Before 2016, testamentary trusts used graduated rates — a widely used income-splitting tool. The 2014 Federal Budget changed this. Post-2016, testamentary trusts are taxed at the top marginal rate UNLESS they qualify as a GRE or QDT.

**Current Tax Treatment (Post-2015):**
- Standard testamentary trust: **53.53% flat rate** in Ontario
- GRE: Graduated rates for up to 36 months
- QDT: Graduated rates indefinitely (with annual election)

**When Testamentary Trusts Are Still Used:**
Despite the rate change, testamentary trusts remain valuable for:
1. **Spousal trusts** — Defer tax on capital gains until spouse's death (s.73(1) rollover)
2. **Control over distribution** — Prevent beneficiaries from receiving assets all at once (e.g., children reaching 25, 30, 35 triggers)
3. **Creditor protection** — Protect inheritance from beneficiary's creditors or divorce
4. **Henson trusts** — Preserve disability benefits
5. **Charitable trusts** — Charitable remainder trusts funded on death

**Probate and Testamentary Trusts:**
Assets held directly by a testamentary trust (via will) go through probate. Ontario probate fees (Estate Administration Tax):
- 1.5% of estate value over $50,000
- Example: $2M estate = $29,250 in probate fees
- Testamentary trust assets are STILL probated (trust is part of estate)
- Contrast: Inter vivos trusts, joint ownership, and named beneficiaries bypass probate

### 2.3 Graduated Rate Estate (GRE)

**Definition (s.248(1)):** A GRE is an estate that arises on the death of an individual after 2015, designated as a GRE in the deceased's final T1 return, with graduated federal tax rates for a maximum of **36 months** from the date of death.

**Critical Requirements:**
- Designated as GRE in the deceased's final T1 return (or first T3)
- Only ONE GRE per deceased individual — the "main" estate
- Must be a testamentary trust (arising on death)
- Must be resident in Canada
- No new contributions to the estate after death (except from registered accounts, life insurance)
- Executor must file T3 for the estate and make the GRE designation

**Tax Advantage:**
GRE income is taxed at graduated rates:
- 20.05% on first $16,129 (Ontario)
- Up to 53.53% at top bracket

**Example (Ontario, 2024 rates):**
GRE with $200,000 of income over 3 years ($66,667/year):
- Tax at graduated rates: ~$27,500/year = $82,500 total
- Tax at flat 53.53%: $107,060 total
- **Savings: ~$24,560 over the GRE period**

**Other GRE Benefits:**
- Can choose any year-end (not calendar year)
- Can designate capital losses against capital gains in any year
- Eligible for alternative minimum tax (AMT) exemptions applicable to individuals
- Can file optional returns (s.70(2) rights/things return)

**Planning Note:** Every will should include GRE provisions. The estate trustee (executor) must be aware of the designation requirement and file the first T3 correctly.

**GRE Expiry:** After 36 months, the estate becomes an ordinary inter vivos trust and is taxed at the top rate. Distributions should be made before expiry.

### 2.4 Qualified Disability Trust (QDT)

**Definition (s.122(3)):** A testamentary trust that jointly elects QDT status with a resident beneficiary who qualifies for the Disability Tax Credit (DTC) under s.118.3.

**Key Features:**
- **Graduated tax rates** — same as GRE but indefinite duration
- Annual election required — both the trust (on T3) and the qualifying beneficiary (on T1) must jointly elect each year
- Only ONE QDT per disabled individual
- Must be a testamentary trust (cannot be an inter vivos trust)
- Qualifying beneficiary must be named in the trust deed

**Qualifying Beneficiary Requirements:**
- Canadian resident
- DTC-eligible (T2201 approved by CRA)
- Designated in the trust as a qualifying beneficiary
- Cannot have more than one QDT designation in the year

**Tax Savings Example:**
QDT with $50,000/year income for a disabled adult with no other income:
- Tax at graduated rates (Ontario): ~$7,000
- Tax at flat 53.53%: ~$26,765
- **Annual savings: ~$19,765**
- Over 20 years (NPV): ~$200,000+

**QDT vs. Henson Trust:**
These are not mutually exclusive. A Henson trust can also be a QDT if it qualifies. The QDT provides the tax rate advantage; the Henson structure protects ODSP eligibility.

**Preferred Beneficiary Election (PBE) — s.104(14):**
For a disabled beneficiary, the trust can make a Preferred Beneficiary Election, accumulating income in the trust (not actually distributing it) while including it in the beneficiary's income at their lower rate. This is used when the beneficiary needs the money to stay in the trust (creditor protection, ODSP) but you want to use their low marginal rates.

### 2.5 Family Trust (Discretionary)

**Definition:** A discretionary inter vivos trust where the trustee has full authority to allocate income and capital among a class of beneficiaries (typically family members).

**Why Discretionary Matters:**
Full discretion means the trustee can:
- Allocate income to the lowest-bracket beneficiaries
- Retain income in the trust when all beneficiaries are in high brackets
- Distribute capital to beneficiaries who need it
- Strategically time capital gains realization

**Standard Structure for an Incorporated Business Owner:**

```
         [CC — Settlor]
               |
         $100 settlement
               |
    [Family Trust — Trustee: CC's parent or professional trustee]
               |
     Holds common shares of OASIS Inc.
               |
    ┌──────────┼──────────┐
    |          |          |
  [CC]    [CC's spouse] [Future children]
  Beneficiary Beneficiary  Beneficiaries
```

**Income Splitting (Subject to TOSI — see Part 5):**
When OASIS pays dividends to the trust, the trust can distribute to adult family members not subject to TOSI, or retain at the trust level and pay top rate, or use s.104(6) deduction to flow to lower-bracket beneficiaries.

**Capital Gains Distribution:**
The trust can distribute the shares of OASIS (on an eventual sale) to beneficiaries, each of whom may use their Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE) of $1.25M (2024 threshold for QSBC shares). This is the most powerful feature — see Part 4.3.

**Attribution Rules — s.75(2) Warning:**
If the settlor retains any power to:
- Revoke the trust
- Receive the trust property back
- Require that property only be disposed of with settlor's consent

...then s.75(2) applies, and ALL income and gains are attributed back to the settlor. This is fatal to the structure. The trust deed must not contain any such reservation.

**The "Arm's Length" Trustee:**
Best practice: Settlor should NOT be the sole trustee. Options:
- Independent trustee (tax lawyer, accountant, trust company)
- Parent or sibling as trustee
- Professional trustee company

If CC is both settlor and sole trustee, s.75(2) risk is elevated and TOSI rules are harder to navigate.

**21-Year Rule — s.104(4):**
A critical planning item. See Part 3.7.

**T3 Filing:**
Annual T3 required even if the trust earns no income. See Part 3.1.

### 2.6 Alter Ego Trust

**Statutory Basis:** s.73(1.01) ITA

**Requirements:**
1. Settlor must be **65 years of age or older** at time of creation
2. Settlor must be a Canadian resident
3. Settlor must be the **sole beneficiary during their lifetime** (income AND capital)
4. No other person may receive income or capital during settlor's lifetime

**Tax Treatment on Creation:**
- Property transferred to alter ego trust at **adjusted cost base** (ACB) — no immediate tax
- No deemed disposition on transfer (unlike a regular trust transfer which is at FMV)
- Normal trust rules apply during settlor's lifetime

**Deemed Disposition at Death:**
- Trust property is deemed disposed at FMV at the settlor's **death**
- 21-year rule does NOT apply (death trigger replaces it)
- Allows deferral until death without the 21-year clock ticking

**Primary Use — Probate Avoidance:**
Ontario probate (Estate Administration Tax):
- 1.5% on estate value over $50,000
- $2M estate = $29,250 in probate fees
- $5M estate = $74,250 in probate fees
- Alter ego trust assets are NOT part of the estate — no probate

**Other Benefits:**
- Privacy (no probate = no public record)
- Avoid will challenges and estate litigation
- Seamless asset transition at death
- Continue managing assets during incapacity (trustee takes over)
- Maintains control during lifetime

**Drawback:**
- Cannot income-split during lifetime (settlor is sole beneficiary)
- Does not multiply LCGE
- Setup cost ($5,000–$15,000)

**Joint Spousal/Common-Law Partner Trust (s.73(1.01)(c)):**
Similar structure but both spouses are beneficiaries during their lifetimes. Same tax-free rollover. Deemed disposition at the LATER spouse's death.

### 2.7 Spousal / Common-Law Partner Trust

**Statutory Basis:** s.73(1) — rollover; s.70(6) — testamentary spousal trust

**Inter Vivos Spousal Trust (s.73(1)):**
When a Canadian resident transfers capital property to a spousal trust, it rolls over at the transferor's ACB — no immediate capital gain.

**Requirements for Tax-Free Rollover:**
- Both settlor and spouse must be Canadian residents
- Spouse must be entitled to receive ALL income of the trust during their lifetime
- No other person may receive or benefit from trust income or capital during spouse's lifetime

**Attribution Warning:**
Despite the rollover, s.74.1(1) and s.74.2 attribute income and capital gains back to the transferor spouse while BOTH spouses are alive. Attribution only stops on:
- Death of the transferor
- Divorce/separation
- Election to not have attribution rules apply

**Testamentary Spousal Trust (s.70(6)):**
Created by will, takes effect at death. Property rolls to the trust at deceased's ACB (deferred capital gains). Gains are triggered at spouse's death or when trust ceases to qualify.

**Deemed Disposition:**
For inter vivos spousal trust: When the beneficiary spouse dies (or the trust ceases to qualify), there is a deemed disposition at FMV. This is when the deferred capital gains are triggered.

**Strategic Use:**
- Defer capital gains tax to the surviving spouse's death
- Maintain control over ultimate distribution of assets (to children, not spouse's new partner)
- Probate planning on second death

### 2.8 Bare Trust / Nominee Arrangement

**Definition:** A trust where the trustee holds legal title but exercises NO discretion — they act purely on the direction of the beneficiary (the beneficial owner). The beneficial owner is the true owner for tax purposes.

**Tax Treatment — Transparency:**
CRA's position (confirmed in multiple cases): A bare trust is NOT a separate taxpayer. The beneficial owner reports all income, gains, and deductions as if the trust didn't exist. The trustee is ignored for tax purposes.

**Reference:** CRA Technical Interpretation 2003-0016235, Folio S6-F2-C1

**Common Uses:**
1. **Real estate** — Developer holds title in nominee; beneficial owner directs and reports income
2. **Private company shares** — Nominee holds shares for beneficial owner (e.g., employee/contractor equity)
3. **Crypto** — Custody arrangement
4. **Privacy** — Land registry shows nominee, not beneficial owner
5. **Brokerage accounts** — Street name holdings

**2024 T3 Filing Requirements — Bare Trusts (Complicated History):**
- 2023 Budget introduced mandatory T3 filing for ALL trusts including bare trusts
- First filing deadline was March 31, 2024 (for 2023 taxation year)
- CRA **deferred** bare trust reporting for 2023 tax year (announced March 28, 2024)
- For 2024 tax year: bare trust T3 filing required UNLESS CRA grants further exemption
- Status: Monitor CRA announcements — this has been contentious
- Penalty for failure to file: $25/day, minimum $100, maximum $2,500 (plus gross negligence penalty)

**Bare Trust vs. Sham:**
A bare trust must be a genuine legal arrangement. If CRA determines it is a "sham" (no real trust relationship), they will disregard it entirely and potentially apply penalties.

**What Creates Bare Trust Status:**
- No independent discretion by trustee
- Trustee bound to act on beneficiary's instructions
- Written agency/nominee agreement
- Trustee has no beneficial interest

### 2.9 Employee Profit Sharing Plan (EPSP)

**Statutory Basis:** s.144 ITA

**Definition:** An arrangement whereby an employer makes payments to a trustee "in trust" for employees, with payments calculated by reference to profits of the employer's business (or a related business).

**Tax Treatment — EPSP:**
- Employer contributions are deductible in year made
- Employee allocated amounts are INCOME (not salary), included in employee's T1
- Amounts are NOT subject to CPP or EI premiums (advantage for both parties)
- The trust itself is tax-exempt (all income flows to employees)

**Advantages:**
1. Employer deducts contributions (same as salary)
2. Employee pays tax, but NO CPP/EI on EPSP allocations
3. Capital gains earned inside EPSP retain capital gains treatment when allocated
4. Eligible dividends retain dividend tax credit treatment

**EPSP vs. DPSP (Deferred Profit Sharing Plan):**

| Feature | EPSP | DPSP |
|---------|------|------|
| Tax timing for employee | When allocated | When withdrawn |
| Registered (RRSP room impact) | No | Yes (reduces room) |
| CPP/EI on allocations | No | No |
| Employer deduction | Year of contribution | Year of contribution |
| Complexity | Lower | Higher (registered) |

**EPSP Abuse — s.144.1 (Specified Employee Rules):**
If a "specified employee" (owns 10%+ of shares, or related to employer) receives excessive EPSP allocations (more than 20% of employer's profit), the excess is a "prohibited allocation." Tax consequences:
- 280% tax on prohibited allocation
- Introduced to stop EPSP as income-splitting tool for owner-managers

**Legitimate Use:**
- Genuine arms-length employees
- Profit-sharing culture companies
- Capital gains and dividend flow-through advantage

### 2.10 Charitable Remainder Trust

**Definition:** A trust where the donor transfers assets to the trust, receives an income stream for life (or a term), and designates a charity as the "remainder" beneficiary.

**Tax Treatment:**
- Donor receives a charitable donation receipt for the **present value** of the charity's remainder interest
- Present value calculated using CRA's prescribed interest rate at time of transfer
- Income earned in the trust is taxed in the donor's hands (if donor is income beneficiary)
- No capital gain on appreciated property transferred IF the trust qualifies as a "charitable remainder trust"

**ITA Reference:** s.118.1 (charitable donations), Reg. 3501 (receipting rules)

**Example:**
Donor, age 70, transfers $500,000 of appreciated securities (ACB: $100,000, FMV: $500,000):
- Capital gain deferred — no immediate disposition trigger
- Donation receipt: PV of remainder = ~$250,000 (depends on age, prescribed rate)
- Donation credit: $250,000 × 53.53% = ~$133,825 in federal/Ontario tax reduction
- Donor receives income stream from trust for life

**Caution:**
- Complex structure requiring specialized legal advice
- CRA scrutiny of valuations
- Not suitable for all assets (illiquid assets problematic)

## PART 3: TRUST TAXATION MECHANICS

### 3.1 T3 Trust Income Tax Return — Filing, Deadlines, Penalties

**Who Must File:**
- Every Canadian resident trust with tax payable
- Every trust that allocated income to a beneficiary
- Every trust that has a taxable capital gain
- Bare trusts (as of 2024 — see 2.8 for current status)
- Non-resident trusts with Canadian-source income

**Deadline:**
- **90 days after the trust's year-end**
- Most trusts: December 31 year-end → T3 due **March 31**
- GREs: Can have off-calendar year-end (e.g., June 30) → T3 due September 28
- Note: If deadline falls on weekend/holiday, due date moves to next business day

**T3 Slips:**
- Due to each beneficiary by the T3 filing deadline
- Boxes:
  - Box 21: Capital gains
  - Box 23: Actual amount of eligible dividends
  - Box 24: Eligible dividend tax credit
  - Box 25: Non-eligible dividends
  - Box 26: Other income
  - Box 30: Capital gains eligible for deduction (s.110.6)

**Penalties:**
- Late filing: $25/day, minimum $100, maximum $2,500
- Gross negligence: 50% of unpaid tax (s.163(2))
- Failure to report: 10% (first failure), 20% (second failure)
- Unreported income: Interest at CRA's prescribed rate + penalty

**Required Information:**
- Trust deed (or will, for testamentary trust)
- Legal name and SIN/Trust Account Number
- Trustee information
- Beneficiary information (SIN required)
- Complete income statement for the year
- All supporting slips (T3, T5, T4PS received by the trust)

**Trust Account Number:**
- Format: TZ 123 456 789
- Obtained by filing RC69E (Trust Account Registration)
- Required before filing first T3

**Tax Installments for Trusts:**
- Trusts are generally NOT required to make quarterly installments unless specific conditions apply
- GREs: No installments required
- Inter vivos trusts: May be required if taxes owing exceed $3,000 in current or either of the two preceding years (same thresholds as individuals)

---

### 3.2 Income Allocation — s.104(6) Deduction

**The Mechanic:**
Under s.104(6), a trust can deduct from its income amounts that are "paid or payable" to beneficiaries in the year. This is the core income-splitting mechanism. Income deducted at the trust level is included in the beneficiary's income.

**"Paid or Payable" Requirement:**
Income does not need to be physically paid to be deductible. It must be "payable" — meaning the beneficiary has a legal entitlement to it by year-end. For discretionary trusts, the trustee resolution allocating income must be passed BEFORE the trust's year-end.

**Critical Deadline:**
For a December 31 year-end trust, the trustee must pass a resolution allocating income to specific beneficiaries **on or before December 31**. Post-year-end resolutions cannot create a payable obligation retroactively.

**Trustee Resolution — Required Elements:**
```
TRUSTEE RESOLUTION — [TRUST NAME]
Date: December 20, [YEAR]

The trustee hereby allocates the trust income for the taxation year ending December 31, [YEAR] as follows:
- [Beneficiary 1 Name, SIN]: $[Amount] — [Type of Income]
- [Beneficiary 2 Name, SIN]: $[Amount] — [Type of Income]
Total allocated: $[Total]

The trustee hereby determines that the above amounts are payable to the respective beneficiaries.

Signed: [Trustee signature, date]
```

**Undistributed Income:**
Income NOT allocated to beneficiaries remains in the trust and is taxed at the trust's rate (53.53% for inter vivos trusts). There is no mechanism to retroactively allocate income after year-end.

**Preferred Beneficiary Election (PBE):**
An exception: Under s.104(14), income can be included in a disabled beneficiary's income without being actually paid (see 3.6).

---

### 3.3 Capital Gains Designation — s.104(21)

**The Rule:**
When a trust allocates capital gains to a beneficiary, the allocation RETAINS ITS CHARACTER as a capital gain in the beneficiary's hands. Without s.104(21), the allocation would be treated as "other income" — taxable at full rates rather than the 50% capital gains inclusion rate.

**Designation Requirement:**
The trust must make a formal designation on the T3 return (Schedule 4) designating the amount as a capital gain payable to the beneficiary.

**LCGE Flow-Through — s.104(21.2):**
This is the most powerful capital gains trust provision. When a trust sells QSBC shares (Qualifying Small Business Corporation) or QFPP (Qualified Farm or Fishing Property), the capital gain can be designated as flowing through to the beneficiaries as an "eligible taxable capital gain" — each beneficiary can then claim their LCGE against it.

**This is how the LCGE multiplication works:**
Trust sells OASIS Inc. shares → $4,000,000 gain → Trust designates gain to 4 beneficiaries → Each uses their $1,000,000 LCGE → $4,000,000 tax-free

See Part 4.3 for the full LCGE multiplication strategy.

### 3.4 Dividend Designation — s.104(19)

**The Rule:**
When a trust allocates taxable dividends to a beneficiary, the dividends retain their character (eligible or non-eligible) in the beneficiary's hands. The beneficiary gets the benefit of the dividend tax credit (DTC).

**Eligible Dividends:**
- Paid by Canadian public companies or CCPCs with full general rate income pool (GRIP)
- 38% gross-up, 15% federal DTC + Ontario DTC
- Effective tax rate in Ontario: 39.34% at top marginal rate (but lower at lower brackets)

**Non-Eligible Dividends:**
- Paid by CCPCs from low-rate income (active business income taxed at small business rate)
- 15% gross-up, 9.0301% federal DTC
- Effective tax rate in Ontario: 47.74% at top marginal rate

**Strategic Planning:**
If the trust holds shares of a CCPC (like OASIS Inc.):
- OASIS pays non-eligible dividends (from SBD income)
- Trust flows non-eligible dividends to lower-bracket beneficiaries
- At $50,000 income, non-eligible dividend effective rate: ~15-20% (after DTC)
- Compare to 53.53% if retained in trust

### 3.5 Foreign Tax Credit Flow-Through — s.104(22)

**The Rule:**
A trust can allocate foreign taxes paid to a beneficiary so the beneficiary can claim the Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) on their T1.

**Without s.104(22):** The trust would pay Canadian tax on foreign income. The beneficiary would not get credit for foreign taxes paid. Result: Double taxation.

**With s.104(22):** Foreign taxes are allocated to beneficiaries proportionally. Beneficiaries claim FTC on their personal returns. Avoids double taxation.

**Application:**
For a trust holding US dividend-paying stocks or international equity funds:
- US withholds 15% on dividends (Canada-US Tax Treaty rate)
- Trust allocates foreign income AND foreign taxes to beneficiaries
- Beneficiaries claim FTC to offset Canadian tax on foreign income

**Designation:** Made on the T3 return (Schedule 7).

### 3.6 Preferred Beneficiary Election — s.104(14)

**Purpose:**
Allow a disabled beneficiary's lower tax rates to be used without actually distributing income to them. The income accumulates in the trust (protected from creditors, maintaining ODSP eligibility) but is taxed in the beneficiary's hands.

**Requirements:**
- Beneficiary must be the settlor, settlor's spouse/common-law partner, OR a child, grandchild, or great-grandchild of the settlor
- Beneficiary must have a physical or mental infirmity that causes prolonged dependency (not required to be DTC-eligible — broader test)
- Joint election by both the trust and the beneficiary (or their legal representative)
- Filed with T3 return for the year

**How It Works:**
```
Example:
- Disabled adult beneficiary, no other income
- Trust earns $30,000 interest income
- PBE election made: $30,000 included in beneficiary's income
- Tax: ~$4,500 (at their low marginal rate)
- Without PBE: $30,000 × 53.53% = $16,059 in trust
- Savings: $11,559 per year
- Income stays in trust — ODSP not affected (if Henson trust structure)
```

**The Combined Henson + QDT + PBE Structure:**
For maximum protection and minimum tax:
1. **Henson trust** structure — preserves ODSP (absolute discretion)
2. **QDT designation** — graduated tax rates
3. **PBE election** — accumulate income at beneficiary's low rates
4. Result: Tax-efficient, ODSP-protected, creditor-protected accumulation

### 3.7 The 21-Year Deemed Disposition Rule — s.104(4)

**The Rule:**
Every inter vivos trust is deemed to have disposed of ALL its capital property at **fair market value** every 21 years from the date of the trust's creation. This triggers a deemed capital gain.

**Why It Exists:**
Parliament designed the rule to prevent property from being held in trust indefinitely, thereby deferring capital gains tax forever. The 21-year rule forces a reckoning.

**When the Clock Starts:**
The 21-year period runs from the date the trust was created (typically the date the trust deed was signed and property transferred). Example:
- Trust created: March 15, 2005
- First 21-year deemed disposition: March 15, 2026
- Next deemed disposition: March 15, 2047

**What Is Deemed Disposed:**
- ALL capital property held by the trust at the 21-year anniversary date
- At **fair market value**
- Trust must report the deemed capital gain on its T3 for that year
- Tax is payable by the trust at the trust's tax rate

**Example — Unplanned 21-Year Hit:**
Family trust created 2005 holds:
- OASIS Inc. shares: ACB $100, FMV $2,000,000
- Deemed capital gain: $1,999,900
- Taxable capital gain (50% inclusion): $999,950
- Tax at 53.53%: **~$535,238 payable by the trust in 2026**

This is catastrophic without planning.

**Planning Strategies to Avoid the 21-Year Tax:**

**Strategy 1: Distribute Before the 21st Year**
Under s.107(2), a Canadian resident beneficiary can receive trust property at the trust's ACB (rollover). No capital gain triggered on distribution. The beneficiary takes the property at the trust's ACB and defers the gain until they personally sell.
- **Best for:** When beneficiaries can hold the property personally
- **Timing:** Must be completed BEFORE the 21-year anniversary date
- **Caution:** Cannot use this if beneficiaries are minors or non-residents

**Strategy 2: Wind Up the Trust**
Distribute all trust assets to Canadian resident beneficiaries at ACB rollover (s.107(2)). Trust ceases to exist. No 21-year tax.
- Same as Strategy 1 but terminates the trust entirely
- Lose future tax planning benefits of the trust structure

**Strategy 3: New Trust with Cost Base Reset**
Transfer property from old trust to a new trust at FMV (triggering a gain, but resetting the clock to year 1). The new trust's 21-year clock starts fresh.
- **Cost:** Pay tax now on the gain
- **Benefit:** New 21-year period with stepped-up ACB
- Used when trust property has modest accrued gains (cost of tax now < cost of larger future tax)
- Requires careful documentation of FMV at time of transfer

**Strategy 4: Corporate Rollout**
Transfer trust assets to a holding company on a tax-deferred basis (s.85(1) rollover). Property moves from trust to corporation. Trust holds shares of Holdco instead of the underlying property.
- Does not eliminate the 21-year rule — shares in Holdco are still subject to it
- But corporations do NOT have a 21-year rule — property can sit in a corporation indefinitely
- Strategy: Transfer property to corp before 21 years, trust holds shares of Holdco, wind up trust later

**Key Deadlines:**

| Trust Creation | 21-Year Date | Action Required By |
|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Jan 1, 2004 | Jan 1, 2025 | ALREADY PASSED |
| Jan 1, 2005 | Jan 1, 2026 | Urgent — plan now |
| Jan 1, 2010 | Jan 1, 2031 | 5 years — begin planning |
| Jan 1, 2015 | Jan 1, 2036 | 10 years — monitor |
| Jan 1, 2020 | Jan 1, 2041 | 15 years — low urgency |

**Exceptions to the 21-Year Rule:**
- Alter ego trust: Deemed disposition at settlor's death (replaces 21-year rule)
- Spousal trust: Deemed disposition at spouse's death (replaces 21-year rule)
- Joint spousal trust: Deemed disposition at last surviving spouse's death
- Qualified disability trust: Same 21-year rule applies (plan accordingly)
- RRSP/TFSA trusts: Exempt (registered plans)
- Charitable trusts: 21-year rule does not apply to charitable purpose trusts

### 3.8 Attribution Rules in Trusts

**Overview:**
Attribution rules prevent income splitting by attributing income earned by one person (the transferee) back to the original transferor for tax purposes. Trusts are a primary battleground for attribution.

**s.74.1(1) — Spousal Attribution (Income):**
When an individual transfers or loans property to their spouse (or a trust for the spouse's benefit), any income or loss from that property is attributed BACK to the transferor while they are married and both resident in Canada.
- Stops on: Death of either, legal separation (if living apart at year-end), election under s.74.5(3)
- Does NOT stop just because the property earns a loss (losses also attributed)

**s.74.1(2) — Minor Child Attribution:**
When an individual transfers or loans property to a minor child (under 18) or a trust for their benefit, income from that property is attributed back to the transferor.
- Stops when the child turns 18
- Capital gains are NOT attributed for minor children (unlike spousal attribution)
- Only interest, dividends, rents — income-type returns

**s.74.2 — Spousal Capital Gains Attribution:**
Capital gains (and losses) on property transferred to a spouse (or spousal trust) are attributed back to the transferor.
- More restrictive than s.74.1 — covers gains too
- Stops on the same events as s.74.1

**s.75(2) — Reversionary Trust (The Fatal Attribution Rule):**
This is the most dangerous attribution rule for trust planning. Under s.75(2), if trust property CAN (directly or indirectly):
1. Revert to the settlor, OR
2. Be distributed only with the settlor's consent, OR
3. Be passed only to persons determined by the settlor after the trust is created

...then ALL income, losses, and capital gains from that property are attributed BACK to the settlor.

**Practical Implication:**
If the trust deed says "the settlor may revoke the trust at any time" → 100% attribution to settlor. Completely defeats the purpose.

If the settlor is sole trustee with no other trustee → CRA may argue s.75(2) applies (no genuine separation).

**Avoiding s.75(2):**
1. Trust deed must be irrevocable
2. Settlor cannot be sole trustee
3. Settlor cannot retain veto power over distributions
4. Trust deed must not allow property to revert to settlor

**s.56(4.1) — Low-Interest Loan Attribution:**
Loans to a trust at less than the prescribed rate (currently 5%) are subject to attribution. The income earned on the loan proceeds is attributed back to the lender.

**The Safe Harbor — Prescribed Rate Loan:**
Loans at the CRA prescribed rate avoid attribution. See Part 4.1.

**Second-Generation Income (The Exception):**
Attribution applies only to income earned on the transferred/loaned property — not to income EARNED ON THAT INCOME. "Second-generation income" (income reinvested from attributed income) is NOT attributed. This is the basis for compounding non-attributed income over time.

## PART 4: ADVANCED TRUST STRATEGIES

### 4.1 Prescribed Rate Loan Strategy

**Statutory Basis:** s.74.5(2) ITA

**The Strategy:**
Instead of gifting money to a spouse or family trust (which triggers attribution), LOAN the money at the CRA prescribed interest rate. If the borrower earns more than the prescribed rate on the invested funds, the spread is income in the borrower's hands — not attributed back.

**CRA Prescribed Rate (2024–2025):** 5% (quarterly reset — check CRA website)
**CRA Prescribed Rate (Historical):** Reached a low of 1% in 2020–2021 — the golden window for low-rate loans that are now locked in.

**Structure:**

```
CC (Lender) ──── Promissory Note at 5% ────▶ Spouse/Trust (Borrower)
                                                     |
                                            Invests in portfolio
                                                     |
                                          Earns 8-12% return
                                                     |
                                  Net income in borrower's hands: 3-7%
```

**How It Works:**
1. Lender (CC) loans $100,000 to trust at the CRA prescribed rate (5%)
2. Trust invests $100,000, earns 10% = $10,000
3. Trust pays $5,000 interest to CC
4. CC reports $5,000 interest income
5. Trust deducts $5,000 interest expense, has $5,000 net income
6. Trust distributes $5,000 to lower-bracket beneficiary
7. Total family tax on $5,000: ~$1,000 vs. ~$2,675 in CC's hands

**The Annual Interest Collection Requirement:**
Interest MUST be paid by **January 30** of the following year. If the trust fails to pay the interest by January 30:
- Attribution kicks in for the ENTIRE year (not just the missed interest)
- The structure is compromised for that year
- There is no cure — you must restart with a new loan

**This is the single most common error in prescribed rate loans. Set a calendar reminder every January.**

**Locking in the Rate:**
The prescribed rate is FIXED at the rate in effect when the loan is made. If the loan was made in Q3 2020 at 1%, the rate is PERMANENTLY 1% — even as CRA's rate rises to 5%+. This is why 2020–2021 loans are still extremely valuable today.

**Documentation Required:**
1. Written promissory note signed by borrower
2. Specify loan amount, interest rate, payment schedule
3. Annual payment of interest by January 30
4. Both lender and borrower maintain records of payments

**Worked Example — Tax Savings Over 10 Years:**

Assumptions:
- CC loans $200,000 to family trust at 1% (2020 rate)
- Trust invests in diversified portfolio, earns 8%/year
- Beneficiary spouse has $40,000 in other income (Ontario)

Annual calculation:
- Trust income: $16,000 (8% × $200,000)
- Less interest to CC: $2,000 (1% × $200,000)
- Net income allocated to spouse: $14,000
- Spouse's total income: $54,000

Tax at marginal rates ($40-54K bracket):
- Spouse pays ~$3,400 on $14,000 increment
- CC would have paid $14,000 × 53.53% = $7,494

Annual saving: $4,094
Over 10 years (compounding): ~$60,000+ tax savings

### 4.2 Estate Freeze with Family Trust

**Concept:**
An estate freeze "freezes" the current value of a business or appreciating asset in the hands of the current owner (the "freezer") and transfers future growth to the next generation (via a family trust holding common shares).

**Why It Works:**
CC holds common shares of OASIS Inc. worth $1,000,000 today. Over 10 years, OASIS grows to $5,000,000. Without a freeze:
- CC's capital gain at sale: $4,000,000 × 50% = $2,000,000 taxable income
- Tax: ~$1,070,600 (Ontario top rate)

With an estate freeze:
- CC's preferred shares are frozen at $1,000,000
- Family trust holds common shares (future $4,000,000 growth)
- At sale, trust distributes gains to 4 beneficiaries with LCGE

**The Section 86 Freeze (Simple):**
OASIS does a share exchange under s.86:
1. CC exchanges common shares for fixed-value preferred shares (FMV = $1,000,000)
2. Family trust subscribes for new common shares for nominal consideration ($100)
3. Preferred shares are "frozen" — they pay dividends but don't appreciate
4. Future growth accrues to common shares (held by trust)
5. No immediate tax — s.86 is a tax-deferred rollover

**The Section 85 Freeze (More Flexible):**
Using s.85(1) rollover:
1. CC transfers common shares to Holdco in exchange for preferred shares (rollover at ACB)
2. No capital gain triggered
3. Family trust holds common shares of Holdco
4. More flexible — allows precise valuation and share structure design

**Preferred Share Terms (Critical for CRA Acceptance):**
- Fixed redemption value equal to FMV at time of freeze
- Non-participating (no right to additional value)
- Annual cumulative dividend (market rate — typically prime + 2%)
- Retractable (CC can require OASIS to redeem)
- Non-voting (or limited voting)

**Caution — s.84.1 (Dividend Strip Prevention):**
If CC sells the preferred shares to a non-arm's length corporation (e.g., sells to the family trust), CRA may deem the proceeds to be a dividend rather than a capital gain. This is a complex anti-avoidance rule. Tax lawyer essential.

**CRA Valuation:**
The freeze must be done at FMV. Undervaluing the company at freeze results in deemed benefit to common shareholders (the trust). Overvaluing results in the preferred shares having too high a redemption value (reduces future LCGE). Independent valuation required for businesses over $500K.

### 4.3 LCGE Multiplication via Family Trust

**The Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE):**
- 2024 LCGE for QSBC shares: **$1,250,000** per individual (indexed to inflation)
- Each Canadian resident individual gets their own LCGE
- LCGE is completely tax-free at both federal and provincial levels

**QSBC Share Requirements (s.110.6(1)):**
To qualify as QSBC (Qualifying Small Business Corporation) shares:
1. **At sale:** Shares of a CCPC where all/substantially all (90%+) assets are used in active business in Canada
2. **24 months preceding sale:** Shares have been owned by the seller (or a related person) continuously
3. **Throughout 24 months:** More than 50% of FMV of assets was used in an active business

**Common QSBC Failure Points:**
- Too much cash/investment assets in the company (fails "all or substantially all" test)
- Shares owned for less than 24 months
- Company recently converted from public company
- Excess passive income (exceeds threshold)

**Purification Strategies for QSBC:**
If OASIS has excess cash (common after profitable years):
- Pay down debt
- Declare large dividends before sale
- Transfer excess cash to Holdco (upstream dividend — may result in Part IV tax)
- Invest cash in qualifying active business assets
- See `docs/ATLAS_INCORPORATION_TAX_STRATEGIES.md` for full purification guide

**LCGE Multiplication Example:**

OASIS Inc. sale in 2030, FMV $5,000,000, ACB $1,000:

**Without family trust:**
- CC sells shares, gain: $4,999,999
- Uses LCGE: $1,250,000 × 50% = $625,000 exempt
- Remaining gain: $3,749,999 × 50% = $1,874,999 taxable
- Tax: ~$1,003,444 (Ontario top rate)

**With family trust (4 beneficiaries: CC + 3 family members):**
- Trust distributes shares to 4 beneficiaries before sale (s.107(2) rollover at ACB)
- Each beneficiary sells shares using their LCGE: 4 × $1,250,000 = $5,000,000 exempt
- Each beneficiary's gain: ~$1,250,000 = exactly covered by LCGE
- Tax: **$0** (if gain does not exceed total LCGE)
- **Total tax savings: ~$1,003,444**

**The Key Mechanics:**
1. **s.107(2) rollover:** Property distributed from trust to Canadian resident beneficiary is transferred at the trust's ACB (no gain)
2. **Each beneficiary holds shares personally**
3. **Each sells using their own LCGE**
4. **s.104(21.2) alternative:** Trust can sell and designate gain to flow through as eligible capital gains — beneficiaries claim LCGE against the designated amount (no need to distribute shares first)

**Limitations:**
- Beneficiaries must be individuals (not corporations)
- Must be Canadian residents
- TOSI does not apply to capital gains (as of the post-2017 rules — capital gains are excluded from TOSI)
- CRA may challenge if beneficiaries were added to trust shortly before sale (substance challenge)
- Must have genuine reasons for trust structure beyond tax (business reason test for GAAR)

**GAAR Consideration (General Anti-Avoidance Rule — s.245):**
CRA has challenged LCGE multiplication in some cases. Best practice:
- Family trust established years before any planned sale
- Beneficiaries are genuine family members with legitimate expectations
- Document non-tax reasons for trust structure
- Business purpose: succession planning, creditor protection, estate planning

### 4.4 Insurance Trust / COLI Integration

**Structure:**
1. **Personal insurance trust:** Trust is named as beneficiary of life insurance policy. Proceeds bypass estate (no probate). Trust holds funds for distribution per trust deed.
2. **Corporate-Owned Life Insurance (COLI):** OASIS Inc. owns policy on CC's life. On death, proceeds credited to Capital Dividend Account (CDA). Tax-free capital dividend to trust or shareholders.

**Insurance Trust Mechanics:**
- Insured: CC
- Owner of policy: Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT)
- Premium payments: CC gifts money to trust; trust pays premiums
- On death: Policy proceeds pay to trust (not estate — no probate)
- Trust distributes per trust deed

**Tax Treatment:**
- Life insurance proceeds: Generally tax-free to Canadian recipients (s.148 — no policy gain)
- Proceeds in trust: No tax on receipt
- Trust distributes to beneficiaries: Character depends on trust income — capital distributions from trust are not income

### 4.5 Henson Trust (Discretionary Trust for Disabled Beneficiaries)

**Origin — Case Law:**
The Henson trust gets its name from the Ontario case **Ontario (Director of Income Maintenance, Ministry of Community & Social Services) v. Henson** (1987), affirmed on appeal (1989). The court held that assets in a properly constituted discretionary trust do NOT constitute "assets" for purposes of ODSP eligibility, because the beneficiary has no right to demand payment.

**Why It Matters:**
ODSP (Ontario Disability Support Program) has an asset limit of **$40,000** for a single person. Without a Henson trust, an inheritance or gift over $40,000 could disqualify the disabled person from ODSP benefits worth $1,228/month + health benefits.

**The Absolute Discretion Requirement:**
The trust deed MUST give the trustee **absolute and unfettered discretion** to make (or withhold) distributions. If the beneficiary has ANY right to demand or compel distributions, the trust fails.

**Required Language:**
The trust deed should state words to the effect of: "The trustee shall have absolute and uncontrolled discretion in determining whether and how much to distribute to the beneficiary. The beneficiary shall have no right to demand, direct, or compel any payment."

**ODSP Trust Recognition (Ontario):**
ODSP Policy Directive 9.2 recognizes Henson-type discretionary trusts. The trust must be:
1. Truly discretionary (trustee has absolute discretion)
2. Not settlor-controlled (settlor cannot direct distributions)
3. Beneficiary cannot demand payment

**Permitted Distributions Without Losing ODSP:**
- Disability-related expenses: Unlimited (no impact on ODSP)
- Luxury items: May reduce ODSP on dollar-for-dollar basis depending on province
- RDSP contributions: Permitted without affecting ODSP

**Choosing the Trustee:**
Critical decision. The trustee must be:
- Someone who will survive the beneficiary (ideally)
- Someone who understands the disability and the person's needs
- Someone who will not be influenced to make improper distributions
- Options: Sibling, trusted friend, Public Guardian and Trustee (PGT), trust company

**When to Set Up:**
- Can be set up inter vivos (now) or testamentary (in will)
- Best practice: Both — inter vivos for immediate use, testamentary for estate proceeds
- RDSP coordination: Henson trust can contribute to RDSP (see 5.5)

**Interaction with Tax:**
- Trust is an inter vivos or testamentary trust for tax purposes
- Testamentary Henson trust can be a QDT (see 2.4) — graduated rates
- PBE election (see 3.6) accumulates income at beneficiary's low marginal rates
- Combined structure: Testamentary Henson trust + QDT designation + PBE = maximum protection + minimum tax

**Worked Example:**
Beneficiary: CC's sibling, age 30, ODSP recipient ($1,228/month + benefits), DTC-eligible.

Structure:
- CC's will creates testamentary Henson trust with $500,000 for sibling
- Trust invests conservatively, earns 5% = $25,000/year
- Trust is designated QDT — graduated rates apply
- PBE election made — $25,000 included in sibling's income

Tax on $25,000 (only income):
- Federal + Ontario tax: ~$3,750 (effective 15%)
- Without QDT (flat 53.53%): $13,382
- Annual saving: $9,632

ODSP Status: Preserved — sibling continues to receive benefits

### 4.6 Double Freeze / Second-Generation Freeze

**Purpose:**
Used when the first freeze is "thawed" — i.e., the company has grown beyond the frozen preferred share value and the common shareholders now have significant value. A second freeze freezes the common shareholders' (trust's) current value.

**Scenario:**
- 2020: Estate freeze. CC holds $1M preferred shares. Trust holds common shares.
- 2030: OASIS worth $10M. Trust's common shares worth $9M.
- Problem: Trust's common shares now have large accrued gain. 21-year rule approaching.

**Solution:**
Second (double) freeze:
1. Trust exchanges its common shares for new preferred shares (frozen at $9M current FMV)
2. New family trust (or existing trust beneficiaries) hold new common shares
3. Beneficiaries' common shares start at nominal value again
4. Future growth (from $10M) accrues to the new common shares
5. Cycle can repeat as needed

**Tax Implications:**
- Trust exchanges shares under s.86 at ACB — no immediate tax
- New preferred shares have the same QSBC issues as the original preferred shares (usually exempt from LCGE as fixed-value preferred shares are not growth shares)
- Tax lawyer essential for structuring

### 4.7 Pipeline Transaction and Trust Windup

**Pipeline Transaction:**
A strategy to convert corporate surplus into capital gain (eligible for LCGE) rather than dividends (not eligible for LCGE and taxed at higher rates) on the death of a shareholder.

**The Deceased's Estate Problem:**
When CC (hypothetically) dies holding OASIS shares, the shares are deemed disposed at FMV (s.70(5)). The capital gain may exceed the LCGE. The remaining gain is taxable capital gain.

**BUT:** The deceased's estate now holds shares with an ACB equal to FMV at death. If the estate sells the shares to a new Holdco (for a promissory note), then OASIS distributes its retained earnings to repay the note, the result is:
- Estate receives cash (capital — not taxable, as ACB = FMV at death)
- OASIS gets to deduct dividends paid (subject to Part III.1 tax considerations)
- Net effect: Corporate surplus extracted as capital return rather than dividend

**CRA Scrutiny:** CRA has challenged pipeline transactions. Recent cases (MacDonald, Collins & Aikman) confirm pipeline is acceptable if:
- There is a genuine winding down of the business
- Adequate time between transactions
- Not a "series of transactions" that abuses the Act

**Trust Windup (s.107(2)):**
When a trust is wound up, property can be distributed to Canadian resident beneficiaries at the trust's ACB — no capital gain at the trust level. The beneficiaries take the property at the trust's ACB and will pay tax only when THEY sell.

**Conditions for s.107(2) Rollover:**
- Beneficiary must be resident in Canada
- Beneficiary must be entitled to receive the property (per trust deed)
- Cannot apply if property was originally contributed to the trust in a non-arm's length transaction where loss is denied (s.107(1) applies instead)

## PART 5: FAMILY FINANCIAL PLANNING

### 5.1 Income Splitting Strategies (Post-TOSI Landscape)

**The TOSI Problem:**
The 2018 TOSI rules (s.120.4) taxed split income received by adult family members from a private corporation at the top marginal rate (53.53%), eliminating most income-splitting strategies. However, TOSI has exceptions.

**Full TOSI analysis in:** `docs/ATLAS_TOSI_DEFENSE.md`

**Summary of TOSI Exemptions (Income Splitting Still Possible):**

| Strategy | TOSI Impact | Conditions |
|----------|------------|------------|
| Salary to family member | TOSI does not apply to employment income | Salary must be reasonable and arm's length |
| Capital gains | TOSI does not apply to capital gains | Subject to LCGE multiplication rules |
| Excluded shares | TOSI does not apply | 10%+ voting and value, not professional corp |
| Reasonable return | TOSI does not apply | Return commensurate with contributions |
| Prescribed rate loan | No attribution, TOSI may apply to interest if not truly at arm's length | Loan at prescribed rate; borrower invests |
| Spousal RRSP | Not TOSI (RRSP system) | 3-year attribution rule on withdrawals |
| CPP/Pension splitting | Statutory — not TOSI | Ages 65+, 60.03 election |

**Strategy 1: Salary to Family Members**
Pay family members who provide genuine services at commercially reasonable rates:
- Spouse for bookkeeping, administration: $30,000–$50,000/year
- Adult children for marketing, IT, social media support
- Must be real work, documented (timesheets), at market rate
- Issue T4 slips, withhold source deductions
- Corporation deducts salary — saves 11–13% corporate tax vs. personal income splitting

**Documentation Required:**
- Employment contract
- Job description
- Timesheets or work records
- Performance reviews

**Strategy 2: Prescribed Rate Loans (See Part 4.1)**

**Strategy 3: Spousal RRSP Contributions**
CC contributes to a spousal RRSP (spouse's name):
- CC gets the tax deduction (same as regular RRSP)
- At retirement, RRSP income is in spouse's hands
- Attribution rule: If spouse withdraws within 3 calendar years of CC's contribution, attributed back to CC
- Wait 3 years after last contribution before withdrawing

**Annual Limit:** CC's RRSP contribution room. No separate limit for spousal RRSP.

**Strategy 4: Capital Gains Splitting**
Transfer appreciated capital property to lower-income spouse at ACB (s.73(1) rollover, but attribution applies on capital gains while married). Wait for attribution rules to stop (s.74.5(3) election, or separation).

Better approach: Use family trust for capital gains splitting (no attribution on capital gains to adult beneficiaries from trust).

**Strategy 5: Second-Generation Income**
Attribution rules do NOT apply to income earned on attributed income. If $10,000 in dividends is attributed back to CC, but the spouse reinvests and earns $1,000 on those dividends, the $1,000 is in the spouse's hands — not attributed. This compounds over time.

### 5.2 Spousal Strategies

**Spousal RRSP — Detailed:**
- Contribution: Credited to CC's RRSP room, in spouse's name
- Withdrawal: In spouse's name (at their marginal rate) — AFTER 3-year attribution period
- Best timing: Contribute in early years of marriage when CC is high-income; withdraw at retirement when both are in lower brackets
- At age 71: Both CC and spouse stop contributing, convert to RRIF

**CPP Sharing (s.65.1 CPP Act):**
- Eligible at age 60 (reduced CPP) or 65 (full CPP)
- Both spouses must apply to share
- CRA allocates portion of each spouse's CPP credits to the other
- Result: Each receives a CPP amount, potentially reducing the higher-earner's CPP (taxable) and increasing the lower-earner's
- Applied to CPP retirement benefit only (not disability or survivor benefit)

**Pension Income Splitting (s.60.03):**
- Both spouses must be 65+ (or receiving eligible pension income)
- Up to 50% of eligible pension income can be allocated to spouse
- Eligible pension income: RRIF withdrawals (at 65+), annuity payments from RPP, DPSP
- NOT eligible at any age: CPP, OAS, RRSP withdrawals (must convert to RRIF first)
- Result: Balances income between spouses, minimizes OAS clawback, reduces marginal rates

**Spousal Loan (Prescribed Rate — Current Rate: 5%):**
Loan to spouse to invest in non-registered account. See Part 4.1.

### 5.3 Registered Account Family Optimization

**Account Priority Framework for a Family:**

| Priority | Account | Reason |
|----------|---------|--------|
| 1 | FHSA | 100% deductible, grows tax-free, first home |
| 2 | RRSP | Deductible, tax-deferred growth |
| 3 | TFSA | Tax-free growth, flexible withdrawals |
| 4 | RESP | CESG grants — free 20% return |
| 5 | RDSP (if disabled) | Matching grants up to $90,000 |
| 6 | Non-registered | After all registered room used |

**TFSA Gift Strategy:**
CC can gift money to a spouse for them to contribute to their own TFSA:
- No attribution — money in a TFSA is exempt from attribution rules
- Spouse invests in TFSA, earns income tax-free
- Doubles the family's TFSA contribution room
- 2024 TFSA limit: $7,000/year (cumulative room since 2009: $95,000)

**RRSP Carry-Forward Room:**
If either spouse has unused RRSP room from prior years, maximize before year-end. Contribution deadline: 60 days after December 31 (for prior year deduction).

**Spousal RRSP Optimization at Retirement:**
Optimal scenario:
- Spouse has spousal RRSP accumulated over working years
- At retirement, both spouses draw RRIF income of equal amount
- Example: Both draw $50,000/year = 2 × $50,000 at 20% effective rate
- vs. CC draws $100,000 at 43% effective rate
- Savings: ~$23,000/year in retirement

### 5.4 RESP — Registered Education Savings Plan

**Overview:**
A tax-advantaged savings account for post-secondary education. Contributions grow tax-free; withdrawals (as Educational Assistance Payments) are taxable in the STUDENT's hands (not the subscriber's).

**Contribution Limits:**
- No annual limit — but limited by grant eligibility
- **Lifetime limit per beneficiary: $50,000**
- No RRSP-style deduction for contributions

**Canada Education Savings Grant (CESG):**
Government adds to RESP contributions:
- **20% on first $2,500/year = $500/year maximum**
- Low-income families (net family income < $52,898): Additional 10-20% on first $500
- Lifetime CESG maximum per beneficiary: **$7,200**
- Carryforward: If you miss a year, can double up the next year ($5,000 to get $1,000 grant)

**Canada Learning Bond (CLB):**
For families receiving National Child Benefit Supplement (NCBS) — income-tested benefit:
- $500 in year of birth (first year eligible)
- $100/year for each subsequent year eligible (up to 15 years)
- Maximum CLB: $2,000
- No contribution required to get CLB

**Alberta Centennial Education Savings Grant (ACES):**
$500 to Alberta children born in 2005 or later (regardless of family income). Not applicable for Ontario families (Ontario cancelled its OESP in 2018).

**Plan Types:**

| Type | Beneficiaries | Flexibility | Notes |
|------|--------------|-------------|-------|
| Individual RESP | One specific beneficiary | Limited — tied to one person | Beneficiary must be changed if plans change |
| Family RESP | Multiple related beneficiaries | Flexible — money can flow to any beneficiary | Beneficiaries must be related by blood or adoption to subscriber |

**Strategic Tip:** Use a **Family RESP** with all children as beneficiaries. If one child doesn't pursue post-secondary, the other children can use the full RESP.

**What Happens if Child Doesn't Go to School:**
Options:
1. Transfer remaining to a sibling's RESP (if family plan)
2. Transfer up to $50,000 to subscriber's RRSP (if subscriber has RRSP room)
3. Withdraw as Accumulated Income Payment (AIP) — taxable + 20% penalty tax
4. Donate to an educational institution (no refund of CESG)

**CESG Must Be Repaid:**
If no beneficiary pursues education and RESP is closed, all CESG must be repaid to government. CESG is grant money — not yours to keep if the plan fails.

**EAP Tax Treatment:**
Educational Assistance Payments are income in the STUDENT's hands. Students typically have little to no income:
- First ~$15,000 covered by basic personal amount (no tax)
- Tuition credits offset remaining income
- Effective tax rate on EAPs: Often 0%

**Optimal RESP Strategy:**
- Open RESP at birth
- Contribute $2,500/year per child to maximize CESG
- Invest in equity ETFs for long-term growth (18-year horizon)
- Switch to GICs/bonds as child approaches 17
- Total by 18: ~$90,000+ (with grants and 7% return)

**RESP Over-Contribution:**
Over $50,000 lifetime = 1% per month penalty on excess. Track carefully.

### 5.5 RDSP — Registered Disability Savings Plan

**Overview:**
A registered savings plan for people with disabilities. The most generous government matching program in Canada.

**Eligibility:**
- Disability Tax Credit (DTC) eligible
- Canadian resident
- Under 60 years old when opening
- SIN required
- No income test for plan itself (but grant amounts are income-tested)

**Contribution Limits:**
- **Lifetime maximum: $200,000**
- No annual contribution limit
- Contributions are NOT tax deductible

**Canada Disability Savings Grant (CDSG) — The Matching:**

| Family Net Income | On first $500 | On next $1,000 | Annual Maximum |
|-------------------|--------------|----------------|----------------|
| < $34,863 | 300% ($1,500) | 200% ($2,000) | $3,500 |
| $34,863 – $53,359 | 300% ($1,500) | 100% ($1,000) | $2,500 |
| > $53,359 | 100% ($500) | 100% ($1,000) | $1,000 |

**Maximum CDSG:** $3,500/year; **lifetime maximum: $70,000**

**Canada Disability Savings Bond (CDSB):**
For low-income families — no contribution required:
- Family income < $34,863: $1,000/year
- $34,863 – $53,359: Reduced amount
- Maximum CDSB: $1,000/year; **lifetime maximum: $20,000**

**10-Year Clawback Rule:**
Grants and bonds must remain in the RDSP for 10 years. If withdrawn before 10 years after the last grant, all grants/bonds received in the preceding 10 years must be repaid. This is called the "assistance holdback."

**Growth and Withdrawals:**
- Growth in RDSP is tax-deferred
- Withdrawals are Disability Assistance Payments (DAPs) — taxable to the beneficiary
- Mandatory withdrawals begin at 60 (Lifetime Disability Assistance Payments — LDAPs)

**Maximize RDSP Strategy:**
For a low-income DTC beneficiary:
- Contribute $1,500/year → receive $3,500 CDSG + $1,000 CDSB = $4,500 government money
- 300% return on contribution is unbeatable
- Maximize to $200,000 lifetime limit over ~20 years
- Total RDSP at age 60 (assuming 5% growth): $400,000+

**Coordination with Henson Trust:**
Henson trust can make contributions to beneficiary's RDSP without affecting ODSP eligibility. A well-structured disability plan includes all three:
1. **RDSP** — long-term savings with government grants
2. **Henson trust** — ongoing living expenses and discretionary distributions
3. **ODSP** — monthly income support and health benefits

### 5.6 Child Tax Benefits and Credits

**Canada Child Benefit (CCB):**
Tax-free monthly payment to families with children under 18:
- Under 6: $7,437/year ($619.75/month) — 2023-24 rates, indexed to inflation
- Age 6–17: $6,275/year ($522.91/month)
- Income-tested: Begins reducing above $36,502 family net income
- Fully phased out: ~$175,000 for a family with 2 children

**Optimization:**
File taxes promptly each year — CCB is based on prior-year family net income. If CC's spouse has low/no income, file their return even if nil income (to establish CCB eligibility).

**Ontario Child Benefit (OCB):**
- Up to $1,607/year per child under 18
- Income-tested, calculated with CCB
- Received as part of monthly CCB payment (combined with federal amount)

**Child Care Expense Deduction — s.63:**
Deductible by the LOWER-INCOME spouse (or single parent):
- Under 7 years: $8,000/child
- Age 7-16: $5,000/child
- Child with disability: $11,000 (or $5,000 if 17+)

Eligible expenses: Daycare, babysitters, day camps, boarding school (portion), in-home caregivers.
Documentation: Receipts with caregiver name and SIN.

**Tuition Transfer (s.118.81):**
Student can transfer up to **$5,000** of tuition credits to:
- Parent, grandparent, spouse, or common-law partner
- After the student uses enough tuition credit to reduce their own tax to zero

**Calculation:**
- Student has $20,000 tuition
- Student's tax: $0 (after their own credits)
- Transferable amount: $5,000 (federal) = $750 credit to parent
- Ontario also allows transfer of provincial tuition amount

**Child Disability Benefit (CDB):**
- Additional amount added to CCB if child is DTC-eligible
- $3,173/year (2023-24) per child with disability
- Income-tested above ~$71,000

## PART 6: SEPARATION, DIVORCE, AND FAMILY BREAKDOWN

### 6.1 Property Division and Equalization

**Ontario Family Law Act (FLA) — Equalization:**
On marriage breakdown in Ontario, each spouse is entitled to an "equalization payment" — a payment that brings both spouses to equal net family property (NFP).

**Net Family Property (NFP) Calculation:**
```
NFP = (FMV of all assets at separation date)
    - (FMV of all debts at separation date)
    - (FMV of all assets owned before marriage, net of debts)
    - (Gifts/inheritances received DURING marriage and kept separate)
    - (Plus: value of matrimonial home CANNOT be deducted from NFP, even if owned before marriage)
```

**Equalization Payment:**
Spouse with higher NFP pays the other:
`Equalization Payment = (Higher NFP - Lower NFP) / 2`

**Tax Treatment of Equalization Payment:**
The equalization payment itself is **NOT TAXABLE** — it is a division of property, not income. However, transfers of property to satisfy the equalization obligation MUST be structured carefully to avoid triggering capital gains.

**s.73(1) — Tax-Free Rollover on Breakdown:**
Property transferred between spouses (or to a trust for the spouse) as part of a written separation agreement or court order transfers at the transferor's ACB — no capital gain triggered.

Requirements:
- Spouses must be living separate and apart at time of transfer, OR
- Transfer is made pursuant to a written separation agreement or court order

**Common Mistake:** If the separation agreement does not properly trigger the s.73(1) rollover, the transfer of appreciated property triggers a capital gain to the transferor.

---

### 6.2 Support Payments — Tax Treatment

**Spousal Support (ITA s.56(1)(b) and s.60(b)):**
- **Payer:** Can DEDUCT periodic support payments from income
- **Recipient:** Must INCLUDE periodic support payments in income
- Requirements: Must be periodic payments (not lump sum), pursuant to written agreement or court order, parties must be living apart

**Why the Tax Treatment Matters:**
If CC pays $2,000/month spousal support at a 40% marginal rate:
- Deduction saves CC: $2,000 × 40% = $800/month
- Recipient (at 20% rate) pays: $2,000 × 20% = $400/month
- Net "government subsidy" on the transaction: $400/month

**Lump-Sum Spousal Support:**
NOT deductible (s.60(b) requires periodic). However, a lump sum paid in lieu of future periodic payments is not deductible. Tax planning: Structure as ongoing periodic if possible.

**Child Support (Post-April 30, 1997):**
- **Payer:** CANNOT deduct child support
- **Recipient:** Does NOT include child support in income
- Pre-May 1, 1997 orders under old rules (deductible/taxable) were grandfathered but most have been varied by now

**Practical Impact:**
If separating spouses are negotiating support:
- High-income payer prefers more spousal support (deductible) vs. child support (not deductible)
- Low-income recipient prefers more child support (not taxable) vs. spousal support (taxable)
- Tax considerations should be explicitly modeled in negotiation

### 6.3 RRSP/RRIF Division on Breakdown

**s.146(16) — RRSP Transfer on Breakdown:**
Tax-free direct transfer of RRSP funds from one spouse's RRSP to the other's RRSP pursuant to a written separation agreement or court order.

Requirements:
- Must be a direct trustee-to-trustee transfer (not withdrawal + recontribution)
- Pursuant to decree, order, judgment, or written separation agreement
- Spouses must be living separate and apart

**RRSP Room Impact:**
The transfer does NOT use either spouse's RRSP contribution room. It is outside the normal RRSP system — a direct transfer.

**RRIF on Breakdown:**
Same rules apply to RRIF as RRSP — direct transfer on breakdown, no tax at time of transfer.

**TFSA on Breakdown:**
TFSA can also be transferred to a spouse's TFSA as an "exempt contribution" — does not reduce the receiving spouse's contribution room. Pursuant to written separation agreement or court order.

### 6.4 Business Valuation on Divorce

**When Needed:**
If one spouse owns a business (e.g., CC's OASIS Inc.), the business value is included in their NFP for equalization purposes.

**Valuation Methods:**

1. **Capitalized Earnings:** Most common for service businesses
   - Normalized EBITDA × Industry Multiple
   - OASIS (AI/SaaS): Multiples typically 3–8× EBITDA for private companies

2. **Discounted Cash Flow (DCF):** Project future cash flows, discount at risk-adjusted rate

3. **Asset-Based:** Net assets at FMV — used when earnings don't reflect true value

**The "Owner-Manager Premium/Discount" Issue:**
For a solo-operator business like OASIS:
- Accountant may argue the business has minimal goodwill beyond CC personally
- Personal goodwill (tied to the owner) vs. enterprise goodwill (tied to the business)
- Personal goodwill may be excluded from NFP in some cases (depends on jurisdiction and case facts)
- Critical distinction: Does the business continue if CC leaves? If not, most "goodwill" is personal.

**Tax Affecting:**
Divorce courts often "tax affect" private company value — deduct a hypothetical corporate tax from earnings before capitalizing. CRA does not tax affect for purposes of s.110.6 (LCGE). Courts in Ontario have varied on whether to tax affect.

**Professional Valuation:**
A Chartered Business Valuator (CBV) should prepare the report. CBV reports withstand court scrutiny better than DIY valuations.

### 6.5 CPP Credit Splitting

**Statutory Basis:** s.65.1 CPP Act

**Overview:**
On divorce or separation, CPP credits earned by both spouses during the period of cohabitation are divided equally between them. This is called "credit splitting" (not "CPP sharing" — that's for couples living together, see 5.2).

**Automatic on Divorce:**
Credit splitting is automatic upon divorce — it happens automatically unless BOTH spouses jointly apply to NOT have their credits split (rare).

**On Separation:**
Requires application by either spouse.

**Effect:**
- Credits are redistributed — not duplicated
- A high-earning spouse may lose CPP credits to the other spouse
- Low-earning spouse gains CPP credits
- Net impact depends on both spouses' earning histories

## PART 7: ELDER CARE AND SUCCESSION PLANNING

### 7.1 Caregiver Tax Credits

**Canada Caregiver Credit (CCC) — s.118(1)(c.1):**
Available for individuals supporting a spouse, common-law partner, or dependant with physical or mental impairment.

**Amounts (2024):**
- For infirm spouse/CLP: $2,616 (addition to spousal amount)
- For infirm dependant (not spouse) age 18+: $7,999
- Reduced dollar-for-dollar when dependant's income exceeds $19,666

**Eligible Dependants:**
- Spouse/common-law partner with impairment
- Children, grandchildren, parents, grandparents, siblings, aunts, uncles, nieces, nephews — if resident in Canada and dependent due to impairment

**No DTC Required:**
The CCC does not require the dependant to have a DTC certificate — just evidence of impairment from a medical professional.

**Ontario Caregiver Credit:**
Ontario does not have a separate caregiver credit but mirrors the federal credit through the Ontario Tax Credit system. Claim on ON428.

### 7.2 Medical Expense Optimization

**s.118.2 — Medical Expense Tax Credit:**
Credit for eligible medical expenses:
- **Federal rate:** 15% on qualifying expenses
- **Ontario rate:** 5.05%
- **Combined:** ~20% credit rate (non-refundable)
- **Threshold:** Expenses must exceed the LESSER of $2,635 (2024) or 3% of net income

**The 12-Month Period Election:**
You can claim medical expenses for ANY 12-month period ending in the tax year — not just the calendar year. This allows you to cluster expenses across two calendar years into one claim.

**Example:**
CC's parent has large dental expenses in Oct–Dec 2024 ($8,000) and Jan–Mar 2025 ($5,000).
- Calendar year: 2024: $8,000 and 2025: $5,000 → Two separate claims
- Optimal 12-month period: Oct 2024 – Sept 2025 (if further expenses): Combines both into one large claim exceeding the threshold more efficiently

**Who Claims:**
Medical expenses can be claimed by either spouse for themselves, their spouse, or their dependent children. The lower-income spouse should generally claim (threshold is 3% of net income — lower threshold = more claimable expenses).

**Eligible Expenses (Selected):**
- Prescription medications
- Dental: cleaning, fillings, orthodontics, oral surgery
- Vision: glasses, contacts, LASIK surgery
- Physician, specialist, and hospital fees
- Medical devices: hearing aids, insulin pumps, CPAP
- Private health insurance premiums (premiums paid to private plan qualify)
- Home adaptations for disability
- Ambulance fees
- Service animals
- Fertility treatments (CRA Folio S1-F1-C1 — embryo freezing, IVF)
- Full-time attendant care for impaired person (up to $10,000; unlimited if DTC-eligible)

**Attendant Care and DTC Interaction:**
If claiming attendant care as a medical expense AND the DTC:
- Can claim attendant care up to $10,000 (or unlimited if the person has a severe and prolonged impairment and is claiming only the attendant care as a medical expense, not the DTC)
- Cannot claim both full attendant care AND full DTC for the same person — must choose

### 7.3 Disability Tax Credit (DTC)

**Overview:**
A non-refundable tax credit for individuals with a severe and prolonged impairment in physical or mental functions.

**DTC Amount (2024 Federal):**
- Base amount: $9,428 × 15% = $1,414 federal tax reduction
- Supplement for under 18: Additional $5,500 × 15% = $825 (if eligible)
- Ontario supplement: $9,428 provincial amount × 5.05% = ~$476

**Total DTC Value (Federal + Ontario):** ~$1,890/year

**Eligibility Requirements:**
- Marked restriction in one of the basic activities of daily living (BADL):
  - Seeing, hearing, speaking, walking, eliminating, feeding, dressing, mental functions
- OR cumulative effect of significant restrictions in two or more BADLs
- Impairment must be severe and prolonged (12+ months, present all or substantially all of the time)
- Certified by a medical practitioner (T2201 form)

**T2201 Certifying Professionals:**
- Seeing: Ophthalmologist, optometrist
- Hearing: Audiologist
- Walking, feeding, dressing, eliminating: Physician, occupational therapist
- Speaking: Physician, speech-language pathologist
- Mental functions: Psychologist, physician

**Retroactive DTC Applications:**
DTC can be applied retroactively for up to 10 years (10-year statute of limitations on RRSP refund requests — similar for DTC). If an individual was impaired in prior years but didn't apply, CRA will reassess prior returns up to 10 years.

**Refundable DTC for Minors:**
If parent claims DTC for a child but has no tax to apply the credit against, the unused portion is refunded as a "Refundable Medical Expense Supplement" in some cases. Complex — review on a case-by-case basis.

**DTC and Registered Plans:**
DTC eligibility is required for:
- RDSP (Registered Disability Savings Plan) — see 5.5
- QDT designation (see 2.4)
- DTC supplement for CCC

### 7.4 Powers of Attorney

**Ontario Substitute Decisions Act, 1992:**

**Two Types:**

**1. Continuing Power of Attorney for Property (CPAP):**
- Authorizes attorney (not a lawyer — just a person appointed) to manage financial affairs
- "Continuing" means it continues even if the grantor becomes mentally incapable
- Covers: bank accounts, investments, real estate, business interests, taxes
- Does NOT cover: personal care decisions

**2. Power of Attorney for Personal Care (PAPC):**
- Authorizes attorney to make personal care decisions if grantor is incapable
- Covers: medical treatment, housing, nutrition, hygiene, safety, clothing, shelter
- Takes effect ONLY when the grantor is incapable of making their own decisions (unlike property POA which can be effective immediately)

**Choosing an Attorney:**
- Must be 18+ and mentally capable
- Should be trusted, available, and organized
- Consider: family member, close friend, trust company (professional trustee)
- Can appoint multiple attorneys to act jointly (require agreement) or jointly and severally (can act independently)

**Revocation:**
Can revoke POA at any time while mentally capable. Must give written notice to the attorney and any third parties who have been dealing with the attorney.

**When POA Is Critical:**
- Serious illness or accident
- Travel abroad
- Business operations need to continue
- Aging parents — set up POA BEFORE they lose capacity (cannot do so after)

**CRA and POA:**
CRA accepts POAs for tax purposes. Provide a copy to CRA if acting on another person's tax matters. CRA My Account allows authorized representatives to be added.

### 7.5 Probate Avoidance Strategies (Ontario)

**Ontario Estate Administration Tax (Probate Fees):**
- 0.5% on first $50,000 of estate
- 1.5% on estate value over $50,000
- Example: $1M estate = $14,250; $3M estate = $44,250; $10M estate = $149,250

**Assets Subject to Probate:**
- Assets solely owned by the deceased
- Assets with no named beneficiary
- Assets not held jointly with right of survivorship

**Assets Exempt from Probate:**
- RRSP/RRIF/TFSA/FHSA with named beneficiary (not estate)
- Life insurance with named beneficiary
- Jointly held property with right of survivorship
- Assets held in an inter vivos trust (Henson trust, alter ego trust, family trust)

**Strategy 1: Named Beneficiaries on Registered Accounts**
Ensure ALL registered accounts have named beneficiaries:
- RRSP: Name spouse as successor annuitant (direct rollover — no tax)
- RRIF: Same — successor annuitant
- TFSA: Name spouse as successor holder (tax-free transfer)
- Life insurance: Name individual beneficiary (not "estate")
- Cost: $0; Savings: $0 in probate, but simplifies estate administration

**Strategy 2: Joint Ownership (Right of Survivorship)**
Hold real estate, bank accounts, and investments as "joint tenants" with right of survivorship:
- On death, property transfers automatically to surviving joint tenant
- Bypasses probate
- Risk: Gifting to joint tenant may trigger capital gains and attribution issues
- Also risk of unintended gift on death if multiple relationships

**Strategy 3: Dual Will (Ontario)**
Ontario allows two wills:
- **Primary will:** Governs assets subject to probate (real estate, non-registered investments)
- **Secondary will:** Governs assets NOT requiring probate (private company shares, shareholder loans, personal property)
- Only the primary will is submitted for probate — secondary will probate fees avoided
- Savings for a business owner with $2M in company shares: ~$29,750 in probate fees avoided

**Strategy 4: Inter Vivos Trust**
Assets held in trust bypass probate. The trust holds assets; at settlor's death, assets remain in trust and are distributed per trust deed — no probate.
- Ideal for: Alter ego trust (65+), family trust (shares of OASIS Inc.)
- Cost: $3,000–$10,000 setup; savings scale with estate size

**Strategy 5: Multiple Wills with Trust Company**
For complex estates with multiple jurisdictions (real property in different provinces), separate wills for each province minimize probate in each.

### 7.6 Ontario Succession Law Reform Act (SLRA)

**Intestate Succession (Dying Without a Will):**
If an Ontario resident dies without a will, assets are distributed under the SLRA:
- Spouse: First $350,000 (preferential share), then 50% of remainder if one child, 33% if multiple children
- Children: Equal shares
- No provision for: Common-law partner (they get nothing under SLRA intestacy rules — must sue under other legislation)

**Why This Matters:**
If CC dies without a will:
- OASIS Inc. shares, trading accounts, crypto would go to parents (if no spouse/children)
- Common-law partner gets nothing under SLRA
- No charitable giving
- No trust provisions for siblings/disabled relatives
- No business succession plan

**Dependent Relief (Part V SLRA):**
Even with a will, a person who was "dependent" on the deceased can apply to court for adequate provision from the estate. Typical claimants: Spouse, common-law partner (cohabiting 3+ years or parents of a child together), children, parents.

**Key Takeaway:**
Every adult should have:
- A will (updated every 5 years or on major life events)
- CPAP and PAPC
- Named beneficiaries on all registered accounts


**Step 3: Issue T3 Slips**
For each beneficiary:
- Box 21: Taxable capital gains (from s.104(21) designation)
- Box 23/24: Eligible dividends and tax credit
- Box 25/26: Other dividends/income
- Slips due by March 31 (same as T3 filing deadline)

**Step 4: Prepare T3 Return (T3 Package includes):**
- T3 General form (front page, income calculation)
- Schedule 1 (federal tax calculation)

- Due: 90 days after year-end (March 31 for Dec 31 year-end)
- Attach all relevant schedules

**Step 6: Remit Balance Owing**
- Any balance owing due by the T3 filing deadline
- Interest on unpaid tax runs from the day after the filing deadline

### 8.7 Worked Example: Prescribed Rate Loan

**Setup:**
- Year: 2024
- Prescribed rate: 5% (current)
- CC loans $150,000 to family trust at 5%
- Trust invests in dividend-paying ETF portfolio, earns 8%/year
- Trust beneficiaries: CC's spouse (income: $35,000/year)

**Year 1 Calculation:**
```
Trust gross income: $150,000 × 8% =              $12,000
Less interest paid to CC: $150,000 × 5% =        ($7,500)
Net trust income:                                  $4,500
Trust allocates $4,500 to spouse

CC reports interest income: $7,500
Spouse reports dividend income: $4,500
```

**Tax Analysis:**
- CC's marginal rate (assume 43%): Tax on $7,500 = $3,225
- Spouse's marginal rate (~29%): Tax on $4,500 = $1,305
- Total family tax: $4,530

**Without Prescribed Rate Loan (CC earns all $12,000):**
- CC's tax at 43%: $12,000 × 43% = $5,160

**Annual savings: $630**

**Over 10 years (with growing portfolio and inflation):**
- Loan balance can grow (not mandatory to maintain at $150,000 — can make additional loans)
- If loan grows to $500,000 over 10 years (from reinvested income and additional loans):
  - Annual spread: $500,000 × 3% (8% − 5%) = $15,000 in spouse's hands
  - Annual savings at 43% vs. 29%: $15,000 × 14% = $2,100/year
- Cumulative 10-year savings: $15,000–$25,000+

**Remember:** Interest must be collected by January 30 each year. Missing this date retroactively kills the attribution protection for the entire year.

### 8.8 Worked Example: Henson Trust for ODSP Preservation

**Scenario:**
- CC's sibling (age 28) is DTC-eligible, receives ODSP ($1,228/month + health benefits = ~$23,000/year total value)
- CC's parent (age 60) wants to leave $400,000 to sibling in will
- Concern: $400,000 inheritance would disqualify sibling from ODSP (asset limit $40,000)

**Option A: Direct Bequest (No Trust)**
- Sibling receives $400,000
- ODSP ends immediately (exceeds $40,000 asset limit)
- Sibling loses $1,228/month + drug benefits + extended health
- Value of lost ODSP over 40 years (NPV at 3% discount): ~$400,000+
- The inheritance essentially replaces ODSP dollar-for-dollar — no net gain

**Option B: Henson Trust in Parent's Will**
- $400,000 left in testamentary Henson trust for sibling
- Absolute discretion — sibling has no right to demand payment
- ODSP continues: ODSP does not count trust assets (Henson trust case law)
- Trustee (CC or other trusted person) distributes for disability-related and quality of life expenses
- Additional government matching: Contribute $1,500/year to sibling's RDSP → receive $3,500 CDSG (low income)

**Tax Structure:**
- Testamentary Henson trust → QDT designation (sibling is DTC-eligible)
- Annual QDT election: Trust and sibling jointly elect
- $25,000 trust income allocated via PBE to sibling
- Tax at graduated rates: ~$3,750 (vs. $13,382 at flat 53.53%)
- Annual tax saving: $9,632

**10-Year Balance Sheet:**
- Trust principal: $400,000
- Annual income (5%): $20,000
- Less tax: $3,750
- Available for distributions: $16,250/year
- Trust value at 10 years (after distributions): ~$300,000
- Sibling continues to receive ODSP throughout

**Outcome:** Sibling benefits from $400,000 trust + ongoing ODSP + RDSP grants, with optimal tax efficiency. Compared to Option A: Sibling is ~$400,000 better off over 40 years.
