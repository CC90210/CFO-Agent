## 1. Accredited Investor Status (Canada)

**Timing:** `[FUTURE-ACCREDITED]` for most products | **CRA Audit Risk:** `[LOW RISK]` — accredited investor status is a securities law concept, not a tax concept. CRA does not audit your qualification.

### What It Is

The Canadian securities regulatory framework (National Instrument 45-106) restricts who can buy privately offered securities. Most investments in this document require you to be an **accredited investor** or to use a specific prospectus exemption. This is the gateway to the exempt market — the market where the real wealth-building products live.

### NI 45-106 Accredited Investor Definition

You qualify as an accredited investor if you meet **any one** of the following:

| Test | Threshold | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| **Individual income** | $200,000+ in each of the last 2 calendar years, with reasonable expectation of the same in current year | T4/T2125 income. Net of expenses for self-employed. |
| **Combined income (with spouse)** | $300,000+ in each of the last 2 years | Common law counts. |
| **Net financial assets** | $1,000,000+ | Financial assets = cash, securities, contracts of insurance, deposits. Excludes real estate, cars, personal property. |
| **Net assets** | $5,000,000+ | Everything minus everything owed. Real estate included. |
| **Registered under securities legislation** | Registered as adviser, dealer, etc. | Not applicable to CC currently. |
| **Company/trust with $5M+ net assets** | Entity-level qualification | Relevant once OASIS incorporates. |

**Key point:** For self-employed income, CRA defines income as net business income (line 23600 on T1). OASIS revenue minus expenses = the number that counts.

### Investor Tiers: Accredited vs. Permitted Client vs. Eligible Investor

| Tier | Definition | What It Unlocks |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| **Accredited Investor** (NI 45-106, s.1.1) | Meets one of the tests above | Exempt market products: private placements, offering memorandums, VC funds, MICs, private credit, hedge funds |
| **Permitted Client** (NI 31-103, s.1.1) | $5M+ net financial assets, OR registered individual, OR government entity | Exemption from suitability obligations. Dealer does not need to assess if the investment is "suitable" for you. Full access to all exempt products with no guardrails. |
| **Eligible Investor** (NI 45-106, s.2.10) | $400K+ net assets, OR $75K+ income + $400K net assets, OR accredited investor | Can purchase offering memorandum (OM) exempt products in Ontario. Lower bar than accredited. |

**Ontario-specific note:** Ontario has the **Offering Memorandum exemption** (NI 45-106, s.2.10) which allows eligible investors (not just accredited) to invest up to $30,000 per issuer per year ($100,000 if they meet the eligible investor test and get risk acknowledgement).

### Self-Certification Process

1. The issuer (or exempt market dealer) provides you with **NI 45-106F9** — the Risk Acknowledgement Form
2. You check which accredited investor category you fall under
3. You sign the form, certifying you meet the criteria
4. **No independent verification is required** — it is self-certified on the honour system
5. The dealer retains the form for 8 years
6. **Misrepresentation risk:** If you lie about qualification and the investment goes south, you lose your right to rescission (the right to get your money back within 48 hours)

### When CC Might Qualify

### Investing as an Angel

**Ontario Securities Commission exempt market rules for angel investing:**

1. **Accredited Investor Exemption (s.2.3):** If you qualify, you can invest in any private company with no dollar cap
2. **Offering Memorandum Exemption (s.2.10):** If you are an "eligible investor" ($400K net assets or $75K income + $400K net assets), you can invest up to $100,000 per issuer per year in Ontario
3. **Friends, Family, and Business Associates (s.2.5):** Founders can raise from close personal friends, family, close business associates, directors/officers — no accredited investor test needed
4. **Startup Crowdfunding (NI 45-110):** Up to $1,500 per offering, $2,500/year. No accreditation needed. FrontFundr, Wefunder Canada, GoTroo operate under this.
5. **Self-Certified Investor Exemption (proposed/some provinces):** Some provinces allow self-certification at lower thresholds

### Tax Treatment of Angel Investments

This is where alternative investments get genuinely powerful from a tax perspective.

#### ABIL — Allowable Business Investment Loss (s.38(c) ITA)

If a startup you invested in **fails** (becomes bankrupt, insolvent, or shares become worthless):

- The loss on shares of a **Canadian-Controlled Private Corporation (CCPC)** carrying on an active business qualifies as a **Business Investment Loss (BIL)**
- **50% of the BIL** is the Allowable Business Investment Loss (ABIL)
- ABIL is deductible against **ALL income** — employment, business, investment, anything
- This is extraordinarily powerful. Normal capital losses can only offset capital gains. ABIL offsets everything.
- **Example:** You invest $50,000 in a startup. It goes to zero. BIL = $50,000. ABIL = $25,000. At a 40% marginal rate, that is $10,000 in tax savings against your OASIS income.

**Conditions for ABIL:**
- Must be shares of a CCPC (or debt of a CCPC)
- The corporation must have been carrying on an active business (>90% of assets used in active business) at any time in the 12 months before disposition
- You must have acquired the shares from the corporation (not secondary market) or it must be a debt owing to you by the corporation
- The corporation must be bankrupt, insolvent, or you can elect a deemed disposition at nil under s.50(1) if the corporation is insolvent and the shares are worthless

**ABIL clawback:** Any ABIL claimed reduces your lifetime capital gains exemption (LCGE) dollar-for-dollar. Use strategically.

#### LCGE — Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (s.110.6 ITA)

If the startup **succeeds** and the shares qualify as QSBC (Qualified Small Business Corporation) shares:

- **$1,016,836** (2024 indexed, rising annually) of capital gains on QSBC shares are **tax-free**
- As of 2024 Budget: gains above $250,000 in a year have a 2/3 inclusion rate (up from 1/2). LCGE still shelters the full amount.
- This is how Canadian founders and early investors pay zero tax on startup exits up to $1M+

**QSBC share conditions (all must be met at time of sale):**
1. Shares of a CCPC
2. At time of disposition: 90%+ of FMV of assets used in active business carried on primarily in Canada (or shares/debt of connected SBCs)
3. 24-month holding period: throughout the 24 months before disposition, the shares were not owned by anyone other than the taxpayer or a related person
4. 24-month asset test: throughout the 24 months before disposition, 50%+ of FMV of assets used in active business

**Purification:** If a corporation has too many passive assets (cash, investments), you may need to "purify" before sale — pay out dividends, repay shareholder loans, or strip passive assets to a HoldCo. This is standard M&A tax planning.

#### Section 44.1 Deferral — QSBC Rollover

If you sell QSBC shares at a gain and reinvest the proceeds into **another QSBC** within **120 days**:

- You can **defer** the capital gain under s.44.1 ITA
- The gain is not recognized until you sell the replacement shares
- This is the Canadian equivalent of a US 1045 rollover
- **Requirements:**
  - Original shares must be QSBC shares
  - Replacement shares must be newly issued shares of a CCPC that is an eligible small business corporation
  - Investment must be made within 120 days after disposition
  - Must file election with T1 return

**Strategy chain:** Angel invest -> startup succeeds -> sell for $500K gain -> claim LCGE on first $1M -> reinvest remainder into next QSBC -> defer remaining gain under s.44.1 -> repeat.

| **Ontario LSVCC credit** | Was 5% (max $250). **ELIMINATED in 2012** for new investments. | **DEAD in Ontario** |

### TFSA and Private Company Shares

**Can you hold private company shares in a TFSA?** Yes, with strict conditions:

- The shares must be a "qualified investment" for a TFSA (s.207.01 ITA, Reg. 4900)
- Private company shares qualify IF:
  - The TFSA holder is **not connected** to the corporation (owns < 10% of shares of any class, directly or through non-arm's-length persons)
  - The shares are not "prohibited investments" (s.207.01(1)) — meaning you cannot hold shares of a corporation where you have a "significant interest" (10%+ ownership)
- **If you violate this:** 50% penalty tax on the FMV of the prohibited investment, plus 100% tax on any income/gains attributable to it
- **Strategy:** Invest $7,000/year TFSA contribution into angel deals where you hold < 10%. If the startup 100x, the entire gain is tax-free inside the TFSA. This is the "Peter Thiel strategy" adapted for Canada.

**Risk:** CRA has signaled increased scrutiny of TFSAs with outsized returns. The rules are clear, but audit risk increases with large balances.

**Tax treatment of SPV interests:**
- LP interests: flow-through taxation. Gains/losses flow to your personal return.
- Corporate SPV shares: capital gains/losses on disposition of shares.
- ABIL still available if underlying investment is in CCPC shares and conditions are met (but must trace through the structure).

- LPP includes: prints, etchings, drawings, paintings, sculptures, jewelry, rare folios/manuscripts, stamps, coins
- $1,000 ACB floor: if you paid less than $1,000, your ACB is deemed $1,000
- $1,000 proceeds floor: if you sell for less than $1,000, proceeds are deemed $1,000
- **LPP losses can only offset LPP gains** — cannot offset other capital gains
- 7-year carryback, unlimited carryforward for LPP losses

### Art & Collectibles

**Listed Personal Property (s.54 ITA) rules apply:**

- Art (prints, etchings, drawings, paintings, sculptures) is specifically enumerated as LPP
- **$1,000 ACB floor** on each item: if you paid $800 for a painting, your ACB is deemed $1,000
- **$1,000 proceeds floor** on each item: if you sell for $600, proceeds are deemed $1,000 (no loss recognized)
- Capital gains on sale above $1,000: standard inclusion rate
- **Losses on LPP can only offset gains on LPP** — this is a trap. If you lose $50K on art and make $50K on stocks, the art loss does NOT offset the stock gain.
- LPP losses carry back 3 years, forward 7 years (against LPP gains only)

**Donation of cultural property:**
- If property is certified by the Canadian Cultural Property Export Review Board, you can donate it and:
  - Claim FMV as a donation (no 75% of income cap — unlimited)
  - **No capital gain recognized** on the donation (s.118.1(10))
  - This is one of the most powerful tax maneuvers in the ITA for art collectors

### Wine & Spirits

- No Canadian tax incentive for wine/spirits investment
- Treated as **personal-use property** (not LPP — wine is not in the LPP enumeration)
- Personal-use property: $1,000 ACB floor, $1,000 proceeds floor. Gains taxable, **losses not deductible at all**
- Wine investment platforms (Vinovest, Cult Wine Investment) are not Canadian-regulated
- Liquor resale is heavily regulated by provincial liquor boards (LCBO in Ontario)

**Verdict for CC:** Avoid. No tax benefit, regulatory friction, illiquid.

## 4. Private Lending & Mortgage Investment Corporations (MICs)

**Timing:** Listed MIC shares (TSX) `[NOW]` | Private MIC shares `[FUTURE-ELIGIBLE]` | Direct mortgage lending `[NOW]` (no securities law restriction, just find a borrower)
**CRA Audit Risk:** `[LOW RISK]` — MIC taxation under s.130.1 is well-established. Interest income treatment is clear. No aggressive tax planning involved.

### Private Mortgage Lending

Direct private lending secured by real property. The classic "be the bank" alternative investment.

**How it works:**
1. Borrower needs a mortgage but does not qualify at a bank (self-employed, poor credit, bridge financing, construction)
2. You lend money secured by a registered mortgage on the property
3. Typical terms: 1-2 year term, 8-14% interest rate, 65-80% LTV (loan-to-value)
4. Interest is paid monthly, principal returned at maturity

**Tax treatment:**
- Interest income: **100% taxable** as ordinary income (full inclusion rate)
- This is the worst possible tax treatment. Hold in registered accounts if possible.
- If borrower defaults: you foreclose on the property. Legal costs and losses may be deductible.
- Bad debt deduction (s.20(1)(p)): if the debt becomes uncollectible, you can deduct it

**Risks:**
- Illiquidity (money is locked for 1-2 years)
- Default/foreclosure risk
- Property valuation risk (appraisals can be inflated)
- Concentration risk (one loan = one property)
- Ontario: mortgage brokers must be licensed under FSRA (Financial Services Regulatory Authority)

### Mortgage Investment Corporations (MICs)

**Section 130.1 ITA** defines a MIC as a Canadian corporation that:
- Has at least 20 shareholders
- Holds at least 50% of assets in residential mortgages, cash, or insured deposits
- Does not manage or develop real property
- Is not controlled by a non-resident

**Key feature:** MICs are **flow-through entities**. They must distribute all net income to shareholders. This income flows through as **interest income** (100% taxable) regardless of the actual source — even capital gains inside the MIC are recharacterized as interest.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Returns** | 6-12% annually (interest distributions) |
| **Tax treatment** | 100% taxable as interest income |
| **RRSP/TFSA eligible** | **YES** — this is the key advantage. Hold MIC shares in registered accounts to shelter the interest income entirely. |
| **Liquidity** | Low. Most MICs have redemption queues. Some publicly listed MICs trade on TSX/TSXV. |
| **Minimum investment** | $5,000-$25,000 typically (accredited investor usually required for private MICs) |

**Major Canadian MICs:**

| MIC | Focus | AUM | Listed? | Notes |
|-----|-------|-----|---------|-------|
| **Firm Capital Mortgage Investment Corp (FC.TO)** | First mortgages, Ontario | $1B+ | TSX | Conservative. 6-8% yield. |
| **MCAN Mortgage Corp (MKP.TO)** | Residential + construction | $500M+ | TSX | Also has uninsured single-family. |
| **Trez Capital Mortgage Investment Corp** | Construction, Western Canada | $4B+ | Private | Higher yield (8-10%), higher risk. |
| **Atrium Mortgage Investment Corp (AI.TO)** | Urban residential, Ontario | $800M+ | TSX | First + second mortgages. |
| **Canadian Western Trust MIC** | Diversified | Private | Private | Institutional-grade. |

**Strategy for CC:** When registered account room allows, allocate MIC shares inside TFSA. 8% return growing tax-free inside TFSA is equivalent to ~12% pre-tax in a non-registered account at CC's marginal rate.

### Promissory Notes (Direct Business Lending)

Lending directly to a business via a promissory note:

- **Tax treatment:** Interest income (100% taxable)
- **Documentation:** Must have a written promissory note with: principal amount, interest rate, payment schedule, maturity date, security (if any), default provisions
- **s.20(1)(p) deduction:** If the debt becomes uncollectible, you can claim a bad debt deduction
- **Risk:** Unsecured promissory notes are essentially goodwill lending. Always take security (personal guarantee, UCC/PPSA filing, specific asset pledge)

### Convertible Notes

A loan that converts into equity upon a triggering event (usually a priced financing round):

**Tax treatment on conversion:**
- The conversion is a **disposition of the debt** and **acquisition of equity**
- Proceeds of disposition of the note = FMV of shares received
- If FMV of shares > ACB of note: capital gain
- If FMV of shares < ACB of note: capital loss (but if the company is a CCPC, may qualify as ABIL)
- **Accrued but unpaid interest** at conversion: still taxable as interest income in the year it accrues or is received (depending on your reporting method)
- **OID (Original Issue Discount):** If the note was issued at a discount, the discount amount is taxable as interest income over the life of the note under s.12(4) accrual rules

**Tip:** If the conversion terms are favourable (e.g., 20% discount to next round valuation), you acquire shares at below-market cost. The difference is not taxable at conversion — it simply means your ACB is lower, and the gain is larger on eventual sale. If QSBC conditions are met, LCGE shelters it.

### Revenue-Based Financing (as Investor)

Providing capital to a company in exchange for a percentage of future revenue until a return cap is reached (typically 1.5-3x the investment):

- **Tax treatment:** CRA has not issued specific guidance. Most likely:
  - Payments received are **interest income** until total return equals the principal
  - Payments above principal: still likely interest income (it is a return on a loan-like instrument)
  - If the instrument converts to equity: follow convertible note treatment above
- **Not well-suited for registered accounts** due to uncertain qualified investment status
- Emerging in Canada through platforms like **Clearco** (formerly Clearbanc) — though Clearco is the borrower-side platform, not investor-side

### Flow-Through Shares (FTS)

**The single most powerful tax deduction available to Canadian individuals.**

**How they work:**
1. A resource company (mining, oil & gas, renewable energy) issues shares
2. The company "flows through" its exploration/development expenses to the investor
3. The investor deducts 100% of the flowed-through expenses against ANY income
4. When shares are sold, ACB is zero (or negative — capped at zero), so the **entire proceeds are a capital gain**
5. Net effect: **convert fully taxable income into capital gains** (50% or 66.67% inclusion rate)

**Tax math example:**
- CC has $100K OASIS income, marginal rate ~33% combined (Ontario + federal)
- Buys $25,000 in flow-through shares
- Deducts $25,000 from income. Tax saving: $25,000 x 33% = **$8,250**
- Eventually sells shares for $20,000 (typical — FTS often drop 10-30% after issuance)
- Capital gain: $20,000 (ACB = $0). Tax: $20,000 x 50% inclusion x 33% = **$3,300**
- **Net benefit: $8,250 saved - $3,300 tax on sale = $4,950 net tax savings on a $25K investment**
- Plus you still have the $20,000 in cash

**Additional super deduction credits:**
- **Canadian Exploration Expense (CEE):** 100% deductible in the year incurred
- **Canadian Development Expense (CDE):** 30% declining balance
- **Mining Exploration Tax Credit (s.127(10.1)):** Additional 15% federal non-refundable credit on certain CEE (surface grassroots mining exploration). This stacks on top of the deduction.
- **Ontario Focused Flow-Through Share Tax Credit:** 5% provincial credit on eligible Ontario mining exploration (check current availability)

**Flow-through share funds/limited partnerships:**

| Issuer | Focus | Min Investment |
|--------|-------|---------------|
| **Ninepoint FTS funds** | Diversified resource | $5,000+ |
| **Middlefield FTS funds** | Mining/energy | $5,000+ |
| **PearTree Financial** | FTS dealer/structurer | $5,000+ (accredited often required) |
| **Resource Capital FTS** | Mining-focused | $5,000+ |

**Timing:** FTS offerings are typically in Q4 (October-December) for tax year deductions. The shares are held for ~2 years before being sold.

**Key risk:** You are buying shares in mining/energy companies. The underlying investment can go to zero. The tax deduction partially compensates for this risk, but not entirely.

### SR&ED Partnerships

**Scientific Research and Experimental Development (SR&ED)** tax credits are Canada's largest business subsidy program ($3B+/year). Normally used by companies, but can be structured for investors:

- Invest in a partnership that conducts SR&ED activities
- Partnership flows through SR&ED expenditures and tax credits to limited partners
- **Federal ITC:** 15% non-refundable credit (was 20% for CCPCs, reduced in recent budgets for non-CCPC claimants)
- **Ontario Innovation Tax Credit (OITC):** 8% of eligible Ontario SR&ED expenditures (refundable for CCPCs)

**Caution:** CRA heavily scrutinizes SR&ED tax shelter partnerships. The "at-risk" rules (s.96(2.2)) limit your deduction to the amount you actually have at risk. Leveraged SR&ED shelters are essentially dead after CRA crackdowns.

**Better path for CC:** If OASIS qualifies for SR&ED directly (software development can qualify if it involves technological uncertainty and systematic investigation), claim the credits directly through the business. More valuable than investing in someone else's SR&ED.

### Community Bonds & Social Impact Investments

**Community bonds** are debt instruments issued by non-profits and social enterprises:

- Typical terms: 3-5 year maturity, 3-5% interest
- Not securities under Ontario exemptions for community organizations (OSC has specific carve-outs)
- Tax treatment: interest income (fully taxable)
- Some may be eligible for Community Economic Development Investment Funds (CEDIFs) tax credits in some provinces (Nova Scotia primarily)

**Social impact bonds (SIBs):**
- Government contracts where investors fund social programs, and returns are based on outcomes

- Tax treatment: likely interest income or business income depending on structure

**Verdict for CC:** Low priority. Returns are low, tax treatment is unfavourable (interest income), and the social benefit is the primary draw rather than wealth building.

### Donation of Cultural Property

One of the most powerful and least-known tax strategies in the ITA:

**How it works:**
1. You acquire property that qualifies as Canadian cultural property (art, artifacts, scientific specimens, archival material)
2. You apply to the **Canadian Cultural Property Export Review Board (CCPERB)** for certification
3. If certified, you donate the property to a designated institution (museum, gallery, library, archive)
4. **Tax benefits:**
   - Donation receipt for **fair market value** (determined by CCPERB)
   - **No capital gains tax** on the disposition (s.118.1(10) — complete exemption)
   - Donation credit has **no limit** as a percentage of income (normally donations are capped at 75% of net income — this cap does not apply to certified cultural property)
   - Unused credit carries forward 5 years

**Example:**
- Buy a painting for $10,000
- Hold for 10 years, FMV appreciates to $100,000
- CCPERB certifies it
- Donate to Art Gallery of Ontario
- Donation receipt: $100,000 (federal credit ~29% + Ontario credit ~11.16% on first $200, higher tier on rest)
- Tax savings: ~$40,000 in credits
- Capital gain of $90,000: **completely exempt**
- Net cost: $10,000 purchase - $40,000 tax savings = **$30,000 net positive** (plus you no longer have the painting)

**Practical note:** This requires genuine cultural property and a real donation to a designated institution. It is not a loophole — it is a deliberate policy to keep cultural property in Canada. But it is extraordinarily tax-efficient for those who collect art.

## 9. Exempt Market Dealers — How to Access Private Placements

**Timing:** `[FUTURE-ELIGIBLE]` for OM products | `[FUTURE-ACCREDITED]` for full exempt market
**CRA Audit Risk:** `[LOW RISK]` — accessing investments through a registered EMD does not itself create tax risk. Risk is in the underlying investment.

### What Is an Exempt Market Dealer (EMD)?

An **Exempt Market Dealer** is a firm registered with provincial securities regulators (OSC in Ontario) under National Instrument 31-103 to trade in **exempt market securities** — private placements that are sold without a full prospectus.

EMDs are the gatekeepers to:
- Private MIC shares
- Private real estate syndications
- Private credit funds
- VC fund units
- Flow-through share partnerships
- Any offering sold under an exemption (accredited investor, OM, close personal friend, etc.)

**Key distinction:** A regular brokerage (IIROC-registered dealer) can only sell publicly listed securities. An EMD can sell private securities under exemptions. Many EMDs are both (dually registered).

### How to Open an Account with an EMD

1. **Find a registered EMD:** Search the CSA National Registration Search at [www.securities-administrators.ca](https://www.securities-administrators.ca). Filter for Ontario, Exempt Market Dealer category.
2. **KYC (Know Your Client) package:** Complete a detailed questionnaire covering:
   - Net worth (financial assets + real estate - liabilities)
   - Annual income (last 2 years)
   - Investment knowledge (none/limited/good/sophisticated)
   - Investment objectives (growth/income/preservation)
   - Risk tolerance (low/medium/high)
   - Time horizon
3. **Accredited Investor / Eligible Investor declaration:** Sign **NI 45-106F9** (Risk Acknowledgement Form) certifying your qualification category.
4. **Suitability assessment:** The EMD's Dealing Representative (DR) must assess that each investment is "suitable" for you based on your KYC profile — unless you are a Permitted Client ($5M+ financial assets).
5. **Review the Offering Memorandum (OM):** Every OM-exempt product must provide you with an OM (like a mini-prospectus). Read it. Key sections: use of proceeds, risk factors, audited financials, management team, distribution policy.
6. **48-hour rescission right (Ontario):** Under s.73.2 of the Ontario Securities Act, you can rescind (cancel) an OM purchase within **2 business days** of signing the subscription. Know this right — it is your safety net.

### The Offering Memorandum — What to Scrutinize

The OM is a legal document. Key sections to read before signing:

| Section | What to Look For | Red Flags |
|---------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Use of Proceeds** | Clear allocation of your capital | Vague language, high "management fees" taken upfront |
| **Management Team** | Track record, prior funds, regulatory history | No verifiable track record, principals with past securities violations |
| **Auditor** | National firm preferred | No auditor, or offshore unknown auditor |
| **Custodian** | Third-party custodian holds assets | Manager controls custody (Bridging Finance failure pattern) |
| **Related-Party Transactions** | Arm's-length dealings | Loans to principals, management company fees that are excessive |
| **Redemption Terms** | Quarterly or better | Annual or "at manager's discretion" — illiquid trap |
| **Conflicts of Interest** | Disclosed and managed | Undisclosed conflicts |
| **Fee Structure** | Management fee + performance fee | Front-end loads above 5%, hurdle rates that benefit manager excessively |

### KYC Suitability — What the EMD Is Assessing

The suitability obligation requires the EMD to ensure:
- The investment fits your **risk tolerance** (high-risk privates should not be recommended to low-risk clients)
- The investment fits your **time horizon** (a 5-year locked product is unsuitable if you need the money in 2 years)
- The investment fits your **investment knowledge** (sophisticated products require sophisticated investors)
- The investment is not **too concentrated** in one sector or issuer given your overall portfolio

**Self-protection:** If a DR recommends an investment and you later lose money, and you can show the investment was unsuitable given your KYC, you may have a complaint/compensation avenue through the **OBSI (Ombudsman for Banking Services and Investments)** or OSC.

### Dealing Representatives — How They Are Compensated

EMD Dealing Representatives are typically compensated by:
- **Trailer fees** (annual % of AUM in the product, paid by the issuer)
- **Upfront commission** (1-3% of investment, paid by the issuer)
- **No fee to you directly** — issuer pays

This creates a conflict of interest: the DR may favor products with higher trailers. Ask the DR directly: "What trailer fee or commission does your firm receive on this product?" They are legally obligated to disclose this.

## 10. Tax Structures for Holding Alternatives

**Timing:** Bare trusts `[NOW]` (but new reporting obligations) | Investment HoldCo `[FUTURE-ACCREDITED]` (triggered at ~$150K+ after-tax income to deploy) | Limited partnerships `[NOW]` (flow-through from day one)
**CRA Audit Risk:** Bare trusts `[HIGH RISK]` (new 2024 reporting, CRA attention) | HoldCo passive income `[MEDIUM RISK]` (standard corporate tax, but AII grind is a trap) | LPs `[MEDIUM RISK]` (T5013 reporting is stringent)

### Limited Partnerships (LPs)

The dominant structure for alternative investments in Canada.

**How it works:**
- A **General Partner (GP)** manages the investment (VC fund, real estate syndication, FTS fund, etc.)
- **Limited Partners (LPs)** provide capital and have liability limited to their investment
- All income, losses, and tax credits **flow through** to LP tax returns in proportion to their partnership interest
- LPs receive a **T5013 (Statement of Partnership Income)** each year
- The LP interest itself is capital property — gains on selling your LP unit are capital gains

**T5013 — Critical reporting requirements:**
- Issued by the partnership's accountant by March 31
- Shows: income/loss allocation by type (business income, interest, rental, capital gains, foreign income), resource expenses (CEE, CDE), investment tax credits (METC, ITC)
- Must be reported on T1 return in the year shown, regardless of whether cash was distributed
- **Common error:** Failing to report T5013 amounts because the investor "didn't receive any cash." CRA will reassess. Always report T5013 items.

**At-risk rules (s.96(2.2) ITA):**
- You can only deduct losses from an LP to the extent of your **at-risk amount** (roughly: amount invested + allocated income - distributions - any guarantees against loss)
- If you borrow to invest in an LP and the lender has recourse against you, the borrowed amount is at-risk
- If you borrow and the lender has NO recourse (non-recourse loans from the GP/promoter), that amount is NOT at-risk
- Leveraged tax shelters were historically structured with non-recourse loans to inflate deductions. CRA cracked down. **Do not use non-recourse leveraged shelters.**

**Superficial loss rules and LPs:**
- Disposing of an LP unit at a loss and reacquiring within 30 days = superficial loss. Denied.
- Relevant if rebalancing across similar FTS funds in the same tax year.

### Bare Trusts — 2024/2025 Reporting Changes

**What is a bare trust?**
A legal arrangement where one person (the bare trustee) holds legal title to property on behalf of another person (the beneficial owner) who has all the economic rights. The trustee has no discretion and must follow the beneficial owner's instructions.

**Common bare trust situations:**
- Parents hold real estate title while adult child is the true owner (e.g., to help with mortgage qualification)
- Business partners hold corporate shares "in trust" for each other during a deal
- Nominee corporations holding title to real estate
- Cryptocurrency held on an exchange in CC's account but economically belonging to someone else (or vice versa)

**Pre-2024 treatment:** Bare trusts were largely invisible to CRA. No separate tax filing required.

**2024 Budget change — New T3 filing obligation (s.150(1)(c.1) ITA):**
- Effective for trust tax years ending after December 30, 2023
- Bare trusts are now required to file a **T3 Trust Income Tax and Information Return**
- Must disclose: identity of trustees, identity of beneficiaries, identity of settlors, value of trust property
- **Penalty for non-filing:** $25/day, minimum $100, maximum $2,500 per failure. Plus gross negligence penalty of 5% of highest FMV of trust property (minimum $2,500) if willful.

**CRA flip-flop:** CRA originally granted administrative relief for bare trusts in 2024 (waived penalties for trusts that otherwise met conditions). For 2025 and forward, the obligation is fully in force. **Do not assume the exemption continues.**

**Practical implications for CC:**
- If CC holds any property "for" or "on behalf of" another person, or another person holds property for CC, document it immediately
- Common scenario: parents co-sign a mortgage or hold title temporarily — this creates a bare trust
- Another scenario: CC transfers crypto to a family member's exchange account — potentially a bare trust depending on arrangement
- **Action item:** Review all asset arrangements where legal title and beneficial ownership diverge. File T3 for all such arrangements going forward.

**Audit risk:** CRA has flagged bare trust non-compliance as a priority area. The new disclosure regime is designed to surface previously invisible arrangements. `[HIGH RISK]` if non-compliant. `[LOW RISK]` if properly filed.

### Investment Holding Corporation (HoldCo) for Alternatives

**Timing:** `[FUTURE-ACCREDITED]` — only makes sense once CC is incorporating OASIS and has $100K+ to deploy annually into investments.

**The structure:**
```
CC (individual)
    |
OASIS OpCo Inc. (active business income — 12.2% small biz rate)
    |
OASIS HoldCo Inc. (passive investment portfolio)
    |
Alternatives: MICs, LP units, private credit, angel investments
```

**Why use a HoldCo:**
- Transfer after-tax profits from OpCo to HoldCo via intercorporate dividend (tax-free under s.112)
- HoldCo invests at the corporate level — higher after-tax dollars deployed
- Tax deferral: if CC earns $100K in OpCo, pays ~12.2% corporate tax ($12,200), transfers $87,800 to HoldCo instead of paying ~40% personal tax ($40,000) and having only $60,000 to invest

**The passive income trap — Aggregate Investment Income (AII):**
This is the critical planning point that most people miss.

Under s.125(5.1) ITA (enacted 2018 Budget), **a CCPC's small business deduction (SBD) access is reduced** when the CCPC has passive income above $50,000:
- For every $1 of AII (passive investment income) above $50,000, the SBD limit is reduced by $5
- At $150,000+ of AII, the SBD is fully eliminated (OpCo pays 26.5% instead of 12.2% on active business income)
- **This can cost $14,300+/year in extra taxes on $100K of active income** if the HoldCo generates too much passive income

**AII threshold math:**
| HoldCo Passive Income | SBD Reduction | Active Income Tax Rate |
|----------------------|--------------|----------------------|
| $0-$50,000 | None | 12.2% |
| $100,000 | 50% reduction | 19.35% blended |
| $150,000+ | Full elimination | 26.5% |

**Mitigation strategies:**
1. **Keep HoldCo passive income below $50K/year** — invest primarily in growth assets that defer income (private equity, non-distribution ETFs, zero-coupon instruments)
2. **Use capital gains instruments** — capital gains are only 50% included in AII. A $100K capital gain = $50K AII. A $50K interest income = $50K AII. For the same dollar amount, prefer capital appreciation over yield inside a HoldCo.
3. **MICs inside registered accounts (TFSA/RRSP)** — MIC distributions are interest income, terrible inside a HoldCo if it pushes AII above $50K. Hold MIC positions in personal registered accounts instead.
4. **Life insurance inside HoldCo** — CSV (cash surrender value) growth inside a corporate-owned life insurance (COLI) policy is NOT included in AII. Using COLI to shelter investment growth is a legitimate strategy (see ATLAS_INSURANCE_ESTATE_PROTECTION.md).
5. **Loan funds to an affiliated trust or operating company** — interest paid between affiliated entities may be deductible at one level and income at another, allowing recharacterization.

**RDTOH (Refundable Dividend Tax on Hand) — the HoldCo offset mechanism:**
- When HoldCo pays tax on passive income, 30.67% of that tax goes into an RDTOH account
- When HoldCo pays taxable dividends to CC, $1 of RDTOH is refunded to HoldCo for every $2.61 in dividends paid
- This creates a "refund" mechanism that partially offsets the corporate-level tax
- Net effect: passive income is taxed at roughly the same rate whether earned personally or corporately — the deferral benefit comes from using the pre-tax dollars, not from a permanently lower rate

**Practical decision rule:**
- Active business income in OpCo? Deploy into business growth first.
- Surplus capital above business needs? Flow to HoldCo.
- HoldCo's annual passive income below $50K? Invest freely in alternatives.
- HoldCo's annual passive income approaching $50K? Shift to capital-gain-focused instruments, COLI, or consider distributing to CC personally if marginal rate differential is small.

## 11. Carried Interest & Fund Manager Tax Treatment

**Timing:** `[FUTURE-ACCREDITED]` — relevant if CC ever manages a fund (VC, hedge fund, private credit) rather than just investing in one.
**CRA Audit Risk:** `[HIGH RISK]` — carried interest characterization is one of the most actively litigated areas in Canadian tax. The capital gains vs. income debate is unresolved in Canada.

### What Is Carried Interest?

When a fund manager raises a private fund (LP structure), the economics are typically:
- **Management fee:** 2% of AUM per year. Paid by the fund. Compensation for managing the fund. **Business income** to the GP — fully taxable at marginal rates.
- **Carried interest (carry):** 20% of fund profits above the hurdle rate (typically 8%). This is the GP's profit share from successful investments. The tax treatment of carry is where the debate lies.

### The Capital Gains vs. Income Debate

**Canada's position (current CRA administrative view):**
- CRA has not issued definitive guidance on carried interest
- Current practice: many Canadian fund managers report carry as **capital gains** on the basis that the carry represents a share of capital appreciation of partnership assets
- The fund manager holds a GP interest (a form of equity interest in the LP), and the appreciation of that interest is capital

**The challenge:**
- The carry is received for performing services (managing the fund). It is economically compensation.
- CRA has assessed some carried interest as **business income** under the "adventure in the nature of trade" doctrine or as a form of employment/service income
- The ITA does not have a specific carried interest provision (unlike the US)
- **Risk:** If CRA characterizes carry as business income, the tax rate doubles (roughly 40-53% vs. 25-27% for capital gains)

**How sophisticated Canadian fund managers structure carry:**
1. **Carry through GP entity (corporation):** The GP interest is held by a CCPC. Carry flows into the corporation as capital gains (50% inclusion). The corporation pays 12.2% on active business income — but wait, is carry active business income? If yes, 12.2%. If no, 26.5% passive income. Ambiguous.
2. **GP interests held personally:** Carry taxed as capital gain at individual level — 50% inclusion, potentially LCGE-eligible if the GP interest qualifies as QSBC shares (extremely rare — requires active business, assets test, etc.)
3. **Structuring the fund as a limited partnership:** Each investment in the LP is a separate property. The GP's share of gains on each portfolio investment may be reported as a capital gain on that specific property.
4. **Retention/co-investment:** GP co-invests real capital alongside LPs. The return on co-investment is clearly capital gains. The carry on top of co-investment is ambiguous.

**Best practice for CC (if ever running a fund):**
- Retain a Big 4 or top-tier boutique tax advisor before structuring the fund
- Consider US-style "profits interest" provisions — these do not exist in Canadian law, but the economic structure can approximate the US treatment
- Document that the carry is contingent on capital appreciation, not on services performed — this strengthens the capital gains argument
- The CRA's position has not been tested in the Tax Court of Canada in a landmark case. This is genuinely unsettled law.

**2024 Budget — Increased Capital Gains Inclusion Rate:**
The federal government's 2024 Budget proposal to increase the capital gains inclusion rate to 2/3 above $250K:
- If carry is capital gains and exceeds $250K in a year: inclusion rate = 66.67% (not 50%)
- This significantly reduced the capital gains vs. income benefit for high-carry fund managers
- Many Canadian fund managers are re-examining their structures post-Budget 2024

### T5013 Reporting for Fund Managers

GP entities must file:
- **T5013 partnership return** (T5013 FIN — financial return, plus schedules for each partner)
- Due March 31 for December 31 year-end partnerships
- Must allocate income/loss to each LP according to partnership agreement
- **SRED credits, resource expenses, ITC** must also be allocated and reported on T5013

**Penalty for late T5013:** $25/day per T5013 slip, minimum $100, maximum $2,500. Plus interest on any tax owing. Filing T5013s is non-negotiable.
