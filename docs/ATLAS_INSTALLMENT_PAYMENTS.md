## 1. What Are Installment Payments?

Installment payments are **prepayments of estimated annual income tax**, remitted to CRA on a quarterly schedule during the tax year itself — rather than in one lump sum after the year ends.

CRA requires installments from taxpayers whose tax is not being withheld at source throughout the year. Employment income (T4) has tax deducted by the employer automatically. Self-employment income, business income, investment income, rental income, DJ gig income, and crypto trading gains do not. When enough un-withheld income accumulates, the government requires you to prepay.

**The core logic:** CRA wants their money throughout the year, not all at once on April 30 of the following year. Installments are their mechanism for enforcing this.

**Who this affects for CC:**
- OASIS AI Solutions revenue (no employer withholding)
- DJ gig income (cash and e-transfer, no withholding)
- Crypto trading gains (no withholding)
- Net rental income (if applicable in future)
- Investment income exceeding the tax withheld at source

**Who this does NOT affect:**
- Nicky's Donuts T4 income — tax is already withheld at source by the employer
- RRSP withdrawals where withholding tax is deducted at source (partial offset)

The installment system is governed primarily by **ITA ss.155, 156, 156.1, 157, 161, and 163.1**. Failure to pay results in interest and potentially a penalty surcharge, but there is **no criminal consequence** for missing installments — it is purely a cost-of-money issue.

## 2. When Are Installments Required?

### 2.1 The Two-Year Lookback Rule (s.156(1))

Individuals are required to pay installments for a taxation year when **both** of the following conditions are met:

```
Condition A: Net tax owing in the CURRENT year exceeds $3,000 (federal)
                         AND
Condition B: Net tax owing exceeded $3,000 in EITHER of the two preceding years
```

**ITA s.156(1):** An individual shall pay a quarterly installment for a taxation year if the individual's net tax owing for the year or for either of the two preceding taxation years exceeds $3,000.

This is the most important rule in the installment system:

**If you cross $3,000 for the first time — no installments are due for that year.** You owe the full amount on April 30 of the following year. But starting the next year, you are in the system.

### 2.2 The First-Year Exception — Illustrated

| Tax Year | Net Tax Owing | Installments Due? | Reason |
|----------|---------------|-------------------|--------|
| 2024 | $0 | No | Below threshold |
| 2025 | $0 | No | Below threshold |
| 2026 | $4,200 | **No** | First year over $3K — no prior year trigger |
| 2027 | $5,500 | **Yes** | 2026 exceeded $3K (one of two prior years) |
| 2028 | $6,800 | Yes | Both 2026 and 2027 exceeded $3K |

**Translation for CC:** In the year CC first breaks the $3,000 net-tax-owing threshold, he will owe that full amount on April 30 of the following year — but he does NOT need to make installments during that year. The installment obligation begins the year after the first threshold-crossing.

### 2.3 "Net Tax Owing" — What This Means

"Net tax owing" is **not** the same as gross federal tax. It is defined in ITA s.156.1(1) as:

```
Net Tax Owing =
  Federal tax payable (Part I)
+ Ontario provincial tax
+ CPP self-employment contributions (both halves)
+ Any other amounts (OAS repayment, minimum tax, etc.)
MINUS:
  Tax withheld at source (T4 box 22 amounts)
  Refundable credits already credited
  CPP/EI credits on employment income
```

**Critical:** CPP on self-employment is included in net tax owing. For a self-employed person paying 11.9% CPP on up to $68,500 of net self-employment income (2024 maximum), the CPP contribution alone can add thousands to the net-tax-owing figure — pushing you over the $3,000 threshold faster than income tax alone would.

**Example — Why CPP accelerates installment triggering:**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| OASIS net income | $30,000 |
| Federal tax (after basic personal amount) | ~$2,100 |
| Ontario tax | ~$1,050 |
| CPP (self-employed, both halves, 11.9%) | ~$3,570 |
| Less: T4 source deductions (Nicky's) | -$4 |
| **Net tax owing** | **~$6,716** |

At $30,000 OASIS income, CC's net tax owing already exceeds $3,000 — primarily because CPP self-employment contributions of 11.9% add up fast.

## 3. The $3,000 Threshold — Exact Calculation

### 3.1 Federal vs. Combined Threshold

The $3,000 threshold is a **combined federal + provincial threshold** for most provinces because the provinces have harmonized their collection with CRA. For Ontario specifically:

- **Federal threshold:** $3,000 (ITA s.156(1))
- **Ontario threshold:** $3,000 (Ontario matches the federal rule under the Tax Collection Agreements Act)
- **Combined test:** Both federal and Ontario installments are assessed together using the same $3,000 test

CRA collects both federal and Ontario taxes in a single installment payment. You do not send separate cheques to Ottawa and Queen's Park.

### 3.2 Step-by-Step Threshold Calculation

**Step 1:** Calculate total federal income tax payable (Part I, Line 42000 on T1)

**Step 2:** Add Ontario provincial income tax (Line 42800 on T1)

**Step 3:** Add CPP contributions on self-employment income (Line 42100 — both employee and employer halves)

**Step 4:** Add any other charges (OAS repayment s.180.2, minimum tax s.127.5, etc.)

**Step 5:** Subtract all tax withheld at source (Box 22 from all T4/T4A slips, Line 43700)

**Step 6:** Subtract refundable credits (GST/HST credit, CAIP, Canada Child Benefit if applicable)

**Step 7:** The result is "net tax owing" (Line 48500 on T1 assessment)

**If Line 48500 > $3,000 in the current year AND > $3,000 in either of the two prior years: installments are required.**

### 3.3 The CPP Self-Employment Trap

Most people think the $3,000 threshold is just about income tax. It is not. For CC as a sole proprietor:

- CPP self-employment rate (2025): **11.9%** on net self-employment income
- CPP maximum pensionable earnings (2025): **$71,300** (after $3,500 basic exemption = $67,800 maximum)
- Maximum CPP contribution (both halves): **$8,068.80** (2025)
- CPP is included in "net tax owing" under ITA s.156.1(1)

**CPP alone crosses $3,000 at approximately $25,210 of net self-employment income:**

| OASIS Net Income | CPP Both Halves (11.9%) | Threshold Status |
|-----------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| $20,000 | $2,378 | Below $3K — no installments |
| $25,210 | $2,999 | At threshold |
| $26,000 | $3,094 | **Over threshold** |
| $35,000 | $4,165 | Solidly over threshold |
| $50,000 | $5,950 | Well over threshold |

Even if income tax is zero (because RRSP deductions and credits bring it to zero), CPP self-employment contributions can still trigger the installment requirement.

## 4. Ontario Threshold Differences

### 4.1 Ontario — Fully Harmonized

Ontario is one of the provinces that has signed a Tax Collection Agreement with the federal government. This means:

- CRA administers Ontario personal income tax on behalf of the province
- Ontario installments are collected together with federal installments in a single remittance
- The same $3,000 threshold applies to the combined federal + Ontario obligation
- Ontario has **no separate installment filing** — everything flows through your CRA T1 and installment process

**Practical result:** When CRA sends you an installment reminder (Form INNS1), it covers both your federal and Ontario tax obligations. Pay one amount to CRA. Done.

### 4.2 What Ontario Tax Is Included

Ontario provincial income tax at 2025 rates:

| Ontario Taxable Income | Rate |
|------------------------|------|
| $0 – $51,446 | 5.05% |
| $51,447 – $102,894 | 9.15% |
| $102,895 – $150,000 | 11.16% |
| $150,001 – $220,000 | 12.16% |
| Over $220,000 | 13.16% |

Ontario also adds:
- **Ontario surtax** (at higher incomes — not relevant until ~$5,874 Ontario tax owed)
- **Ontario Health Premium** ($0 under $20K; up to $900 at higher incomes — included in net tax owing calculation)
- **Dividend Tax Credit** (reduces Ontario tax on eligible/ineligible dividends — relevant post-incorporation)

### 4.3 Quebec Exception

Quebec is the **only** province that runs its own separate personal income tax system. Quebec residents file with both CRA (federal) and Revenu Québec (provincial) separately. If CC ever moves to Montreal (as planned), this creates a separate provincial installment obligation to Revenu Québec. The federal threshold remains $3,000 but Quebec has its own installment rules. **Flag for relocation planning.**

## 5. Quarterly Due Dates

### 5.1 Individual (Sole Proprietor) Installment Dates

For individuals including self-employed persons, installments are due **quarterly** on:

| Quarter | Due Date | Period Covered |
|---------|----------|----------------|
| Q1 | **March 15** | January 1 – March 31 |
| Q2 | **June 15** | April 1 – June 30 |
| Q3 | **September 15** | July 1 – September 30 |
| Q4 | **December 15** | October 1 – December 31 |

**ITA s.156(1):** Four equal installments on the 15th day of March, June, September, and December of the taxation year.

**If the 15th falls on a weekend or holiday:** The installment is due the next business day. CRA builds this grace into their processing automatically.

### 5.2 Business Day Rule

If March 15 is a Sunday, your installment is due Monday March 16. CRA does not charge interest for payments received on the first business day after a weekend or holiday due date.

### 5.3 Payment Methods

CRA accepts installment payments by:

1. **CRA My Payment (online banking)** — Pay directly from your bank account via CRA's portal. Recommended.
2. **Pre-authorized debit** — Set up automatic quarterly withdrawals through CRA My Account.
3. **Financial institution online bill payment** — Add CRA as a payee (use "CRA (revenue) – 2024 personal income tax installment"). Use your SIN as the account number.
4. **In person at a bank** — With a CRA remittance voucher (Form INNS1 or INNS3).
5. **Mail** — Cheque payable to "Receiver General for Canada" with your SIN and tax year on the memo line.

**Note:** Credit card payments to CRA are not available directly — only through third-party services that charge a fee (typically 1.4-2.5%). Never worthwhile given installment interest is only the prescribed rate + 2%.

### 5.4 How CRA Notifies You

CRA sends **Form INNS1** (installment reminder) in February and August of each year to taxpayers who made installments the prior year or who owed > $3,000 in either of the two prior years. The form includes:

- Amount to pay under the **no-calculation option** (Method 1)
- Due dates for the upcoming two installments
- Payment instructions

CRA also sends **Form INNS3** (final installment reminder) showing all four installments if you did not pay the first two.

**If you do not receive a reminder:** You are still legally required to pay if you meet the threshold. Check CRA My Account. Ignorance of the obligation does not eliminate the interest charge.

## 6. The Three Calculation Methods

ITA s.156(1) provides three distinct methods for calculating installment amounts. You may choose any method — but CRA assesses interest based on the method that would have resulted in the **lowest installment obligation**. This is the safe-harbor protection.

### 6.1 Method 1 — No-Calculation Option (CRA's Estimate)

**Applicability: [NOW/SOON] | Risk: [RISK: LOW]**

Use the amounts printed on CRA's installment reminder (Form INNS1/INNS3). CRA calculates these for you based on your prior year's assessed tax.

**Calculation basis:** CRA takes your prior year's net tax owing and splits it across four equal quarterly payments.

**Formula:**
```
Each installment = Prior year net tax owing ÷ 4
```

**Example — $6,000 net tax owing in 2025:**
```
Q1 (Mar 15, 2026): $1,500
Q2 (Jun 15, 2026): $1,500
Q3 (Sep 15, 2026): $1,500
Q4 (Dec 15, 2026): $1,500
Total paid: $6,000
```

**When to use:** When you are uncertain about your current year income, or when current-year income is similar to or lower than the prior year. Zero calculation required — just pay what the form says.

**Key advantage:** As long as you pay the amounts on Form INNS1 on time, you face **zero installment interest** regardless of what you actually owe for the year. This is the safe harbor.

**Key disadvantage:** If your income is rising significantly, this method may result in a large balance owing on April 30 of the following year (though no installment interest — just the balance due).

---

### 6.2 Method 2 — Prior-Year Method

**Applicability: [SOON] | Risk: [RISK: LOW]**

Pay one-quarter of last year's total net tax owing each quarter.

**Formula:**
```
Each installment = (Year N-1 net tax owing) ÷ 4
```

This is mathematically identical to Method 1 when using prior-year numbers — the distinction matters when CRA's reminder uses a different year than you expect, or when you are calculating manually rather than relying on the form.

**Example — CC in 2027, 2026 net tax owing was $5,200:**
```
Each quarterly installment = $5,200 ÷ 4 = $1,300
Total annual installments: $5,200
```

**When to use:** Rising income trajectory. If your 2026 net tax owing was $5,200 but your 2027 income is on track for $8,000 net tax owing, paying $5,200 via the prior-year method is better than paying $8,000 via the current-year method. You defer $2,800 until April 30, 2028 — interest-free if the prior-year installments were paid on time.

**For CC with a rising income curve:** Prior-year method is almost always optimal. You are borrowing $2,800 from CRA at 0% effective rate (no installment interest if Method 2 amounts were paid). Invest the float in your TFSA or high-interest savings account.

---

### 6.3 Method 3 — Current-Year Method

**Applicability: [SOON/FUTURE] | Risk: [RISK: LOW] (if estimated accurately)**

Pay one-quarter of your estimated current-year net tax owing each quarter.

**Formula:**
```
Each installment = (Estimated Year N net tax owing) ÷ 4
```

**Example — CC in 2027, estimates $8,000 net tax owing:**
```
Q1 (Mar 15): $2,000
Q2 (Jun 15): $2,000
Q3 (Sep 15): $2,000
Q4 (Dec 15): $2,000
Total: $8,000
```

**When to use:** Falling income. If your income dropped significantly this year versus last year, paying prior-year installments means massive overpayment. Use the current-year method, underpay the prior-year amount, and as long as you are within the safe-harbor band, no installment interest is charged.

**Risk:** Underestimating current-year income triggers installment interest on the shortfall. CRA will compare your actual current-year method liability to what you paid and charge interest on the difference. If you are wrong by a large margin, you lose the safe-harbor protection on that quarter.

**For CC:** Use this method only when income has materially dropped versus the prior year. For a growing business, stick to the prior-year method.

---

### 6.4 Method 4 — Prior-Prior-Year Hybrid (Two-Part Method)

**Applicability: [FUTURE] | Risk: [RISK: LOW]**

This method splits the year into two halves using two different base years. It is the most technically correct method and the one CRA uses when calculating whether you have underpaid.

**Formula — ITA s.156(1)(a):**
```
Q1 and Q2 (March + June): Based on Year N-2 (two years ago)
  Each = (Year N-2 net tax owing) ÷ 4

Q3 and Q4 (September + December): Based on Year N-1 (last year)
  Each = (Year N-1 net tax owing) ÷ 2  MINUS  Q1 and Q2 amounts already paid
```

**Example — CC in 2028:**
- 2026 net tax owing: $4,000
- 2027 net tax owing: $7,200

```
Q1 (Mar 15, 2028): $4,000 ÷ 4 = $1,000
Q2 (Jun 15, 2028): $4,000 ÷ 4 = $1,000
Q3 (Sep 15, 2028): ($7,200 ÷ 2) - $2,000 already paid = $1,600
Q4 (Dec 15, 2028): ($7,200 ÷ 2) - $2,000 - $1,600 = $0 (or remainder)

Recalculating properly:
  Q1 + Q2 = $4,000 ÷ 2 = $2,000 total ($1,000 each)
  Total for year = $7,200 (Year N-1)
  Q3 + Q4 = $7,200 - $2,000 = $5,200 ($2,600 each)
```

**Why this matters:** CRA uses this two-part hybrid calculation internally to determine whether your installments were sufficient. This is the **benchmark** CRA compares your payments against when calculating installment interest. Understanding the formula lets you compute exactly what CRA will expect — and avoid surprises.

---

### 6.5 Method Comparison — Rising vs. Falling Income

| Income Trajectory | Optimal Method | Reason |
|-------------------|---------------|--------|
| Strongly rising (CC's situation) | **Prior-Year (Method 2)** | Pay less now, owe balance on Apr 30 at no installment interest. Free float. |
| Flat / stable | **No-Calculation (Method 1)** | Zero effort, fully protected. |
| Falling | **Current-Year (Method 3)** | Avoid overpaying installments that you then wait to recover via refund. |
| Complex / large change | **Hybrid (Method 4)** | Use when N-2 and N-1 are very different — matches CRA's own calculation. |

**The safe-harbor guarantee:** If you pay the amounts CRA specifies on Form INNS1 on time (Method 1), you face zero installment interest no matter how much you actually owe for the year. This is the simplest protection available.

---

### 6.6 How CRA Applies the Safe-Harbor Test

When assessing installment interest, CRA does not simply check whether you paid enough. They run all applicable methods against your actual payments and charge interest based on whichever method would have resulted in the **lowest required payment**:

```
CRA calculates the minimum required installments under:
  (a) Method 1 (prior-year amounts from Form INNS1)
  (b) Method 2 (1/4 of last year's net tax owing per quarter)
  (c) Method 4 (hybrid two-year method)

Installment interest is charged only if your actual payments fell below
the LOWEST of these three benchmarks.
```

**Practical implication:** If you paid less than the Method 1 amount but still more than the Method 4 (hybrid) amount, you may owe no installment interest at all. Always calculate all three before concluding you owe interest.

## 7. Penalty and Interest Rules

### 7.1 Installment Interest — s.161(2)

When installment payments are late or insufficient, CRA charges **installment interest** at the prescribed rate plus 2%.

**Formula:**
```
Installment interest = Shortfall × (Prescribed rate + 2%) × (Days late ÷ 365)
```

**Prescribed rate (quarterly, set by CRA):** Currently 7% (2025). Updates each quarter based on 90-day T-bill rate. Check CRA's website at the start of each quarter.

**Effective installment interest rate (2025):** 7% + 2% = **9% annually**.

**Example — Q1 installment $1,500 paid 45 days late:**
```
Interest = $1,500 × 9% × (45 ÷ 365) = $16.64
```

**Example — Missed entire year's installments ($6,000 owing):**
```
Q1 shortfall $1,500 × 9% × (275 days from Mar 15 to Dec 15) = $101.71
Q2 shortfall $1,500 × 9% × (183 days from Jun 15 to Dec 15) = $67.73
Q3 shortfall $1,500 × 9% × (91 days from Sep 15 to Dec 15) = $33.70
Q4 shortfall $1,500 × 9% × (0 days — paid Dec 15) = $0
Total installment interest: ~$203
```

This is not catastrophic — it is the cost of a short-term float. For a rapidly growing business, it is sometimes rational to pay this interest rather than tie up cash in installments.

### 7.2 The Offset Rule — Critical Planning Tool

**ITA s.161(4):** Installment interest on a shortfall in one quarter is reduced (offset) by installment credit generated by overpayment in another quarter.

If you overpay Q1 and Q2, those overpayments generate an "installment credit" at the same prescribed rate + 2% that offsets interest charges on underpayments in Q3 and Q4.

**Practical use:** If you receive a large lump-sum contract payment in November (Q4), you can overpay Q4 significantly and use the resulting credit to offset any Q1-Q3 interest — all within the same tax year.

**The offset mechanics:**
```
Overpayment credit = Amount overpaid × (Prescribed rate + 2%) × (Days overpaid ÷ 365)
Underpayment interest = Amount underpaid × (Prescribed rate + 2%) × (Days short ÷ 365)
Net interest = Underpayment interest - Overpayment credit
```

As long as total installments paid for the year equal or exceed the prior-year net tax owing by December 15, the offset rule typically eliminates all installment interest.

### 7.3 The $1,000 Penalty Threshold — s.163.1

**ITA s.163.1:** A penalty (surcharge) is added on top of installment interest when:

```
(a) Installment interest for the year EXCEEDS $1,000, AND
(b) The installment interest exceeds 25% of the installment interest that would
    have been payable if you had made zero installments all year
```

**The penalty formula (when triggered):**
```
Penalty = 50% × (Actual installment interest - $1,000)
```

**Example — $3,000 in installment interest owed:**
```
Penalty = 50% × ($3,000 - $1,000) = $1,000 additional penalty
Total cost = $3,000 interest + $1,000 penalty = $4,000
```

**When can $1,000 in installment interest accumulate?** Only on very large underpayments:

```
At 9% annual rate, $1,000 installment interest requires:
  - Entire year underpayment of ~$11,111 (all four quarters missed)
  - OR equivalent weighted underpayment across quarters
```

For CC, the penalty threshold becomes a real concern only when net tax owing exceeds approximately $40,000-$50,000 and all installments are missed. At OASIS income of $30K-$80K, properly managing even two of the four quarterly installments eliminates all penalty exposure.

**Bottom line:** The s.163.1 penalty is an aggressive failsafe for egregious non-compliance. As long as you pay at least two of four installments at reasonable amounts, you will not hit it.

### 7.4 Interest on Balance Owing vs. Installment Interest

These are two distinct charges — often confused:

| Charge | ITA Reference | Rate | Triggered By |
|--------|--------------|------|-------------|
| **Installment interest** | s.161(2) | Prescribed + 2% | Underpaying installments during the year |
| **Balance-owing interest** | s.161(1) | Prescribed + 2% | Balance owing after April 30 payment deadline |
| **Late-filing penalty** | s.162(1) | N/A | Filing after June 15 (self-employed) |

**Key distinction:** You can face zero installment interest (by using the prior-year safe harbor) but still owe balance-owing interest if you do not pay the residual balance by April 30. These are sequential charges — installment interest covers the year; balance-owing interest covers the April 30 deadline.

## 8. Corporate Installments (Post-Incorporation)

### 8.1 When CC Incorporates (OASIS > $80K Revenue)

Upon incorporation, the installment rules change entirely. The personal quarterly system no longer applies to corporate income — the corporation files separately and has its own installment obligations under ITA s.157.

### 8.2 Regular CCPC — Monthly Installments (s.157(1))

The default rule for a Canadian-Controlled Private Corporation:

- **Frequency:** Monthly (12 installments per year)
- **Due date:** Last day of each month
- **Calculation:** 1/12 of estimated current-year tax OR 1/12 of prior-year tax (same three-method safe harbor as personal, applied monthly)
- **Threshold:** Applies when tax payable > $3,000

**Formula (prior-year method for corporations):**
```
Monthly installment = Prior year Part I tax ÷ 12
```

### 8.3 Small CCPC Exception — Quarterly Installments (s.157(1.1))

A corporation qualifies for quarterly installments (instead of monthly) if **all three** conditions are met:

```
Condition 1: Taxable income for the year or prior year ≤ $500,000 CAD
Condition 2: Taxable capital employed in Canada ≤ $10,000,000 CAD
Condition 3: Claimed (or eligible to claim) the Small Business Deduction in the current or prior year
```

**For a newly incorporated OASIS AI Solutions:** All three conditions will easily be met. The corporation qualifies for quarterly installments — same timing as personal (March 15, June 15, September 15, December 15 of the corporate fiscal year, adjusted to month-end).

**Corporate quarterly due dates:** Last day of the third, sixth, ninth, and twelfth months of the fiscal year.

**Example — OASIS fiscal year ending December 31:**
```
Q1: March 31
Q2: June 30
Q3: September 30
Q4: December 31
```

**Note:** The corporate installment due dates are month-end (not the 15th as for individuals). This is a common source of confusion when transitioning from sole proprietorship to corporation.

### 8.4 Corporate vs. Personal Installment Comparison

| Feature | Personal (Sole Prop) | Corporation (CCPC) | Small CCPC Exception |
|---------|---------------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Frequency | Quarterly | Monthly | Quarterly |
| Due dates | Mar/Jun/Sep/Dec 15 | Last day of each month | Last day of Q months |
| Threshold | $3,000 net tax owing | $3,000 tax payable | Same |
| Safe harbor | 3 methods (same) | 3 methods (same) | Same |
| Interest rate | Prescribed + 2% | Prescribed + 2% | Same |
| Penalty threshold | $1,000 interest | $1,000 interest | Same |

### 8.5 Dual Installment Obligations Post-Incorporation

When CC incorporates, there will be a **transition year** where both personal and corporate installments may apply:

- **Corporate:** OASIS AI Solutions Inc. pays installments on corporate taxable income
- **Personal:** CC personally still pays installments on:
  - Salary/dividends paid from the corporation (declared during the year)
  - Any personal investment income
  - Any remaining sole proprietorship income from the transition period

**Strategy for transition year:** Minimize salary draws until year-end. This defers personal income recognition and reduces (or eliminates) the need for personal installments in the transition year, while the corporation accumulates retained earnings at the lower small business rate (9% federal + ~3.2% Ontario).

## 9. Strategic Installment Planning

### 9.1 Installment Reserve Account — Forced Savings Strategy

**Applicability: [SOON] | Risk: [RISK: LOW]**

Do not treat your tax obligation as a surprise at April 30. Treat it as a line item that accrues with every invoice you issue.

**The discipline:**
```
Every dollar of OASIS revenue received:
  Set aside 25-30% immediately in a segregated savings account
  This covers: income tax + Ontario tax + CPP (both halves)
  Label the account: "Tax Reserve — Do Not Touch"
```

**Recommended allocation for $1,000 of OASIS revenue (approximate, $50K income bracket):**

| Reserve Item | Rate | Amount per $1,000 |
|--------------|------|-------------------|
| Federal income tax (after basic personal, rough) | ~12% | $120 |
| Ontario income tax | ~7% | $70 |
| CPP self-employment (both halves) | 11.9% | $119 |
| **Total reserve** | **~30.9%** | **$309** |

Holding this reserve in a HISA (High-Interest Savings Account) at 4-5% means you earn interest on money you already owe CRA — essentially free interest income from the float.

**Optimal structure:** Open a separate Wise CAD savings bucket or RBC secondary savings account. Auto-transfer 30% of every invoice payment into this account the moment it arrives. Never comingle with operating funds.

### 9.2 RRSP Timing to Reduce March Installment

**Applicability: [SOON] | Risk: [RISK: LOW]**

The March 15 installment is the first payment due each year. It is calculated based on the prior year's net tax owing. Making an RRSP contribution in January or February (before the March 15 installment) **does not reduce the March 15 installment** — because the installment is based on last year's tax, not this year's.

**However:** A large RRSP contribution made before March 1 reduces your total T1 balance owing for the year. If you are using the current-year method (Method 3), a January RRSP contribution reduces your estimated current-year tax, which reduces all four installments for that year.

**Optimal RRSP timing for installment minimization:**

| Action | Timing | Installment Impact |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| Max RRSP contribution | January 1 – March 1 | Reduces current-year method installments for all 4 quarters |
| Large RRSP contribution | March 2 – December 31 | Reduces balance owing on April 30; no installment reduction |
| RRSP contribution in Q4 | October – December | No installment impact; only reduces April 30 balance |

**Key insight:** If you want installments reduced, contribute to RRSP before March 1 and elect to use the current-year method. If you just want to reduce the April 30 balance owing, any contribution before March 1 of the following year works.

### 9.3 Tax-Loss Harvesting to Reduce Q4 Installment

**Applicability: [NOW/SOON] | Risk: [RISK: LOW]**

If CC is using the current-year method (Method 3), realizing capital losses in Q4 reduces the estimated current-year tax owing and directly reduces the Q4 installment amount.

**Mechanism:**
```
1. In October/November, identify crypto positions with unrealized losses
2. Sell to realize losses — reduces current-year net capital gains
3. Recalculate current-year net tax owing (lower)
4. Reduce Q4 installment by 1/4 of the reduction in current-year tax estimate
```

**Example:**
```
Estimated current-year net tax owing (before loss harvest): $8,000
Loss harvest reduces capital gains by $4,000
  - Capital gains inclusion reduces by $2,000 (50% inclusion rate, or $4,000 at 2/3 for >$250K)
  - Tax reduction at combined 26.3% marginal rate: ~$526
Q4 installment reduction: $526 ÷ 4 installments (prorated) ≈ $131
```

Note: The superficial loss rule (ITA s.54 "superficial loss") prevents you from repurchasing the same or identical property within 30 days. Crypto is not currently subject to the identical property rule in the same way as securities — but CRA's position continues to evolve. Document carefully.

### 9.4 Timing Income Recognition to Manage Installment Obligations

**Applicability: [SOON] | Risk: [RISK: MEDIUM]**

Self-employment income is recognized when it is earned (accrual basis), not necessarily when cash is received. However, for small businesses using the cash basis informally, the timing of invoicing and collection matters.

**Legal deferral strategies:**
- Issue December invoices for large projects in the first week of January (defer income to next year)
- If using cash-basis reporting (acceptable for very small businesses), delay December receipts until January
- **ITA s.34.1:** Elective business income deferral for unincorporated professionals in certain industries (not applicable to AI services)

**The installment implication:**
```
Deferring $10,000 of December income into January:
  - Reduces current-year net tax owing by ~$2,630 (at 26.3% combined rate)
  - Reduces Q4 installment (current-year method) by ~$658
  - Pushes the income and its tax into next year
```

**Warning:** This strategy has legitimate limits. CRA expects income to be reported in the year it is earned, not merely collected. Cash-basis reporting is generally acceptable for small businesses but cannot be used to deliberately distort income timing in a material way.

### 9.5 Requesting a Reduction When Income Drops

**Applicability: [FUTURE] | Risk: [RISK: LOW]**

If CC's income drops significantly mid-year (lost a major client, slow quarter, crypto losses), there is no formal CRA mechanism to "request a reduction" of installments. Instead:

**Action:** Switch to the current-year method (Method 3) and pay installments based on your revised lower income estimate.

**Risk of underestimating:** If actual income exceeds your estimate, installment interest applies to the shortfall. But CRA's safe-harbor test means that as long as you paid at least the Method 1 (INNS1 amounts) or Method 2 (1/4 of last year's tax), no interest applies regardless.

**The correct process when income falls:**
```
1. Continue paying the INNS1 amounts (Method 1) — eliminates all installment interest risk
2. OR recalculate using Method 3 with lower income estimate
3. If underpaying Method 3, ensure payments at least equal Method 2 (prior year ÷ 4)
4. Any residual interest is simply the cost of cash flow management
```

### 9.6 Using the Float Intelligently

**Applicability: [SOON] | Risk: [RISK: LOW]**

With a rising income, the prior-year method (Method 2) creates a float — the difference between what you owe at April 30 and what you paid in installments. This float accrues interest-free.

**Example — 2027 installment float:**
```
2026 net tax owing: $5,200
2027 actual net tax owing: $9,800

Installments paid (Method 2): $5,200
Balance owing on April 30, 2028: $9,800 - $5,200 = $4,600

This $4,600 float was available to CC from January 1 to April 30, 2028 — 4.5 months.

Invested in TFSA at 5% HISA: $4,600 × 5% × (4.5/12) = $86.25 in earned interest
```

Small at low income levels, but as OASIS scales to $150K-$300K revenue, this float strategy generates meaningful compounding returns on money that is already owed to CRA.
