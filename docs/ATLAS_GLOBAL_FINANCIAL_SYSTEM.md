## 1. How Money Actually Works

### What Is Money?

Money is not a thing. It is a shared belief system. A $100 bill has no intrinsic value — it is
cotton and ink. What gives it value is that the Canadian or US government demands taxes be paid
in it, courts enforce contracts denominated in it, and everyone else accepts it because everyone
else accepts it. This circular logic is the entire foundation of the modern financial system.

There are three properties of money:
- **Medium of exchange** — you can buy things with it
- **Unit of account** — prices are quoted in it
- **Store of value** — it holds purchasing power over time (debatable with inflation)

Gold served these functions for millennia. Today, fiat currency does.

### Fiat Currency — What Backs It

"Fiat" comes from Latin, meaning "let it be done." Fiat currency is money by government decree.

What backs it:
- **Taxation power** — the government can force you to earn and pay in its currency
- **Legal tender laws** — businesses must accept it for debts
- **Military and institutional power** — the US dollar is backed by the world's largest military, economy, and the petrodollar system (oil traded in USD)
- **Central bank credibility** — if people trust the Bank of Canada to manage inflation, CAD holds value

What does NOT back it:
- Gold (the gold standard ended in 1971 when Nixon closed the gold window)
- Any physical commodity
- Any mathematical formula or algorithm (that is what Bitcoin attempts to provide)

> **For Business Owners:** Your business revenue, bank deposits, and accounts receivable are all
> denominated in fiat currency. Understanding that this currency can be devalued by government
> policy (inflation, money printing) is the first step to protecting your purchasing power.
> Holding excess cash is a guaranteed loss to inflation.

### Money Creation: Fractional Reserve Banking

This is where most people's understanding breaks down. Banks do not lend out deposits. Banks
create new money when they make loans. This is not conspiracy theory — it is how the system
works, confirmed by the Bank of England, the Federal Reserve, and the Bank of Canada.

**How it works:**

1. You deposit $10,000 at RBC
2. RBC is required to hold a fraction in reserve (historically ~10%, though Canada eliminated
   reserve requirements in 1994; the US did so in 2020)
3. RBC lends $9,000 to another customer — this $9,000 is **new money** that did not exist before
4. That customer deposits $9,000 at TD
5. TD lends $8,100 to someone else — more new money
6. This cascades through the system

In practice, modern banks do not wait for deposits to lend. They create the loan (an asset on
their balance sheet) and the deposit (a liability) simultaneously. The deposit IS the money
creation. Reserves are settled afterward in the interbank market.

**The money multiplier:**
```
Money Supply = Monetary Base x (1 / Reserve Ratio)
```
If the reserve ratio is 10%, each dollar of base money can support $10 of total money supply.
With no reserve requirement (Canada, US post-2020), the constraint becomes capital ratios
(Basel III) rather than reserves.

### Money Supply Measures

Central banks track money supply in layers:

| Measure | What It Includes | Why It Matters |
|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| **M0 (Monetary Base)** | Physical currency + bank reserves at the central bank | The "real" money central banks directly control |
| **M1** | M0 + demand deposits (checking accounts) | Money available for immediate spending |
| **M2** | M1 + savings accounts + money market funds + small time deposits | Broader liquidity — the most-watched measure |
| **M3** | M2 + large time deposits + institutional money market funds | Discontinued by the Fed in 2006 (suspiciously) |

**Key insight:** M2 in the US went from $15.4 trillion (Feb 2020) to $21.7 trillion (Apr 2022) —
a 41% increase in 26 months. This is why inflation exploded in 2022. When you increase the money
supply by 41% and goods/services only increase by a few percent, prices must rise.

Canada's M2 followed the same pattern: ~$1.7T to ~$2.3T during COVID stimulus.

> **For Business Owners:** When M2 is expanding rapidly, your cash is losing purchasing power.
> This is when you want to own assets (real estate, equities, equipment) rather than hold cash.
> When M2 is contracting (as in 2023), cash becomes relatively more valuable and asset prices
> tend to fall — a buying opportunity for well-capitalized businesses.

### Velocity of Money

The quantity theory of money is expressed as:

```
M x V = P x Q

Where:
M = Money supply
V = Velocity (how fast money changes hands)
P = Price level (inflation)
Q = Real output (GDP)
```

You can increase the money supply (M) dramatically, but if nobody spends it (V drops), prices
(P) may not rise. This is exactly what happened from 2009-2019: the Fed printed trillions via QE,
but velocity collapsed because the money went into bank reserves and financial assets, not the
real economy. Inflation stayed below 2%.

In 2020-2021, the government sent stimulus checks directly to consumers (velocity went up) while
simultaneously expanding M2. Both M and V increased — and inflation finally arrived.

**Velocity in Canada (2025):** ~1.4x, meaning each dollar of M2 generates $1.40 of GDP per year.
Historically it was 2.0x+ in the 1990s. Lower velocity means the economy needs more money supply
to generate the same GDP — this is why central banks struggle to hit inflation targets.

### Inflation — The Hidden Tax

Inflation is not rising prices. Inflation is the decline in purchasing power of your currency.
Prices rise as a consequence.

**At 3% annual inflation:**
- $100 today buys $97 worth of goods next year
- In 10 years, that $100 buys only $74 worth of goods
- In 24 years, your purchasing power is cut in half

This is why holding cash in a savings account earning 1% while inflation runs at 3% means you
are losing 2% per year in real terms. Banks love this — they pay you 1% on deposits and lend at
6%, pocketing the spread while inflation erodes your savings.

**Who benefits from inflation:**
- Debtors (loans are repaid in cheaper dollars)
- Asset owners (real estate, stocks, commodities rise in nominal terms)
- Governments (tax brackets creep up, debt burden shrinks)

**Who loses:**
- Cash holders
- Fixed-income earners (pensions, bonds with fixed coupons)
- Workers whose wages lag inflation

> **For Business Owners:** Inflation is your signal to: (1) raise prices — your costs are rising,
> pass them through, (2) lock in long-term debt at fixed rates — you repay in cheaper dollars,
> (3) invest cash into productive assets, (4) avoid long-term fixed-price contracts on the
> revenue side. A business sitting on $200K in a checking account at 0.5% interest is losing
> $4,000-$6,000/year in real purchasing power.

### Deflation — Why Central Banks Fear It More

Deflation (falling prices) sounds great for consumers, but it is an economic death spiral:

1. Prices fall → consumers delay purchases ("it will be cheaper tomorrow")
2. Businesses see falling revenue → cut wages and staff
3. Unemployment rises → less spending → prices fall further
4. Debt becomes MORE expensive in real terms (you owe $100K but your income is falling)
5. Defaults cascade → bank losses → credit contraction → more deflation

Japan experienced this from 1991-2012 — the "Lost Decades." The Bank of Japan tried everything:
zero rates, QE, negative rates. Nothing worked for 20 years.

This is why every central bank targets **positive** inflation (usually 2%). They would rather have
slightly too much inflation than any deflation. The 2% target gives a buffer above zero.

## 2. Central Banks and Monetary Policy

Central banks are the most powerful financial institutions on Earth. A single sentence from a
central bank governor can move trillions of dollars in asset prices within minutes.

### Bank of Canada (BoC)

**Mandate:** Maintain low, stable, and predictable inflation. Target: 2% (within a 1-3% band).

**Key tools:**
- **Overnight rate (policy rate):** The interest rate at which major banks lend to each other
  overnight. This is the BoC's primary tool. As of 2025, it sits in the 2.75-3.25% range after
  aggressive cuts from the 5% peak.
- **Large-scale asset purchases (QE):** Buying Government of Canada bonds to push down longer-term
  rates. Used during COVID. Balance sheet peaked at ~$575B.
- **Quantitative tightening (QT):** Letting bonds mature without reinvesting, shrinking the
  balance sheet. Currently underway.
- **Forward guidance:** Telling markets what the BoC plans to do — this moves rates before the
  BoC actually acts.

**Rate decisions:** Eight times per year (fixed schedule). Each decision is accompanied by a
Monetary Policy Report (quarterly) or a press conference.

**Transmission mechanism:**
```
BoC raises overnight rate by 0.25%
→ Banks raise prime rate by 0.25% (currently ~5.45%)
→ Variable-rate mortgages cost more
→ Business lines of credit cost more
→ Consumer spending slows
→ Business investment slows
→ Hiring slows
→ Inflation falls (with a 12-18 month lag)
```

### Federal Reserve (US)

**Mandate:** Dual mandate — (1) maximum employment and (2) stable prices (2% inflation).

**Key tools:**
- **Federal funds rate:** The US equivalent of Canada's overnight rate. The most important
  interest rate in the world because of USD's reserve currency status.
- **Open market operations:** Buying/selling US Treasuries
- **Discount window:** Emergency lending to banks
- **Reverse repo facility:** Absorbs excess liquidity from the system
- **Dot plot:** Each Fed governor's projection for future rates, published quarterly. Markets
  obsess over this.

**Why the Fed matters to Canadians:** The CAD/USD exchange rate is heavily influenced by the
interest rate differential between Canada and the US. If the Fed holds rates at 5% and the BoC
cuts to 3%, capital flows from Canada to the US (chasing higher yields), weakening the CAD.
This makes imports more expensive and contributes to Canadian inflation.

### Bank of England (BoE)

**Mandate:** 2% CPI inflation target, set by the UK Treasury.

**Key tools:** Bank Rate (equivalent to overnight rate), gilt purchases (QE), forward guidance.

**Notable events:**
- 2022 gilt crisis: Liz Truss's mini-budget caused a pension fund (LDI) crisis. The BoE had to
  intervene with emergency gilt purchases while simultaneously trying to tighten. Demonstrated
  that bond market vigilantes can override government fiscal policy.

### European Central Bank (ECB)

**Mandate:** Price stability (below but close to 2% inflation).

**Key tools:**
- Main refinancing rate, deposit facility rate
- **TLTROs (Targeted Longer-Term Refinancing Operations):** Loans to banks at negative rates to
  encourage lending — essentially paying banks to lend
- APP (Asset Purchase Programme) and PEPP (Pandemic Emergency Purchase Programme)

**Notable:** The ECB maintained **negative interest rates** from 2014-2022. Banks were charged to
hold deposits at the ECB. This was an extraordinary experiment that distorted European financial
markets for nearly a decade.

### How Interest Rate Decisions Ripple Through the Economy

The transmission chain from a rate hike to its economic effect:

```
Central bank raises rates
│
├─→ Short-term borrowing costs rise immediately
│   ├─→ Variable-rate mortgages: monthly payments increase
│   ├─→ Business lines of credit: cost of operating capital rises
│   ├─→ Credit card rates: rise (already high, but marginal effect)
│   └─→ Auto loans: new car financing becomes more expensive
│
├─→ Bond yields rise (prices fall)
│   ├─→ Fixed-rate mortgage rates rise (priced off 5-year bond yields in Canada)
│   ├─→ Corporate borrowing costs rise
│   └─→ Government debt servicing costs rise
│
├─→ Stock prices tend to fall
│   ├─→ Higher discount rate → lower present value of future earnings
│   ├─→ Growth stocks hit hardest (distant future cash flows discounted more)
│   └─→ Wealth effect: people feel poorer → spend less
│
├─→ Currency tends to strengthen (higher rates attract foreign capital)
│   ├─→ Exports become more expensive (hurts exporters)
│   └─→ Imports become cheaper (helps contain inflation)
│
└─→ 12-18 months later: inflation starts to decline (the "long and variable lag")
```

> **For Business Owners:** When the BoC or Fed signals rate hikes: (1) lock in fixed-rate debt
> before rates rise further, (2) expect slower customer spending 6-12 months out, (3) build
> cash reserves for the downturn, (4) delay large capital expenditures if possible. When they
> signal cuts: (1) prepare to refinance at lower rates, (2) expect asset prices to recover,
> (3) consider strategic acquisitions when competitors are distressed.

### Quantitative Easing (QE) — Simply Explained

QE is the central bank creating new money electronically and using it to buy government bonds
(and sometimes corporate bonds or mortgage-backed securities) from banks and financial
institutions.

**Step by step:**
1. BoC creates $1 billion in new electronic money (literally types numbers into a computer)
2. Uses that money to buy Government of Canada bonds from banks
3. Banks now have $1B in cash reserves instead of bonds
4. This pushes bond prices up and yields down (supply of bonds in the market decreases)
5. Lower yields mean lower borrowing costs for government, businesses, and consumers
6. Banks (theoretically) lend out the excess cash, stimulating the economy

**What actually happened:** Banks often did not lend the money into the real economy. Instead,
the cash went into financial assets — stocks, real estate, corporate bonds. This is why QE
inflated asset prices dramatically while doing relatively little for Main Street. The S&P 500
tripled from 2009-2020 while median wages barely kept pace with inflation.

QE is sometimes called "printing money," though no physical bills are printed. The effect is
the same: the money supply increases.

### Quantitative Tightening (QT)

QT is the reverse: the central bank lets bonds on its balance sheet mature without reinvesting
the proceeds. This removes money from the system.

**Why QT is dangerous:** Financial markets became addicted to QE liquidity. When the Fed
attempted QT in 2018 (the "autopilot" tightening), the S&P 500 dropped 20% in Q4 2018 and the
Fed reversed course within months.

During 2022-2024, the Fed and BoC both ran QT at aggressive pace. Combined with rate hikes,
this created the tightest financial conditions in 15 years.

### Forward Guidance

Central banks discovered that they can move markets simply by talking. If the BoC governor says
"we expect to hold rates steady for the next three meetings," markets immediately price this in.
Bond yields adjust. Mortgage rates adjust. The real economy adjusts — all before the central
bank actually does anything.

Key phrases to watch:
- "Data dependent" — we have no idea what we are doing, watching the numbers
- "Transitory" — we think inflation is temporary (the Fed's disastrous 2021 call)
- "Whatever it takes" — Mario Draghi's famous 2012 phrase that saved the Euro
- "Measured pace" — rate changes will be gradual and predictable
- "All tools available" — we are panicking but want to sound calm

### The Yield Curve

The yield curve plots interest rates of government bonds across different maturities (3-month,
2-year, 5-year, 10-year, 30-year).

**Normal yield curve (upward sloping):**
- Short-term rates are lower than long-term rates
- This is healthy — lenders demand more compensation for locking up money longer
- Economy is expected to grow

**Inverted yield curve (short rates ABOVE long rates):**
- This is the most reliable recession predictor in history
- It has preceded every US recession since 1955 with only one false signal
- It means the market expects the central bank to CUT rates in the future (because a recession
  is coming and they will need to stimulate)
- The 2-year/10-year spread inverted in mid-2022 — the deepest inversion since the early 1980s
- The recession signal has a 12-24 month lead time

**Flat yield curve:**
- Short and long rates are similar
- Transitional phase — either heading toward inversion or normalizing
- Banks struggle to profit (they borrow short and lend long — a flat curve compresses margins)

> **For Business Owners:** When the yield curve inverts, start preparing for a recession 12-18
> months out. Build cash reserves, reduce variable costs, secure fixed-rate credit facilities
> while you can still qualify, and identify competitors who may become acquisition targets. The
> yield curve is your early warning system — use it.

### Why 2% Inflation Target Exists

The 2% target was essentially invented by New Zealand in 1990 and adopted globally. The logic:

- **0% is too close to deflation** — any shock could tip into the deflationary spiral
- **2% gives a buffer** above zero while being low enough that people do not notice it much
- **It allows real interest rates to go negative** — if inflation is 2% and the nominal rate
  is 0%, the real rate is -2%, which stimulates borrowing and spending
- **Higher targets (3-4%) would erode purchasing power too quickly** and distort planning

There is growing academic debate about raising the target to 3%, particularly after the
2020-2024 inflation episode showed how hard it is to get from 3% back to 2% without causing
a recession.

## 3. The Banking System

### Types of Banks

**Commercial banks (retail banks):**
- RBC, TD, BMO, Scotiabank, CIBC (Canada's Big Five)
- JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo (US)
- Barclays, HSBC, Lloyds (UK)
- Take deposits, make loans, process payments
- Heavily regulated, deposit-insured

**Investment banks:**
- Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, RBC Capital Markets, BMO Capital Markets
- Underwrite securities (IPOs, bond issuances), advise on M&A, trade for clients
- Do NOT take retail deposits (post-Glass-Steagall repeal, lines blurred)
- Higher risk, higher reward, less regulated historically

**Central banks:**
- Bank of Canada, Federal Reserve, Bank of England, ECB
- Issue currency, set monetary policy, act as lender of last resort
- Not commercial entities — they serve the public interest (theoretically)

**Credit unions / Caisses populaires:**
- Member-owned, not-for-profit
- Often offer better rates than Big Five
- In Canada: Desjardins (Quebec), Meridian, Vancity
- Deposit insurance: provincial (not CDIC), typically $250K in Ontario (DICO)

### How Banks Make Money

Banks are extraordinarily profitable businesses. Here is how:

**1. Net Interest Margin (NIM) — the core profit engine:**
```
Bank pays you:    1.5% on savings account
Bank charges:     6.5% on mortgage
Net Interest Margin: 5.0%
```
On a $500K mortgage, the bank earns ~$25,000/year in interest spread on YOUR money. Multiply
this by millions of mortgages, and you understand why the Big Five are the most profitable
companies in Canada.

**2. Fees:**
- Monthly account fees ($4-$30/month)
- Overdraft fees ($5 per item)
- Wire transfer fees ($25-$50 domestic, $40-$80 international)
- Credit card interchange fees (1.5-3% of every transaction)
- Wealth management fees (1-2% of AUM annually)
- Mortgage prepayment penalties (can be $10K+)

**3. Trading and capital markets:**
- Proprietary trading (reduced post-2008)
- Market making (earning bid-ask spreads)
- Underwriting fees (IPO commissions of 3-7%)
- M&A advisory fees (1-3% of deal value)

**4. Insurance and wealth management:**
- Mutual fund management fees (MER of 1.5-2.5% in Canada — among the highest globally)
- Insurance premiums
- Trust and estate services

> **For Business Owners:** Your bank is not your friend. They are a counter-party with
> misaligned incentives. They profit from your ignorance (high-fee mutual funds), your
> inertia (not shopping mortgage rates), and your loyalty (staying with one bank when a
> competitor offers better terms). Shop every product: mortgage, LOC, credit card, merchant
> processing. Negotiate everything. Banks have authority to waive fees and offer rate
> discounts — but only if you ask.

### Deposit Insurance

If your bank fails, deposit insurance protects your money — up to a limit.

**Canada — CDIC (Canada Deposit Insurance Corporation):**
- Coverage: **$100,000 per eligible deposit category per member institution**
- Categories (each separately insured):
  - Deposits in your name
  - Joint deposits
  - RRSP deposits
  - TFSA deposits
  - FHSA deposits
  - RESP deposits
  - RRIF deposits
- This means you can have up to $700K+ insured at ONE bank across all categories
- DOES NOT cover: mutual funds, stocks, bonds (even if purchased at the bank), crypto, foreign
  currency deposits

**US — FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation):**
- Coverage: **$250,000 per depositor per bank per ownership category**
- Categories: individual, joint, retirement (IRA), trust, business
- Practical maximum at one bank: $1M+ across categories

**UK — FSCS (Financial Services Compensation Scheme):**
- Coverage: **GBP 85,000 per person per authorized firm**
- Joint accounts: GBP 170,000
- Temporary high balances (home sale, inheritance): GBP 1M for 6 months

> **For Business Owners:** Business bank accounts in Canada have ZERO CDIC coverage unless
> specifically structured. Business deposits at CDIC-member institutions are covered as a
> separate category ($100K), but confirm with your institution. If your business holds more
> than $100K, spread across multiple institutions or use Government of Canada T-Bills for
> amounts above the insured limit.

### Basel III/IV Capital Requirements

After the 2008 crisis exposed how under-capitalized banks were, global regulators (via the
Basel Committee on Banking Supervision) imposed strict capital rules:

- **Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) ratio:** Banks must hold at least 4.5% of risk-weighted assets
  as core equity (common shares + retained earnings)
- **Total capital ratio:** At least 8% of risk-weighted assets
- **Leverage ratio:** At least 3% of total exposure
- **Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR):** Must hold enough liquid assets to survive 30 days of
  stress outflows
- **Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR):** Long-term assets must be funded by long-term liabilities

Canadian banks (OSFI-regulated) typically hold CET1 ratios of 12-14% — well above minimums.
This is why no Canadian bank failed during 2008 (that, plus conservative mortgage underwriting
and CMHC insurance).

**Basel IV (2025 implementation):** Tightens the calculation of risk-weighted assets, reducing
banks' ability to use internal models to understate risk. Expected to increase capital
requirements by 5-10% for large banks globally.

### Payment Networks and Wire Transfers

**How international wire transfers work (correspondent banking):**

If you wire $10,000 from RBC in Canada to Barclays in the UK:
1. You instruct RBC to send $10,000 (converted to GBP) to Barclays
2. RBC does not have a direct relationship with Barclays
3. RBC sends via a **correspondent bank** (e.g., JPMorgan in New York)
4. JPMorgan has relationships with both RBC and Barclays (or their correspondents)
5. The money "hops" through 2-4 intermediary banks
6. Each bank takes a fee ($15-$50 per hop)
7. The FX conversion happens at whatever rate the intermediary chooses (with markup)
8. Total time: 1-5 business days. Total cost: $30-$80+ in fees plus FX spread

This is why Wise exists.

**Key payment networks:**

| Network | Region | Speed | Use Case |
|---------|--------|-------|----------|
| **SWIFT** | Global | 1-5 days | International bank transfers (messaging, not settlement) |
| **Fedwire** | US | Same day | High-value USD transfers between US banks |
| **CHIPS** | US | Same day | Clearing House Interbank Payments System — 95% of international USD transfers |
| **SEPA** | Europe | 1 day (instant available) | EUR transfers within Europe |
| **Interac e-Transfer** | Canada | Minutes-hours | Domestic person-to-person and business payments |
| **LVTS/Lynx** | Canada | Same day | Canada's high-value payment system (replaced LVTS in 2022) |
| **CHAPS** | UK | Same day | UK high-value payments |
| **Faster Payments** | UK | Seconds | UK instant payments up to GBP 1M |

### Why Wise is Cheaper Than Banks

Traditional banks convert currency at the "interbank rate" plus a 2-4% markup that they do
not disclose. On a $10,000 transfer, this hidden markup costs $200-$400.

Wise (formerly TransferWise) operates differently:
1. Charges a transparent fee (typically 0.4-1.0%)
2. Converts at the **mid-market rate** (the real exchange rate with zero markup)
3. Uses local payment networks on each end (Interac in Canada, ACH in US, Faster Payments in UK)
4. Avoids correspondent banking hops entirely for major currency pairs
5. Net savings: 50-80% cheaper than bank wire transfers

**Multi-currency accounts:** Wise, OFX, Airwallex, and Payoneer all offer business accounts
that hold multiple currencies. This lets you receive USD from US clients, hold it in USD, and
convert only when the rate is favorable.

> **For Business Owners:** If you earn revenue in multiple currencies, NEVER use your bank for
> FX conversion. Open a Wise Business account (or equivalent), receive each currency natively,
> and convert when rates favor you. On $100K/year in USD revenue, this saves $2,000-$4,000
> annually versus bank conversions. This is free money.

## 4. The Bond Market — The Real Power

### Why Bonds Matter More Than Stocks

The global bond market is approximately **$130 trillion** in value. The global stock market is
roughly $100 trillion. Bonds are bigger, and they are the foundation on which all other financial
assets are priced.

Here is why:
- Government bond yields set the **risk-free rate** — the baseline return with zero default risk
- Every asset is priced as: risk-free rate + risk premium
- When bond yields rise, ALL asset prices tend to fall (stocks, real estate, crypto)
- When bond yields fall, ALL asset prices tend to rise
- The bond market is where governments borrow — it is where fiscal policy meets financial markets

The expression "the bond market is the adult in the room" exists because the bond market has
repeatedly forced governments to reverse bad policy (UK gilt crisis 2022, Greek debt crisis
2010, etc.).

### Types of Government Bonds

**Canada (Government of Canada bonds):**
- Treasury Bills (T-Bills): 3-month, 6-month, 1-year. Sold at discount, no coupon.
- Government of Canada bonds: 2, 3, 5, 10, 30-year maturities. Semi-annual coupon.
- Real Return Bonds (RRBs): Inflation-indexed (coupon adjusts with CPI).
- Benchmark 10-year GoC yield: ~3.0-3.5% as of early 2026.

**United States (Treasuries):**
- T-Bills: up to 1 year. Most liquid instrument on Earth.
- T-Notes: 2-10 years.
- T-Bonds: 20-30 years.
- TIPS: Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (inflation-indexed).
- The 10-year Treasury yield is the most important number in global finance.

**United Kingdom (Gilts):**
- Conventional gilts: Fixed coupon, semi-annual.
- Index-linked gilts: Inflation-adjusted.
- The 2022 LDI crisis revealed how pension funds had leveraged gilt positions 3-4x.

### Corporate Bonds

Companies borrow by issuing bonds. They are rated by credit rating agencies:

| Rating (S&P/Fitch) | Moody's | Category | Meaning |
|---------------------|---------|----------|---------|
| AAA | Aaa | Investment grade | Highest quality (Microsoft, Johnson & Johnson) |
| AA | Aa | Investment grade | Very high quality |
| A | A | Investment grade | Upper medium grade |
| BBB | Baa | Investment grade | Medium grade (lowest IG — "fallen angel" risk) |
| BB | Ba | High yield ("junk") | Speculative |
| B | B | High yield | Highly speculative |
| CCC | Caa | High yield | Substantial risk |
| CC-D | Ca-C | Default/near-default | In or near default |

The spread between a corporate bond yield and the equivalent government bond yield is the
**credit spread**. When credit spreads widen, it means the market is worried about defaults.
Credit spreads are a real-time measure of financial stress.

### Bond Math: Price and Yield

**The fundamental law of bonds: price and yield move in opposite directions.**

When interest rates rise:
- Existing bonds with lower coupons become less attractive
- Their prices fall until their yield matches the new market rate
- A 2% coupon bond is worth less when new bonds pay 5%

When interest rates fall:
- Existing bonds with higher coupons become more valuable
- Their prices rise
- A 5% coupon bond is worth more when new bonds only pay 2%

**Duration:** Measures how sensitive a bond's price is to interest rate changes. A bond with
10-year duration will lose approximately 10% of its value if interest rates rise by 1%.
This is why long-term bond holders got destroyed in 2022 when the Fed hiked from 0% to 5.5%.

### The Risk-Free Rate

The risk-free rate (government bond yield) is the foundation of ALL asset pricing:

- **Stock valuation (DCF):** Future cash flows are discounted at risk-free rate + equity risk premium
- **Real estate cap rates:** Based on bond yields + illiquidity premium + property risk premium
- **Corporate borrowing:** Risk-free rate + credit spread
- **Mortgage rates:** 5-year GoC yield + bank spread (typically 1.5-2.5%)

When the risk-free rate rises from 1% to 4%, every asset in the world gets repriced downward.
This is exactly what happened in 2022.

> **For Business Owners:** Understanding bonds is critical for cash management. If your business
> has $200K in operating reserves, do not leave it in a checking account earning 0%. Options:
>
> - **T-Bills:** Buy Government of Canada T-Bills (3-month or 6-month). Zero credit risk, better
>   rates than savings accounts, highly liquid.
> - **GICs:** Higher rates than T-Bills but locked for the term (1-5 years). CDIC insured.
> - **Bond ladder:** Buy bonds maturing at staggered dates (3, 6, 9, 12 months). Provides
>   regular liquidity while earning yield.
> - **Money market funds:** Low-risk funds investing in T-Bills and short-term paper. Liquid
>   daily, yields close to the overnight rate.
>
> On $200K at 4% vs 0%, the difference is $8,000/year. That is real money for a small business.

## 5. Stock Markets and Capital Markets

### Primary vs Secondary Markets

**Primary market:** Where securities are issued for the first time.
- IPO (Initial Public Offering): company sells shares to the public for the first time
- SPO (Secondary Public Offering): company issues additional shares
- Bond issuance: company or government sells new bonds
- Investment banks underwrite these offerings (take a 3-7% fee)

**Secondary market:** Where existing securities trade between investors.
- Stock exchanges: TSX, NYSE, NASDAQ, LSE
- This is what most people think of as "the stock market"
- Companies do NOT receive money when their stock trades in the secondary market
- Liquidity is the key value — you can buy or sell at any time during market hours

### Major Exchanges

| Exchange | Country | Market Cap | Notable Listings |
|----------|---------|------------|------------------|
| **NYSE** | US | ~$28T | Berkshire, JPMorgan, Walmart |
| **NASDAQ** | US | ~$25T | Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Nvidia, Tesla |
| **TSX** | Canada | ~$3.5T | Big 5 banks, Shopify, Enbridge, Barrick Gold |
| **LSE** | UK | ~$3.3T | Shell, AstraZeneca, HSBC, Unilever |
| **Euronext** | EU | ~$7T | LVMH, ASML, TotalEnergies |
| **JPX** | Japan | ~$6T | Toyota, Sony, Softbank |
| **SSE/SZSE** | China | ~$10T combined | Industrial & Commercial Bank, Tencent |

**TSX Venture Exchange (TSXV):** Canada's junior exchange for small-cap and early-stage
companies. Lower listing requirements. Higher risk, higher potential reward.

### Market Microstructure

**Order books:** Every exchange maintains a central limit order book — a ranked list of all
buy orders (bids) and sell orders (asks) at every price level.

```
ASKS (sellers)          BIDS (buyers)
$10.05 x 500 shares    $10.00 x 1,200 shares
$10.04 x 800 shares    $9.99  x 3,500 shares
$10.03 x 1,200 shares  $9.98  x 2,000 shares
                  ↑ BID-ASK SPREAD: $0.03
```

**Bid-ask spread:** The difference between the best buy price and best sell price. This is the
market maker's profit and the investor's transaction cost. Highly liquid stocks (Apple) have
$0.01 spreads. Illiquid stocks may have $0.10-$0.50 spreads.

**Market makers:** Firms that continuously quote both bid and ask prices, providing liquidity.
They profit from the spread. In return, they take on inventory risk — they might get stuck
holding shares that drop in value. Citadel Securities and Virtu Financial are the largest.

**Dark pools:** Private exchanges where institutional investors trade large blocks anonymously.
If a pension fund wants to sell $500M of Apple shares, doing it on the public exchange would
move the price against them. In a dark pool, they can match with another institution privately.
Dark pools handle roughly 40% of US equity volume.

### How ETFs Actually Work

ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds) are one of the most important financial innovations of the last
30 years. Understanding their mechanics is valuable.

**Creation/redemption process:**
1. An ETF issuer (e.g., Vanguard, iShares) defines a basket of securities (e.g., all S&P 500 stocks)
2. **Authorized Participants (APs)** — large institutions like Goldman Sachs — can create new ETF
   shares by delivering the basket of underlying securities to the issuer
3. The issuer gives the AP new ETF shares in exchange
4. The AP sells these ETF shares on the stock exchange to retail investors
5. **Redemption** works in reverse: AP buys ETF shares on the market, delivers them to the issuer,
   receives the underlying basket

This mechanism keeps the ETF price very close to the Net Asset Value (NAV) of the underlying
securities. If the ETF trades at a premium, APs create shares (increasing supply, pushing price
down). If it trades at a discount, APs redeem shares (decreasing supply, pushing price up).

**Key Canadian ETFs:**
- XIU (iShares S&P/TSX 60) — Canada's original ETF
- VFV (Vanguard S&P 500 Index) — US market exposure in CAD
- XEQT/VEQT — All-in-one global equity portfolios (80%+ stocks)
- ZAG — Canadian aggregate bond index

> **For Business Owners:** If your business invests surplus cash in equities (growth portfolio,
> not operating reserves), use low-cost index ETFs. The average Canadian mutual fund charges
> 2.0% MER. A comparable ETF charges 0.05-0.25%. On a $500K portfolio, that is $8,750-$9,750
> per year in saved fees — compounded over 20 years, the difference is six figures.

### Private Markets vs Public Markets

Public markets offer liquidity (you can sell any day) but require extensive disclosure, regulatory
compliance, and exposure to short-term market sentiment.

Private markets offer:
- No public disclosure requirements
- Ability to focus on long-term value creation
- Higher potential returns (illiquidity premium of 2-5%)
- Access to earlier-stage companies

The venture capital pipeline:
```
Angel/Seed → Series A → Series B → Series C → ... → IPO (or acquisition)
$250K-$2M    $5M-$15M   $15M-$50M  $50M-$200M       Public listing

Each stage: higher valuation, lower risk, lower return potential
```

As of 2025, companies are staying private longer. Uber was private for 10 years before IPO.
Stripe was private for 13+ years. The best returns increasingly happen in private markets,
which is why accredited investor access matters for wealth building.

## 6. Foreign Exchange Markets

### The Largest Market in the World

The FX market trades approximately **$7.5 trillion per day**. For context, the NYSE trades
about $25 billion per day. FX is 300x larger.

The market operates 24 hours a day, 5 days a week, following the sun:
- **Sydney session:** 5pm-2am EST
- **Tokyo session:** 7pm-4am EST
- **London session:** 3am-12pm EST (highest volume)
- **New York session:** 8am-5pm EST

The London-New York overlap (8am-12pm EST) is the most liquid period.

**Major currency pairs (85% of volume):**
- EUR/USD (most traded pair globally)
- USD/JPY
- GBP/USD ("Cable" — named after the transatlantic telegraph cable)
- USD/CHF
- AUD/USD
- USD/CAD ("Loonie")

### Exchange Rate Determination

Exchange rates are driven by:

**1. Interest rate differentials (most important):**
If Canadian rates are 3% and US rates are 5%, capital flows from Canada to the US seeking
higher returns. This increases demand for USD and decreases demand for CAD, weakening CAD/USD.

**2. Purchasing Power Parity (PPP):**
In theory, exchange rates should adjust so that identical goods cost the same in both countries.
The Big Mac Index is a simplified version. In practice, PPP holds only over very long periods
(10+ years).

**3. Trade balances:**
Countries with trade surpluses (exporting more than importing) see their currency strengthen
because foreign buyers must purchase the domestic currency to pay for exports.

**4. Capital flows:**
Foreign investment into a country increases demand for its currency. If global investors are
buying Canadian real estate and equities, they need CAD, strengthening it.

**5. Political stability and institutional quality:**
Capital flees uncertainty. This is why the USD, CHF, and JPY are "safe haven" currencies.

### CAD/USD Dynamics

CAD is a **commodity currency** — its value is heavily correlated with oil prices because
Canada is the world's 4th-largest oil producer and oil is Canada's largest export.

```
Oil rises  → CAD strengthens vs USD → good for importers, bad for exporters
Oil falls  → CAD weakens vs USD → bad for importers, good for exporters
```

Other CAD drivers:
- BoC vs Fed rate differential
- Housing market health (housing is ~7% of Canadian GDP)
- Trade balance (Canada exports resources, imports manufactured goods)

Historical range (2015-2025): 1.20-1.45 CAD per USD. The long-term average is approximately
1.30.

### GBP and EUR Dynamics

**GBP (British Pound):**
- Lost ~15% on the Brexit referendum day (June 23, 2016) — largest one-day move in decades
- Crashed again in September 2022 during the Truss mini-budget (reached near-parity with USD)
- Now driven primarily by BoE policy, UK-EU trade friction, and London's financial center status

**EUR (Euro):**
- Challenged by Eurozone divergences: Germany's economy is fundamentally different from Italy's
  or Greece's, yet they share one monetary policy
- The ECB's negative rate experiment (2014-2022) weakened EUR significantly
- EUR/USD hit parity (1:1) in 2022 for the first time since 2002

### Currency Hedging for Business

If your business earns USD and spends CAD, you have **natural FX exposure**. A 10% move in
CAD/USD on $200K in USD revenue means a $20K swing in your CAD income.

**Hedging strategies:**

| Strategy | Cost | Protection | Best For |
|----------|------|------------|----------|
| **Forward contract** | Low (built into rate) | Locks in a future rate | Known future payments |
| **Currency options** | Premium (1-3%) | Right but not obligation to convert | Uncertain amounts |
| **Natural hedge** | Zero | Match USD revenue with USD expenses | Businesses with USD costs |
| **Multi-currency account** | Account fees only | Hold USD and convert when favorable | Ongoing USD income |
| **Invoice in home currency** | Zero | Shifts FX risk to customer | Strong bargaining position |

> **For Business Owners:** The simplest hedge for a Canadian business earning USD: open a Wise
> or RBC USD account. Receive USD natively. Pay USD expenses from it. Convert to CAD only when
> needed and when the rate is favorable. This natural matching eliminates most FX risk without
> any cost. Set rate alerts (e.g., convert when CAD/USD drops below 1.30) to optimize timing.
> NEVER convert large amounts at the bank counter without comparing to the mid-market rate.

## 7. The Credit System

### Personal Credit Scores (Canada)

Canada has two credit bureaus: **Equifax** and **TransUnion**. Credit scores range from 300 to
900.

| Range | Rating | Meaning |
|-------|--------|---------|
| 800-900 | Excellent | Best rates, highest approval odds |
| 720-799 | Very Good | Near-best rates, easy approvals |
| 660-719 | Good | Decent rates, most products available |
| 600-659 | Fair | Higher rates, some restrictions |
| 300-599 | Poor | Limited options, secured products only |

**Score components:**
- **Payment history (35%):** Have you paid on time? A single 30-day late payment can drop your
  score 50-100 points.
- **Credit utilization (30%):** How much of your available credit are you using? Keep below 30%.
  Ideal is below 10%.
- **Credit history length (15%):** Average age of accounts. Do not close your oldest credit card.
- **Credit mix (10%):** Having different types (credit card, line of credit, mortgage) is better
  than only one type.
- **New credit inquiries (10%):** Each hard inquiry drops your score 5-10 points temporarily.

### Business Credit

Business credit is separate from personal credit, but for sole proprietors and small businesses,
they are often intertwined because owners must personally guarantee loans.

**Building business credit:**
1. Register your business (provincial registration or federal incorporation)
2. Get a Business Number (BN) from CRA
3. Open a business bank account (never mix personal and business)
4. Get a business credit card (pay on time, every time)
5. Register with Dun & Bradstreet to get a DUNS number
6. Start with trade credit from suppliers (Net 30 terms)
7. Pay early — D&B rewards early payment with higher scores
8. After 12 months of history, apply for a business line of credit

**Business credit reports:**
- **Dun & Bradstreet PAYDEX:** 0-100 scale. Score of 80+ = paying on time. 100 = paying early.
- **Equifax Business:** 0-100 risk score, payment index, credit utilization.

> **For Business Owners:** Your business credit score determines the interest rate and terms on
> business loans, lines of credit, and even commercial leases. Start building it from day one.
> The most common mistake: mixing personal and business finances, which prevents building
> separate business credit history. Keep them completely separate.

### Small Business Financing Options

**1. Canada Small Business Financing Program (CSBFP):**
- Government-guaranteed loans up to **$1,000,000**
- Eligible: businesses under $10M revenue
- Up to $500K for equipment and leasehold improvements
- Up to $500K for real property (can combine for $1M total)
- Government guarantees 85% of the loan — the bank takes minimal risk
- Interest rate: prime + 3% (variable) or chartered bank residential mortgage rate + 3% (fixed)
- Registration fee: 2% of loan amount
- Apply through any chartered bank or credit union

**2. BDC (Business Development Bank of Canada):**
- Crown corporation focused on small business lending
- More flexible than banks — will lend to businesses banks reject
- Working capital loans, term loans, growth financing
- Often higher rates than banks (prime + 4-6%) but more accessible

**3. Export Development Canada (EDC):**
- If your business exports (including SaaS sold to foreign customers)
- Trade credit insurance, working capital guarantees, direct loans

**4. Business line of credit:**
- Revolving facility, draw and repay as needed
- Typically $10K-$250K for small businesses
- Interest only on drawn amount
- Prime + 1-3% for good credit

**5. Invoice factoring / AR financing:**
- Sell your outstanding invoices at a discount (typically 2-5%)
- Get 80-90% of invoice value immediately, rest when customer pays
- Useful for businesses with long payment terms (Net 60/90)

**6. Revenue-based financing (Clearco, Lighter Capital, Pipe):**
- No equity dilution
- Repay as a percentage of monthly revenue (typically 5-10%)
- Total repayment: 1.1-1.5x the borrowed amount
- Fast approval based on revenue data (connected to Stripe/bank)
- Best for SaaS with predictable MRR

## 8. Payment Systems and Fintech

### How Credit Card Processing Works

When a customer taps their Visa at your business, here is what happens in 2 seconds:

```
Customer → POS Terminal → Payment Processor → Card Network → Issuing Bank
                                    ↓
                            Card Network verifies
                                    ↓
                            Issuing Bank authorizes
                                    ↓
         Funds settle (next business day) → Acquiring Bank → Your bank account
```

**The fee stack on a $100 transaction (Visa):**

| Component | Fee | Goes To |
|-----------|-----|---------|
| Interchange fee | ~1.5% ($1.50) | Issuing bank (customer's bank) |
| Network fee (assessment) | ~0.13% ($0.13) | Visa/Mastercard |
| Processor markup | ~0.3-1.3% ($0.30-$1.30) | Stripe, Square, Moneris |
| **Total** | **~2.0-2.9%** | |

**Interchange-plus pricing:** The processor charges interchange + a fixed markup. This is the
most transparent model. Example: interchange + 0.30% + $0.10/transaction.

**Flat-rate pricing:** Stripe charges 2.9% + $0.30 regardless of card type. Simpler but more
expensive on debit cards (which have low interchange).

In Canada, debit card interchange was capped at $0.01-$0.05 per transaction by the Interac
network — drastically cheaper than credit cards. This is why many Canadian businesses
incentivize debit (or charge a credit card surcharge).

> **For Business Owners:** If you process more than $50K/month in credit cards, switch from
> flat-rate pricing (Stripe's 2.9% + $0.30) to interchange-plus pricing. On $100K/month:
> - Flat rate: $2,900 + $300 = $3,200/month
> - Interchange-plus: ~$1,800-$2,200/month (depending on card mix)
> - Annual savings: $12,000-$16,800
>
> Negotiation leverage increases with volume. At $500K+/month, processors will compete
> aggressively for your business.

### Payment Methods Compared

| Method | Speed | Cost | Best For |
|--------|-------|------|----------|
| **Interac e-Transfer** | Minutes-hours | Free-$1.50 | Canadian domestic payments |
| **EFT (Electronic Funds Transfer)** | 1-3 business days | $0-$1 | Payroll, recurring payments |
| **Wire transfer** | Same day-next day | $25-$80 | Large, urgent payments |
| **ACH (US)** | 1-3 business days | $0.20-$1.50 | US domestic payments |
| **SEPA (Europe)** | 1 day (instant available) | EUR 0.20-EUR 1 | EUR payments in Europe |
| **Wise** | 1-3 days | 0.4-1.0% | International transfers |
| **PayPal** | Instant (to PayPal) | 2.9% + fixed | Consumer payments, small merchants |
| **Cryptocurrency** | Minutes-hours | Variable ($0.01-$20+) | Cross-border, unbanked |

### Cryptocurrency Payment Rails

Crypto is increasingly used for business payments, particularly cross-border:

**Lightning Network (Bitcoin Layer 2):**
- Near-instant, fees under $0.01
- Used by El Salvador, major payment processors integrating
- Limitation: requires channel liquidity management

**Stablecoins (USDC, USDT):**
- Pegged to USD, minimal volatility
- USDC (Circle) is regulated, transparent reserves, audited
- USDT (Tether) has larger market share but less transparent
- Increasingly used for B2B payments, freelancer payments, cross-border settlement
- Settle on Ethereum (higher fees), Solana, or Tron (low fees)

**Note for Canadians:** Ontario restricts USDT. Use USDC or USD pairs.

### Open Banking

Open banking requires banks to share customer data (with consent) with third-party providers
via secure APIs.

**EU (PSD2):** Implemented since 2018. Enabled fintechs to build products on bank data.
**UK:** Implemented since 2018. One of the most advanced open banking ecosystems.
**Canada:** Consumer-Driven Banking Framework being implemented. Full rollout expected 2026-2027.
**US:** CFPB's Section 1033 rule in development.

**What open banking enables:**
- Instant bank account verification (no more void cheques)
- Aggregated financial dashboards
- AI-powered bookkeeping (auto-categorize transactions)
- Better lending decisions (lenders see real cash flow, not just credit scores)
- Payment initiation without card networks (lower fees)

> **For Business Owners:** Open banking will fundamentally change small business finance. When
> lenders can see your real-time bank data (with your consent), approval becomes faster and
> more accurate. Revenue-based lending becomes frictionless. Start preparing by keeping clean
> banking records and separating business/personal accounts completely.

## 9. Offshore Banking and International Finance

### Why Businesses Use Offshore Accounts (Legitimate Reasons)

"Offshore" has negative connotations, but there are many legal, legitimate reasons:

1. **Multi-currency revenue:** You earn GBP, USD, and EUR — you need accounts in those currencies
2. **Market access:** Operating in Singapore requires a Singapore bank account
3. **Payment processing:** Some payment processors require accounts in specific jurisdictions
4. **Treasury diversification:** Not putting all eggs in one basket (one banking system)
5. **Regulatory compliance:** Some industries require segregated client funds in specific jurisdictions
6. **Tax treaty optimization:** Structuring entities in treaty-favorable jurisdictions (legal with substance)

### Major Banking Jurisdictions

| Jurisdiction | Strengths | Corporate Tax | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|-------|
| **Switzerland** | Privacy (reduced since CRS), stability, CHF | 14.9% (federal+cantonal varies) | Minimum balances often CHF 250K+ |
| **Singapore** | Asia gateway, English-speaking, efficient | 17% | Strong IP protection, DTA network |
| **Channel Islands (Guernsey/Jersey)** | 0% corporate, Crown Dependencies | 0% (most companies) | UK-adjacent, English-speaking |
| **Isle of Man** | 0% corporate, 0% CGT, GBP 200K tax cap | 0% | Ideal for digital businesses |
| **Ireland** | EU access, 6.25% KDB rate, English-speaking | 12.5% (trading) / 6.25% (KDB) | Tech hub, extensive treaty network |
| **Dubai/UAE** | 0% personal income tax, free zones | 9% (above AED 375K) | Growing financial center |
| **Cayman Islands** | 0% everything, fund domicile | 0% | No CRS reporting (but FATCA applies) |
| **Estonia** | 0% on retained earnings, e-Residency | 0% retained / 20% distributed | Digital-first, EU member |

### International Reporting Requirements

**CRS (Common Reporting Standard) / AEOI:**
- 100+ countries automatically exchange financial account information
- Your offshore bank reports your account details to your home country's tax authority
- There is effectively no financial privacy between CRS countries anymore
- This means offshore accounts for tax evasion is dead — but tax optimization through legitimate
  structures remains legal and effective

**FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act — US):**
- US citizens and residents must report ALL foreign accounts
- Foreign banks must report accounts held by US persons to the IRS
- Non-compliance = 30% withholding on US-source payments

**CARF (Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework — 2026+):**
- Similar to CRS but for cryptocurrency exchanges
- Exchanges must report user transaction data to tax authorities
- Being implemented globally starting 2026

**Canadian Foreign Reporting:**
- **T1135:** Foreign property exceeding $100K CAD in aggregate (bank accounts, foreign stocks,
  foreign real estate, etc.)
- **T1134:** Information about foreign affiliates
- **T1141/T1142:** Trust-related foreign property
- Penalties for non-filing: $25/day, min $100, max $2,500 per form

> **For Business Owners:** If you hold more than $100K CAD in foreign property (including US
> bank accounts, foreign brokerage accounts, or foreign real estate — but NOT personal-use
> property or RRSP/TFSA foreign content), you MUST file T1135 annually. The penalty for
> non-filing is severe: $2,500/year per form, plus potential reassessment of unreported
> foreign income with no time limit (CRA can go back indefinitely for unreported foreign
> income). File this form. Every year. No exceptions.

### Transfer Pricing

When a business has entities in multiple countries and they transact with each other, the price
must be at **arm's length** — the same price unrelated parties would charge.

**Example:** Your Canadian operating company pays your Irish IP-holding company a royalty for
using software IP. If the royalty is inflated to shift profits to Ireland's lower tax rate, CRA
will reassess under Section 247.

**OECD Transfer Pricing Methods (ranked by reliability):**
1. Comparable Uncontrolled Price (CUP) — find a comparable transaction between unrelated parties
2. Resale Price Method — subtract a normal margin from the resale price
3. Cost Plus Method — add a normal markup to costs
4. Transactional Net Margin Method (TNMM) — compare net margins to comparable companies
5. Profit Split Method — split profits based on each entity's contribution

**Documentation requirements:** CRA expects contemporaneous transfer pricing documentation. If
you cannot produce it on audit, penalties are 10% of the adjustment.

## 10. Economic Cycles and Business Implications

### The Business Cycle

Every market economy cycles through four phases:

```
         PEAK
        /    \
       /      \
EXPANSION      CONTRACTION
     /            \
    /              \
TROUGH ──────────── TROUGH
```

**Expansion (Growth):**
- GDP growing, unemployment falling
- Corporate earnings rising, consumer confidence high
- Credit is easy to get, banks lend aggressively
- Asset prices rise (stocks, real estate)
- Duration: 3-10 years (average ~5 years)

**Peak:**
- Economy overheating, inflation rising
- Central bank hiking rates to cool things down
- Euphoria in markets ("this time is different")
- Speculation increases (crypto bubbles, meme stocks, SPACs)

**Contraction (Recession):**
- Two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth (informal definition)
- Unemployment rising, consumer confidence falling
- Credit tightens, banks restrict lending
- Asset prices fall
- Duration: 6-18 months (average ~11 months)

**Trough:**
- Economy hits bottom
- Central bank has cut rates aggressively
- Pessimism is maximum ("it will never recover")
- This is historically the best time to invest and acquire

### Leading, Coincident, and Lagging Indicators

**Leading indicators (predict the future):**
| Indicator | What It Signals | Where to Find |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| Yield curve (2y-10y spread) | Inversion = recession in 12-18 months | Bank of Canada, FRED |
| PMI (Purchasing Managers Index) | Below 50 = contraction | IHS Markit, ISM |
| Building permits | Future construction activity | Statistics Canada |
| Consumer confidence | Future spending | Conference Board of Canada |
| New orders / inventory ratio | Manufacturing momentum | Statistics Canada |
| Stock market (S&P 500, TSX) | Markets are forward-looking (6-9 months) | Any financial site |
| Copper price ("Dr. Copper") | Industrial demand proxy | Commodity exchanges |
| Initial jobless claims | Early labor market weakness | Statistics Canada, BLS |

**Coincident indicators (confirm current conditions):**
- Real GDP growth
- Industrial production
- Retail sales
- Employment (non-farm payrolls in US, Labour Force Survey in Canada)

**Lagging indicators (confirm what already happened):**
- Unemployment rate (peaks well AFTER a recession ends)
- CPI (inflation responds to policy with 12-18 month lag)
- Corporate profits (reported quarterly, backward-looking)
- Prime rate (follows central bank rates, which are already responding to data)

### Positioning Your Business Through Cycles

**During expansion:**
- Invest in growth (hire, market, expand)
- Build cash reserves for the downturn (target 6 months operating expenses)
- Lock in long-term fixed-rate debt while credit is easy
- Be cautious of overpaying for acquisitions (multiples are high)

**During peak (euphoria):**
- Reduce discretionary spending
- Maximize cash reserves
- Renegotiate and extend credit facilities while banks are still lending
- Prepare a list of assets/companies you want to acquire in the downturn
- Stress test: what happens if revenue drops 20-30%?

**During contraction (recession):**
- Cut variable costs (contractors, marketing experiments, unused software)
- Protect core team (your best people are your moat)
- Acquire distressed competitors at discount valuations
- Renegotiate leases, contracts, supplier terms (everyone is flexible in a recession)
- Do NOT cut R&D if you can afford it — emerge stronger

**During trough:**
- Deploy cash into investments (stocks, real estate, acquisitions)
- Hire top talent that was laid off by others
- Negotiate aggressively (landlords, suppliers, partners are desperate)
- Invest in brand/marketing — competition has pulled back, your voice is louder

> **For Business Owners:** The #1 mistake small businesses make is being procyclical: spending
> when times are good (because they can), cutting when times are bad (because they must). The
> wealthy are counter-cyclical: they build reserves in good times and deploy them in bad times.
> Warren Buffett's famous advice: "Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others
> are fearful." This applies to business operations as much as investing.

### SaaS and Recurring Revenue Through Cycles

SaaS businesses have a structural advantage in recessions:

1. **Switching costs:** Customers are less likely to migrate software during a downturn
2. **Mission-critical:** If your product is embedded in their workflow, it is the last thing cut
3. **Contract revenue:** Annual contracts provide visibility even as new sales slow
4. **Net revenue retention (NRR):** Expansion revenue from existing customers can offset churn

However:
- Sales cycles lengthen dramatically in recessions (more decision-makers, more approvals)
- Downgrades increase (customers move to cheaper tiers)
- SMB customers have higher churn risk than enterprise
- CAC payback periods extend

The key SaaS metric in a downturn is **Rule of 40:** Growth Rate + Profit Margin should
exceed 40%. If growth slows from 50% to 20%, you need 20% margins (meaning you must become
profitable, not just grow).

## 11. Tax Systems Globally — CFO-Level Understanding

### Types of Tax Systems

**Progressive taxation:** Higher income = higher rate (Canada, US, UK, most of Europe)
- Effective rate is always lower than marginal rate (common misconception)
- Canadian example: On $100K income, marginal rate is 33% (combined), but effective rate is ~22%

**Flat tax:** One rate for everyone (Russia 13%, Romania 10%, Hungary 15% personal)
- Simpler but more regressive

**Consumption-based taxation:** Tax spending, not income (VAT/GST/HST)
- Encourages saving and investment
- More regressive (lower-income people spend a higher % of income)
- Estonia's model (0% retained corporate earnings, 20% on distributions) is consumption-based

**Territorial taxation:** Only tax income earned within the country's borders (Singapore, Hong Kong)
- Citizens/residents are not taxed on foreign-source income
- Contrast with: US (worldwide taxation of citizens) and Canada (worldwide taxation of residents)

### VAT/GST/HST Systems

Value Added Tax (VAT) or Goods and Services Tax (GST) is a consumption tax applied at each
stage of the supply chain, but only on the **value added** at each stage.

**How it works (Canada HST at 13% in Ontario):**
1. Manufacturer sells widget to distributor for $100 + $13 HST = $113
2. Distributor sells to retailer for $150 + $19.50 HST = $169.50
   - Distributor remits $19.50 - $13.00 = $6.50 to CRA (only the value added)
3. Retailer sells to consumer for $200 + $26 HST = $226
   - Retailer remits $26.00 - $19.50 = $6.50 to CRA

Total HST collected by CRA: $13 + $6.50 + $6.50 = $26 (= 13% of $200 final price)
Each business only pays tax on its own value added.

**Input Tax Credits (ITCs):** Businesses recover the HST they pay on business expenses. This is
why HST registration (even below the $30K mandatory threshold) can be beneficial — you recover
HST on expenses but must charge it to customers.

**Global rates:**
- Canada GST: 5% (+ PST varies by province, or combined HST)
- US: No federal VAT/GST (state sales tax 0-10.25%)
- UK VAT: 20%
- EU VAT: 17-27% (Luxembourg lowest, Hungary highest)
- Australia GST: 10%
- Japan: 10%
- Singapore GST: 9%

### Transfer Pricing and BEPS

**BEPS (Base Erosion and Profit Shifting):** The OECD's framework to prevent multinational
companies from shifting profits to low-tax jurisdictions through artificial arrangements.

**Pillar One:** Reallocates some taxing rights to countries where customers are located
(targeting large tech companies that profit from users without physical presence).

**Pillar Two: Global Minimum Tax (15%):**
- Applies to companies with revenue over EUR 750M
- If a subsidiary pays less than 15% effective tax in any jurisdiction, the parent country
  collects a "top-up tax" to bring it to 15%
- Implemented starting 2024 in Canada, UK, EU, Japan, South Korea, and others
- The US has NOT implemented Pillar Two (as of early 2026)
- Impact: reduces but does not eliminate the benefit of low-tax jurisdictions for large MNCs
- Does NOT apply to small businesses — the EUR 750M threshold means this is irrelevant for most

### The Double Irish with a Dutch Sandwich (Historical)

This was the most famous corporate tax avoidance structure, used by Google, Apple, and others:

```
US Parent Company
    │
    ├── Irish Company #1 (tax resident in Bermuda via management & control)
    │       │
    │       └── Dutch Company (Netherlands — conduit)
    │               │
    │               └── Irish Company #2 (tax resident in Ireland)
    │                       │
    │                       └── Customers worldwide
    │
    Flow: Revenue → Irish Co #2 → Royalty to Dutch Co → Royalty to Irish Co #1 (Bermuda)
    Result: Profits taxed at ~0% instead of 12.5% or 21%
```

Ireland closed this structure in 2015, with grandfathering until 2020. It has been replaced by
less aggressive structures using Ireland's Knowledge Development Box (6.25%), patent boxes in
other countries, and more substance-based planning.

### Why Companies Hold Trillions Offshore

As of peak (2017, before US Tax Cuts and Jobs Act):
- Apple: $252B offshore
- Microsoft: $124B
- Cisco: $68B
- Google/Alphabet: $60B
- Oracle: $51B

These companies were not breaking the law. Under US worldwide taxation, foreign profits were
only taxed when **repatriated** (brought back to the US). So companies left money abroad
indefinitely, investing it in foreign operations or US bonds through foreign subsidiaries.

The 2017 TCJA imposed a one-time repatriation tax (15.5% on cash, 8% on other assets) and
moved the US closer to a territorial system. Most of that money has since been repatriated.

**Canada's FAPI rules** prevent this for CCPCs — Canadian-controlled companies are taxed on
certain foreign passive income immediately, regardless of repatriation.

> **For Business Owners:** International tax optimization is real and legal, but it requires
> substance (real operations, real employees, real decision-making in the foreign jurisdiction).
> The era of shell companies with no substance is over. If you are earning $300K+ and have
> international operations or customers, consult a cross-border tax specialist. The savings
> can easily be $30K-$100K+/year, but the complexity and compliance cost must be factored in.
> Below $120K revenue, the compliance cost usually exceeds the tax savings.

### The Three Financial Statements

**1. Income Statement (Profit & Loss / P&L)**

Shows revenue and expenses over a period (monthly, quarterly, annually).

```

  - Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)           -$150,000
  ─────────────────────────────────────
Gross Profit                             $350,000    (Gross Margin: 70%)
  - Operating Expenses
      Salaries & Wages                   -$120,000
      Rent                                -$24,000

      Professional Fees                    -$8,000

      Other                               -$10,000
  ─────────────────────────────────────

  - Depreciation/Amortization             -$15,000

    Vehicles           $25,000          ─────────────────────────
  ─────────────────────────             Total Liabilities    $107,000
  Total Non-Current    $80,000
                                        EQUITY
                                          Owner's Equity     $121,000
─────────────────────────               ─────────────────────────
TOTAL ASSETS          $228,000          TOTAL L + E          $228,000
```

**3. Cash Flow Statement**

Shows actual cash movements. You can be profitable and still run out of cash (and vice versa).
This is the most important statement for survival.

```

  Net Profit                              $93,600
  + Depreciation (non-cash expense)       +$15,000
  - Increase in Accounts Receivable       -$10,000
  + Increase in Accounts Payable          +$5,000
  ─────────────────────────────────────

  Equipment Purchases                     -$20,000
  ─────────────────────────────────────

  ─────────────────────────────────────

```

### Key Financial Ratios

| Ratio | Formula | Healthy Range | What It Tells You |
|-------|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Gross Margin** | (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue | 50-80% (SaaS: 70-85%) | Efficiency of core delivery |
| **Net Margin** | Net Profit / Revenue | 10-25% (varies by industry) | Overall profitability |
| **Current Ratio** | Current Assets / Current Liabilities | 1.5-3.0 | Can you pay short-term obligations? |
| **Quick Ratio** | (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities | 1.0-2.0 | Same, but without inventory |
| **Debt-to-Equity** | Total Liabilities / Total Equity | 0.5-2.0 | How leveraged is the business? |
| **Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)** | (AR / Revenue) x 365 | 30-45 days | How fast do customers pay? |
| **Burn Rate** | Monthly cash outflow | N/A | Months of cash remaining |
| **Runway** | Cash / Monthly Burn Rate | 12+ months ideal | How long before you run out of cash? |
| **LTV:CAC** | Lifetime Value / Customer Acquisition Cost | 3:1 or higher | Unit economics — is each customer profitable? |
| **Rule of 40** | Growth Rate + Profit Margin | > 40% | SaaS health metric |

### Accrual vs Cash Accounting

**Cash accounting:** Record revenue when cash hits the bank. Record expenses when you pay.
- Simple, intuitive
- Required for most sole proprietors in Canada
- Problem: a $50K invoice sent in December but paid in January shows as January revenue

**Accrual accounting:** Record revenue when earned (invoice sent). Record expenses when incurred.
- Matches revenue to the period it was earned
- Required for corporations and businesses over $1M revenue (in many jurisdictions)
- Better for decision-making (shows true economic activity per period)
- CRA allows either method for businesses under the revenue threshold

### Investment Decision Metrics

**Net Present Value (NPV):**
The value today of a series of future cash flows, discounted at your required rate of return.

```
NPV = -Initial Investment + CF1/(1+r) + CF2/(1+r)^2 + ... + CFn/(1+r)^n

Example: Invest $50,000 now, receive $20,000/year for 4 years, discount rate 10%
NPV = -$50,000 + $20,000/1.10 + $20,000/1.21 + $20,000/1.331 + $20,000/1.464
NPV = -$50,000 + $18,182 + $16,529 + $15,026 + $13,660
NPV = +$13,397

Positive NPV → the investment generates value above your required return → DO IT
```

**Internal Rate of Return (IRR):**
The discount rate that makes NPV exactly zero. If IRR > your required return, the investment
is worthwhile. In the example above, the IRR is approximately 22%.

**Return on Investment (ROI):**
```
ROI = (Net Profit from Investment / Cost of Investment) x 100%

Example: Spend $10,000 on marketing, generate $35,000 in revenue ($15,000 profit after COGS)
ROI = ($15,000 / $10,000) x 100% = 150%
```

**Payback Period:**
How long until you recover your initial investment.
```
Payback = Initial Investment / Annual Cash Flow
$50,000 / $20,000 = 2.5 years
```

> **For Business Owners:** Every significant business expenditure should pass at least one of
> these tests. Hiring a new developer for $120K? What is the NPV of the revenue they will
> help generate? Buying $30K in equipment? What is the payback period? Running $50K in ads?
> What ROI do you need to justify it? (Hint: at minimum 3:1, ideally 5:1+). If you cannot
> estimate the return, you are gambling, not investing.

### Working Capital Management

Working capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities. It is the cash available for day-to-day
operations.

**The working capital cycle:**
```
Cash → Purchase inventory/materials → Deliver product/service → Invoice → Collect payment → Cash
         (Days Payable)                                          (Days Sales Outstanding)
```

The cash conversion cycle (CCC) measures how long your cash is tied up:
```
CCC = Days Inventory Outstanding + Days Sales Outstanding - Days Payable Outstanding

Example:
  DIO = 15 days (you hold inventory for 15 days before selling)
  DSO = 45 days (customers take 45 days to pay)
  DPO = 30 days (you take 30 days to pay suppliers)
  CCC = 15 + 45 - 30 = 30 days

Your cash is tied up for 30 days on average.
```

**How to improve working capital:**
- **Reduce DSO:** Invoice immediately, offer early payment discounts (2/10 Net 30), use
  automated payment reminders, require deposits for large projects
- **Increase DPO:** Negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers (Net 60 instead of Net 30)
  — but maintain relationships
- **Reduce DIO:** Just-in-time inventory, drop underperforming products
- **Get paid upfront:** SaaS annual billing, deposits, retainers

For SaaS businesses: DSO is minimal (payment at subscription time), DIO is zero (no physical
goods), so working capital is typically strong. The main risk is deferred revenue (recognizing
annual contracts over 12 months while collecting cash upfront).

### Break-Even Analysis

The point where total revenue equals total costs (zero profit).

```
Break-Even Revenue = Fixed Costs / Gross Margin %

Example:
  Monthly fixed costs: $15,000 (rent, salaries, software, insurance)
  Gross margin: 70%
  Break-even: $15,000 / 0.70 = $21,429/month in revenue

Below $21,429: losing money
Above $21,429: every additional dollar contributes $0.70 to profit
```

For SaaS:
```
Break-Even MRR = Monthly Operating Costs / (1 - COGS% per customer)

If operating costs are $20,000/month and COGS is 20% per customer:
Break-even MRR = $20,000 / 0.80 = $25,000 MRR (~$300K ARR)
```

### Board-Level Financial Reporting

If you have investors, advisors, or a board, they expect standardized reporting:

**Monthly metrics (dashboard format):**
- Revenue (MRR/ARR for SaaS), growth rate MoM/YoY
- Gross margin %
- Operating expenses by category
- Net income / EBITDA
- Cash position and runway
- Key SaaS metrics: churn, NRR, LTV:CAC, payback period
- AR aging (how old are unpaid invoices?)

**Quarterly review:**
- Full financial statements (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow)
- Variance analysis (actual vs budget)
- Cash flow forecast (next 6-12 months)
- Key risks and mitigation
- Strategic initiatives and their financial impact

**Annual:**
- Audited or review engagement financial statements (if required)
- Tax planning review (timing of income/expenses)
- Budget for next year
- Capital allocation plan (reinvest, distribute, reserve)

> **For Business Owners:** Even if you have no board or investors, produce monthly financial
> statements. The discipline of reviewing your numbers monthly will save you from the #1
> cause of business failure: running out of cash without realizing it until too late. Use
> accounting software (Wave for free, QuickBooks Online for growing businesses, Xero for
> international). Automate bank feeds. Do a 30-minute financial review on the 1st of every
> month. This single habit separates surviving businesses from thriving ones.
