## 1. HOW VEHICLE DEDUCTIONS ACTUALLY WORK

### 1.1 The Core Principle (Plain English First)

Think of your car as a tool that you use for two purposes: earning money (business) and living your
life (personal). The tax system lets you write off the business portion. CRA does not care what kind
of car you drive — they care about the **percentage** you use it for work.

The formula is simple:

```
Business use % = Business kilometres ÷ Total kilometres driven in the year

Example:
Total km driven January–December: 25,000
Business km (client meetings, supply pickups, site visits): 15,000
Business use % = 15,000 ÷ 25,000 = 60%
```

Every vehicle expense — gas, insurance, oil changes, interest on your car loan — gets multiplied by
that percentage to arrive at your deduction.

**What counts as "business use":**
- Driving to client locations (not your regular office)
- Picking up supplies or equipment
- Going to the bank, post office, accountant, lawyer — on business days for business purposes
- Business site inspections or deliveries
- Travel between two work locations (e.g., your home office to a client site)

**What does NOT count as "business use":**
- Driving from home to your regular fixed workplace (this is commuting — it is personal)
- Running personal errands on a day you also did business driving
- Vacations, weekend trips, driving your family around

**The ITA authority:** s.18(1)(a) — expenses must be incurred for the purpose of earning income.
s.18(1)(h) — personal living expenses are explicitly not deductible. Vehicle expenses are subject to
both rules simultaneously, which is why the business use percentage is the dividing line.

### 1.2 Sole Proprietor vs Employee vs Corporation — Three Different Regimes

Before diving into the rules, understand which regime applies to you:

| Taxpayer Type | Where Claimed | Rules Apply |
|---------------|--------------|-------------|
| **Sole proprietor / self-employed** | T2125, Part 7 | Most flexible — this guide's primary focus |
| **Employee** | T777 (must have T2200 from employer) | Much more restrictive (s.8(1)(h.1)) |
| **Corporation (vehicle owned by corp)** | T2 Schedule — deducted at corp level | Standby charge applies to shareholder-employees |
| **Shareholder using personal vehicle for corp business** | T2200 required | Same as employee rules |

`[CC]` CC is currently a sole proprietor. All deductions flow through T2125 Part 7. This is the
most advantageous position — no need for a T2200, no employer sign-off, full discretion over
business use % (supported by logbook).

## 2. THE TWO CALCULATION METHODS

### 2.1 Method 1 — Detailed (Actual Expenses) Method

**How it works:** Track every dollar you spend on your vehicle during the year. Multiply the total
by your business use percentage.

```
Total actual vehicle expenses for the year:
  Gas and oil:                    $2,400
  Insurance:                      $1,800
  Repairs and maintenance:          $900
  Licence and registration:         $120
  Interest on car loan (capped):  $2,400 (see Section 3.7 for cap rules)
  CCA (depreciation):             $4,500 (see Section 4 for calculation)
  ─────────────────────────────────────
  Total vehicle expenses:        $12,120

Business use %:                       60%
─────────────────────────────────────────
Total vehicle deduction:          $7,272
```

This method usually gives you MORE money back — especially if you have a newer car, significant
repairs, or are also claiming CCA. It requires keeping all receipts.

**Best for:**
- High actual expenses (newer or expensive car, lots of repairs)
- Cars with significant depreciation (large CCA claim)
- Larger business use percentages (60%+)
- Anyone who wants to maximize their deduction

### 2.2 Method 2 — Simplified (Per-Kilometre) Method

**How it works:** CRA publishes a flat rate per kilometre. Multiply the rate by your business km.
No receipts required. No CCA calculation.

**2024 CRA rates (Treasury Board):**
- First 5,000 km: **$0.70/km**
- Each km after 5,000: **$0.64/km**
- Territories: $0.74/km and $0.68/km respectively

```
Example: 15,000 business km in 2024 (Ontario)
First 5,000 km:  5,000 × $0.70 = $3,500
Next 10,000 km: 10,000 × $0.64 = $6,400
Total deduction:                  $9,900
```

**Compare to detailed method above:** $7,272 detailed vs $9,900 simplified.
In THIS example, simplified wins. But swap in an $80K EV with immediate expensing and the
detailed method beats simplified by $20,000+ in year 1.

**Best for:**
- Older, fully depreciated cars (no CCA left to claim)
- Low actual expenses (cheap, reliable car)
- Very high business km (the per-km rate rewards mileage)
- People who hate keeping receipts

**Cannot use simplified if:** You are also claiming CCA on the vehicle. You must pick one or the
other — you cannot mix. If you are claiming CCA (depreciation), use the detailed method.

**Authority:** IT-521R paragraph 8; CRA RC4064 (Medical and Disability-Related Information — but the
per-km rate table is published by Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat and CRA adopts it each year)

### 2.3 Which Method to Choose — Decision Framework

```
Is your car fully depreciated (UCC = $0)?
  YES → Simplified method likely wins (no CCA to claim)
  NO  → Continue

Are you incorporating soon and want maximum year-1 deduction?
  YES → Detailed method + immediate expensing (Section 5) can be massive
  NO  → Continue

Are your actual expenses (gas + insurance + repairs + interest) above the
per-km equivalent?
  YES (actual expenses high) → Detailed method wins
  NO (cheap, reliable car)   → Simplified method may win

Rule of thumb: Run both calculations. Pick the higher number. CRA allows you
to change method year-to-year as long as you are consistent within each year.
```

## 3. EVERY DEDUCTIBLE VEHICLE EXPENSE

### 3.1 Fuel and Oil

**What it is:** Every fill-up, every oil change, every top-up of washer fluid — any fluid that goes
into the car to make it run.

**Deductible:** Business use % of total annual fuel costs.

**How to track:** Credit/debit card statements work perfectly. Cash fill-ups need receipts. Gas
station apps (Shell, Esso, Petro-Canada) often have digital receipts going back 12+ months.

**Dollar impact example:**
- Annual fuel: $3,000
- Business use: 65%
- Deduction: $1,950

`[NOW]` Use one credit card exclusively for vehicle expenses. Simplifies record-keeping massively.

### 3.2 Insurance

**What it is:** Your annual car insurance premium.

**Deductible:** Business use % of total premium.

**Special case — if you have add-on rideshare or commercial insurance:**
If you added a commercial endorsement to your policy specifically because of business use, that
premium add-on is 100% deductible (it exists solely for the business use). The base personal
insurance is still split by business use %.

**Dollar impact example:**
- Annual insurance: $1,800
- Business use: 65%
- Deduction: $1,170

**Authority:** s.20(1)(a) (via T2125 Part 7 instructions); IT-521R paragraph 4

### 3.3 Repairs and Maintenance

**What it is:** Oil changes, tire rotations, brake jobs, new tires, windshield replacement, battery
replacement, body work, detailing, emissions testing, wheel alignments — anything that keeps the
car running or fixes damage.

**Deductible:** Business use % of total repair and maintenance costs.

**Special situation:** If you damage the car on a business trip (e.g., backing into a pillar at a
client's parking lot), that repair is arguably 100% business-related — document the incident.

**Dollar impact example:**
- Annual maintenance: $1,200 (includes 2 tire sets and oil changes)
- Business use: 65%
- Deduction: $780

### 3.4 Licence and Registration

**What it is:** Annual Ontario licence plate renewal sticker, driver's licence renewal fee (if
applicable to business), vehicle permit.

**Deductible:** Business use % of licence and registration fees.

**Dollar impact example:**
- Annual plate renewal: $120
- Business use: 65%
- Deduction: $78

It is a small deduction but every dollar counts. Include it.

### 3.5 Parking

**What it is:** Parking fees paid while conducting business — meter fees, parkade costs, client site
parking.

**Deductible:** 100% when parking for a business purpose (not your daily office parking).

**What is NOT deductible:** Parking at your regular place of work. If you drive to your own office
or studio every day, that parking is personal (same rule as the commute itself).

**What IS deductible:**
- Parking at a client's building for a meeting
- Parking at a government office for a business errand
- Parking at your accountant's, lawyer's, or bank's location

`[RISK]` CRA specifically audits large parking claims. Keep a log of where you parked and why.

**Authority:** IT-521R paragraph 5

### 3.6 Highway Tolls

**What it is:** 407 ETR charges, bridge tolls, highway tolls on business trips.

**Deductible:** 100% when incurred while travelling for business purposes.

**Pro tip:** The 407 ETR sends annual statements — download it in January for the prior year. It
shows every trip with date and time. This doubles as logbook corroboration.

### 3.7 Interest on a Car Loan — The $300/Month Cap

**Plain English:** If you borrowed money to buy the car and you pay interest on that loan, you can
deduct the business % of the interest. BUT CRA caps how much interest qualifies — no matter how
high your actual interest is, CRA only allows you to deduct as if you paid a maximum of $300/month
(=$10/day) in interest.

**The formula:**
```
Step 1: Determine actual monthly interest paid
Step 2: Cap it at $300/month if higher
Step 3: Multiply capped amount by business use %

Example A (interest under cap):
Actual monthly interest: $250
Capped amount: $250 (already under $300)
Business use: 65%
Monthly deduction: $250 × 65% = $162.50
Annual deduction: $162.50 × 12 = $1,950

Example B (interest over cap):
Actual monthly interest: $450
Capped amount: $300 (cap applies)
Business use: 65%
Monthly deduction: $300 × 65% = $195
Annual deduction: $195 × 12 = $2,340
(Note: you paid $5,400 in interest but can only deduct $2,340)
```

**Why the cap exists:** CRA designed it to prevent deducting interest on luxury vehicles that cost
far more than the $37,000 CCA threshold. If you finance a $120,000 car, the interest is enormous —
the $300/month cap limits your deduction to match what's reasonable on a $37,000 car.

**ITA Authority:** s.67.2; Regulation 7307(2) — the $10/day limit

**For vehicles used in a short period (not full year):** The cap is $10/day × number of days the
vehicle was financed and available for use. E.g., bought July 1 = 184 days × $10 = $1,840 maximum
interest base.

`[RISK]` If you have a high-interest loan on an expensive car, the actual interest you pay may
significantly exceed the deductible amount. Factor this into your lease vs. buy analysis.

### 3.8 Lease Payments — The $950/Month Cap

**Plain English:** If you lease your car instead of buying it, the monthly lease payment is
deductible (business % of the capped amount). The cap for 2024 is $950/month (before HST).

If your lease payment is under $950/month (before tax), you deduct all of it times your business %.
If your lease payment is over $950/month, you are stuck at $950/month regardless.

**The formula:**
```
Monthly lease payment: $1,200 (before HST) — leasing an $80K BMW
Cap applies: $950/month (2024 limit)
Business use: 65%
Monthly deduction: $950 × 65% = $617.50
Annual deduction: $617.50 × 12 = $7,410

Without the cap it would be: $1,200 × 65% × 12 = $9,360
You lose: $1,950/year in potential deductions due to the luxury cap
```

**Additional cap for expensive vehicles:** There is a secondary formula under s.67.3 that reduces
the deductible lease amount when the manufacturer's list price of the vehicle exceeds the CCA cost
limit ($37,000 for 2024). The formula ensures you cannot deduct more through leasing than you could
through CCA if you had bought the car.

**The s.67.3 formula:**
```
Deductible lease amount = lesser of:
(a) ($950 × 30 days in month × lease payment) ÷ (30 × 0.85 × greater of:
    $37,000 and manufacturer's list price)  [simplified version]
(b) Actual lease payment

In practice: just use the $950/month cap for planning purposes.
For very expensive leases, have your accountant run the exact s.67.3 formula.
```

**ITA Authority:** s.67.3; Regulation 7307(1) — the $950/month limit

**Lease payment includes:** The base payment portion. Does not include HST (HST is claimed
separately as an ITC if you are HST-registered — see Section 13).

**Year of acquisition and year of termination:** Prorate the cap by the number of days the vehicle
was leased in that year.

### 3.9 CAA and Roadside Assistance

**What it is:** CAA membership, AMA, roadside assistance plan built into your insurance or added
separately.

**Deductible:** Business use % of the annual membership cost.

**Dollar impact:** CAA Plus runs ~$130/year. At 65% business: $84.50 deduction. Small but real.

### 3.10 Car Wash and Detailing

**What it is:** Vehicle cleaning required to maintain a professional appearance for client-facing
work, or cleaning after business use (delivery, construction, landscaping).

**Deductible:** Business use % of wash/detailing costs. If your car needs cleaning specifically
because of a business job (mud from a construction site visit), argue 100%.

**Dollar impact:** $300/year in car washes at 65% = $195 deduction.

### 3.11 Accessories and Add-Ons Used for Business

**What it is:** Phone mount ($25), cargo organizers, roof rack for equipment, tow hitch installed
for business purposes, GPS unit, dashcam.

**Deductible:** If the accessory is primarily for business use, deduct the business %.
A dashcam on a rideshare or delivery vehicle = 100% deductible.
A phone mount used in both personal and business driving = business %.

**CCA vs expense:** Accessories costing more than $500 and attached to the vehicle may need to be
added to the vehicle's CCA class rather than expensed directly.

## 4. CCA ON VEHICLES — THE BIG ONE

CCA is Capital Cost Allowance — the tax word for depreciation. It lets you write off a portion of
the car's purchase price each year. Think of it as CRA acknowledging that the car wears out over
time while you are using it to earn money.

This is usually the **largest single deduction** on a vehicle — often bigger than gas, insurance,
and interest combined.

**Cross-reference:** ATLAS_CCA_DEPRECIATION_GUIDE.md — Section 13 (Automobile CCA Optimization)
has the complete technical CCA framework. This section covers the automobile-specific rules in
plain language plus dollar examples.

### 4.1 The Most Important Rule: Class 10 vs Class 10.1

CRA divides vehicles into two categories based on cost. This determines which CCA class applies
and completely changes the rules.

**The dividing line:** $37,000 (before HST/PST) for 2024.

**Cheaper car (under $37,000 + tax) → Class 10:**
- 30% declining balance rate
- No cap on cost — full purchase price enters the class
- Can claim terminal loss if you sell for less than the UCC (you get a full deduction for the
  "stranded" value)
- All Class 10 vehicles pool together (one total pool for all your under-$37K cars)

**Expensive car (over $37,000 + tax) → Class 10.1:**
- 30% declining balance rate (same %)
- CCA base CAPPED at $37,000 (regardless of what you paid)
- Each car gets its OWN separate class (no pooling)
- CANNOT claim terminal loss — if you sell for less than UCC, that value disappears
- CANNOT trigger recapture — CRA will not add money back to your income when you sell
- In the year of disposal, you STILL get a half-year CCA (unlike normal rules)

**The practical implication in plain English:**

You buy a $55,000 car. CRA says: "That's over $37,000 so we're only going to let you depreciate
as if you paid $37,000. The other $18,000 you spent? Not our problem."

```
Year 1 CCA calculation for $55,000 vehicle in Class 10.1 (2024, no AII):
CCA base:          $37,000 (capped — not $55,000)
Half-year rule:    × 50% (only in year 1 under normal rules)
CCA rate:          × 30%
Pre-use adjustment: × business use % (e.g., 65%)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Year 1 deduction:  $37,000 × 50% × 30% × 65% = $3,608

Compare to buying a $35,000 car (Class 10):
Year 1 deduction:  $35,000 × 50% × 30% × 65% = $3,413
```

The $20,000 difference in purchase price yields only $195 more deduction in year 1. The $37K cap
essentially erases most of the tax benefit of the more expensive car.

### 4.2 The Declining Balance Method — How It Works Over Time

Each year, you take CCA on the remaining balance (called the UCC — Undepreciated Capital Cost).

```
$30,000 car, Class 10 (30%), 65% business use:

Year | Opening UCC | × 30% CCA | × 65% Business | Deduction | Closing UCC
-----|-------------|-----------|----------------|-----------|------------
1    | $30,000*    | $4,500    | $2,925         | $2,925    | $25,500
2    | $25,500     | $7,650    | $4,973         | $4,973    | $17,850
3    | $17,850     | $5,355    | $3,481         | $3,481    | $12,495
4    | $12,495     | $3,749    | $2,437         | $2,437    | $8,746
5    | $8,746      | $2,624    | $1,706         | $1,706    | $6,122

*Year 1: half-year rule applies — opening UCC treated as $15,000 (half of $30,000 added to class)
 so actual calculation: ($30,000 × 50%) × 30% × 65% = $2,925
```

Notice how the deductions shrink each year. That is the declining balance — you always take % of
whatever is LEFT. The car never fully depreciates on paper (mathematically approaches zero but never
gets there). When you sell or trade in, the remaining UCC is resolved via recapture or terminal loss.

**CCA is OPTIONAL:** You do not have to claim CCA in any given year. If your income is low and you
want to save the deduction for a higher-income year, skip CCA (or claim less than the maximum).
The UCC carries forward unchanged. CRA does not penalize you for under-claiming CCA.

**CCA is limited to income:** You cannot use CCA to create a business loss from vehicle expenses
alone (s.13(7.1) and the general CCA = income limitation). Plan your CCA claims around your actual
business income level.

### 4.3 The Prescribed CCA Cost Limit — Annual Increases

The $37,000 threshold is not permanently fixed. It has increased slightly each year:

| Year | CCA Prescribed Limit | With Ontario HST (13%) |
|------|---------------------|----------------------|
| 2019 | $30,000 | $33,900 |
| 2020 | $30,000 | $33,900 |
| 2021 | $30,000 | $33,900 |
| 2022 | $34,000 | $38,420 |
| 2023 | $36,000 | $40,680 |
| 2024 | $37,000 | $41,810 |
| 2025 | $37,000 (confirmed) | $41,810 |

**Why this matters:** A car bought for $34,500 in 2022 is Class 10.1 (over the $34,000 limit).
The same car in 2023 would be Class 10 (under the $36,000 limit). The year of purchase determines
which class applies permanently for that vehicle.

**Authority:** Regulation 7307(1) (sets the prescribed limit, updated annually by Order in Council)

### 4.4 What Goes Into the CCA Cost Base

The capital cost for CCA purposes is NOT always the sticker price. It includes:

**Add to capital cost:**
- Purchase price of the vehicle
- Delivery charges, freight
- Dealer-installed accessories and add-ons (roof rack, window tint, running boards — if built into
  the purchase price)
- HST/PST paid on purchase (yes, the tax you paid on the car goes into the CCA base)
- Extended warranty or service plan if capitalized (not expensed)
- Import duties (on foreign-purchased vehicles)

**Reduce capital cost:**
- Government rebates received (e.g., federal iZEV EV rebate of up to $5,000 — this reduces your
  CCA base dollar-for-dollar)
- Trade-in allowance (the amount credited for your old car reduces what you "paid" for the new one)
- GST/HST rebates (if you are an HST registrant, the HST on the car is reclaimed as an ITC and
  therefore does not go into the CCA base — see Section 13)

```
Example: Buying a $35,000 car in Ontario
Purchase price:        $35,000
+ Ontario HST (13%):   + $4,550
= Total cost:          $39,550
This exceeds the $37,000 CCA limit → Class 10.1

UNLESS you receive the federal iZEV rebate ($5,000):
$39,550 - $5,000 = $34,550
CCA base capped at $37,000 → but effective base is $34,550 → Class 10
(because the net acquisition cost is under the threshold — confirm with accountant)
```

**Authority:** ITA s.13(21) definition of "capital cost"; IT-521R paragraphs 12-14

## 5. FIRST-YEAR ACCELERATION AND IMMEDIATE EXPENSING

### 5.1 The Half-Year Rule (Default)

**Plain English:** In the year you buy a car, CRA assumes you only owned it for half the year (no
matter when you actually bought it). So your first-year CCA is cut in half.

This rule exists so that someone who buys a car on December 30 does not get a full year of CCA for
one day of ownership.

```
$30,000 car, Class 10, Year 1:
Without half-year rule: $30,000 × 30% = $9,000 CCA
With half-year rule:    $30,000 × 30% × 50% = $4,500 CCA
```

The half-year rule has been partially replaced (see 5.2 and 5.3 below), but it remains the default
for any property NOT eligible for the newer incentives.

**Authority:** Regulation 1100(2) and (2.2)

### 5.2 Accelerated Investment Incentive (AII) — 1.5× First-Year CCA

**Plain English:** The federal government introduced AII in 2018 to encourage business investment.
Instead of the 50% half-year restriction, AII gives you **1.5 times** the normal first-year CCA.
That is THREE times what the half-year rule gives.

**The formula with AII:**
```
Year 1 CCA = Full capital cost × 1.5 × CCA rate × business use %

$30,000 car, Class 10, 65% business use:
Without AII (half-year rule): $30,000 × 0.5 × 30% × 65%  = $2,925
With AII:                     $30,000 × 1.5 × 30% × 65%  = $8,775
```

**AII is 3 times bigger than the half-year rule.** For a 65% business-use, $30,000 car, that is an
$8,775 deduction in year 1 vs. $2,925. At a 35% marginal rate, that is $2,044 more tax saved in
year 1 alone.

**AII phase-out schedule:**
| Period | First-Year Multiplier |
|--------|----------------------|
| November 21, 2018 – December 31, 2023 | 1.5× |
| 2024 | 1.25× (partial phase-out year) |
| 2025 | 1.0× (back to normal half-year rule) |
| 2026+ | 0.5× (standard half-year rule restored fully) |

`[RISK]` AII is nearly phased out as of 2025. If you are buying a vehicle in 2025, you get only
1.0× (same as normal rules). Plan vehicle purchases accordingly — the 2023 window for maximum AII
has closed.

**Authority:** Regulations 1100(2)(a)(ii), 1100(2)(a.2); Budget 2018 implementation

### 5.3 CCPC Immediate Expensing — 100% in Year 1

`[FUTURE]` This section applies when CC incorporates as a CCPC.

**Plain English:** If your business is incorporated as a Canadian-Controlled Private Corporation
(CCPC), you can write off **100% of eligible depreciable property in year 1**, up to $1.5 million
combined across all assets. No half-year rule. No declining balance. Full cost in one shot.

This was introduced in Budget 2021 and is permanent (unlike AII which is phasing out).

**For vehicles specifically:**
- Eligible under immediate expensing: YES (both Class 10 and Class 10.1 vehicles)
- Still subject to the Class 10.1 cap of $37,000 — immediate expensing does not override the CCA
  cost limit. You can expense 100% of $37,000, not 100% of $55,000.

```
Example: CCPC buys $35,000 car, 70% business use

Year 1 — Normal (half-year rule):
  $35,000 × 30% × 50% × 70% = $3,675 deduction

Year 1 — AII (2024 at 1.25×):
  $35,000 × 30% × 1.25 × 70% = $9,188 deduction

Year 1 — CCPC Immediate Expensing:
  $35,000 × 100% × 70% = $24,500 deduction
```

The CCPC immediate expensing gives you $24,500 vs. $3,675 under normal rules. At a 12.2% corporate
tax rate (Ontario CCPC small business rate), that is $2,989 more tax saved in year 1 compared to
$1,286 at the personal rate. But crucially, at a 29.65%+ personal rate when not incorporated, the
deduction saves even more — $7,266 vs. $1,089.

## 7. THE LOGBOOK — CRA'S FAVOURITE AUDIT TARGET

### 7.1 Why the Logbook is Non-Negotiable

**Plain English:** CRA cannot audit your gas receipts to figure out how much was for business.
They audit the LOGBOOK to challenge your claimed business use percentage. If you claim 65% business
use but cannot prove it, CRA can disallow 100% of your vehicle claim. Not a reduction — a complete
disallowance. Every dollar of every vehicle expense. Gone.

The logbook is the single most important document in your vehicle deduction claim. Not the receipts.
Not the CCA calculation. The logbook.

**Authority:** ITA s.230 (record-keeping requirement); IT-521R paragraphs 8-11; CRA Publication
T4002 page 25

### 7.2 What a Full Logbook Must Contain

For each business trip:
1. **Date** of the trip
2. **Destination** — where you drove to (city, address, or description: "Downtown Barrie — TD Bank")
3. **Business purpose** — why you went ("client meeting," "pick up office supplies," "OASIS
   project kickoff with Client X")
4. **Kilometres driven** — for that specific trip

At the start of the year (January 1):
- **Opening odometer reading**

At the end of the year (December 31):
- **Closing odometer reading**

```
The math CRA checks:
Closing odometer - Opening odometer = Total km for the year
Sum of all logged business trips = Business km
Business km ÷ Total km = Business use %

If these numbers do not add up consistently, CRA challenges the claim.
```

**You do NOT need to log personal trips** — only business trips. CRA infers that everything not
logged is personal. But you should record the odometer readings on both ends so the total km
denominator is verifiable.

### 7.3 The Representative Period (3-Month Logbook) — Year 2 Onward

**Plain English:** Keeping a full logbook every year is a lot of work. CRA allows a shortcut after
the first year: keep a full logbook in Year 1, then in subsequent years you only need a 3-month
sample period IF the sample period shows your business use is within 10% of the base year.

**The rule:**
- Year 1: Full 12-month logbook required (no exceptions)
- Year 2+: Keep a 3-month sample. If sample business % is within ±10% of Year 1, use Year 1 %.
- If the sample diverges by more than 10%, you need a new full year logbook

```
Year 1 full logbook: 63% business use
Year 3 sample (3 months): 67% business use
Difference: 4 percentage points (within 10%)
Allowed to use: 63% for Year 3 (the base year rate)

BUT if Year 3 sample shows 75% business use (12 point difference):
Must keep a new full-year logbook in Year 3 to establish new base.
```

**Authority:** IT-521R paragraph 9; CRA Automobile Expenses and Logbooks publication (RC4070)

### 7.4 Digital Logbook Apps (CRA-Accepted)

The following apps automatically log trips via GPS and allow you to tag trips as business or
personal:

| App | Cost | Key Feature |
|-----|------|-------------|
| **MileIQ** | ~$60 USD/year | Auto-detection, swipe classification, CRA-export |
| **Driversnote** | Free/paid tiers | Auto-detect, team features, PDF reports |
| **Everlance** | Free/paid tiers | GPS tracking, receipt scanning, tax export |
| **Hurdlr** | $100/year | Full self-employed expense tracker + mileage |
| **Google Maps Timeline** | Free | Recorded automatically — CRA has accepted as corroborating evidence |

`[NOW]` Start logging TODAY. Enable Google Maps Timeline on your phone immediately — it records
every trip automatically and has been accepted by CRA as corroborating evidence when a formal
logbook is incomplete. Do this regardless of whether you have another app.

`[RISK]` If you are audited and have no logbook, CRA will zero out 100% of your vehicle claim.
The risk is not "CRA estimates your business use at 30% instead of 65%." The risk is zero.

### 7.5 What CRA Considers Suspicious (Audit Red Flags for Logbooks)

**Round numbers:** Claiming exactly 10,000 business km, 15,000 business km. Real logbooks rarely
produce perfectly round numbers. CRA's computers flag them.

**Very high business use % (>80%):** CRA gets suspicious when someone claims their personal
vehicle is used for business 80–100% of the time. It is possible (e.g., a courier), but it requires
exceptional documentation. Anything over 75% increases your audit risk.

**No personal trips at all:** Everyone drives their car personally sometimes. Claiming 100% business
use on a personal vehicle is an automatic flag.

**Business km that exceed total km:** If odometer shows 20,000 total and you claim 22,000 business
km, CRA sees the math does not work and denies everything.

**Inconsistent years:** If you claimed 40% in 2022, 42% in 2023, and then suddenly 78% in 2024
without a change in business activity, CRA will want to know why.

### 7.6 What "Business Travel" Includes and Excludes — The Commuting Rule

**Plain English:** The single most common mistake is treating your daily commute as business travel.
It is not. CRA is very clear.

**Home-to-regular-workplace = personal travel.** Even if you work from home 3 days and drive to
clients 2 days, the drives to clients are business, but if you have a fixed office you regularly
work from, driving there is commuting.

**The exception — Home Office:** If your home is your principal place of business (which it is for
most sole proprietors working from home), then trips FROM HOME to client locations are business
travel. There is no "commute" because home IS the office.

`[CC]` CC works from home in Collingwood. Every drive to a client, bank, government office, or
business supplier is business travel. There is no "commuting" because there is no fixed external
office. This is a significant advantage — nearly every business drive is deductible.

**Authority:** IT-521R paragraph 7; ITA s.18(1)(h) (personal/living expenses)

## 8. LEASE VS BUY VS FINANCE — TAX DECISION TREE

### 8.1 The Core Trade-Off

Buying and leasing create different tax deductions:
- **Buy with cash/financing:** Claim CCA (depreciation) year after year, plus interest deduction
- **Lease:** Claim lease payments directly (simpler, no CCA tracking)

Neither is always better. The right answer depends on your income level, corporate status, car
price, and tax bracket.

### 8.2 Leasing — The Tax Case For It

**Advantages:**
1. **Simplicity:** No CCA tracking, no UCC to manage, no recapture risk on disposal
2. **Luxury car workaround:** If you want a $70,000 car, buying it puts you deep in Class 10.1
   with a $37,000 cap. Leasing lets you deduct the full lease payment (up to $950/month), which
   on a 4-year lease effectively allows deducting more than $37,000 × 30% over the lease term
3. **Walk away:** No residual value risk, no disposal tax issues
4. **Cash flow:** Lower monthly outflow keeps cash in the business

**Calculation example — Leasing a $52,000 car, 70% business use:**
```
Monthly lease payment: $850 (before HST — a typical 4-year lease on a $52K car)
Annual lease deduction: $850 × 12 × 70% = $7,140/year
4-year total deduction: $28,560
```

**Disadvantages:**
1. No terminal loss (you do not own the asset)
2. Cannot use CCPC immediate expensing (lease payments are operating expenses, not capital)
3. Kilometre restrictions (common in leases) may not suit high-business-use situations
4. Mileage overage fees are deductible but add cost unpredictability

### 8.3 Buying / Financing — The Tax Case For It

**Advantages:**
1. **CCPC immediate expensing** is only available when you OWN (buy) the asset
2. **Terminal loss** — if you sell below UCC and it is the last asset in the class, you get a
   full deduction (only available on Class 10, not Class 10.1)
3. **Ownership** — the car is an asset on your balance sheet
4. **High-km use** — no mileage restrictions

**Disadvantages:**
1. Higher monthly cash outflow
2. Class 10.1 cap on expensive vehicles limits CCA base to $37,000
3. Recapture risk if car value holds above UCC (you pay it back as income)
4. More complex records (CCA tracking, UCC pooling, disposition math)

### 8.4 The Decision Framework — Step by Step

```
Step 1: Are you a CCPC?
  YES → Buying almost always wins (immediate expensing is massive)
  NO (sole proprietor) → Continue

Step 2: Is the car over $37,000 (pre-tax)?
  YES → Leasing is more competitive (avoids Class 10.1 cap)
  NO  → Buying likely better (full cost in CCA base)

Step 3: Is this a zero-emission vehicle?
  YES → Buy (Class 54/55, $61K limit or no limit, immediate expensing)
  NO  → Continue

Step 4: What is your business use %?
  > 80% → Buy (maximizes CCA deduction, terminal loss available)
  50-80% → Run both calculations
  < 50% → Lease (simpler, lower administrative burden)

Step 5: Do you need the car for < 3 years?
  YES → Lease (avoids recapture risk when selling before CCA is absorbed)
  NO  → Buying likely better long-term
```

### 8.5 Full 5-Year Net Present Value Comparison

The following compares three scenarios on a $35,000 gas car with 70% business use at a 35% marginal
tax rate, 6% discount rate:

```
SCENARIO A: Lease at $600/month (before HST, 36-month term)
Annual lease deduction: $600 × 12 × 70% = $5,040
Annual tax savings:     $5,040 × 35%   = $1,764/year
3-year total tax savings: $5,292
NPV of tax savings (6%): ~$4,710

SCENARIO B: Finance at 6.99% (5-year loan)
Year 1 CCA (AII 2024 at 1.25×): $35,000 × 30% × 1.25 × 70% = $9,188
Tax saving Year 1: $9,188 × 35% = $3,216
Interest deduction year 1 (capped at $300/mo): ~$2,400 × 70% × 35% = $588
Year 1 total savings: $3,804
Years 2-5 combined CCA + interest savings: ~$6,800 (declining)
Total 5-year NPV: ~$8,900

SCENARIO C: Buy cash (CCPC, immediate expensing, 12.2% corp rate)
Year 1 deduction: $35,000 × 100% × 70% = $24,500
Year 1 tax savings: $24,500 × 12.2% = $2,989
(Plus years 2-5 on remaining UCC — minimal)
5-year total: ~$3,400

Summary:
Sole prop, financed, AII:   ~$8,900 NPV → WINNER (sole prop)
Sole prop, leased:           ~$4,710 NPV
CCPC, immediate expense:    ~$3,400 NPV → Lower rate but simpler

Key insight: AII financing beats leasing for under-$37K cars because you get
more deduction faster (front-loaded), even at the same marginal rate.
CCPC looks worse in year 1 NPV due to lower tax rate but preserves cash better.
```

## 9. CORPORATION-OWNED VEHICLES AND STANDBY CHARGES

### 9.1 The Basic Setup

When a corporation buys a vehicle and makes it available to a shareholder-employee (e.g., CC as
the sole owner of their CCPC), two things happen simultaneously:

1. **The corporation deducts** all vehicle expenses (CCA, insurance, gas, etc.) — the costs reduce
   corporate income at the 12.2% small business rate
2. **The shareholder-employee includes** a "standby charge" and "operating cost benefit" as
   employment income on their personal T4 — taxed at their marginal personal rate

This creates a tax cost to the shareholder that partially or fully offsets the corporate deduction.

**The critical question:** Does the net tax effect favour a corporation-owned vehicle vs.
personal ownership?

**Authority:** ITA s.6(1)(e) (standby charge), s.6(1)(k) (operating cost benefit), s.6(2) (standby
charge formula), s.6(2.1) (reduced standby charge)

### 9.2 The Standby Charge — How to Calculate It

**Plain English:** CRA says: "If your employer (even if it's your own corporation) gives you a car,
that is a taxable benefit to you. We're going to add phantom income to your T4 to represent the
value of having that car available."

**The standard formula:**
```
Standby charge = 2% × original cost of vehicle × number of months available

Example: Corporation buys $35,000 car, available to shareholder all 12 months:
Standby charge = 2% × $35,000 × 12 = $8,400

This $8,400 is ADDED to the shareholder's personal income on their T4.
At 35% marginal rate: $8,400 × 35% = $2,940 personal tax owing
```

**For leased vehicles (corporation leases the car):**
```
Standby charge = 2/3 × monthly lease payment × months available
= 2/3 × $800/month × 12 = $6,400 standby charge
```

**Authority:** ITA s.6(2) — standby charge calculation formula

### 9.3 The Reduced Standby Charge — The Important Exception

`[$$$]` If personal use is limited, you can reduce the standby charge significantly.

**The reduction formula applies when both conditions are met:**
1. The vehicle is used primarily for business (>50% business use); AND
2. Personal use is less than 1,667 km per month (20,004 km per year)

**Reduced standby charge formula:**
```
Reduced standby charge = Standard charge × (Personal km ÷ 1,667 km/month × months)

Example: $35,000 car, 12 months, personal km = 800 km/month:
Standard charge = 2% × $35,000 × 12 = $8,400
Reduction ratio = 800 ÷ 1,667 = 0.48
Reduced charge = $8,400 × 0.48 = $4,032 (vs. $8,400 standard)
Personal tax saved: ($8,400 - $4,032) × 35% = $1,529
```

**Authority:** ITA s.6(2.1) — the reduced standby charge formula

### 9.4 The Operating Cost Benefit

In addition to the standby charge, the shareholder must also declare an "operating cost benefit"
if the corporation pays operating expenses (gas, insurance, oil changes) for a vehicle that has
any personal use.

**Option A — Standard operating cost benefit:**
```
Operating cost benefit = $0.33/km × personal km driven in the year
(2024 rate — updated annually)

Example: 10,000 personal km × $0.33 = $3,300 operating cost benefit
Added to T4 income.
```

**Option B — 50% of standby charge election:**
If the reduced standby charge method is used AND your vehicle is used primarily for business,
you can elect to use 50% of the standby charge as the operating cost benefit instead of the
per-km calculation.
```
50% election: 50% × $4,032 = $2,016 (vs. $3,300 per-km method above)
Election saves $1,284 in operating cost benefit income.
```

**The election must be made in writing to the employer corporation before the end of the year.**
For a sole shareholder corporation, this means a written note in your corporate minute book.

**Authority:** ITA s.6(1)(k)

### 9.5 Net Tax Analysis — Corporation vs Personal Ownership

**When corporate ownership WINS:**
- Very high business use (85%+)
- Luxury car (corporation deducts full cost including above-$37K portion via operating expenses
  even if CCA is capped; and the standby charge is calculated on actual cost not CCA-capped cost)
- High corporate income (maximizing deductions at corporate level more valuable)

**When personal ownership WINS:**
- Mixed business/personal use (50-75%)
- Moderate-cost car (under $37,000 before tax)
- Sole proprietor stage (no corporation yet — take the deduction personally)

**The math for the typical case:**
```
$35,000 car, 70% business, 30% personal:
CORPORATE OWNERSHIP:
  Corp deduction: $35,000 × 70% expenses + CCA = ~$8,000/year tax saving at 12.2%
  Shareholder taxable benefit: $8,400 standby charge + $2,100 operating cost benefit
  = $10,500 personal income × 35% = $3,675 personal tax
  Net benefit: $976 corp tax saved - $3,675 personal tax = -$2,699 NET COST

PERSONAL OWNERSHIP (sole prop):
  T2125 deduction: vehicle expenses × 70%
  Annual deduction: ~$12,000 × 70% = $8,400 × 35% = $2,940 tax saved
  Net benefit: $2,940 tax saved

Verdict: Personal ownership saves $2,940 vs corporate ownership costing $2,699.
Swing: $5,639 better with personal ownership in this scenario.
```

`[RISK]` Many small business owners assume incorporating and running a car through the corporation
saves tax. In the 50–75% business use range, it often does the opposite. Corporate vehicle
ownership makes sense at 85%+ business use or for very expensive vehicles.

## 11. TRADE-INS, DISPOSITIONS, AND CCA CONSEQUENCES

### 11.1 What Happens When You Get Rid of a Car

**Plain English:** The tax system treats selling, trading in, or scrapping your car as a
"disposition" — a taxable event for CCA purposes. CRA looks at what you received for the car
(proceeds) vs. what is left in the CCA pool (UCC). The difference creates either:
- **Recapture** — you get money back to income (bad — taxed at full marginal rate)
- **Terminal loss** — you get a deduction (good — fully deductible)
- **Capital gain** — if you sell for more than you paid (taxed at 50% inclusion)

**The Class 10 pool (multiple vehicles):**
When you have multiple vehicles under $37,000, they all share one Class 10 pool. Selling one car
reduces the pool by the proceeds. The pool carries on. Recapture or terminal loss only crystallizes
when the CLASS is empty (no more vehicles) and the UCC is positive (terminal loss) or negative
(recapture).

**The Class 10.1 (each car is its own class):**
Every Class 10.1 vehicle has its own pool. Disposing of it ALWAYS forces a reckoning.
- Terminal loss: NOT ALLOWED under s.13(2). The remaining UCC disappears.
- Recapture: NOT REQUIRED under s.13(2). A negative UCC in Class 10.1 is forgiven.
- In the year of disposal: You ARE allowed a half-year's CCA (a special rule under Reg. 1101(1af))

### 11.2 Trade-In Mechanics

**Plain English:** Trading in your old car is exactly like selling it — the trade-in value is
treated as "proceeds of disposition" for CCA purposes. The fact that the money immediately goes
towards a new car does not change the tax treatment of the old car.

```
Example: Class 10, opening UCC $12,000 (only car in class)
Trade-in value on old car: $15,000

Proceeds of disposition = $15,000
Lesser of: cost or proceeds = $15,000 (assuming original cost was $30,000)
UCC reduction: $12,000 - $15,000 = -$3,000

Negative UCC = RECAPTURE = $3,000 added to income (taxed at full marginal rate)
```

**How to avoid recapture:** Keep the class "open" by buying the new car into the same class in the
same tax year. The new car's addition to Class 10 brings the UCC back above zero.

```
Same example, with new car purchase:
Opening UCC:          $12,000
+ New car added:      $32,000 (purchased same year)
- Trade-in proceeds:  $15,000
= Closing UCC:        $29,000 (positive — no recapture)
```

**Authority:** ITA s.13(1) (recapture), s.20(16) (terminal loss), s.13(21) (definitions)

### 11.3 Class 10 Terminal Loss

**Plain English:** If you sell your LAST vehicle in Class 10 and the UCC is still positive, you
get to deduct the remaining amount all at once. This is called a terminal loss and it is one of
the best deductions available.

```
Opening UCC of Class 10 (your only car): $8,000
Sell car for: $5,000

Proceeds: $5,000
UCC after proceeds: $8,000 - $5,000 = $3,000 (positive, class now empty)
TERMINAL LOSS: $3,000 — fully deductible against income
At 35% marginal rate: $1,050 tax saved
```

**Timing strategy:** If you know you are closing Class 10 (selling your last car), do it in a
high-income year. Terminal loss at 46% saves more than terminal loss at 26%.

**Class 10.1 CANNOT claim terminal loss.** This is one of the key trade-offs — a luxury car owner
gives up the terminal loss protection.

### 11.4 Capital Gain on Vehicle Sale

If you sell a vehicle for MORE than its original cost, the excess is a capital gain.
```
Original cost: $30,000
Sell for: $35,000
Gain: $5,000

$5,000 × 50% inclusion = $2,500 taxable capital gain
Note: The first $30,000 of proceeds reduces CCA UCC (potential recapture).
The $5,000 above original cost = capital gain (different tax treatment).
```

Vehicles rarely sell above cost (they depreciate). But collector vehicles, certain trucks, and
hot-market used cars can exceed cost in strong markets.

### 12.1 Cross-Reference

For Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, Skip the Dishes, and similar platform income, see

### 12.2 Mandatory HST Registration for Rideshare

**Plain English:** The moment you drive even one ride for Uber or Lyft, you MUST register for
HST. There is no $30,000 threshold for rideshare. You must register from the first dollar.

This is because rideshare is deemed a "taxi business" under s.240(1.1) of the Excise Tax Act —
HST applies to taxi services from the first dollar, regardless of income level.

**Authority:** ETA s.240(1.1); CRA Technical Note TN-78

### 12.3 Vehicle Expenses for Rideshare

If you are a rideshare or delivery driver:
- All the same vehicle expense rules apply (gas, insurance, CCA, repairs, etc.)
- Business use % is calculated based on km driven for rideshare vs. total km
- The app's own mileage tracking (Uber app, DoorDash app) can supplement your logbook
- Additional deductible costs: phone mount, commercial insurance endorsement, car wash
  (customers expect a clean vehicle), seat covers, chargers for passenger phones

### 12.4 The Mixed-Use Problem

Many drivers use their car for rideshare AND personal driving AND other business. Track each
separately:

```
Total km for the year:      40,000
Rideshare km (Uber trips):  18,000 (45%)
Other business km (OASIS):   4,000 (10%)
Personal km:                18,000 (45%)

Total business km: 22,000 (55% business use)
Apply 55% to all vehicle expenses.

For HST purposes: track HST paid on vehicle expenses separately to claim ITCs
proportional to business use (55%).
```

## 13. HST INPUT TAX CREDITS ON VEHICLE EXPENSES

### 13.1 How HST Works on Vehicle Expenses

`[HST]` If you are HST-registered (mandatory if over $30,000 revenue, or mandatory from day 1
for rideshare), you can claim Input Tax Credits (ITCs) on the HST you pay on vehicle expenses.

**Plain English:** When you fill up gas and pay $100 + $13 HST, the $13 is technically recoverable
from CRA if you are HST-registered — but only for the business portion. This reduces your vehicle
operating costs and is independent of your income tax deduction.

### 13.2 ITC Calculation on Vehicle Expenses

```
Annual vehicle expenses (ex-HST):   $10,000
HST paid on those expenses (13%):    $1,300
Business use %:                         65%
ITC claim:                   $1,300 × 65% = $845

This $845 is CREDITED on your HST return (reduces HST remittance or creates refund).
This $845 is NOT deducted on T2125 (you already claimed it back via HST).
```

**Important:** If you are HST-registered, the HST you paid on expenses is NOT deductible as an
expense — it is recovered via the ITC. Only the HST-exclusive amount goes into your T2125 deduction
calculation.

### 13.3 ITC on Vehicle Purchase

**For the car itself:**
If you are HST-registered and buy a car for business use, you can claim the HST as an ITC
proportional to business use.

```
Car purchase price: $35,000
HST: $4,550
Business use: 70%
ITC claim: $4,550 × 70% = $3,185

Effectively, the car only "costs" you $35,000 + $1,365 HST (personal portion) = $36,365
Instead of $39,550 total.
The CCA base is reduced by the ITC claimed.
```

**Class 10.1 interaction:** The ITC reduces your capital cost for CCA purposes, which could bring
a car under the $37,000 CCA threshold or reduce your CCA base.

**Authority:** ETA s.169 (ITC general rule), s.170(1)(b) (automobile passenger vehicle ITC limit),
s.201 (passenger vehicle ITC restriction — 50% for certain mixed-use vehicles)

### 13.4 The 50% ITC Restriction on Passenger Vehicles

For passenger vehicles (cars, SUVs, station wagons — the same vehicles covered by Class 10.1),
there is a 50% ITC restriction:

```
HST on car purchase: $4,550
50% restriction: $4,550 × 50% = $2,275 maximum ITC (regardless of business use %)

This restriction does NOT apply to:
- Vans and pickups (not "passenger vehicles" under the ETA definition)
- Vehicles used exclusively (90%+) in your commercial activity
- Trucks, buses, and other commercial vehicles
```

**Planning note:** A cargo van used for business (even partially) avoids the 50% ITC restriction
and allows full business % ITCs on the purchase HST. Another reason commercial vehicles get better
tax treatment than luxury cars.

**Authority:** ETA s.201

## 14. MULTI-VEHICLE STRATEGY

### 14.1 Dedicated Business Vehicle

The cleanest approach: have one vehicle that is used exclusively (or near-exclusively — 95%+) for
business, and a separate personal vehicle for family/personal driving. This avoids the mixed-use
calculation entirely.

```
Vehicle 1 (business only): Toyota Cargo Van — 95% business use
Deduct: 95% of all expenses. Near-total deduction.

Vehicle 2 (personal): Family SUV — 5% business use
Claim 5% of expenses. Minimal deduction (not worth the paperwork unless significant).
```

**CRA position:** A 95-100% business use claim is defensible on a commercial vehicle (cargo van,
truck) where the vehicle's nature clearly supports pure business use. CRA is more skeptical of 95%
on a luxury sedan.

### 14.2 Two Vehicles, Both Partially Business

If you use two vehicles for business:
- Keep separate logbooks for each vehicle
- Calculate business use % for each separately
- Claim each on T2125 separately
- Pool Class 10 vehicles together in the UCC pool; each Class 10.1 vehicle stays separate

### 14.3 Changing Between Vehicles

When you switch from one car to another during the year:
- Dispose of the old car (proceeds = trade-in or sale value)
- Add the new car to the appropriate class
- Logbook tracks km for EACH vehicle for the period it was used
- Business use % can differ between the two vehicles

### 14.4 Family Members Using the Business Vehicle

If a spouse or family member uses the business vehicle for personal trips, those km are personal
km — they reduce the business use % even if that person is also an employee or partner.

Separate vehicles for family members may be preferable from a tax standpoint.

## 15. CRA AUDIT DEFENSE FOR VEHICLE CLAIMS

### 15.1 Why Vehicle Claims Are a High-Audit Area

Vehicle claims are among the most commonly audited items on T2125. CRA knows that:
1. It is easy to overstate business use without contemporaneous records
2. Self-employed people frequently use their personal car for both purposes
3. Logbook-less claims are easy to disallow with zero additional research

CRA auditors are trained specifically to scrutinize vehicle claims. The bar is higher than for
most other deductions.

### 15.2 What CRA Can Ask For (Audit Requests)

In a vehicle audit, CRA will request:
- Your logbook (the primary document — if missing, expect full disallowance)
- Odometer readings (CRA may contact dealerships or mechanics who serviced the car)
- All receipts for claimed expenses
- Bank or credit card statements showing fuel and maintenance purchases
- Evidence of business activities matching logbook trips (client invoices, contracts, calendar)
- Insurance policy (to verify vehicle type, address, and confirm usage declarations match claims)
- T2125 for prior years (looking for consistency)

### 15.3 Defense Strategy — The Documentation Stack

Build a "documentation stack" that makes each business trip verifiable from multiple independent
sources:

```
Layer 1: Logbook (primary record)
Layer 2: Google Maps Timeline (automatic GPS record)
Layer 3: Credit card statements (gas purchases at locations matching logbook destinations)
Layer 4: Client invoices (show client visits matching logbook dates)
Layer 5: Calendar entries (business meetings matching logbook)
Layer 6: 407 ETR statements (toll records showing route and date)
Layer 7: Business email confirmations ("See you Tuesday at your office")
```

A CRA auditor who sees five independent corroborating sources for the same trip cannot argue
the trip did not happen.

### 15.4 Common Scenarios and How to Defend Them

**Scenario 1: You claimed 75% business use on a regular car**
- CRA will ask: How do you achieve 75% business use on what appears to be a personal vehicle?
- Your answer (if true): Home is principal place of business. All client visits start from home.
  Logbook shows 23,000 business km out of 30,000 total km. Attach client list and calendar.

**Scenario 2: You forgot to keep a logbook for 6 months**
- CRA disallows the 6 months, prorates the remaining 6 months, and averages
- You CAN reconstruct a logbook from other records (Google Maps, credit cards, calendars, invoices)
- CRA will accept reconstructed records if they are internally consistent and corroborated
- The reconstruction must be done honestly — inflating after the fact is fraud

**Scenario 3: Your claimed business use is higher this year than last year**
- Prepare an explanation: "I started making weekly client visits to Toronto in March 2025 when I
  signed Client X. My business drives increased from 12,000 to 22,000 km."
- Have the client contract ready. Have the invoice dates ready. Have the calendar entries ready.

**Scenario 4: CRA challenges an expense category (e.g., "car wash is personal")**
- For a client-facing consultant: "My work requires appearing professional at client offices.
  Vehicle cleanliness is a legitimate business requirement."
- Reference: IT-521R paragraph 4 includes maintenance costs as deductible

### 15.5 The Voluntary Approach — Proactive Documentation

**Plain English:** Do not wait for an audit. Build your documentation system as if you are going
to be audited. The 15-second habit of logging each trip in MileIQ is infinitely less painful than
reconstructing a year's worth of trips during a CRA audit.

`[RISK]` CRA has 3 years to audit a standard T1 return (s.152(3.1)). If they suspect careless or
negligent errors, they can go back further. If they suspect fraud, there is no limitation period.
A logbook kept contemporaneously (in real time) is the only reliable defense.

ZEV non-passenger (truck/van)           → Class 55 (40%, NO cost limit)

KEY DOLLAR LIMITS (2024):

Maximum deductible lease payment:       $950/month
Operating cost benefit rate (2024):     $0.33/km personal use
Standby charge rate:                    2% × cost × months (for purchased vehicles)

FIRST-YEAR CCA RATES (2025 — AII phased out):
Gas car Class 10 — normal rule:         30% × 50% = 15% of cost in Year 1
Gas car Class 10 — CCPC imm. exp.:     100% of cost in Year 1
EV Class 54 — normal rule:             30% × 50% = 15% of cost in Year 1
EV Class 54 — CCPC imm. exp.:          100% of cost in Year 1 (up to $61K)
EV Class 55 — CCPC imm. exp.:          100% of FULL cost (no limit)
