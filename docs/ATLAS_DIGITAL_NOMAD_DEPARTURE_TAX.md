## PART 1: WHAT "LEAVING CANADA" ACTUALLY MEANS FOR TAX

---

### 1. Residency vs Citizenship

**Plain English first:**

Imagine you have a gym membership. As long as you're a member, you pay the monthly fee. The moment you cancel, you stop paying. Canada works the same way — Canadian **tax residency** is the membership. You can cancel it by genuinely moving abroad. Your Canadian **citizenship** is your passport — it never goes away unless you renounce it, but it doesn't determine who you pay taxes to.

Canada is NOT like the United States. The US taxes its citizens worldwide, forever, no matter where they live. If you're American and move to the Isle of Man, the IRS still wants 37% of every dollar you make anywhere on earth. Nightmare.

Canada is different. Canada taxes based on **where you live**, not where you're from. If you genuinely relocate to the Isle of Man, establish real residency there, and sever your ties to Canada — CRA stops taxing your worldwide income. Full stop.

You keep your Canadian passport. You can still visit. You just stop being a Canadian **tax resident**.

**The technical rule:**

Canadian tax residency is determined under **ITA s.250(1)** (deemed residents) and the common law "ordinarily resident" test established in *Thomson v. MNR* [1946] SCR 209. The Supreme Court's test: "customary mode of life" — where do you regularly, normally, or customarily live?

A person is a **factual resident** of Canada if they are **ordinarily resident** in Canada — meaning Canada is the place where they have their settled, routine way of life.

**For CC:**
You're Canadian by citizenship and currently Ontario tax-resident. Your exit from Canadian taxation requires genuinely relocating — not just spending time abroad, but making the Isle of Man (or Guernsey or Ireland) your actual home.

**The treaty tie-breaker:**

If you move to a country that has a tax treaty with Canada (UK does, Ireland does) and both countries claim you as a resident, the treaty's Article IV "tie-breaker" resolves the conflict. The tie-breaker tests, in order:
1. Permanent home available (which country do you have a home in?)
2. Centre of vital interests (where are your personal and economic relations?)
3. Habitual abode (where do you spend more time?)
4. Nationality (what passport do you hold?)
5. Mutual agreement procedure (as a last resort)

**Bottom line:** The tie-breaker almost always follows where you actually live. Have a home there, run your business there, keep your social life there — treaty protects you.

### 2. The Residential Ties Test

**Plain English first:**

CRA doesn't take your word for it when you say "I moved." They look at the evidence. Do you still have a Toronto apartment? Still have OHIP? Still have your RBC account and Canadian driver's licence? If yes — CRA says you never really left, and they'll tax you like you never left.

The test is about **residential ties** — the physical and financial threads connecting you to Canada. You need to cut them.

**Primary ties — having ANY of these means CRA presumes you're still a resident:**

| Primary Tie | Plain English | What to Do |
|-------------|---------------|------------|
| Home available in Canada (owned or rented) | Your name is on a lease or you own property in Canada | Don't keep a Canadian apartment. If you own, rent it out properly (arms-length lease) or sell. |
| Spouse/common-law partner in Canada | Your partner stays behind in Canada | If your partner stays, CRA will almost certainly say you're resident. This is the hardest tie to sever. |
| Dependants in Canada | Kids in Canadian schools, etc. | N/A for CC at 22. |

**Secondary ties — CRA looks at the pattern and circumstances:**

| Secondary Tie | Risk Level | Action |
|---------------|-----------|--------|
| Canadian bank accounts | Medium | Keep one RBC account for Canadian income but close all others. Mark remaining accounts as non-resident. |
| Canadian driver's licence | Medium | Cancel or convert to destination country licence. |
| Ontario Health Card (OHIP) | Medium | Must cancel on departure date. OHIP coverage ends when you leave anyway. |
| Canadian club memberships (gym, professional) | Low | Cancel. |
| Canadian mailing address | Medium | Cancel. Use destination address for everything. |
| Personal property in Canada (furniture, car, clothing) | Medium | Ship abroad or sell. Don't leave a fully furnished apartment sitting in Canada. |
| Seasonal property (cottage) | High | Don't own a cottage in Canada after you leave. Sell it. |
| Canadian registered investments | Low | TFSA/RRSP can be maintained as non-resident accounts. Not a tie that defeats non-residency. |
| Canadian passport | Low | Not determinative on its own. |
| Canadian business | High | If you're running an active Canadian business from abroad, CRA may say you're still resident OR that the business has a Canadian permanent establishment (PE). |

**The overall picture:**

CRA looks at everything together. No single secondary tie defeats non-residency, but five or six secondary ties combined with occasional visits to Canada creates a serious risk CRA reassesses you as a Canadian resident.

**ITA reference:** ITA s.250(3), CRA IT-221R3 (Determination of an Individual's Residence Status), Folio S5-F1-C1.

**For CC specifically:**

You're in an ideal position. At 22, no spouse, no kids, no real estate, renting with parents in Collingwood. Your ties are minimal: RBC account, TFSA, FHSA, OHIP card. This is one of the cleanest exit profiles possible. A 45-year-old with a house, a spouse, and kids in school has a much harder departure.

### 3. The Clean Break

**Plain English first:**

Think of it like moving out of a rental apartment. You can't just stop paying rent and pretend you moved out — you have to return the keys, cancel utilities, get your name off the lease, forward your mail, and actually live somewhere else. The clean break from Canada is the same concept, just with more paperwork.

You need to make it **undeniable** that you left Canada and established real life somewhere else. The stronger the evidence of your new life in the destination, the harder it is for CRA to challenge your non-residency.

**The key date:**

Your "departure date" is the day you **physically leave Canada with the intention of not returning as a resident**. That's the legal standard. Not when you filed paperwork, not when you signed an apartment lease abroad — the day you physically left with genuine intent.

Document it with:
- Boarding pass stub (or digital record)
- Signed lease start date in the destination country
- Evidence you cancelled ties on or before that date

**CRA notification:**

You notify CRA of your departure in two ways:
1. **Include a departure date on your final T1 return** — your last Canadian tax return covers the period from January 1 to your departure date. You write in the departure date. No special form required.
2. **File Form NR73** (Determination of Residency Status) — optional but recommended if you want CRA's formal opinion on whether your departure is clean. See Appendix A for NR73 walkthrough.

**The "final return" covers:**
- Canadian-source income from January 1 to departure date
- Deemed dispositions (the departure tax — all capital property "sold" at fair market value)
- Departure tax owing
- Last claim for registered account contributions

**What CRA does NOT require:**
- You don't need permission to leave Canada
- You don't need to renounce citizenship
- You don't need to close all Canadian accounts (some can remain as non-resident accounts)

### 4. Deemed Residency Traps

**Plain English first:**

There are a few rules that can make CRA treat you as a Canadian resident even when you've left — and most people don't know about them.

**Sojourner Rule — ITA s.250(1)(a):**

If you **physically spend 183 days or more in Canada in a calendar year**, CRA deems you a Canadian resident for that entire year — even if you've genuinely moved abroad.

Plain English: If you leave in January, go live in the Isle of Man, but come back and spend summer in Canada (July, August, September = 93 days), that's fine. But if you keep coming back and the total hits 183 days in a year, CRA says you're Canadian for that year.

**For CC:** Don't spend half the year visiting Canada after you leave. Keep your Canadian visits under 183 days per year — ideally under 60 days to be safe and eliminate any grey zone.

**Government Employee Rule — ITA s.250(1)(b)-(f):**

Federal/provincial government employees serving abroad are deemed Canadian residents. Not relevant for CC.

**Continuing Resident Rule:**

Even if you spend less than 183 days, if CRA determines you maintained significant residential ties to Canada (home available, spouse here, etc.), they can reassess you as a factual resident regardless of where you "say" you live.

**The family trap:**

If CC starts a family someday and the spouse/children stay in Canada while CC lives abroad, CRA will almost certainly consider CC a Canadian resident. The "centre of vital interests" tie-breaker almost always points to where your family is. Plan this.

### 5. Section 128.1 — The Deemed Disposition on Leaving

**Plain English first:**

Here's the honest truth about leaving Canada: CRA will send you one final bill on your way out.

On the day you leave Canada, CRA legally pretends you **sold everything you own**. Not just what you actually sell — everything. Your stocks, your crypto, your business shares, your investment property, your collectibles. All of it. At today's market price. And you owe capital gains tax on any profit.

They call this the "deemed disposition on emigration." It's like a checkout fee on the way out of the hotel. Pay it once, and you're free. No more paying 53% on every dollar you earn for the rest of your life.

Why does this rule exist? Because if CRA didn't charge you on the way out, you could grow Apple stock in Canada at 53% tax, then move to the Isle of Man the day before you sell — and pay 0% instead of 53%. The departure tax closes that loophole. You pay on the gains that accrued while you were a Canadian resident.

**The technical rule:**

**ITA s.128.1(4)(b)** — Every person who ceases to be a Canadian resident is deemed to have disposed of each property owned at the time of emigration for proceeds equal to the fair market value (FMV) of that property at that time, and to have reacquired each property at a cost equal to that FMV.

Translation: Departing = deemed sold at today's price. Gains = (today's FMV) minus (what you originally paid, your ACB).

**The tax calculation:**

```
Departure Tax Formula:
  Gain = Fair Market Value at Departure - Adjusted Cost Base (ACB)
  Taxable Gain = Gain × 50% inclusion rate (for capital gains)
  Tax Owing = Taxable Gain × your marginal rate at departure

  Example at $100K income year:
    $50K gain → $25K taxable → $25K × 29.65% = ~$7,400 tax
```

**This is a one-time cost.** You pay it, you're done. Every future dollar you earn in the Isle of Man at 0-20% tax is unencumbered.

### 6. What's Included and What's Excluded

**Plain English first:**

Not everything you own is subject to departure tax. Some things are excluded entirely — you keep them, let them grow, pay nothing on departure. Understanding the exclusions is where real money is saved.

#### INCLUDED in the Deemed Disposition (CRA taxes this on departure):

| Asset Type | Plain English | ITA Reference |
|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| Canadian and foreign stocks | Any shares held outside your registered accounts | s.128.1(4)(b) |
| Crypto (Bitcoin, ETH, etc.) | All crypto on any exchange | s.128.1(4)(b), treated as capital property |
| Private company shares (OASIS Inc.) | The biggest one for CC — your business shares | s.128.1(4)(b) |
| Foreign property (stocks, bonds held abroad) | Investments in foreign brokerages | s.128.1(4)(b) |
| Investment real estate (NOT principal residence) | A rental property — NOT your home | s.128.1(4)(b) |
| Collectibles, art, jewellery over $1,000 ACB | Expensive stuff | s.128.1(4)(b) |
| Foreign company shares | Shares in any foreign business you own | s.128.1(4)(b) |
| Partnership interests | If you're a partner in something | s.128.1(4)(b) |

#### EXCLUDED from the Deemed Disposition (no tax on departure):

| Asset Type | Plain English | Why Excluded |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| **Real property in Canada** | Your house, land, buildings IN Canada | Stays under Canadian tax forever — CRA taxes it when you actually sell it (s.2(3)). Not deemed disposed because Canada can always tax it later. |
| **RRSP / RRIF** | Your retirement savings account | ITA s.128.1(10) "excluded right or interest" — CRA taxes this when you withdraw, not on departure. |
| **TFSA** | Tax-Free Savings Account | Also an "excluded right or interest." No departure tax. Stays tax-free until you withdraw. |
| **FHSA** | First Home Savings Account | Excluded right or interest. |
| **Pension rights** | CPP entitlements, employer pension plans | Excluded — taxed when paid out. |
| **Stock options** | Employee options not yet exercised | Generally deferred to exercise date (s.7, s.128.1 exceptions). |
| **RESP** | Education savings plan | Excluded right or interest. |
| **Personal-use property under $10K cost** | Laptop, furniture under $10K per item | Personal-use property exemption |
| **Life insurance policies** | Policy value (not proceeds) | Excluded right or interest |
| **Canadian business property used in a PE** | Assets of an actual Canadian business with a PE | CRA taxes these under Canadian PE rules instead |

**The most important exclusion for CC:**

Your **TFSA** and **RRSP** are completely excluded from departure tax. Max them out before you leave — every dollar in there leaves Canada tax-free on departure and continues growing in the destination tax-free (TFSA) or tax-deferred (RRSP).

**Canadian real estate exclusion — the trick:**

If you own a rental property in Canada when you leave, it's NOT subject to departure tax. CRA tracks it as "taxable Canadian property" and taxes the gain when you eventually sell it (subject to the s.116 non-resident withholding rules). So real estate stays in Canada's tax net forever, but you don't pay departure tax on it when you leave. This matters for future planning — if OASIS grows and you buy a Canadian rental property, leaving doesn't trigger tax on it.

### 7. Calculating Your Departure Tax Bill

**Step-by-step calculation:**

**Step 1: List all capital property you own on departure day**
Everything in the "INCLUDED" table above. Get fair market values.

**Step 2: Find your Adjusted Cost Base (ACB) for each**
- Stocks: what you originally paid (weighted average for identical securities)
- Crypto: ACB under the Canadian weighted average method (see ATLAS_DEFI_TAX_GUIDE.md)
- Business shares: ACB of your shares in the corporation (usually the paid-up capital = what you paid in)
- If you started a CCPC and issued shares to yourself for $1 — your ACB is $1

**Step 3: Calculate the gain on each asset**
```
Gain = FMV at departure - ACB
If FMV > ACB: you have a capital gain
If FMV < ACB: you have a capital loss (can offset other gains)
```

**Step 4: Apply the inclusion rate**
- Capital gains: 50% inclusion rate (on the first $250K of gains per year)
- Above $250K/year: 66.67% inclusion rate (for gains post-Budget 2024)
- Note: Business income (active trading, etc.) has 100% inclusion — departure tax applies at 50% to capital gains only

**Step 5: Add all taxable amounts to your final T1**
Your departure year return covers Jan 1 to departure date. All deemed dispositions are included. You pay at your marginal rate based on that year's total income.

**Step 6: Apply any LCGE (if applicable)**
If OASIS qualifies as a QSBC (Qualified Small Business Corporation), you can shelter up to $1,250,000 of the gain from your OASIS shares with the Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption. This is the most powerful tool for entrepreneurs leaving Canada. See Section 10.

**Step 7: Consider deferral (T1244)**
You don't have to write a cheque for the full amount on departure day. You can elect to defer payment and provide security. See Section 9.

### 9. The Deferral Option

**Plain English first:**

You don't have to write a cheque for the departure tax on the day you leave. You can elect to **defer the payment** — basically tell CRA "I'll pay when I actually sell this stuff." But CRA isn't a charity: they'll charge you interest at the prescribed rate (currently ~4-5% per year) while you wait.

And they'll want **security** — collateral to guarantee you'll pay eventually. Think of it like putting a deposit on your departure tax.

**How it works — ITA s.220(4.5):**

1. File **Form T1244** with your departure return (or within 30 days of departure)
2. CRA will determine the maximum amount that can be deferred
3. You provide "acceptable security" (letter of credit, federal or provincial bonds, pledge of shares, etc.)
4. CRA holds the security until you actually dispose of the property
5. Interest accrues at the prescribed rate (ITA s.220(4.5)) on the deferred amount

**When deferral makes sense:**

- You have significant departure tax owing (say $50K+)
- You don't have cash available to pay it upfront
- The underlying assets haven't been sold and you need time
- The prescribed rate (currently ~4-5%) is cheaper than liquidating your assets at a bad time

**When deferral doesn't make sense:**

- You can pay the tax from other cash
- The interest cost exceeds the benefit of keeping assets
- You plan to sell the assets within 1-2 years anyway

**For CC:**

At the projected $300K OASIS revenue scenario, departure tax is ~$1,500 — no deferral needed. At the $2M scenario with no planning, deferral might be necessary while you arrange payment. But with proper LCGE planning, you may never need deferral at all.

**Form T1244 — Election to Defer Payment of Tax on Income Relating to Emigration.** File with your final T1 return or within 30 days of filing.

### 10. LCGE on Departure — The Single Biggest Opportunity

**Plain English first:**

The government gives every Canadian entrepreneur a gift: the first $1,250,000 of profit you make selling shares in your small business is completely **tax-free**. This is called the Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE).

Most people use it when they sell their business to a buyer. But you can also use it on departure — when CRA deems you sold your OASIS shares on the way out. If OASIS is worth $1.25M or less on the day you leave, you literally pay $0 departure tax on your business, no matter how much it's grown.

Think of it as a coupon: "First $1.25M profit from your business = free." You can only use it once (it's lifetime), but if you structure properly, family members can each have their own coupon.

**The QSBC test — does your business qualify?**

To use the LCGE, OASIS must be a **Qualified Small Business Corporation (QSBC)** at the time of departure. The test has three parts:

**Test 1: CCPC — Is it a Canadian-controlled private corporation?**
Yes — as long as CC owns majority shares and no public company or non-Canadian controls it.

**Test 2: The 90% Active Asset Test (at departure)**
At least 90% of the fair market value of ALL assets in the corporation must be used in an active business carried on in Canada. This is the one people fail.

Why do people fail it? Cash. If OASIS has been profitable and sitting on $500K cash in a bank account alongside a $600K business, the corporation's assets are 50% cash (passive) / 50% active business. That fails the 90% test. This is why **purification** matters.

Purification strategy: Before departure, strip the passive assets (cash, investments) out of the operating company. Pay yourself a salary/dividend to drain the cash, or set up a holding company and transfer the passive assets there. The operating company ends up clean — 90%+ active assets — and qualifies for LCGE.

See `ATLAS_INCORPORATION_TAX_STRATEGIES.md`, Section 7 for full purification playbook.

**Test 3: The 24-Month Holding Period + 50% Active Test (during holding period)**
For at least 24 months before departure, CC must have held the shares, AND during that 24-month period, more than 50% of the FMV of the corporation's assets must have been used in active Canadian business.

Plain English: You need to have owned the shares for at least 2 years, and the company needs to have been actively operating the whole time. Not just holding cash or investments.

**For CC:**

```
Optimal LCGE strategy:
  1. Incorporate OASIS as soon as income consistently exceeds $80K (~now or Q3 2026)
  2. Hold shares for 24+ months before departure
  3. Purify corporation 6 months before departure (ensure 90%+ active assets)
  4. At departure, deem disposed at FMV
  5. Apply LCGE to shelter up to $1,250,000 of gain
  6. Tax on first $1.25M of OASIS value: $0

Timeline: Incorporate Q3 2026 → earliest LCGE-eligible departure: Q3 2028
```

This means if CC leaves in 2028 or later and OASIS is worth $1.25M or less at departure, the entire business exit is **tax-free**.

**ITA s.110.6(2.1):** The $1,250,000 LCGE for QSBC shares (2024 Budget increase from $1,016,602).

**ITA s.110.6(1):** Definitions — "qualified small business corporation share," "qualifying disposition."

## PART 3: WHAT HAPPENS TO YOUR CANADIAN ACCOUNTS AFTER YOU LEAVE

### 11. RRSP as a Non-Resident

**Plain English first:**

Your RRSP is like a jar of money you've been sealing with Canadian dollars. When you leave Canada, the jar doesn't get emptied — it just changes rules. You can still have the jar, the money still grows tax-deferred, but you can no longer add to it (no more contributions after you become a non-resident). And when you eventually take money out, there's a withholding tax instead of Canadian income tax.

**The departure day treatment:**

RRSP is an **"excluded right or interest"** under ITA s.128.1(10). It is NOT deemed disposed on departure. You do NOT pay departure tax on your RRSP.

**After departure:**

- **Contributions:** You cannot contribute to an RRSP as a non-resident (you have no Canadian "earned income" to generate RRSP room)
- **Growth:** Continues to grow tax-deferred inside the plan
- **Withdrawals:** Subject to **Part XIII withholding tax** at source
  - Default withholding rate: 25%
  - **Canada-UK treaty Article 17:** Reduced to 25% on lump sum withdrawals, potentially 15% on periodic pension payments
  - You receive net of withholding. Generally no need to file a Canadian return for Part XIII income.

**The deregistration option:**

As a non-resident, you can deregister your RRSP and receive the proceeds (with 25% withholding). Whether this makes sense depends on your destination country's treatment of the RRSP and whether a tax treaty provides credit for the Canadian withholding.

**The IoM / Guernsey treatment of RRSP:**

Isle of Man and Guernsey do not have tax treaties with Canada. There is no provision to credit the Canadian Part XIII withholding against IoM/Guernsey personal tax. However, IoM personal income tax is already 20% (below the 25% withholding), so your effective combined rate might be higher than expected if you withdraw large RRSP sums from the IoM.

**Optimal strategy for CC:**

RRSP is currently $0. Build it before departure, then leave it. In retirement (or low-income years), withdraw slowly. Target $50-70K/year RRSP withdrawals in a year where you have minimal other income — the withholding is your only tax, and at moderate sums it may be acceptable.

**What CC should do NOW:**

Max out RRSP contributions before departure. At $280K-$480K projected 2026 income, RRSP room accumulates fast: 18% of prior year earned income (up to $32,490 for 2026). Every dollar contributed before departure is taxed at ~50% rates in Canada and grows tax-deferred. Withdraw in retirement at Part XIII withholding rates (25%) or in years where you've returned to Canada as a resident.

### 12. TFSA as a Non-Resident

**Plain English first:**

The TFSA is Canada's best gift to young people. Money in there grows completely tax-free. No tax when you contribute, no tax on gains, no tax when you withdraw. It's a tax-free vault.

The departure rule: TFSA is NOT deemed disposed when you leave. The money stays tax-free. The vault stays sealed. But you can no longer add new money to it — contribution room stops accumulating while you're a non-resident.

**The departure day treatment:**

TFSA is an "excluded right or interest" under ITA s.128.1(10). NOT deemed disposed. No departure tax. All gains inside remain tax-free.

**After departure:**

- **Cannot contribute** while non-resident (ITA s.207.01(1) definition of "excess TFSA amount" — any non-resident contribution creates a 1%/month penalty tax)
- **Can still hold the account** — money stays, grows tax-free
- **Can still withdraw** — no withholding tax on TFSA withdrawals (unlike RRSP)
- **Contribution room does NOT accumulate** while non-resident
- **If you return to Canada:** Room resumes accumulating from the year you re-establish residency

**WARNING — The 1% Monthly Penalty:**

If you make even $1 in TFSA contributions while you're a non-resident (even if you're not sure if you've technically left yet), CRA will charge 1% per month on that amount until it's withdrawn. This is a painful, automatic penalty. Do not contribute to TFSA from the date you intend to depart, even if you haven't filed the departure return yet.

**The US exception (not relevant for CC):**

The US does NOT recognize TFSAs as tax-exempt. If CC ever moved to the US, TFSA gains would be taxed by the IRS as if the account were a regular taxable account. For Crown Dependencies and Ireland, this is not an issue — they have no CFC rules that would pick up TFSA income for a non-resident individual.

**Optimal strategy for CC:**

**Max out TFSA before you leave.** You have current room of ~$95,000 (2024 cumulative limit = $95,000 for those born in 2003 who have been eligible since age 18 in 2021). Currently $155 in the TFSA. You have ~$94,845 in available room right now. Maximize this with OASIS income before departure. Every dollar of gains on that $95K is permanently tax-free — no departure tax, no future tax, no withholding on withdrawal.

Example:
```
$95,000 in TFSA, invested at 8% for 30 years:
  Future value: ~$953,000
  Tax payable in Canada on that growth: $0
  Tax payable from Isle of Man on withdrawal: $0 (no withholding on TFSA)
  You keep: $953,000 free and clear
```

This is genuinely one of the best financial moves available to CC.

### 13. FHSA as a Non-Resident

**Plain English first:**

The FHSA is specifically for buying your first home. CC just opened one (March 27, 2026). It accumulates $8,000 in room per year, up to a lifetime maximum of $40,000.

The problem: the FHSA was designed for Canadian residents who plan to buy a Canadian home. If you leave Canada without using it, CRA says "time's up" — you have one year after becoming a non-resident to either:
1. Buy a qualifying Canadian home with the FHSA proceeds (unlikely if you've moved abroad), OR
2. Transfer the FHSA balance to your RRSP (no tax, just moves to RRSP), OR
3. Withdraw the balance as taxable income (pay income tax on the full amount)

**The practical rule:**

If you're planning to leave Canada within 1-5 years, the FHSA strategy depends on whether you'll ever buy a Canadian home. If yes — great, use it for the purchase and get the full deduction and tax-free withdrawal. If no — transfer to RRSP before you leave (best option) or withdraw and pay tax.

### 14. Canadian Bank Accounts

**Plain English first:**

You don't have to close all your Canadian bank accounts when you leave. Having a Canadian bank account as a non-resident is normal — lots of non-residents have Canadian accounts to receive payments, manage RRSP distributions, etc. The key is letting the bank know you're a non-resident so they apply the correct withholding rates.

4. **Close accounts you don't need.** Extra bank accounts you're not using are unnecessary ties. Keep what you need, close the rest.

- GIC interest from Canadian banks: same treatment
- Interest credited to your RRSP or TFSA: not subject to Part XIII withholding (excluded accounts)

## PART 4: WHAT CRA STILL TAXES AFTER YOU LEAVE

### 16. Part XIII Withholding Tax

**Plain English first:**

Even after you leave Canada, CRA still taxes money that COMES FROM Canada. It's like a toll booth at the border for income — every time money flows from a Canadian source to a non-resident, Canada takes a cut at the source.

This tax is called **Part XIII withholding** because it lives in Part XIII of the Income Tax Act. It's withheld at the source — meaning the Canadian payor (the tenant, the bank, the Canadian company paying dividends) deducts the tax and sends it to CRA before you ever see the money. You receive the amount net of withholding.

**Default rate: 25%**

The default rate under ITA s.212 is 25% on most types of Canadian-source passive income. Tax treaties reduce this for residents of treaty countries.

**Canada-UK Treaty reductions (relevant for CC in Isle of Man — NOTE: IoM has no treaty):**

Important: The **Isle of Man does NOT have a tax treaty with Canada**. Guernsey doesn't either. Jersey doesn't either. These Crown Dependencies are NOT the UK — they're separate jurisdictions that happen to use British passports.

If CC moves to the **Isle of Man** or **Guernsey**, there is no treaty with Canada. The default 25% Part XIII withholding applies to ALL Canadian-source income.

If CC moves to **Ireland** (which HAS a treaty with Canada), the Canada-Ireland treaty reduces withholding rates.

This is a critical distinction that most people miss. Let's walk through both scenarios:

**Scenario A: CC in Isle of Man (no Canada-IoM treaty)**

| Income Type | Withholding Rate | Notes |
|-------------|-----------------|-------|
| Dividends from Canadian stocks | 25% | No treaty reduction |
| Interest from Canadian bank | 25% | No treaty reduction |
| Rent from Canadian property | 25% (gross) | Can elect s.216 |
| RRSP withdrawals | 25% | No treaty reduction |
| Royalties from Canada | 25% | No treaty reduction |
| Canadian employment income | Part I tax (standard) | If physically in Canada |

**Scenario B: CC in Ireland (Canada-Ireland treaty)**

| Income Type | Default Rate | Canada-Ireland Treaty Rate | Article |
|-------------|-------------|---------------------------|---------|
| Dividends | 25% | 15% (participating) / 5% (≥25% shareholding) | Article XI |
| Interest | 25% | 10% | Article XII |
| Royalties | 25% | 0-10% (type-dependent) | Article XIII |
| Rent (gross) | 25% | 25% (treaty doesn't reduce rental) | s.212(1)(d) |
| RRSP withdrawals | 25% | 25% (treaty doesn't reduce) | — |

**Strategic implication for CC:**

If you anticipate having significant ongoing Canadian-source income (dividends from Canadian investments, RRSP withdrawals), Ireland's treaty position is actually better than the Crown Dependencies from a withholding perspective. However, the Isle of Man's 0% corporate tax rate and £200K personal tax cap likely outweigh the withholding disadvantage for most scenarios at CC's income levels.

**The mitigation strategy:**

Move Canadian-source income to the RRSP/TFSA before departure (where withholding doesn't apply to growth), and minimize ongoing Canadian-source passive income. If you've sold your Canadian stocks on departure (deemed disposition), you no longer receive Canadian dividends — the withholding issue goes away. The only remaining Canadian-source income would be RRSP withdrawals and any rental income from Canadian property.

### 17. Section 216 Election

**Plain English first:**

If you own a Canadian rental property as a non-resident, the default rule is: the tenant withholds 25% of the **gross rent** and sends it to CRA. On $2,000/month rent, that's $500/month — even if your expenses (mortgage, maintenance, insurance, etc.) mean you're barely breaking even.

Section 216 lets you opt out of the gross withholding and instead file a Canadian non-resident tax return, reporting **net rental income** (after expenses) at graduated rates. Almost always results in a lower tax bill.

**How it works:**

1. You file **Form NR6** with CRA before the start of the rental year — this reduces the withholding to 25% of NET income (agent/manager withholds 25% of net rent instead of gross)
2. After the year ends, you file a **Section 216 return** (a simplified T1) reporting actual rental income and expenses
3. CRA refunds any over-withholding or you pay any shortfall

**Example:**

```
Canadian rental property: $2,000/month gross rent
Expenses: $1,500/month (mortgage interest, property tax, insurance, CCA)
Net rental income: $500/month = $6,000/year

Without s.216 election:
  Withholding: 25% × $24,000 gross = $6,000/year
  Net income to CC: $24,000 - $1,500 expenses - $6,000 withholding = $16,500/year

With s.216 election:
  Withholding: 25% × $6,000 net = $1,500/year
  Net income to CC: $24,000 - $1,500 expenses - $1,500 withholding = $21,000/year
  Savings: $4,500/year
```

**For CC:** No rental property currently. File this away for future planning — if OASIS scales and CC buys investment real estate in Canada before departure, this election is essential.

### 18. Section 116

**Plain English first:**

Section 116 is the rule that affects you when you **sell Canadian real estate** (or shares of a private Canadian company) **after** you've already left Canada as a non-resident.

Say CC leaves Canada, moves to the Isle of Man, and five years later decides to sell a Canadian rental property. CRA requires the BUYER to withhold 25% of the full purchase price and remit it to CRA on your behalf. The BUYER. So if you sell a $500K property, the buyer sends $125,000 to CRA immediately.

You then file a Canadian non-resident return reporting the actual capital gain, and CRA refunds the excess.

**Why does this matter:**

If you sell "taxable Canadian property" (TCP) as a non-resident without getting a clearance certificate first, the buyer is legally on the hook for your Canadian tax. Sophisticated buyers (real estate lawyers, private equity) will refuse to close without a clearance certificate. This can delay or kill deals.

**The clearance certificate — ITA s.116(2):**

Apply to CRA before closing. CRA reviews your estimated capital gain, determines the actual tax owing, and issues a certificate specifying the withholding required. The buyer withholds that specific amount instead of the full 25%.

**Applies to:**

- Canadian real estate
- Shares of private Canadian companies (where Canadian real estate represents >50% of value)
- Resource properties
- Options on the above

**Does NOT apply to:**

- Publicly traded Canadian stocks (portfolio investments — different rules)
- TFSA/RRSP withdrawals

**For CC:** This primarily matters if OASIS remains a CCPC registered in Canada after CC departs, and CC later sells those shares to a buyer. The buyer would be required to withhold. Get the clearance certificate (s.116(2)) in advance of any private share sale.

### 21. Guernsey

**Plain English first:**

Guernsey is another Crown Dependency, similar to the Isle of Man, but with some key differences. It's located off the coast of Normandy, France — so it's actually closer to France than England. About 67,000 people live there.

The tax structure is slightly different from IoM:

**Tax summary:**

| Tax Type | Rate | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|
| Corporate tax | 0% | Most companies |
| Personal income tax | 20% flat | On Guernsey-source income. Non-Guernsey income: standard charge available |
| Capital gains tax | 0% | |
| Inheritance tax | 0% | |
| Document duty (stamp duty) | 0.5-2.5% on property transfers | |
| Standard charge (tax cap) | £130,000 on non-Guernsey income | Annual cap on tax from non-local income |

**The standard charge:**

Instead of a flat tax cap (like IoM's £200K), Guernsey uses a "standard charge" system. You can elect to pay £130,000 as a flat charge on all non-Guernsey income, plus 20% on Guernsey-source income. This caps your tax on foreign income at £130,000/year.

For someone earning £1M internationally, this means effective rate on that income is 13% + 20% on any Guernsey earnings.

**Housing — the catch:**

Guernsey's housing market is highly controlled. To live in Guernsey, you must qualify under the Housing (Control of Occupation) (Guernsey) Ordinance. There are two categories:
- **Local Market:** For people with 5+ years Guernsey residency or special employment. Can buy/rent anywhere.
- **Open Market:** For newcomers. Must buy/rent on the Open Market register, which is more limited and premium-priced.

Open Market properties are typically 20-40% more expensive than Local Market equivalents. Budget £25,000-£45,000/year for Open Market housing in a decent area.

**For CC:**

Guernsey is slightly harder to enter than Isle of Man (housing restrictions) and has a higher tax cap (£130K vs IoM £200K personal cap for most). IoM is generally the cleaner choice unless you specifically prefer Guernsey for personal reasons.

### 24. Substance Requirements

**Plain English first:**

You can't just register a company on the Isle of Man, live in Canada, and tell CRA "I'm an Isle of Man company now." That doesn't work. Tax authorities everywhere have **substance requirements** — the idea that you need to actually be THERE for the favorable tax to apply.

"Substance" means: real office (or at minimum a real place of business), real employees or contractors, real management decisions made in the jurisdiction, real economic activity.

For individuals (CC personally), substance is simpler: you need to **actually live there.** Rent an apartment, have a local bank account, spend most of your time there, make your home there. Simple.

For companies (OASIS IoM Ltd.), substance means:
- Registered office in the jurisdiction (can be a registered agent)
- At minimum, meetings of the board held in the jurisdiction
- Key management decisions made from the jurisdiction
- At least one local director who has real authority (not a rubber stamp)
- Proper books and records maintained locally

**BEPS (Base Erosion and Profit Shifting) — why substance matters more than ever:**

The OECD's BEPS project (which Canada participates in) cracked down on "brass plate" companies — empty shell companies registered in low-tax jurisdictions with no real activity. Post-BEPS, most jurisdictions (including Crown Dependencies) have passed substance legislation requiring companies that benefit from 0% corporate tax to demonstrate genuine economic activity in the jurisdiction.

**IoM Substance Legislation (Income Tax Act 1970, as amended 2018):**

IoM companies that carry on "relevant activities" (including holding companies, finance, IP holding, HQ businesses) must demonstrate:
- Managed and directed in IoM (board meetings in IoM, majority of directors IoM-resident)
- Core income-generating activities performed in IoM
- Adequate number of qualified employees in IoM
- Adequate operating expenditure incurred in IoM
- Adequate physical assets in IoM

**Practical minimum for CC as a one-person SaaS founder:**

- You live in the Isle of Man (most important — you ARE the substance)
- You run OASIS from there (work from your IoM apartment/office)
- IoM bank account used for business
- Registered office (can be a registered agent ~£500/year)
- Simple: you're already doing the work — just do it from IoM

This is the key insight: for a software business run by one person, the founder moving to the Isle of Man IS the substance. You don't need employees or a physical office building when you are the business.

## PART 7: ANTI-AVOIDANCE AND RISKS

### 30. GAAR Risk

**Plain English first:**

The General Anti-Avoidance Rule (GAAR) is CRA's big stick. It says: even if you followed every rule to the letter, if your ONLY reason for doing something was to avoid tax, and the result "abuses" the purpose of the tax law, CRA can undo it and tax you anyway.

Think of it like this: the tax rules have a spirit. If you follow the letter of the law but clearly violate its spirit, GAAR applies.

When does GAAR apply to departure planning?

It's most relevant when: you "leave" Canada for just long enough to avoid a specific tax event (like selling a business), then return immediately. You never genuinely relocated — you just did a temporary tax dodge.

Example: You're about to sell OASIS for $5M. You technically establish non-residency for 30 days, sell the shares as a non-resident (paying 0% tax), then return to Canada. GAAR — and CRA would win. This is precisely the kind of abuse GAAR was designed to prevent.

**What protects you from GAAR:**

Genuine, long-term relocation with real economic substance in the destination country. If you move to the Isle of Man for 2+ years, build your life there, run your business from there, and THEN sell OASIS — GAAR has no traction. The relocation was real.

The test: is there a "bona fide purpose" for the departure other than tax? For CC: building a global business, leveraging British citizenship, accessing EU markets via Ireland, lower cost of living for a 22-year-old scaling a startup. These are all legitimate non-tax reasons.

**ITA s.245:** The GAAR provision. Applies when: (1) there is an "avoidance transaction," (2) resulting in a "tax benefit," and (3) the transaction is "abusive" (misuses or abuses the Act's provisions).

---

### 31. Factual Residency Recharacterization

**Plain English first:**

CRA's most common challenge to departing Canadians is simply: "You never really left." They'll point to your Canadian apartment, your spouse in Canada, your frequent Canada trips, your Canadian cell phone plan, your Canadian doctor — and say you're still a factual resident.

This isn't GAAR. It's not even tax avoidance. CRA is just saying your departure wasn't genuine.

**How CRA finds out:**

- You file a departure return — they now have reason to review
- CRA's international tax auditors specifically audit departure cases
- Your T4 slips from Canadian employment show up — looks like you were working in Canada
- NR73 determination comes back saying "you're still a resident"
- Travel records (if requested) show 183+ days in Canada

**How to make the case undeniable:**

Evidence that CRA accepts:
- Lease/purchase agreement in destination country (signed, dated)
- Employment records or client contracts in destination country
- Destination country tax returns filed
- Destination country health insurance
- Destination country driver's licence
- Destination country bank account statements (active)
- Social and club memberships in destination country
- Children enrolled in schools in destination country (N/A for CC now)
- Cell phone contract in destination country

And equally important — the absence of Canadian ties:
- No Canadian home available to you (closed lease, property rented at arms length, or sold)
- No Ontario health card usage
- No Canadian driver's licence
- No Canadian club memberships
- Minimal Canadian banking (non-resident accounts only)

**The test case:**

In *Dahl v. The Queen* (2007 TCC 418), the Tax Court upheld a taxpayer's non-resident status partly based on his genuine economic ties to the destination country and severed Canadian ties — even when he spent significant time visiting Canada. The key: the **pattern of life** pointed to the destination.

### 32. Re-Entry Risk

**Plain English first:**

If you leave Canada, use the LCGE to eliminate departure tax on OASIS shares, and then come back to live in Canada within 5 years — CRA may partially unwind the benefit.

Specifically: the **stop-loss rule** in ITA s.40(3.7) and the interaction with ITA s.128.1(7) (returning residents). When you return to Canada as a resident, you are deemed to have re-acquired your property at the same FMV you used for the departure. So you don't double-pay departure tax, but you don't get a free stepped-up ACB either.

More importantly: if you used LCGE on departure and return too quickly, CRA may argue your departure wasn't genuine and the LCGE claim was improper.

**The practical guidance:**

Plan your departure as permanent. If after 5+ years you want to return, you can — but be aware of the ACB reset rules and re-entry tax implications. The longer you're gone, the cleaner the departure looks.

**The 5-year reference:**

Various provisions (the deemed disposition unwind under s.128.1(7), CRA administrative positions) reference 5 years. It's not a hard rule that automatically unwinds things at year 4 — but CRA auditors use it as a reference point when evaluating whether a departure was genuine.

### 33. The Canadian 183-Day Trap

**Plain English first:**

Once you've established non-residency, you can still visit Canada. CC will presumably want to visit family in Collingwood, attend business meetings, etc. But there's a hard limit: 183 days per calendar year.

If you spend 183 or more days in Canada in any calendar year, CRA deems you Canadian resident for that entire year — even if you have a genuine home and life in the Isle of Man.

**ITA s.250(1)(a):** A person who sojourns in Canada for 183 or more days in a year is deemed to be resident in Canada throughout that year.

**The 183 days applies to any year, not just the departure year.** If you leave in 2028 but visit Canada extensively in 2030 and hit 183 days — you become a Canadian resident again for all of 2030.

**Best practice:**

Keep a **travel diary.** Note every day you enter and leave Canada. If you're getting close to 90-100 days in a year, stop visiting. The cost of tripping the 183-day rule is enormous — full Canadian worldwide income tax for the year.

**Safe range:** CRA practice suggests anything under 90 days per year is very safe. Under 120 days is generally safe with clean ties. 120-182 days creates risk if your ties are not clearly severed. 183+ days = automatic Canadian resident for the year.

### 34. US Comparison — Why This Strategy Doesn't Work for Americans

**Plain English first:**

Canada is not the United States. The US taxes its citizens **no matter where they live** — a phenomenon called "citizenship-based taxation" (CBT). If you were American, none of this playbook would work. Moving to the Isle of Man wouldn't save you a cent in US federal taxes because the IRS still wants its share of every dollar you earn worldwide.

The only way to stop paying US taxes is to **renounce US citizenship** — which triggers an "exit tax" (similar to Canada's departure tax but with additional rules for "covered expatriates") and then you're out of the US tax system forever.

**For CC:** You're Canadian (and British). Canada uses **residence-based taxation**. Leave Canada, stop paying Canadian tax. Simple. No citizenship renunciation required.

**WARNING — Do not get a US green card or US citizenship without fully understanding the tax consequences:**

If CC ever qualifies for US permanent residency (green card) through a business, investment, or marriage — the IRS immediately treats you as a US tax person. All your TFSA gains? US-taxable. All your OASIS income? US-taxable. Foreign tax credits reduce double taxation but add enormous compliance complexity (FBAR, PFIC, Form 8938, FATCA, etc.).

This is particularly relevant for CC because OASIS serves US clients. You might be offered US-based opportunities, US residency arrangements, or enter into business structures that inadvertently trigger US tax status. Always consult a US tax advisor before accepting anything that might create US tax exposure.

**The only country worse than Canada for high earners:** The US, if you're a citizen or green card holder.

## APPENDIX A: NR73 FORM WALKTHROUGH

**What NR73 is:**

Form NR73 (Determination of Residency Status [Leaving Canada]) is an optional form you submit to CRA to get a formal written opinion on whether your departure qualifies you as a non-resident. It's not required by law, but it's valuable because:
- It gives you CRA's position in writing — strong protection if later audited
- It forces you to document all your tie-severing in one place
- CRA's written determination is hard for them to later reverse without new facts

**When to file:**

After your departure date but ideally within the same tax year. CRA International Tax Services Office processes it. Response typically takes 6-12 weeks.

**Key questions on NR73 (and how CC should answer):**

## APPENDIX B: T1244 ELECTION GUIDE

**What T1244 is:**

Form T1244 (Election to Defer Payment of Tax on Income Relating to Emigration) is used when you cannot or choose not to pay the full departure tax on your final T1 return. It lets you defer the payment until you actually sell the property.

**When to use it:**

- You have a large departure tax bill (say $50K+) and don't have the cash
- You've been deemed to dispose of property that you haven't actually sold
- You want to defer the tax until actual disposition

**How it works:**

1. Complete Form T1244 — list each property being deferred, its FMV, ACB, and the deferred tax amount
2. Attach to your final T1 return (filed by June 15 following departure year)
3. CRA calculates the acceptable security required
4. You provide the security:
   - Letters of credit from a Canadian financial institution
   - Bonds of Canada or a province
   - A pledge of the actual property itself (if appropriate)
   - Real estate in Canada (with mortgage details)
5. CRA registers a lien or holds the security
6. Interest accrues at the CRA prescribed rate (currently ~4-5%) on deferred amounts
7. When you actually sell the property, you pay the deferred tax (plus accrued interest) and security is released

**Maximum deferral:**

The election is available for all property subject to deemed disposition EXCEPT:
- Property that is immediately sold before departure
- Property that is an interest in a partnership
- Property that is a life insurance policy

**For CC:**

Given the current asset picture ($0-$5K departure tax expected at optimal departure window), T1244 is unlikely to be needed. File it away as a tool for large transactions.
