# SECTION 1: Canadian Business Structures

## 1.1 Sole Proprietorship

The simplest structure. No legal separation between you and the business.

**How it works:**
- You ARE the business. All income reports on your T1 personal return via T2125.
- Unlimited personal liability — creditors can pursue personal assets.
- Business name registration (Ontario): Master Business Licence via ServiceOntario, ~$60 (5-year term).
- No annual corporate filings, no minute book, no separate tax return.

**Tax treatment:**
- Net business income taxed at your marginal rate (Ontario 2026 brackets: 20.05% on first ~$55K, scaling to 53.53% above ~$235K).
- Full access to business deductions: home office, vehicle, equipment (CCA), supplies, subcontractors.
- Self-employed CPP contributions: both employer and employee portions (total ~11.9% on net income between $3,500–$73,200 for 2026).
- No access to Small Business Deduction (SBD), no income splitting, no lifetime capital gains exemption (LCGE).

**When to use:**
- Revenue under ~$50K–$80K CAD (below the incorporation breakeven).
- Testing a business idea before committing to corporate overhead.
- CC's current structure with OASIS AI Solutions.

**Risks:**
- Unlimited liability (mitigated partly by E&O insurance for professional services).
- No tax deferral — all income taxed immediately at personal rates.
- Harder to raise capital or bring on equity partners.

## 1.2 General Partnership

Two or more persons carrying on business together with a view to profit.

**How it works:**
- Created by agreement (written or verbal, but always get it in writing).
- Each partner reports their share of income on their personal T1.
- Partnership files an information return (T5013) if >5 partners or if a partner is a corporation.
- Joint and several liability — each partner is liable for ALL partnership debts.

**Tax treatment:**
- Partnership itself does not pay tax. Income flows through to partners.
- Each partner deducts their share of expenses.
- Partners are self-employed for CPP purposes.

**When to use:**
- Two individuals starting a business together without wanting corporate formality.
- Professional practices (some professions require partnership structure).

**Why CC should avoid it:**
- Joint and several liability is a dealbreaker for most tech businesses.
- No liability protection whatsoever.
- Use an LP or incorporate instead.

---

## 1.3 Limited Partnership (LP)

A partnership with at least one General Partner (GP) and one or more Limited Partners (LPs).

**How it works:**
- GP manages the business and has unlimited liability.
- LPs contribute capital but do NOT manage — their liability is limited to their investment.
- The GP is typically a corporation (so the human behind it has corporate liability protection).
- Registered under Ontario's Limited Partnerships Act. Registration: ~$210 (provincial).

**Tax treatment:**
- Flow-through — income/losses allocated to partners per the LP agreement.
- LPs can only deduct losses up to their "at-risk amount" (capital contributed + share of profits - distributions).
- No entity-level tax.

**When to use:**
- Real estate investment pools.
- Venture capital / private equity fund structures.
- Tax planning: flow losses to high-income individuals while limiting their liability.

**Relevance to CC:**
- If CC creates an investment fund or brings in passive investors for trading capital.
- The GP would be a corporation CC controls; LPs would be investors.

---

## 1.4 Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)

A general partnership where each partner is shielded from liability caused by OTHER partners' negligence.

**How it works:**
- Available in Ontario only for certain regulated professions (lawyers, accountants, doctors — NOT software/AI).
- Each partner still has unlimited liability for their OWN acts and the partnership's general debts.
- Must include "LLP" in the name and carry mandatory insurance.

**Relevance to CC:**
- Not available for AI/software businesses in Ontario.
- Mentioned for completeness only.

## 1.5 Corporation — The Main Event

A separate legal entity. The single most important structure for a growing Canadian business.

### 1.5.1 Types of Canadian Corporations

| Type | Definition | Tax Rate (Ontario, 2026) | Key Feature |
|------|-----------|-------------------------|-------------|
| **CCPC** | Canadian-Controlled Private Corporation | 12.2% on first $500K active business income (SBD) | Small Business Deduction |
| **Non-CCPC** | Private corp controlled by non-residents or public corps | 26.5% (general rate) | No SBD access |
| **Public** | Listed on a designated stock exchange | 26.5% (general rate) | Public market access |

### 1.5.2 CCPC — Canadian-Controlled Private Corporation

This is what CC will incorporate as. The workhorse of Canadian small business.

**Requirements to qualify as CCPC:**
- Private corporation (not listed on a stock exchange).
- Incorporated in Canada (federal or provincial).
- Not controlled directly or indirectly by non-residents or public corporations.
- CC, as sole shareholder and Canadian resident, qualifies automatically.

**Tax advantages:**

| Income Type | Combined Federal + Ontario Rate |
|------------|-------------------------------|
| Active business income (first $500K) | **12.2%** (SBD rate) |
| Active business income (above $500K) | **26.5%** (general rate) |
| Investment income (passive) | **50.17%** (refundable via RDTOH) |
| Capital gains (50% inclusion) | **25.08%** effective (refundable) |
| Eligible dividends received | **38.33%** (refundable via RDTOH) |

**The SBD sweet spot:**
- First $500K of annual active business income taxed at 12.2%.
- The $500K limit is shared among associated corporations (anti-avoidance).
- SBD grinds down when aggregate investment income exceeds $50K/year or taxable capital exceeds $10M.
- This means: keep passive investment income inside the corp under $50K to preserve SBD.

**RDTOH (Refundable Dividend Tax on Hand):**
- When the corp earns investment income, it pays ~50.17% tax, but ~30.67% is added to the RDTOH account.
- When the corp pays taxable dividends, it gets a refund from RDTOH ($38.33 per $100 of dividends).
- This mechanism ensures investment income inside a CCPC is eventually taxed at roughly the same rate as if earned personally — but gives a deferral advantage while retained.

**LCGE (Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption):**
- On sale of qualifying small business corporation (QSBC) shares: up to ~$1.25M (2026, indexed) of capital gains tax-free.
- Requires: 90% of assets used in active business, 24-month holding period, >50% active business assets test.
- This is the big exit play — build OASIS, sell shares, shelter $1.25M+ of gains.

### 1.5.3 Professional Corporations (Ontario)

**Who qualifies (Ontario Business Corporations Act, s. 3.2):**
- Lawyers, physicians, surgeons, dentists, optometrists, chiropractors, chartered accountants, engineers, architects, pharmacists, veterinarians, and certain others as prescribed by regulation.
- Software developers, AI consultants, and DJs do NOT qualify.

**What's different:**
- Shares can only be held by the professional and certain family members.
- The professional remains personally liable for professional negligence (the corp shields from business debts, not malpractice).
- Still gets SBD, LCGE, and corporate tax deferral benefits.

**Relevance to CC:** None. AI/software is not a regulated profession in Ontario.

### 1.5.4 Cooperative Corporation

**What it is:**
- A corporation owned and democratically controlled by its members (one member, one vote — not proportional to shares).
- Incorporated under the Canada Cooperatives Act (federal) or Ontario Co-operative Corporations Act.

**When it makes sense:**
- Worker cooperatives (shared ownership among employees).
- Agricultural co-ops, housing co-ops, retail co-ops.
- When democratic governance is a core value of the business.

**Tax treatment:**
- Taxed similarly to a regular corporation, but can deduct patronage dividends (distributions to members based on usage).
- No SBD access unless it also qualifies as a CCPC (rare for co-ops).

**Relevance to CC:**
- Could theoretically structure OASIS as a worker co-op if bringing on co-founders as equal partners.
- Realistically: no. CC wants control. A standard CCPC is the right call.

## 1.6 The Incorporation Process (Step by Step)

### Federal (Corporations Canada) vs. Ontario Provincial

| Factor | Federal (CBCA) | Ontario (OBCA) |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| **Cost** | $200 online / $250 paper | $300 + NUANS $75 |
| **Name protection** | Canada-wide | Ontario only |
| **Extra-provincial registration** | Required in each province you operate | Not needed if only in Ontario |
| **Filing** | Corporations Canada online portal | ServiceOntario / Ontario Business Registry |
| **Timeline** | Same day (online) to 5 business days | Same day (online) to 5 business days |
| **Annual filings** | Annual return ($12/year) | Annual return ($40/year via CRA) |
| **Name search** | NUANS report required ($13.80 online) | NUANS report required (~$75 via search house) |
| **Recommendation for CC** | **Federal** — Canada-wide name protection, cheaper annual filings, same speed | |

### Step-by-Step: Federal Incorporation (CBCA)

**Step 1: Choose a Name or Use a Numbered Company**
- Named corporation: order a NUANS (Newly Upgraded Automated Name Search) report. Valid for 90 days. Cost: ~$13.80 from Corporations Canada or ~$40–$75 through a search house that also checks trademarks.
- Numbered corporation (e.g., "12345678 Canada Inc."): no NUANS needed. Faster. Can register a trade name later.
- For CC: Incorporate as a numbered company, register "OASIS AI Solutions" as a trade name. Cheapest and fastest.

**Step 2: Prepare Articles of Incorporation**
- Filed online at Corporations Canada Online Filing Centre.
- Key decisions:
  - **Registered office address:** Must be in Canada. Can be CC's home address or a registered agent.
  - **Directors:** Minimum 1 director for a non-distributing (private) corporation. Must be a Canadian resident. CC is the sole director.
  - **Share structure:** Standard setup for a CCPC:
    - Class A Common Shares (voting, participating, no fixed dividend) — CC holds these.
    - Class B Preferred Shares (non-voting, redeemable, fixed dividend) — for future income splitting or estate freeze.
    - Class C Preferred Shares (non-voting, redeemable) — for future investors or family members.
  - **Restrictions on share transfer:** Include a restriction to maintain CCPC and private corporation status ("No shares shall be transferred without approval of the directors").
  - **Number of shareholders:** Limit to 50 (maintains private corporation status under securities law).
  - **Business restrictions:** None (allows the corp to do anything lawful — keep it broad).

**Step 3: File Online**
- Go to: https://ised-isde.canada.ca/cc/lgcy/fdrlCrpSrch.html (or current Corporations Canada portal).
- Upload articles, pay $200 fee.
- Receive Certificate of Incorporation (usually same day for online filing).

**Step 4: Organize the Corporation (First Directors' Meeting)**
- The incorporator (CC) signs an Initial Registered Office Address and First Board of Directors form.
- First directors' meeting (can be just CC signing resolutions):
  - Adopt corporate by-laws (template available from law society or online).
  - Appoint officers (CC as President, Secretary, Treasurer — all the same person is fine).
  - Approve the corporate seal (optional but traditional).
  - Approve the form of share certificates (or resolve to issue uncertificated shares).
  - Authorize the opening of corporate bank accounts.
  - Adopt a fiscal year-end (choose strategically — see below).
  - Issue initial shares to CC (typically 100 Class A Common for $1 each = $100 paid-up capital).
  - Appoint an accountant.

**Step 5: Set Up the Minute Book**
- Physical or digital binder containing:
  - Certificate of Incorporation
  - Articles of Incorporation
  - By-laws
  - Directors' and shareholders' resolutions
  - Share certificates (or register of uncertificated shares)
  - Register of directors and officers
  - Register of shareholders and share transfers
- Cost: $200–$500 from a legal supply store, or DIY with a binder and templates.
- **Critical:** The minute book must be maintained. CRA can request it on audit.

**Fiscal Year-End Selection:**
- Must end within 53 weeks of incorporation.
- Strategic considerations:
  - **December 31:** Simplest. Aligns with personal tax year.
  - **January 31 or February 28:** Maximum 11-month deferral on first salary/bonus — declare bonus at corp year-end, pay within 180 days, deduct in corp year, include in personal year when received.
  - **Non-calendar year-end:** Creates planning opportunities but adds complexity.
  - **Recommendation for CC:** January 31 or February 28 year-end for maximum deferral on first year.

### Post-Incorporation Setup

**1. CRA Business Number (BN) and Program Accounts**

Register with CRA for a Business Number. This is the master identifier. Under it, you open program accounts:

| Account | When Required | Registration |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| **RT (HST/GST)** | Mandatory once revenue > $30K in 4 consecutive quarters. Register voluntarily earlier to claim ITCs. | CRA My Business Account or RC1 form |
| **RP (Payroll)** | When paying salary or dividends requiring T4/T5 | CRA My Business Account |
| **RC (Corporation Income Tax)** | Automatically assigned on incorporation | Comes with BN |

- Register online at CRA My Business Account (need to link corp BN to CC's CRA account).
- Timeline: instant for online; 2–4 weeks by mail.

**2. HST Registration**

- Register for HST (ON rate: 13%) once you expect to exceed $30K revenue, or immediately to claim Input Tax Credits (ITCs) on business purchases.
- **For CC:** Register immediately. OASIS is already past $30K threshold as a sole prop — the corp inherits the obligation.
- Quick Method of Accounting: if revenue is mostly services (no ITCs to claim), consider the Quick Method — remit 8.8% of revenue (incl. HST) instead of tracking ITCs. Saves time. Evaluate annually.
- Filing frequency: annual (revenue < $1.5M), quarterly ($1.5M–$6M), monthly (>$6M). CC will be annual.

**3. Corporate Bank Account**

- Required documents: Certificate of Incorporation, articles, directors' resolution authorizing account opening, personal ID.
- Options:
  - **RBC Business:** CC already banks with RBC personally. $6.95/month (e-Business plan) or $0 with minimum balance.
  - **TD Business:** Competitive, good integration with accounting software.
  - **Wise Business:** Multi-currency (hold USD, EUR, GBP natively). No monthly fee. Best for international clients.
  - **Neo Financial / KOHO Business:** Free, but limited features.
- **Recommendation:** Open RBC Business (primary CAD) + Wise Business (multi-currency for international invoicing).

**4. Payroll Setup**

If paying salary to CC (vs. dividends):
- Register RP account with CRA.
- Deduct CPP (employer + employee), EI (optional for shareholders who control >40% — CC can opt out of EI), and income tax.
- Remit deductions by the 15th of the following month (or quarterly if average monthly remittance < $3,000).
- File T4 by end of February each year.

**5. WSIB (Workplace Safety and Insurance Board)**

- **Mandatory for:** construction, manufacturing, certain service industries with employees.
- **Not mandatory for:** sole-director corporations with no arm's-length employees in non-mandatory industries.
- CC as sole director/employee of an AI consulting company: WSIB registration is NOT required but can voluntarily register for workplace injury coverage.
- **Recommendation:** Skip for now. Revisit if hiring employees.

**6. Corporate T2 Filing**

- Due 6 months after fiscal year-end (e.g., January 31 FYE = T2 due July 31).
- Tax payment due 2 months after FYE (3 months if qualifies for small CCPC extension: prior year taxable income ≤ $500K AND prior year taxable capital ≤ $10M–$15M).
- Must file electronically if gross revenue > $1M. Practically, always e-file.
- Requires financial statements (Review Engagement or Notice to Reader — a NTR is cheapest, ~$1,500–$3,000/year from an accountant).
- **GIFI (General Index of Financial Information):** Coded financial statements required in the T2. Accounting software (QBO, Xero) maps to GIFI automatically.
- **Schedule 1 (Net Income for Tax Purposes):** Reconciles accounting income to taxable income.
- **Schedule 8 (CCA):** Capital cost allowance claims.
- **Schedule 125 (Income Statement):** Coded by GIFI.
- **Schedule 100 (Balance Sheet):** Coded by GIFI.

# SECTION 2: International Entity Structures

## 2.1 US C-Corporation (Delaware)

The default structure for VC-funded startups targeting the US market.

### Why Delaware

- Delaware Court of Chancery: specialized business court, judge-only (no jury), vast body of precedent. Investors and lawyers love it.
- Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL): the most flexible corporate statute in the US. Allows blank-check preferred stock, staggered boards, etc.
- No state income tax on corporations that do not operate within Delaware (i.e., if your operations/employees are elsewhere).
- Privacy: directors and officers not listed in public filings (only a Registered Agent).

### Formation

| Item | Cost | Timeline |
|------|------|----------|
| Delaware filing fee | $89 (online) | Same day to 24 hours |
| Registered Agent (required) | $50–$300/year | Included with filing services |
| Stripe Atlas (turnkey formation) | $500 one-time | 1–3 business days |
| Franchise tax (annual) | $400 minimum (Assumed Par Value method — calculate carefully) | Due March 1 each year |
| Lawyer setup (optional) | $2,000–$5,000 | 1–2 weeks |

### What You Get

- Certificate of Incorporation
- EIN (Employer Identification Number) — the US equivalent of a BN. Apply via IRS Form SS-4.
- Bylaws, initial board resolutions, stock issuance.
- Can then open a US bank account (Mercury, Relay, SVB, Brex).

### Tax Treatment for a Canadian

**The trap:** If CC (a Canadian resident) controls a US C-Corp, it is a **Controlled Foreign Corporation (CFC)** for US tax purposes AND a **Controlled Foreign Affiliate (CFA)** for Canadian tax purposes.

**US side:**
- C-Corp pays US federal corporate tax: 21% flat rate.
- No state tax if incorporated in Delaware with no nexus in other states.
- If CC pays himself salary from the US corp, the corp deducts it, but CC must file a US nonresident tax return (1040-NR) if compensation is for services performed in the US.
- Dividends from US corp to Canadian individual: 15% US withholding tax (under Canada-US tax treaty), then included in CC's Canadian income with a foreign tax credit.

**Canadian side:**
- The US C-Corp is a CFA. Foreign Accrual Property Income (FAPI) rules apply to passive income earned by the CFA.
- Active business income earned by the CFA is NOT taxed in Canada until repatriated (dividends). When paid as dividends, grossed up and credited under the foreign tax credit system.
- **Key planning:** If the US corp earns active business income (AI consulting for US clients), Canada defers tax until dividends flow. This creates a real deferral advantage.

### When to Use

- Raising US VC capital (investors demand Delaware C-Corp).
- Holding US IP (especially if licensing to Canadian OpCo).
- Large US client base that wants to pay a US entity.
- Planning a US-market exit (M&A buyer expects C-Corp).

### When NOT to Use

- Revenue under ~$200K USD. The compliance costs ($3K–$5K/year for US tax return + $400+ franchise tax + registered agent) don't justify it.
- If all clients are Canadian or non-US. The treaty structure adds complexity with no real benefit.

## 2.2 US LLC — The Canadian Tax Trap (and Solutions)

### The Problem

A US LLC is a pass-through entity for US tax purposes (no entity-level tax — income flows to members). But Canada treats it as a **corporation** (because it has limited liability). This creates:

- **US:** LLC income flows through to CC. CC files US 1040-NR and pays US tax on US-source income at individual rates.
- **Canada:** CRA sees the LLC as a foreign corporation. CC must report the LLC's income as FAPI (if passive) or as CFA active business income. The foreign tax credits don't align cleanly because the US is taxing CC personally while Canada is taxing the entity.
- **Result:** Potential double taxation. The foreign tax credit for US individual tax paid by CC may not fully offset Canadian tax on the corporate income, because the character of the income differs between the two countries.

### Solutions

1. **Elect to Treat as Corporation (US Check-the-Box):** File Form 8832 to elect C-Corp treatment for the LLC. Now both countries see a corporation. Clean foreign tax credit. But you now pay US corporate tax (21%) + Canadian tax on dividends when repatriated. This works if you want the Delaware C-Corp treatment anyway.

2. **Disregarded Entity + Treaty Claim:** If single-member LLC, it's disregarded for US tax purposes. Income is CC's directly. File US 1040-NR, claim treaty benefits (Article VII — business profits only taxable in Canada unless US permanent establishment). If no US PE, no US tax. Canada taxes normally as sole proprietor income. **This is the cleanest structure if CC has no US office/employees.**

3. **ULC (Unlimited Liability Company):** Use a Nova Scotia ULC or Alberta ULC instead. Canada treats ULCs as corporations, but the US treats them as pass-through (because liability is unlimited). This is the reverse of the LLC problem and is used in inbound US investment structures. Not directly helpful for CC's situation.

4. **Just Incorporate a C-Corp:** If you need a US entity, skip the LLC and go straight to Delaware C-Corp. Cleaner from day one.

### Bottom Line for CC

- Do NOT form a US LLC unless you have a very specific reason and a cross-border tax advisor.
- If you want a US entity: **Delaware C-Corp** (Section 2.1).
- If you want a US bank account without a US entity: **Wise Business** or **Mercury** (some allow foreign-owned accounts, or use Stripe Atlas to create a C-Corp as a wrapper).

## 2.3 UK Limited Company (Ltd)

### Why Consider It

- UK is the gateway to EU/UK enterprise clients.
- Companies House is transparent and efficient. Formation takes hours.
- Corporation tax: 25% (profits > GBP 250K), 19% small profits rate (< GBP 50K), marginal relief between.
- No withholding tax on dividends paid to non-UK parent companies (under UK domestic law — no treaty needed).
- VAT registration (required if UK taxable turnover > GBP 90K): allows charging VAT to UK/EU clients and reclaiming VAT on UK expenses.

### Formation

| Item | Cost | Timeline |
|------|------|----------|
| Companies House filing | GBP 12 (online) / GBP 30 (paper) | Same day (online) |
| Registered office (virtual) | GBP 50–200/year | Instant |
| Confirmation Statement (annual) | GBP 13 | Annual |
| Accountant (annual accounts) | GBP 500–2,000/year | |
| Corporation tax return (CT600) | Included with accountant or ~GBP 500 standalone | |

### Tax Treatment for a Canadian Owner

- UK Ltd pays UK corporation tax on UK-sourced profits.
- Canada-UK tax treaty: dividends from UK Ltd to Canadian parent/individual — 5% withholding (if >10% ownership) or 15% (portfolio). But UK charges 0% withholding on dividends domestically, so the treaty rate is academic — no UK withholding.
- In Canada: dividends from UK Ltd included in income. Foreign tax credit for underlying UK corporate tax may be available via the "underlying foreign tax credit" mechanism if CC holds >10% of the UK company through a Canadian corporation.
- FAPI rules apply if the UK Ltd earns passive income. Active business income earns an exemption surplus — effectively deferred until dividends are paid to Canada.

### When to Use

- Significant UK/EU client revenue (>GBP 50K/year to justify compliance costs).
- Need to appear as a "local" company to UK enterprise clients (some procurement departments require it).
- Hiring UK-based contractors or employees.

## 2.4 Estonian e-Residency Company

### The Pitch

Estonia's e-Residency program allows non-residents to incorporate and manage an Estonian private limited company (OÜ) entirely online.

### How It Works

- Apply for e-Residency card (~EUR 100, 4–8 weeks processing, pick up at Estonian embassy — nearest to CC: Ottawa or Toronto consulate).
- Incorporate an OÜ (private limited company) via the Estonian Business Register. Minimum share capital: EUR 2,500 (can be unpaid initially).
- Manage the company remotely: digital signatures, e-filing, e-banking.
- Corporate tax: **0% on retained earnings.** Tax is only paid when profits are distributed as dividends: 20% (or 14% on regular distributions after the first year of distributions).

### The Reality Check

- **Banking is the hard part.** Estonian banks (LHV, Swedbank) have tightened KYC for e-residents. Expect 2–6 months to get a business bank account, if approved at all. Alternatives: Wise Business, Payoneer.
- **Substance requirements:** Post-BEPS, Estonia and the EU require real economic substance. A letterbox company with no employees, no office, and a Canadian director managing everything from Collingwood will not pass muster. CRA will look through it.
- **CRA treatment:** The Estonian OÜ is a foreign corporation. CFA/FAPI rules apply. If CC controls it and it earns primarily service income from CC's personal efforts, CRA will argue it's FAPI (income from services — a specific FAPI category under ITA s. 95(2)(b) if services are performed by a related person in a country other than the CFA's country). This means the income is taxed in Canada immediately, regardless of the 0% Estonian rate. The deferral evaporates.

### When It Actually Works

- You have real operations in Estonia (employees, office).
- The company earns income from activities performed in Estonia.
- You use it as a holding company for EU investments/subsidiaries that have their own substance.

### Verdict for CC

- **Not recommended as a primary structure.** The 0% retained earnings rate is attractive on paper but CRA's FAPI rules neutralize it for a one-person AI consultancy controlled from Canada.
- **Possible future use:** If CC hires EU-based developers and needs an EU entity for employment contracts, an Estonian OÜ could serve as the EU OpCo (with real Estonian substance).

## 2.5 Singapore Private Limited Company (Pte. Ltd.)

### Why Singapore

- Consistently ranked #1 or #2 for ease of doing business.
- Corporate tax: 17% headline rate, but effective rates are much lower:
  - First SGD 100K of chargeable income: 75% exemption on first $10K, 50% exemption on next $190K (for first 3 years under Start-Up Tax Exemption Scheme). Effective tax on first $200K: ~4.25%.
  - Partial exemption after year 3: 75% on first $10K, 50% on next $190K.
- No capital gains tax (zero — on anything).
- No withholding tax on dividends (zero — regardless of treaty).
- Territorial tax system: only income sourced in or remitted to Singapore is taxable (with some nuances for service income).
- Extensive tax treaty network (>90 treaties).

### Formation

| Item | Cost | Timeline |
|------|------|----------|
| ACRA registration | SGD 315 (name + incorporation) | 1–3 days |
| Registered office (virtual) | SGD 500–2,000/year | Required — must be Singapore address |
| Company Secretary (mandatory) | SGD 300–800/year | Must be Singapore resident |
| Local Director (mandatory — at least 1 Singapore resident director) | SGD 2,000–5,000/year (nominee) | Required |
| Annual filing (XBRL) | SGD 60 | Annual |
| Corporate tax return | SGD 500–2,000/year (accountant) | Annual |

### Substance Requirements

- At least 1 Singapore-resident director (can be nominee, but CRA may scrutinize).
- Registered office in Singapore.
- IRAS (tax authority) may challenge companies with no economic activity in Singapore.
- Post-BEPS: if the company's key decisions are made from Canada (by CC), it may be considered Canadian tax resident under the tiebreaker rules — "central management and control" test.

### CRA Treatment

- Same as any CFA: FAPI rules apply to passive income and income from services performed by a related person outside Singapore.
- Active business income with real Singapore operations: qualifies for exempt surplus — no Canadian tax until dividends are paid.
- The nominee director trick does not create substance by itself. CRA has successfully challenged these structures (GE Capital Canada, 2009 TCC).

### When to Use

- Real Asia-Pacific business operations (clients in ASEAN, Australia, Japan).
- Hiring Singapore-based team members.
- Holding IP that is genuinely managed from Singapore.
- Revenue from APAC clients > SGD 100K/year.

### Verdict for CC

- **Not now.** No APAC revenue. Compliance costs (~SGD 5K–8K/year minimum) not justified.
- **Future consideration:** If OASIS expands to APAC clients. Singapore is an excellent hub — but it needs real substance.

## 2.6 Dubai (UAE) — Mainland LLC vs. Free Zone vs. Freelancer Permit

### Overview

The UAE has zero personal income tax and zero corporate tax on most income (since June 2023, 9% corporate tax on profits > AED 375K, but Free Zone entities with "qualifying income" get 0%).

### Three Main Structures

| Structure | Corporate Tax | Ownership | Banking | Visa | Setup Cost |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|---------|------|-----------|
| **Mainland LLC** | 9% on profits > AED 375K | 100% foreign ownership (since 2020) | Easy — all UAE banks | Residence visa included | AED 15K–30K ($5K–$10K USD) |
| **Free Zone LLC** | 0% on qualifying income (must be Free Zone-sourced or foreign-sourced) | 100% foreign ownership | Harder — many banks won't open for FZ companies | Residence visa included | AED 10K–50K depending on Free Zone |
| **Freelancer Permit** | 0% personal income tax | Individual | Personal banking (Emirates NBD, Mashreq) | Residence visa included | AED 7K–15K ($2K–$5K USD) |

### The Real Costs (Annual)

Dubai structures have significant annual costs that the promoters downplay:

| Item | Annual Cost (USD) |
|------|------------------|
| Trade license renewal | $2,000–$8,000 |
| Office/desk rental (required for visa) | $3,000–$15,000 |
| Health insurance (mandatory) | $1,500–$3,000 |
| Visa renewal | $500–$1,000 |
| Accounting/audit | $1,500–$3,000 |
| **Total minimum** | **$8,500–$30,000/year** |

### CRA Treatment (The Dealbreaker for Non-Movers)

- **If CC remains a Canadian tax resident:** All worldwide income is taxable in Canada regardless of where a company is incorporated. A Dubai entity changes nothing for Canadian tax purposes — CC still pays Canadian tax on all income. The only benefit would be if the Dubai entity earned active business income that qualified for exempt surplus (requiring real substance in Dubai).
- **If CC becomes a UAE tax resident:** Must sever Canadian tax residency (departure tax on deemed disposition of all assets — see `docs/ATLAS_TREATY_FIRE_STRATEGY.md`). Canada-UAE tax treaty has a tiebreaker. Must establish "centre of vital interests" in UAE — not just a visa. CRA has challenged people who spend 6 months in UAE and 6 months in Canada.
- **Substance requirements:** UAE now has Economic Substance Regulations (ESR). Companies must demonstrate adequate employees, expenditures, and decision-making in the UAE.

### When to Use

- CC physically relocates to Dubai and severs Canadian residency.
- Running a business primarily serving Middle East / Africa clients.
- Crypto trading from a jurisdiction with no capital gains tax (only works if non-resident of Canada).

### Verdict for CC

- **Not recommended from Canada.** Zero tax benefit while remaining Canadian tax resident. High annual costs.
- **Future FIRE play:** If CC achieves financial independence and wants to relocate to a 0% tax jurisdiction, Dubai is one of the strongest options. But it requires actually living there.

## 2.7 Hong Kong Limited Company

### Why Hong Kong

- Territorial tax system: only income sourced in Hong Kong is taxable.
- Profits tax: 8.25% on first HKD 2M, 16.5% above. But **offshore income is 0%.**
- No capital gains tax, no VAT/GST, no withholding tax on dividends.
- Simple formation, English-language legal system (common law).

### The Offshore Claim

- Before 2023: companies could easily claim "offshore profits" and pay 0%.
- Since January 2023: new foreign-sourced income exemption (FSIE) regime. Passive income (dividends, interest, IP royalties, disposal gains) received by Hong Kong entities is now taxable UNLESS the company meets economic substance requirements in HK.
- Active business income from foreign clients remains exempt if services are not performed in HK and contracts are negotiated/concluded outside HK.

### Banking Challenge

- Hong Kong banks (HSBC, Hang Seng, Bank of China) have extremely strict KYC for non-resident-owned companies.
- In-person visit typically required for account opening.
- Rejection rates are high for companies with no HK business nexus.

### CRA Treatment

- Same CFA/FAPI analysis as all foreign entities.
- If CC controls the HK company from Canada: central management and control argument applies.
- No Canada-HK tax treaty (Hong Kong is not a separate treaty partner — it's part of China, and the Canada-China treaty has specific HK provisions that are limited).

### Verdict for CC

- **Not recommended.** Banking is too difficult for remote-only operations, and the FSIE changes have closed the easiest offshore planning. No treaty protection.

## 2.8 Labuan (Malaysia) — International Trading Company

### What It Is

- Labuan is a federal territory of Malaysia with its own business and tax framework (Labuan Business Activity Tax Act, LBATA).
- Labuan entities can elect to pay either:
  - 3% of net audited profits (for trading activities including financial trading), OR
  - Flat MYR 20,000 (~USD 4,300) per year.

### How It Works

- Incorporate a Labuan company (minimum 1 director, 1 shareholder, no residency requirement).
- Must carry on business in/from/through Labuan in a "Labuan business activity" (broadly defined).
- Must have adequate employees and annual operating expenditure in Labuan (substance test — minimum 2 full-time employees in Labuan since 2019 amendment).

### Relevance to CC

- The 3% tax on trading profits is extremely attractive for an algorithmic trading operation.
- **BUT:** CRA will apply FAPI rules if CC controls the entity from Canada. Trading income earned by a CFA where investment decisions are made by a Canadian resident is textbook FAPI — taxed immediately in Canada at full rates.
- The Labuan structure only works if:
  - CC relocates to Malaysia (or another non-Canadian jurisdiction).
  - The trading decisions are genuinely made by employees in Labuan.
  - Neither condition is realistic for CC right now.

### Verdict for CC

- **Attractive on paper, unusable from Canada.** FAPI destroys the benefit.
- **Future play:** If CC builds an autonomous trading AI that genuinely operates from Labuan servers with Labuan-based oversight... still risky. CRA would argue the AI was programmed and controlled from Canada.

## 2.9 Georgia (Country) — Micro-Business and Virtual Zone

### Two Interesting Regimes

**1. Micro-Business Status:**
- Available to individuals with annual revenue < GEL 500K (~USD 190K).
- Tax rate: **1% of gross revenue.** No income tax, no VAT.
- Cannot have employees (subcontractors OK).
- Only available to Georgian tax residents.

**2. Virtual Zone Entity (VZE):**
- For IT companies that create software, develop digital products.
- Corporate tax: **0% on income from IT services supplied outside Georgia.**
- Must be a Georgian-registered LLC.
- Must primarily provide IT/digital services.
- Application process through the Ministry of Finance.

### The Catch

- Must be a Georgian tax resident (for micro-business) or a Georgian entity with substance (for VZE).
- Georgia has no tax treaty with Canada (as of 2026). This means:
  - No treaty protection against double taxation.
  - Canadian withholding tax on payments from Canada to Georgia at full statutory rates (25%).
  - CRA can tax CC on worldwide income regardless.
- **For CC:** Only works if CC physically relocates to Georgia and severs Canadian residency. The 1% rate is real but requires genuine residency.

### Verdict for CC

- **Digital nomad play.** If CC ever goes nomad, Georgia's 1% micro-business tax is one of the lowest in the world for a solo consultant.
- **Not usable from Canada.** No treaty, FAPI rules, CRA worldwide taxation.

# SECTION 3: Multi-Entity Architectures

## 3.1 Canadian OpCo + US SubCo

The most common structure for Canadian tech companies expanding to the US.

```
CC (Individual)
  └── OASIS Canada Inc. (CCPC — Canadian OpCo)
        └── OASIS US Inc. (Delaware C-Corp — US SubCo)
```

### How It Works

- Canadian OpCo is the parent. Holds 100% of US SubCo shares.
- US SubCo invoices US clients, holds US contracts, may hold US IP.
- Profits in US SubCo taxed at 21% US federal corporate rate.
- Dividends from US SubCo to Canadian OpCo: 5% US withholding tax (Canada-US treaty, Article X — direct ownership >10%).
- In Canada: dividends from US SubCo are foreign affiliate dividends. If US SubCo earns active business income, the dividends come from "exempt surplus" — included in Canadian OpCo's income but offset by a deduction, resulting in minimal additional Canadian tax.

### Tax Efficiency

| Step | Tax | Cumulative |
|------|-----|-----------|
| US SubCo earns $100 USD | 21% US corporate tax | $79 after-tax |
| Dividend to Canadian OpCo | 5% withholding on $79 = $3.95 | $75.05 received |
| Canadian OpCo — exempt surplus deduction | ~0% additional Canadian tax on active business dividends | $75.05 retained |
| Distribute to CC as eligible dividend | ~39% personal rate on eligible dividends (Ontario) | ~$45.78 in CC's pocket |

**Compare to earning directly in Canadian OpCo:**
- $100 at 12.2% SBD = $87.80 retained.
- Distribute as non-eligible dividend: ~47% personal rate = ~$46.53 in CC's pocket.
- **Result:** Roughly similar after full integration. The US SubCo is not a tax play — it's a market access and liability play.

### When to Use

- US revenue > $100K USD/year.
- US clients requiring a US entity on contracts (government, enterprise).
- Holding US IP separately from Canadian operations.
- Eventual US exit/acquisition.

## 3.2 Canadian HoldCo + International SubCos

For a global business with operations in multiple countries.

```
CC (Individual)
  └── OASIS HoldCo Inc. (CCPC — pure holding company)
        ├── OASIS Canada OpCo Inc. (CCPC — Canadian operations)
        ├── OASIS US Inc. (Delaware C-Corp — US operations)
        ├── OASIS UK Ltd (UK operations)
        └── OASIS SG Pte. Ltd. (APAC operations)
```

### Why a Separate HoldCo

- **Liability isolation:** If Canadian OpCo gets sued, the assets in HoldCo (and other SubCos) are protected.
- **LCGE multiplication:** Each of CC's family members could hold shares in HoldCo and potentially each claim their own LCGE (~$1.25M each) on a sale. Requires careful planning with a tax lawyer (family trust or direct shareholding, 24-month holding period, QSBC status).
- **Creditor protection:** HoldCo's assets (investments, retained earnings, shares of SubCos) are insulated from operational liabilities.
- **Flexibility:** Can sell any one SubCo independently without restructuring.

### When to Use

- Multiple lines of business with different risk profiles.
- Significant retained earnings that need protection.
- Planning for eventual sale of the business.
- Multiple family members involved in estate/tax planning.

### Cost

- Each additional corporation adds ~$2,000–$5,000/year in accounting/legal compliance.
- Only justified when total revenue exceeds ~$300K–$500K CAD.
- Associated corporation rules: all CCPCs associated with CC share the $500K SBD limit. Two corps don't mean two SBD limits.

## 3.3 IP Licensing Structures

How tech companies use IP to allocate profits across jurisdictions.

### The Basic Structure

```
CC (Individual)
  └── OASIS HoldCo (CCPC)
        ├── OASIS Canada OpCo (develops AI products)
        └── OASIS IP Co (holds IP, licenses it back to OpCo and foreign SubCos)
              └── Licenses IP to:
                    ├── OASIS Canada OpCo (royalty fee)
                    ├── OASIS US Inc. (royalty fee)
                    └── OASIS UK Ltd (royalty fee)
```

### How It Works

- IP Co owns the intellectual property (software, algorithms, brand).
- OpCos in each country license the IP and pay royalties to IP Co.
- Royalty payments are tax-deductible for the OpCos.
- IP Co receives royalty income — taxed where IP Co is resident.

### Why Companies Do This

- If IP Co is in a low-tax jurisdiction (Ireland at 6.25% KDB rate, Singapore with IP incentives, etc.), the royalty income is taxed at a low rate.
- The OpCos deduct the royalties, reducing their taxable income.
- Net effect: profits shift from high-tax OpCos to low-tax IP Co.

### Why CC Should Be Cautious

- **Transfer pricing:** Royalty rates must be "arm's length" — what an unrelated party would pay. CRA (and other tax authorities) actively audit intercompany royalty rates.
- **FAPI:** If IP Co is a CFA and doesn't have real substance, royalty income is FAPI — taxed in Canada immediately. Destroys the benefit.
- **Withholding taxes:** Royalty payments across borders attract withholding taxes (reduced by treaties, but rarely zero).
- **Economic substance:** Post-BEPS, the jurisdiction where IP Co is resident will require proof that IP is genuinely developed, enhanced, maintained, protected, and exploited (DEMPE functions) there.
- **Cost:** Transfer pricing documentation requirements add $10K–$50K/year in advisor fees for multi-jurisdictional structures.

### What Actually Works for CC's Scale

At CC's current revenue level, IP licensing structures are overkill. The break-even point is roughly $1M+ in global revenue. Below that, the compliance costs exceed the tax savings.

**Practical approach:**
- Keep IP in the Canadian OpCo.
- Use direct invoicing from Canada to foreign clients.
- Claim SR&ED credits on R&D expenditures in Canada (up to 35% refundable for CCPCs).
- Revisit IP licensing when revenue hits $1M+ and there's a genuine multi-country operation.

## 3.4 Transfer Pricing Documentation Requirements

Transfer pricing applies whenever CC's Canadian entity transacts with a related foreign entity.

| Jurisdiction | Documentation Requirement | Penalty for Non-Compliance |
|-------------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Canada** | Form T106 (annual). Contemporaneous documentation required if transactions with related non-residents. | 10% of transfer pricing adjustment (minimum $500) |
| **US** | Form 5471 (CFC), Form 1120-F (if US filing). Documentation under IRC s. 6662(e). | 20%–40% accuracy penalty on underpayment |
| **UK** | Country-by-Country Reporting (CbCR) for groups > GBP 750M. Local file for all related-party transactions. | Tax-geared penalties |
| **Singapore** | Transfer pricing documentation required if group revenue > SGD 10M or related-party transactions > SGD 15M. | Surcharge of 5% on TP adjustment |
| **OECD Standard** | Three-tiered: Master File, Local File, CbCR. Most countries have adopted this since BEPS Action 13. | Varies by country |

### Practical Advice for CC

- At current scale: file T106 in Canada if transacting with a foreign related entity. Keep invoices and rationale for pricing.
- Arm's length pricing: comparable uncontrolled price method (CUP) is simplest — charge what you'd charge an unrelated party.
- Document the rationale NOW, even if informal. CRA can go back 7 years on transfer pricing adjustments (no time limit if fraud or neglect).

## 3.5 The "Sandwich" Structures — What's Dead, What's Evolved

### Double Irish with a Dutch Sandwich (DEAD)

**How it worked (pre-2020):**
- Irish OpCo (tax resident in Ireland, 12.5% rate) earned revenue from EU clients.
- Paid royalties to Dutch BV (Netherlands — 0% withholding on outbound royalties under Dutch domestic law).
- Dutch BV paid royalties to Irish HoldCo (incorporated in Ireland but tax resident in Bermuda — old Irish rule allowed companies managed and controlled outside Ireland to be non-resident).
- Bermuda: 0% tax.
- Net effect: profits ended up in a 0% jurisdiction.

**Why it died:**
- Ireland closed the "stateless company" loophole in 2015 (fully phased in by January 2020). All Irish-incorporated companies are now Irish tax residents.
- EU Anti-Tax Avoidance Directives (ATAD I & II) introduced CFC rules, anti-hybrid rules, and interest limitation rules across all EU member states.
- BEPS Action 5: nexus approach for IP regimes — must have real R&D activity to get preferential IP rates.

### What's Evolved

**1. IP Box / Knowledge Development Box (KDB) Regimes:**
- Ireland: 6.25% on qualifying IP income (must have real R&D in Ireland).
- Netherlands: 9% Innovation Box rate.
- UK: 10% Patent Box rate.
- These are legitimate — but require real substance (employees doing R&D in the jurisdiction).

**2. Principal Structure:**
- One entity (the "principal") bears the entrepreneurial risk and owns the IP.
- Other entities act as limited-risk distributors or contract manufacturers.
- The principal earns the residual profit.
- Must have genuine decision-making substance where the principal is located.

**3. OECD Pillar Two (Global Minimum Tax):**
- Effective from 2024 in many jurisdictions (Canada implementing).
- 15% global minimum tax on large multinationals (revenue > EUR 750M).
- Does NOT affect CC at current scale. But it signals the direction: the era of sub-15% effective rates for large companies is ending.

## 3.6 Substance Requirements by Jurisdiction (Post-BEPS)

The single most important concept in international tax planning since 2018.

| Jurisdiction | Substance Test | What You Need |
|-------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Canada** | Central management and control (common law), plus CFA/FAPI rules for outbound structures | Directors meetings in Canada, key decisions made here |
| **Ireland** | Must be managed and controlled in Ireland if incorporated there | Board meetings, key management in Ireland |
| **Netherlands** | Genuine economic activity (employees, office, decision-making) | Dutch employees performing core functions |
| **Singapore** | Central management and control + economic substance for certain activities | Singapore-resident directors, employees, expenditure |
| **UAE** | Economic Substance Regulations (ESR) for relevant activities | UAE employees, expenditure, core income-generating activity in UAE |
| **Hong Kong** | FSIE regime requires adequate economic substance for passive income exemption | HK employees, expenditure, decision-making for FSIE |
| **Cayman Islands** | Economic Substance Law (2019) | Adequate employees, expenditure, premises, CIGA in Cayman |
| **BVI** | Economic Substance Act (2019) | Adequate employees, expenditure, CIGA in BVI |

### The Key Takeaway

**Letterbox companies are dead.** Every major jurisdiction now requires some form of economic substance. A company with a registered agent address, a nominee director, and no employees/activity will not provide tax benefits. It will only provide compliance costs and audit risk.

For CC: any international structure must have REAL operations in the target jurisdiction — employees, customers, or genuine business activity. Otherwise, CRA's FAPI rules ensure Canadian taxation regardless.

## 4.4 FX Optimization

### When to Convert

The single biggest FX mistake: converting foreign currency immediately upon receipt.

**Better approach:**
1. **Hold in foreign currency** until you need CAD for expenses.
2. **Batch conversions** — convert once per month or quarter, not per invoice.
3. **Rate alerts** — set rate alerts on Wise (e.g., convert when USD/CAD hits 1.38+).
4. **Natural hedging** — if you have USD income AND USD expenses (e.g., US servers, US contractors), keep USD and pay USD expenses from USD balance. Zero FX conversion needed.

### Forward Contracts

- Lock in an exchange rate for future settlement (e.g., lock USD/CAD at 1.37 for delivery in 90 days).
- Available through: bank FX desks (RBC, TD), specialized FX brokers (AFEX, Cambridge Global, Moneycorp).
- **Minimum amounts:** Typically $10K+ per contract.
- **When to use:** When you have a known future CAD obligation (e.g., quarterly tax payment) and want to eliminate FX risk.
- **Not recommended for CC yet:** Revenue is too variable to predict future FX needs. Natural hedging + Wise rate alerts are sufficient.

### FX Cost Comparison

| Provider | Typical Spread | Wire Fee | Best For |
|----------|---------------|----------|----------|
| **Wise** | 0.35–0.65% | $0–$5 | Most conversions < $50K |
| **RBC / TD** | 2.0–3.0% | $0–$25 | Convenience (same bank) |
| **AFEX** | 0.1–0.3% | $0–$15 | Large conversions > $50K |
| **Interactive Brokers** | 0.002% (interbank) | $2 | Lowest rate, but need IBKR account |

**Recommendation for CC:**
- Wise for all FX < $50K. The spread is transparent and competitive.
- If FX volume grows past $50K/quarter, look at IBKR IdealPro (interbank rates, effectively zero spread) or a dedicated FX broker.
- NEVER convert FX through RBC/TD — you're paying 2–3% for convenience you don't need.

# Key Principles

1. **Substance over form.** Every international structure requires real economic activity in the jurisdiction. Letterbox companies do not work post-BEPS.

2. **CRA sees everything.** Canada taxes worldwide income of its residents. No foreign entity changes this unless CC ceases to be a Canadian tax resident.

3. **FAPI is the gatekeeper.** Any income earned by a foreign entity CC controls — if it's passive, or from services performed by CC — is taxed in Canada immediately. The foreign low rate is irrelevant.

4. **Compliance costs are real.** Each additional jurisdiction adds $3K–$10K/year in legal/accounting fees. Only justified by real revenue from that jurisdiction.

5. **Incorporate domestically first.** The Canadian CCPC at 12.2% SBD rate is one of the best small business tax rates in the developed world. Use it.

6. **International expansion follows revenue, not the other way around.** Do not create entities in anticipation of revenue. Create them when the revenue justifies the compliance cost.
