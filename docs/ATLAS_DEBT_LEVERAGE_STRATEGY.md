## 1. Interest Deductibility — s.20(1)(c) ITA

**Applicability: [NOW] | Risk: [RISK: LOW] (when rules followed precisely)**

### The Supreme Rule

**ITA s.20(1)(c):** Interest paid on money borrowed for the purpose of earning income from a business or property is **fully deductible** as a business or investment expense.

This is the single most powerful debt tool available to Canadian taxpayers. Properly structured, every dollar of interest on borrowed investment capital reduces your taxable income dollar-for-dollar.

### The Four Requirements

All four must be met simultaneously (ITA s.20(1)(c)(i)):

```
1. Interest must actually be paid or payable in the taxation year.
2. The borrowed money must be used for the purpose of earning income
   from a business or property. (NOT for earning capital gains from
   non-income-producing assets — this is a critical distinction.)
3. The interest rate must be reasonable (arm's length rate).
4. There must be a legal obligation to pay the interest.
```

### "Income from Property" — What Qualifies

| Investment | Deductible? | Rationale |
|------------|-------------|-----------|
| Dividend-paying stocks | YES | Dividends = income from property |
| REITs | YES | Distributions = income from property |
| Bonds / GICs | YES | Interest = income from property |
| Rental property | YES | Rental income = income from property |
| Business investment | YES | Business income = income from business |
| Non-dividend growth stocks | CONTESTED | No income stream — CRA may deny |
| Crypto (trading as business) | YES | Business income treatment |
| Crypto (capital gains treatment) | NO | Capital gains are not income from property |
| RRSP/TFSA investments | NO | Registered accounts — no direct income earned by borrower |

**Critical note on growth stocks:** CRA's administrative position (IT-533) is that a reasonable expectation of income (including dividends) is sufficient, even if no dividends have yet been declared. The Supreme Court in *Ludco Enterprises v. Canada* (2001 SCC 62) held that a non-dominant purpose of earning income is sufficient for deductibility — you do not need income-earning as your primary motive, only an expectation of income.

### Key Case Law

**Singleton v. Canada (SCC 2001, 2 SCR 1046) — THE LANDMARK CASE**

> The Supreme Court confirmed that interest deductibility follows the **direct use** of borrowed funds, not the "economic reality" of a transaction. A lawyer withdrew equity from his law firm, used it to buy a house, then borrowed an equal amount and injected it back into the firm. CRA argued the "true" use was buying the house. The SCC rejected this and held: trace the borrowed money directly to its use. The borrowed money went into the business — deductible.

**Practical implication for CC:** If you borrow money and invest it directly in income-producing assets, the interest is deductible regardless of what you did with other money before or after. The "direct tracing" principle means structure matters enormously. Keep borrowed funds and personal funds in separate accounts.

**Ludco Enterprises v. Canada (SCC 2001, 2 SCR 1082) — THE PURPOSE TEST**

> CRA argued that borrowing to invest in offshore companies with minimal dividends (primary goal = capital gains) was not deductible. The SCC held that as long as there is a **bona fide, non-incidental purpose of earning income** from the investment, deductibility stands. The taxpayer does not need to prove income was the dominant purpose.

**Shell Canada Ltd v. Canada (SCC 1999, 3 SCR 622) — FORM OVER SUBSTANCE**

> The SCC confirmed that CRA must respect the legal form of transactions, not the economic substance. Taxpayers are entitled to arrange their affairs to minimize tax. This case established that complex but legitimate financing structures (Shell borrowed in NZ dollars at a higher rate, swapped to US dollars at a lower rate) are respected if they have legal validity.

**Lipson v. Canada (SCC 2009, 1 SCR 3) — GAAR LIMITS**

> A warning: the SCC upheld CRA's application of the **General Anti-Avoidance Rule (GAAR, ITA s.245)** when a couple structured transactions specifically to transfer the Smith Manoeuvre benefit to a spouse with higher income. The transactions were technically valid but the series was found to be an avoidance arrangement abusing the attribution rules (ITA s.74.1). **Lesson:** the strategy must have economic substance beyond tax reduction. Lipson does NOT overturn Singleton — it applies GAAR to the specific attribution-shifting manipulation, not to the Smith Manoeuvre itself when used by the property owner.

### Tracing and Commingling — The Critical Execution Rule

**[RISK: MEDIUM]** if borrowed and personal funds are commingled.

If you borrow $10,000 and deposit it into an account that already has $5,000 of personal cash, then invest $10,000 from that account — which $10,000 was invested? CRA will apportion. Keep borrowed investment funds in a **dedicated, separate account** with a clear paper trail:

```
Step 1: Draw credit line / margin loan → dedicated investment account (empty, no personal cash)
Step 2: Invest directly from that account
Step 3: Document the transaction: date, amount, account, investment purchased
Step 4: Report interest paid on line 22100 (carrying charges) or T2125 if business income
```

**Where to report:**
- **Investment income (capital gains treatment):** Line 22100 — "Carrying charges and interest expenses"
- **Business income treatment (active trader):** T2125, Part 2 — "Business expenses"
- **Rental property:** Form T776, "Interest and bank charges"

## 2. Smith Manoeuvre Deep Dive

**Applicability: [FUTURE: requires home ownership + readvanceable mortgage] | Risk: [RISK: MEDIUM]**

### The Core Concept

The Smith Manoeuvre (coined by Fraser Smith, "Is Your Mortgage Tax-Deductible?") converts **non-deductible mortgage interest** into **deductible investment loan interest** over time. In Canada, mortgage interest on a personal residence is NOT deductible (unlike the US). The Smith Manoeuvre works around this structural asymmetry.

**The mathematical advantage:**

| Scenario | $300,000 Mortgage @ 5%, 25-year amortization |
|----------|----------------------------------------------|
| Standard mortgage (no SM) | Total interest paid: ~$214,000. None deductible. |
| Smith Manoeuvre (ongoing) | Interest paid on reborrowed HELOC: FULLY deductible. At 40% marginal rate: saves ~$85,600 over the life |
| Net after-tax interest cost reduction | ~$85,600 over 25 years = ~$3,400/year average |

### Required Setup

1. **Readvanceable mortgage** — A mortgage where as you pay down principal, that amount automatically becomes available on an attached HELOC. Products:
   - **Manulife One** (all-in-one account, highest flexibility)
   - **Scotia STEP** (Scotiabank Total Equity Plan)
   - **National Bank All-In-One**
   - **BMO Homeowner ReadiLine**
   - NOT a standard RBC or TD fixed mortgage — those are not readvanceable

2. **HELOC attached to mortgage** — The HELOC credit limit increases as your mortgage principal decreases.

3. **Separate investment account** — All HELOC draws go here, never commingled with personal cash.

### Step-by-Step Mechanics

```
Month 1:
  - Make regular mortgage payment: $300,000 @ 5% → payment = ~$1,754/month
  - Of that: ~$500 goes to principal, ~$1,254 goes to interest (early in amortization)
  - Your HELOC limit increases by $500 (principal paid down)

  → Draw $500 from HELOC into dedicated investment account
  → Invest the $500 in dividend-paying stocks (e.g., Royal Bank, BCE, Fortis)
  → HELOC interest on $500 @ ~7% = $2.92/month → DEDUCTIBLE (ITA s.20(1)(c))

Month 2:
  - Repeat. Principal portion slightly larger (~$502).
  - Draw another $502 from HELOC. Invest.
  - Total HELOC balance: $1,002. Total monthly deductible interest: ~$5.85

Year 1 (cumulative):
  - Approximately $6,000 principal paid → $6,000 drawn from HELOC
  - Annual HELOC interest: ~$420 → FULLY DEDUCTIBLE
  - At 40% marginal rate: $168 tax refund

Year 10 (cumulative):
  - ~$90,000 in HELOC, invested in dividend portfolio
  - Annual HELOC interest: ~$6,300 → tax saving ~$2,520/year
  - Portfolio has grown (assuming 7% return): ~$130,000

Year 25 (completion):
  - Mortgage paid off. Full original principal in HELOC (~$300,000).
  - Annual HELOC interest: ~$21,000 → tax saving ~$8,400/year
  - Portfolio value (7% return, dividends reinvested): ~$680,000
```

### Cash Flow Dam Variant `[RISK: MEDIUM]`

The Cash Flow Dam accelerates the Smith Manoeuvre by routing **all of CC's business income** through the HELOC first, then paying business expenses from HELOC draws.

**Mechanism:**
```
1. All OASIS AI income deposited → pays down HELOC balance immediately
2. HELOC is immediately reborrowed to pay all legitimate business expenses
3. The business expense portion of the HELOC is NOW DEDUCTIBLE (borrowed for business = s.20(1)(c))
4. Net effect: business income flows through, increasing the deductible HELOC faster
```

**CC applicability:** Requires home ownership first. When CC buys a home with a readvanceable mortgage, this is a Day 1 priority setup.

**GAAR risk (Lipson reminder):** The Cash Flow Dam is legitimate only if CC actually uses each redrawn HELOC amount for genuine business expenses. Do NOT fabricate expenses to artificially inflate the deductible portion. Every HELOC redraw must map to a real business expense with receipts.

### Acceleration with DRIPs

- Enroll dividend-paying stocks in **DRIPs (Dividend Reinvestment Plans)**
- Dividends are reinvested buying more shares automatically
- New shares earn more dividends — the compounding effect is substantial over 25 years
- The DRIP shares are NOT traced to the HELOC loan (they came from dividends) — only the original HELOC-funded purchases are deductible
- However, the compounding effect significantly improves long-term portfolio size

**Recommended DRIP stocks for SM portfolio:**
- Canadian banks (RY, TD, BNS, BMO) — 4-5% dividend yield, eligible dividends
- Utilities (FTS, EMA) — 4-6% dividend yield, stable and regulated
- BCE, T (telecoms) — 6-8% dividend yield (monitor payout sustainability)
- REITs (RioCan, Canadian Apartment REIT) — 4-8% distribution yield

## 3. OSAP / Student Loan Optimization

**Applicability: [NOW] — CC has ~$9K OSAP debt from Bishop's University | Risk: [RISK: LOW]**

### Interest Tax Credit — s.118.62 ITA

**ITA s.118.62:** A 15% **federal** non-refundable tax credit on interest paid on qualifying student loans (NSLSC loans, provincial equivalents; private bank student lines of credit do NOT qualify).

**What qualifies:**
- National Student Loans Service Centre (NSLSC) federal portion
- Ontario Student Assistance Program (OSAP) provincial portion
- Any provincial student loan equivalent

**What does NOT qualify:**
- Private student lines of credit (RBC, TD, Scotiabank) — no s.118.62 credit
- Interest on credit card balances used for tuition

**Credit calculation example:**
```
Student loan balance: $9,000 @ 6.2% interest
Annual interest paid: $558
Federal credit (15%): $558 × 15% = $83.70 federal tax reduction
Ontario credit: ELIMINATED in 2020 (Ontario Student Grant expansion replaced it)
Total annual credit: ~$84/year
```

**Ontario provincial equivalent:** Ontario's student loan interest credit was **eliminated** in 2020 as part of the Ontario Student Grant expansion. Only the federal 15% credit under s.118.62 remains.

**Carry forward:** Unused s.118.62 credits carry forward **5 years**. If CC's income is low in the year interest is paid, the unused credit can be applied against higher income in a future year (within the 5-year window).

### Repayment Assistance Program (RAP)

If OSAP repayment creates cash flow pressure, the **RAP** caps monthly payments:

| Gross Monthly Income | Maximum Monthly Payment |
|---------------------|------------------------|
| Under $2,083/month (~$25,000/year) | $0 — no payment required |
| $2,083 - $3,333/month | 5% of gross income |
| $3,333 - $5,000/month | 10% of gross income |
| Over $5,000/month | 20% of gross income, capped |

**CC note:** If OASIS income is lumpy (large months followed by slow months), RAP can reduce payments in lean months. Apply through NSLSC online portal. Reassess annually.

**RAP Stage 2 (forgiveness):** After 10 years on RAP, or 15 years of total loan life (whichever comes first), the government begins covering the principal shortfall. After a maximum of **15 years on RAP** (or a total loan age of **40 years**), the remaining balance is **forgiven entirely**. This is a legitimate strategy if debt is large relative to income.

### OSAP Grant vs Loan Split (Ontario)

OSAP provides a mix of grants (free) and loans (repayable). Ontario Student Grant (OSG):
- Grant does NOT need to be repaid — it is not taxable income (ITA s.56(1)(n) exempts bursaries up to $500 + cost of program; OSG is fully exempt as a government program)
- Only the loan portion generates s.118.62 interest

**Action for CC:** Verify through NSLSC whether the Bishop's OSAP was fully grant or partially loan. Request a loan statement showing original principal, interest accrued, and current balance.

### Optimal Repayment Strategy

Given the 15% federal credit, the **after-tax cost** of OSAP interest is: `Rate × (1 - 0.15)`.

| OSAP Rate | After-Tax Cost | Compare to |
|-----------|---------------|------------|
| Prime + 1% = ~6.2% | 5.27% | TFSA return (tax-free) |
| Prime + 0% = ~5.2% (fixed option) | 4.42% | Investment return needed to beat it |

**Conclusion for CC's $9K balance:** The balance is small enough that aggressive repayment is not mandatory. If CC can earn more than 5.27% inside a TFSA, invest the difference there. However, psychological and cash flow benefits of eliminating the debt in 12-18 months are also valid.

## 4. Margin Investing

**Applicability: [NOW] (small scale) / [FUTURE] (larger scale) | Risk: [RISK: MEDIUM-HIGH]**

### Margin Interest Deductibility

**ITA s.20(1)(c)** applies equally to margin loans from a broker. If CC borrows on margin to purchase dividend-paying stocks, the margin interest is **fully deductible** on line 22100 (carrying charges).

Requirements are identical to Section 1:
- Investments must be income-producing (dividends, or business income for active trading)
- Margin loan directly traces to the purchased securities
- Interest must be at a reasonable market rate

**Where to report:** Line 22100 — "Carrying charges and interest expenses" (carrying charges schedule on T1).

### T-Bill Yield vs Margin Rate Arbitrage

As of Q1 2026:

| Rate | Approximate Level |
|------|------------------|
| Bank of Canada Policy Rate | ~3.0% |
| Government of Canada 91-day T-bill | ~3.1% |
| Interactive Brokers CAD margin rate | ~6.0-6.5% |
| Canadian bank discount brokerage margin | ~7.5-9.0% |
| After-tax margin rate at 40% marginal | ~3.6-3.9% (IBKR) |

**Arbitrage logic:** When after-tax margin rate approaches or falls below the risk-free T-bill yield, margin is essentially free leverage. This window opens when the Bank of Canada cuts rates aggressively (margin rates lag the policy rate).

**Example — dividend carry trade:**
```
Margin balance: $10,000 @ 6.0% = $600 interest/year
Tax deduction at 40% marginal: saves $240
After-tax interest cost: $360 = 3.6%

Invested in dividend portfolio (RY, TD, FTS, BCE):
Dividend yield: ~5.0% = $500/year
After-tax dividend income at 40% marginal (with dividend tax credit): ~$400-$430

Net carry: $400 - $360 = ~$40 positive carry per year
Plus: any capital appreciation of underlying stocks
```

**Use Interactive Brokers**, not Canadian bank brokerages, for margin. IBKR's margin rate is roughly half of RBC Direct Investing or TD Direct Investing.

### Leveraged ETFs — HXU, HXD

- **HXU (BetaPro S&P 500 Bull 2x ETF)** — 2x daily leveraged long S&P 500
- **HXD (BetaPro S&P 500 Bear 2x ETF)** — 2x daily leveraged short S&P 500

**Tax treatment:** These use total return swaps (TRS) — no distributions are paid. All return accrues as **capital gains** upon sale, making them tax-efficient in non-registered accounts. Inside a TFSA, capital gains compound tax-free — optimal placement.

**If borrowing on margin to buy HXU:** Margin interest is deductible (HXU generates investment income through its swap structure).

**Volatility decay warning (beta slippage):** 2x daily leveraged ETFs suffer decay in choppy markets:
```
Example: $100 → up 10% → $120 → down 10% → $96 = -4% on a flat market
A non-leveraged ETF in the same scenario: $100 → $110 → $99 = -1%
```
HXU is appropriate for **confirmed trending markets only**. Atlas monitors regime — only use HXU when regime = BULL_TREND. Never hold through CHOPPY or HIGH_VOL regimes.

### Risk Management for Margin

- Never exceed **50% margin utilization** — broker can force-sell at the worst possible moment
- Maintain a cash buffer of at least **25% of margin balance**
- Margin call level: most Canadian brokers call at 140-150% of requirement
- Stress test: if portfolio drops 30%, can CC meet the margin call without selling at a loss?

## 5. Credit Optimization

**Applicability: [NOW] | Risk: [RISK: LOW]**

### Canadian Credit Score Mechanics (Equifax / TransUnion Canada)

Canadian credit scoring (Beacon Score / CreditVision) uses these factors:

| Factor | Weight | How to Optimize |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| Payment history | ~35% | Never miss a payment. Set autopay for minimum. |
| Credit utilization | ~30% | Keep below 30% per card; ideally 1-9% |
| Credit history length | ~15% | Keep oldest cards open even at $0 balance |
| Credit mix | ~10% | Mix of revolving (credit cards) + installment (LOC, car loan) |
| New credit inquiries | ~10% | Apply for new credit sparingly — hard inquiries drop score ~5-10 pts each |

**Target for business borrowing:** 720+
**Target for prime mortgage rates:** 740+ (can mean 0.15-0.40% better rate = $2,000-$8,000 saved on a $400K mortgage over 5 years)

### Optimal Utilization Strategy

- **Report date vs. billing date:** Credit bureaus receive utilization data at statement close, not payment date. Pay down balances **before** the billing cycle closes to show low utilization.
- **Per-card utilization matters:** Having $10,000 on a $10,000 limit card AND $0 on a $30,000 limit card = 25% overall but 100% on Card 1 = score penalty regardless of overall utilization.
- **Sweet spot:** 1-9% utilization per card for maximum score. $0 on all cards shows as no activity and is slightly penalized vs. 1-9%.

**Action for CC:**
```
Current OASIS business expenses (Claude API, hosting, subscriptions): ~$500-1,000/month
Put ALL on one business credit card → pay in full every month
Result: Shows positive payment history, low utilization, 1-3% cash back on expenses
        that are also fully deductible on T2125
```

### Authorized User Strategy

- Being added as an **authorized user** on a credit card with long history (parent, trusted family member) adds that card's history to your credit file at both Equifax and TransUnion Canada
- If the primary cardholder has a 10-year-old card with perfect history, that history partially transfers to CC's credit file
- **Risk:** If the primary cardholder misses payments, it damages CC's score. Use only with someone who has a flawless payment record.

**ITA deductibility:** Interest on a business LOC drawn for business expenses = fully deductible on T2125. Document every draw with a notation of the business purpose (e.g., "equipment purchase," "contractor invoice," "software subscription").

## 8. Debt Stacking vs Avalanche

**Applicability: [NOW] | Risk: [RISK: LOW]**

### The Two Methods

**Debt Avalanche (mathematical optimum):**
- List all debts by interest rate, highest to lowest
- Pay minimums on everything
- All extra cash goes to the highest-rate debt first
- Result: minimizes total interest paid and time to debt-free

**Debt Snowball (behavioral optimum):**
- List all debts by balance, smallest to largest
- Pay off smallest balance first regardless of rate
- Result: quick wins provide motivation to continue — research shows people actually finish the snowball more reliably than the avalanche

### Mathematical Comparison

**Example — CC's hypothetical debt stack:**

| Debt | Balance | Nominal Rate | After-Tax Rate | Minimum Payment |
|------|---------|-------------|---------------|----------------|
| Credit card | $1,000 | 19.99% | 19.99% (no deduction) | $25/month |
| Private student LOC (bank) | $5,000 | 9.0% | 9.0% (no s.118.62) | $75/month |
| OSAP | $9,000 | 6.2% | 5.27% (15% s.118.62 credit) | $120/month |

**Avalanche order by after-tax rate:** Credit card → Private student LOC → OSAP

```
Avalanche total interest paid: ~$3,200 over 4.5 years
Snowball total interest paid: ~$3,800 over 5.2 years
Difference: ~$600 saved and ~8 months faster
```

**The key insight for CC:** Because OSAP carries a 15% federal tax credit (s.118.62), its after-tax rate (5.27%) is LOWER than the private bank LOC (9%). The after-tax adjusted avalanche correctly prioritizes the private LOC over OSAP even though OSAP has the higher nominal balance.

### Hybrid Approach (Recommended for CC)

```
1. Eliminate any credit card balance immediately (19.99% is never justified)
2. Build $1,000 cash buffer (prevents new credit card debt in emergencies)
3. Apply avalanche using after-tax effective rates (see above)
4. Once all consumer debt is cleared, shift 100% of surplus to
   TFSA / FHSA / RRSP (registered account returns beat low-rate deductible debt)
```

**The decision matrix:**

| Debt After-Tax Rate | Recommended Action |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| > 10% | Pay aggressively — guaranteed 10%+ return, risk-free |
| 7-10% | Hybrid — split 50/50 between debt repayment and investing |
| 4-7% | Lean toward investing, especially in TFSA (returns tax-free) |
| < 4% | Invest — especially if the debt is already tax-deductible |

## 9. Leverage for Real Estate

**Applicability: [FUTURE: first home purchase] | Risk: [RISK: MEDIUM]**

### CMHC Mortgage Insurance

Required when down payment is less than 20%. Insured by CMHC, Sagen (formerly Genworth), or Canada Guaranty.

| Down Payment | CMHC Premium | On $400,000 Home | Added to Mortgage |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 5% ($20,000) | 4.00% | $15,200 | $395,200 |
| 10% ($40,000) | 3.10% | $11,160 | $371,160 |
| 15% ($60,000) | 2.80% | $9,520 | $349,520 |
| 20% ($80,000) | 0% | $0 | $320,000 |

**CC's first home strategy:**
- FHSA ($8K/year, $40K lifetime — tax-deductible contributions, tax-free withdrawals for qualifying first home)
- RRSP HBP (Home Buyers' Plan) — withdraw up to **$35,000** tax-free from RRSP for first home (repay over 15 years, or include in income)
- **Combined:** $40K FHSA + $35K RRSP HBP = $75K toward down payment, tax-advantaged
- At $75K down on $400K home = 18.75% — CMHC applies (3.1% = ~$9,500 premium)
- Saving an additional $5K to reach exactly 20% eliminates ~$9,500 in CMHC premiums — 100%+ return on that $5K in savings in year one

### CMHC MLI Select (Multi-Unit Residential) `[FUTURE: rental / house-hack]`

For **rental property or house-hacking (living in one unit, renting others)**:

| Tier | Max LTV | Insurance Premium | Key Requirement |
|------|---------|------------------|----------------|
| MLI Select — top tier | Up to 95% LTV | 3.30-4.00% | Energy efficiency + affordability + accessibility scoring |
| MLI Select — standard | Up to 85% LTV | 2.75-3.50% | MLI Select application, multi-unit |

**Why MLI Select matters:** Conventional rental property financing maxes at 80% LTV (20% minimum down). MLI Select allows 5-15% down on multi-unit properties if the property meets sustainability and affordability criteria. This is how sophisticated investors leverage 5:1 to 20:1 on income-generating properties.

### Mortgage Interest Deductibility (Rental Property)

**ITA s.20(1)(c):** Mortgage interest on a **rental property** is **fully deductible** against rental income on Form T776.

- Mortgage principal payments: NOT deductible (capital repayment, not interest)
- Mortgage interest: FULLY deductible
- CMHC insurance premium: deductible over the mortgage term (amortize, or deduct in year paid — CRA allows both; amortization is more conservative)

**Example:**
```
Rental property: $400,000 @ 5% interest rate
Year 1 interest portion: ~$17,500
At 40% marginal rate: saves $7,000 in tax
Effective mortgage cost after deduction: ~$10,500 on $400K property
Effective after-tax rate: 2.625%
```

vs. principal residence: same mortgage, zero deduction.

### Multi-Property Portfolio Financing `[FUTURE: 3+ properties]`

- Each new rental property up to 80% LTV (conventional) or 85-95% (MLI Select)
- After **4 CMHC-insured properties**, financing must shift to conventional — banks apply more stringent debt service coverage ratio (DSCR) tests
- **DSCR requirement:** Rental income / Total debt service >= 1.1-1.25x (property must cash-flow at minimum)
- **Rental offset:** Most major banks apply 50-80% of gross rental income to offset mortgage qualification
- Use a mortgage broker (not one bank) for portfolio financing — they access multiple lenders and find the highest rental offset and best DSCR treatment

## 10. Corporate Debt Strategies

**Applicability: [FUTURE: post-incorporation at $80K+ revenue] | Risk: [RISK: MEDIUM-HIGH]**

### Shareholder Loans — ITA s.15 Traps

When CC incorporates OASIS AI Solutions Inc., the corporation and CC become separate legal entities. Money moving between them is governed by **ITA s.15**.

**s.15(1) — Shareholder benefit:** Any amount conferred on a shareholder that is not salary, dividend, or loan repayment = **taxable benefit** included in CC's personal income for that year. Common traps:
- Corporation pays CC's personal expenses (car, home office) without formal documentation
- Corporation buys personal-use assets and lets CC use them

**s.15(2) — Shareholder loans:** If the corporation makes a loan to CC (as shareholder), the **full loan amount is included in CC's personal income** in the year received — UNLESS one of these exceptions applies:

| Exception | Condition |
|-----------|-----------|
| Employee loan for home purchase | CC is an employee of the corp; loan used to buy a home; bona fide arrangement |
| Employee loan for vehicle | Loan used to purchase a vehicle for employment duties |
| Full repayment within 1 year | Loan repaid before the end of the corporation's fiscal year following the year the loan was received |

**The 1-year rule — commonly misunderstood:** If CC takes a shareholder loan in January 2027 and the corporate fiscal year end is December 31, 2027, the loan must be repaid by **December 31, 2028** (the NEXT fiscal year end after the year received). This is NOT "12 months from the date of borrowing." The window can be as long as 23 months depending on when in the year the loan is taken.

**ITA s.80.4 — Imputed interest benefit:** If a shareholder loan is outstanding and the interest charged is below the CRA prescribed rate (currently 4-5%), the difference is a **taxable benefit** to CC. To avoid this, always charge the prescribed rate on all shareholder loans and document it in a formal loan agreement.

### Corporate LOC vs Personal LOC (Post-Incorporation)

| Feature | Corporate LOC | Personal LOC |
|---------|--------------|--------------|
| Rate | Prime + 2-4% (based on corporate credit) | Prime + 2-6% (personal credit dependent) |
| Personal credit impact | Usually none (if no personal guarantee required) | YES — shows on personal bureau |
| Interest deductibility | Corporate expense (reduces 12.2% SBD taxable income) | Only if used for investment/business |
| Credit limit | Based on corporate revenue and assets | Based on personal income and assets |
| Availability | After 2+ years incorporated with financials | Available immediately (personal credit) |

### Thin Capitalization Rules — ITA s.18(4)

**Applicability: FUTURE (relevant when CC has non-resident investors or foreign debt)**

**ITA s.18(4):** Limits interest deductibility when a Canadian corporation has excessive debt owed to "specified non-residents" (non-resident shareholders owning 25%+).

**The 1.5:1 ratio:** Interest is only deductible on debt up to **1.5 times the equity** (paid-up capital + retained earnings + contributed surplus) of the corporation. Interest on debt above this ratio is denied.

**Example:**
```
OASIS AI Solutions Inc. equity: $100,000
Maximum deductible non-resident debt: $150,000 (1.5 × $100,000)
If non-resident investor provides $200,000 in debt:
  - $150,000 → interest deductible
  - $50,000 → interest NOT deductible (excess over 1.5:1 ratio)
```

**CC applicability:** Does not apply until OASIS incorporates AND takes on debt from non-resident investors or shareholders. Keep this in mind when seeking US or foreign investors or structuring cross-border licensing deals.

### Back-to-Back Loan Rules — ITA s.18(6)

**ITA s.18(6)-(6.1):** Prevents Canadian companies from using intermediary entities to circumvent the thin capitalization rules. If a third party lends to CC's corporation, but that third party was funded by a non-resident who owns 25%+, the rules treat the original non-resident as the direct lender.

**Practical implication:** When CC incorporates and structures cross-border deals (e.g., a US angel investor provides a convertible note), get tax counsel before signing the loan agreement. Structuring the investment as equity (shares with a shareholders' agreement) rather than debt avoids s.18(4)-(6) entirely and is often cleaner for both parties.

### Prescribed Rate Loans — Income Splitting `[FUTURE: when CC has a spouse or common-law partner]`

**ITA s.74.1-74.5:** Attribution rules force investment income earned by a spouse back onto the higher-earning spouse. **Exception: prescribed rate loans (ITA s.74.5(1)(b)).**

A **prescribed rate loan** allows CC to lend money to a spouse/partner at the current CRA prescribed rate. The rate is **locked in at inception** (historically as low as 1-2% when BoC rates are low).

```
CC lends $50,000 to partner at prescribed rate of 2% (locked in when rate is low)
Partner invests in dividend stocks earning 5%
Partner's investment income: $2,500/year
Less: interest paid back to CC: $1,000/year
Partner's net taxable income from portfolio: $1,500

Tax saving:
  $1,500 taxed at partner's 20% marginal rate = $300 tax
  vs. $1,500 taxed at CC's 40% marginal rate = $600 tax
  Annual saving: $300/year
  Over 20 years with compounding principal: significant long-term saving
```

**Formalities required to avoid attribution:**
1. Written loan agreement (signed, dated, specific terms)
2. Interest must be paid annually, no later than January 30 of the following year
3. Rate must be at or above the prescribed rate **at the time the loan is made** (rate is locked for the life of the loan — the incentive is to establish these loans when the prescribed rate is at a cyclical low)
