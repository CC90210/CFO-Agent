## 1. The $30,000 Small Supplier Threshold

### 1.1 The Legal Definition (ETA s.148)

Under **ETA s.148(1)**, a person is a "small supplier" — and therefore NOT required to register for
GST/HST — if their **total worldwide taxable supplies** in the previous four consecutive calendar
quarters do not exceed **$30,000**, AND their taxable supplies in any single calendar quarter do not
exceed $30,000.

This is **$30,000**, not $40,000. The $40,000 figure is a persistent misconception in accounting
circles — it does not exist in the ETA. The $30,000 threshold has been in place since 1990.

**The two-part test (both must be satisfied to remain a small supplier):**

| Test | Rule | Authority |
|------|------|-----------|
| Rolling four-quarter test | Taxable supplies in any consecutive 4 calendar quarters ≤ $30,000 | ETA s.148(1)(a) |
| Single-quarter test | Taxable supplies in any single calendar quarter ≤ $30,000 | ETA s.148(1)(b) |

Fail either test and you must register. Fail the single-quarter test and you must register **by the
end of the month following the quarter in which you exceeded $30,000** (ETA s.240(1.1)).

### 1.2 The Day-of-Supply Rule — The Most Misunderstood Trigger

**ETA s.240(1):** Once your taxable supplies exceed $30,000, you become a registrant effective **the
day the supply that caused you to exceed the threshold was made** — not the end of the quarter, not
the next month. You must collect and remit HST on that supply and all subsequent supplies.

**Example:**
- CC has $28,000 in OASIS revenue through October
- November 15: CC invoices a client for $3,500 — pushing total to $31,500
- CC must charge HST on the $3,500 November 15 invoice itself
- Every invoice from November 15 onward must include HST
- CC has until December 15 (30 days after the supply) to register — but the obligation to collect
  HST begins on November 15 regardless

**Practical risk:** If CC crosses the threshold without realizing it and issues invoices without HST,
CC is personally liable for the unremitted HST on those invoices. CRA can and does assess the
registrant for HST that should have been collected, whether or not the client paid it. CRA guidance:
GST/HST Memorandum 2.2.

### 1.3 The Four-Quarter Window is a Rolling Test

The four quarters are the **last four consecutive calendar quarters** — not the calendar year. This
means the window shifts every quarter.

| Quarter End | Window Covers | Check |
|-------------|--------------|-------|
| March 31, 2026 | Apr 1, 2025 → Mar 31, 2026 | ≤ $30,000? |
| June 30, 2026 | Jul 1, 2025 → Jun 30, 2026 | ≤ $30,000? |
| September 30, 2026 | Oct 1, 2025 → Sep 30, 2026 | ≤ $30,000? |
| December 31, 2026 | Jan 1, 2026 → Dec 31, 2026 | ≤ $30,000? |

A business with seasonal revenue spikes — like a DJ with heavy summer bookings — can breach the
threshold in Q3 and remain unregistered for years if they don't run this rolling calculation.

### 2.1 Taxable Supplies — INCLUDED in the $30,000 Calculation

activity. The following income streams count:

| Income Source | Counts Toward $30K? | Notes |

## 3. Voluntary Registration: Cost-Benefit Analysis

### 3.1 The Right to Register Voluntarily (ETA s.240(3))

Any person engaged in commercial activity in Canada may voluntarily register for GST/HST, even if
taxable supplies are below $30,000. Registration takes effect on a date agreed to with CRA — either
retroactively or prospectively.

### 3.2 Why Register BEFORE Hitting $30,000

**Reason 1: ITC Recovery on Business Expenses**

The single most compelling reason for early registration is the right to claim Input Tax Credits on
all business purchases. Every dollar CC spends on computers, software, cloud services, and
professional fees from Canadian vendors includes 13% Ontario HST. As a non-registrant, that HST is
a dead cost. As a registrant, it comes back as a credit.

At current OASIS expense levels, this is conservatively $1,000–$3,000/year in recovered HST
(see Section 4 for detailed calculations).

**Reason 2: B2B Client Expectations**

`[OASIS]` OASIS's target market is businesses — AI automation clients, PropFlow users, and
enterprise development contracts. Every B2B client can claim back the HST you charge them via their
own ITC. To these clients, the 13% HST is cost-neutral — they pay it and claim it back. A GST/HST
registration number signals that OASIS is a legitimate, CRA-recognized business, not a side hustle.

**Reason 3: Retroactive ITC Claims**

Once registered, CC can claim ITCs on qualifying purchases made **before** registration — up to
4 years for capital property (ETA s.171(1)) and back to the fiscal year of registration for
operating expenses (subject to the normal 4-year claim limitation in ETA s.225(4)). Every laptop,
monitor, and software subscription purchased since OASIS launched is potentially claimable.

**Reason 4: Zero-Rated Export Advantage (OASIS-Critical)**

`[OASIS]` Once registered, CC is in a net **refund position** on international sales. CC charges
0% HST to US clients (zero-rated) but claims full ITCs on expenses. This creates a situation where
CRA owes CC money — a quarterly refund. Non-registrants can never access this. For a SaaS business
deliberately targeting US clients, early registration is a cash flow accelerator. See Section 6.

### 3.3 Why NOT Register Early

**Reason 1: B2C Price Inflation**

If any of CC's clients are consumers (not businesses), registration makes CC 13% more expensive
overnight. A DJ booking for a private party: the client pays $500 and suddenly the invoice is $565.
The consumer gets no ITC recovery. Competitive disadvantage against unregistered DJ competitors.

**Reason 2: Administrative Burden**

Filing HST returns (even annual) requires tracking HST collected and ITCs separately from income.
This is a ~2-4 hour annual commitment if books are clean, but a 20+ hour project if records are
disorganized. Without accounting software already in place, the administrative cost is real.

**Reason 3: Cash Flow Discipline Required**

The HST collected on invoices is NOT CC's money. It belongs to CRA and must be remitted. Businesses
that spend their HST float create serious cash flow problems at remittance time. The discipline
required: maintain a segregated HST reserve account (see `ATLAS_BOOKKEEPING_SYSTEMS.md` — Account
1050: Business Savings — HST Reserve).

### 3.4 Decision Matrix

| Revenue Level | Client Type | Expense Level | Register? | Reason |
|---------------|-------------|---------------|-----------|--------|
| < $30K | B2B (businesses only) | Any | **YES** | ITCs + professional appearance + no cost to clients |
| < $30K | B2C (consumers) | Low | **NO** | 13% price increase with minimal ITC recovery to offset |
| < $30K | B2C (consumers) | High (>30% of revenue) | **MAYBE** | Run ITC calculation — may still be worth it |
| < $30K | Mixed (B2B + B2C) | Any | **YES, with staging** | Register when B2B revenue dominates |
| < $30K | SaaS to US clients | Any | **YES** | Zero-rated exports + full ITC claims = net refund |
| > $30K | Any | Any | **MANDATORY** | No choice — ETA s.240(1) |

`[OASIS]` CC's situation maps to Row 1 and Row 5: B2B SaaS targeting businesses and US clients.
Early registration is the correct call. The detailed recommendation is in Section 13.

## 4. Input Tax Credits (ITCs) — Getting Your 13% Back

### 4.1 The Legal Basis (ETA s.169)

**ETA s.169(1):** A registrant may claim an ITC for the GST/HST paid or payable in respect of a
supply acquired for use in a commercial activity. The core formula:

```
ITC = (Tax paid on acquisition) × (Extent of use in commercial activity)
```

If a purchase is 100% for business: ITC = 100% of HST paid.
If a purchase is partially personal: ITC = (business use %) × HST paid.
No allocation is needed for items that are exclusively business-use.

### 4.2 What Qualifies for Full ITC

The following expenses used entirely in OASIS or DJ commercial activity qualify for 100% ITC:

| Expense Category | Ontario HST | ITC Recovery | Notes |
|-----------------|------------|-------------|-------|
| Computer/laptop purchase ($2,500) | $325 | $325 | 100% business use |
| Additional monitor(s) ($400) | $52 | $52 | 100% business use |
| Mechanical keyboard, peripherals ($200) | $26 | $26 | 100% business use |
| External hard drives, USB equipment ($150) | $19.50 | $19.50 | 100% business use |
| GitHub Pro subscription ($150/yr) | $19.50 | $19.50 | 100% business use |
| Anthropic Claude API (Canadian billing) | 13% | 100% | 100% business use |
| Vercel / Netlify / hosting | 13% | 100% | If billed from Canadian entity |
| Domain registrations (Canadian registrar) | 13% | 100% | 100% business use |
| Professional services (accountant, lawyer) | $130 on $1,000 | $130 | 100% business use |
| Office supplies (paper, ink, cables) | 13% | 100% | 100% business use |
| DJ equipment (controller, speakers) | 13% | 100% | If used for DJ gig income |
| DJ music software subscriptions | 13% | 100% | If billed from Canadian entity |

**Important note on US/international software:** AWS, DigitalOcean, Google Cloud, Stripe, GitHub
(billed from US entities) and most major SaaS platforms do NOT charge Canadian HST — they bill from
outside Canada or use non-registered vendors. No ITC available on these. This does not reduce the
value of registration — it simply means most cloud infrastructure spend does not generate ITCs.
The ITCs come primarily from Canadian-purchased hardware, local software, and professional services.

### 4.3 What Qualifies for Partial ITC (Proportional Use)

| Expense | Calculation Method | Typical Business % | ITC Approach |
|---------|-------------------|--------------------|-------------|
| Home internet | Business use % (hours on business / total hours, or rooms method) | 60–80% | (Business %) × HST paid |
| Cell phone | Business use % (log-based or reasonable estimate) | 50–70% | (Business %) × HST paid |
| Home office (rent or owned) | Square footage of dedicated office / total sq ft | 10–25% | (Office sq ft %) × HST paid on utilities, strata, maintenance |
| Vehicle | Business km / total km (logbook required) | Varies | (Business km %) × HST paid on gas, repairs, insurance |
| Desk, chair, office furniture | If in a shared space vs dedicated home office | 100% if dedicated | 100% ITC if exclusively used for business |

`[NOW]` For the home internet and cell phone, a documented reasonable estimate is acceptable.
CRA expects consistency year over year. Document the basis once and apply it consistently.

### 4.4 What Does NOT Qualify for ITC

| Exclusion | Authority | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Personal expenses (groceries, clothing, entertainment) | ETA s.170(1) | Not acquired for commercial activity |
| Club memberships (golf, fitness) | **ETA s.170(1)(b)** — explicitly excluded | Specifically listed in the ETA |
| Expenses for exempt supplies | ETA s.169(1) — no commercial activity on exempt side | The exempt activity doesn't generate output tax |
| Life insurance premiums | ETA Sch. V, Part VII | Financial service — no HST charged = no ITC |
| Interest on loans | ETA Sch. V, Part VII | Financial service — no HST charged = no ITC |
| Meals and entertainment (50% income tax rule) | **100% ITC for HST purposes** | Note: ITA s.67.1 restricts income deduction to 50%, but the HST ITC is 100% claimable under ETA s.236 — this is one of the most commonly missed rules |
| Passenger vehicles over $36,000 (capital cost) | ETA s.201 — prescribed amount | Limits ITC on luxury vehicles |

`[NOW]` The meals and entertainment ITC point is critical and frequently misunderstood even by
accountants. The **income tax deduction** is limited to 50% under **ITA s.67.1**. But the **HST ITC
claim** on meals and entertainment is **100%** under **ETA s.236**. CC should be claiming 100% of
the HST on any qualifying business meals, then applying the 50% income tax restriction separately
on the deduction side. These are two separate calculations under two separate statutes.

## 5. ITC Documentation Requirements

### 5.1 The Three-Tier Receipt Rule (ETA s.169(4), Input Tax Credit Information Regulations)

The **Input Tax Credit Information (GST/HST) Regulations** establish documentation requirements
based on the size of the purchase. These are not optional — missing documentation is grounds for
CRA to deny an ITC claim on audit.

**Tier 1: Purchases under $30**

- No specific documentation required by regulation
- Best practice: keep anyway (bank/credit card statement is sufficient evidence)
- CRA rarely denies sub-$30 claims but auditors can if no record exists

**Tier 2: Purchases $30 to $149.99 (ETA Reg. s.3(b))**

Required on the receipt or invoice:
- Vendor name (or trade name)
- Date of supply (or of payment if different)
- Total amount of the supply
- Amount of GST/HST charged OR a statement that the price includes applicable taxes
- No buyer information required at this tier

**Tier 3: Purchases $150 and over (ETA Reg. s.3(c)) — The Full Invoice Requirement**

Required:
- Vendor name (or trade name) **and address**
- Vendor's GST/HST registration number (15-digit Business Number ending in RT0001)
- Date of supply
- Description of the supply (what was purchased)
- Terms of payment (net 30, etc.)
- Total amount of the supply
- Amount of HST charged OR a statement indicating HST is included and the applicable rate
- **Buyer's name or trade name** (i.e., CC's name or "OASIS AI Solutions" must appear)

**Practical implication:** For any purchase over $150, a generic cash register receipt is often
insufficient. CC needs a proper invoice with the vendor's HST number and CC's name on it. When
ordering equipment or professional services, always request a formal invoice before paying.

### 5.2 Digital Documentation

CRA accepts digital/electronic records under **ITA s.230.1** and **ETA s.286(3)**. Photos of
receipts, PDF invoices, and accounting software records all satisfy the requirement. The key
standard: records must be **legible, accurate, and retrievable**. Screenshots with all required
fields visible qualify.

**Best practice:** Run all business purchases through a dedicated business credit card. The monthly
statement combined with stored digital receipts creates a clean, auditable record. (See
`ATLAS_BOOKKEEPING_SYSTEMS.md` Section 6 — Monthly Close Routine.)

### 5.3 Record Retention

**ETA s.286(3):** GST/HST records must be kept for **6 years** from the end of the fiscal year to
which they relate. This is the same standard as income tax records under ITA s.230(4). Keep
everything — paper or digital — for a minimum of 6 years.

### 5.4 The Vendor GST/HST Number Verification Problem

CRA maintains an online registry at **cra-arc.gc.ca/gsthstregistry** where any GST/HST number can
be verified in real time. If a vendor gives CC a fake or expired registration number, CRA will deny
the ITC claim regardless of whether CC paid the HST in good faith.

`[NOW]` For large one-time purchases from new vendors (contractors, equipment dealers), verify the
registration number before paying. This takes 30 seconds and prevents ITC denials on audit.

## 6. Place of Supply Rules for SaaS and Services

### 6.1 The Core Framework (ETA s.142, s.144)

The place of supply rules determine **which rate of GST/HST applies** (or whether the supply is
zero-rated). For digital services and SaaS, the rules are primarily based on where the **recipient
(customer) is located**, not where CC is.

### 6.2 Canadian Customers — Province-by-Province

| Province/Territory | Rate | What CC Remits | Notes |
|-------------------|------|---------------|-------|
| Ontario | 13% HST | CRA (federal + provincial combined) | Full HST |
| British Columbia | 5% GST | CRA | BC levies PST separately — BC PST is CC's client's problem |
| Alberta | 5% GST | CRA | No provincial sales tax in Alberta |
| Saskatchewan | 5% GST | CRA | SK PST separate — CC not responsible |
| Manitoba | 5% GST | CRA | MB RST separate — CC not responsible |
| Quebec | 5% GST | CRA (GST portion only) | QST (9.975%) is a separate QST registration — required if CC's taxable supplies to Quebec residents exceed $30,000 |
| Nova Scotia | 15% HST | CRA | Atlantic HST provinces |
| New Brunswick | 15% HST | CRA | Atlantic HST provinces |
| PEI | 15% HST | CRA | Atlantic HST provinces |
| Newfoundland & Labrador | 15% HST | CRA | Atlantic HST provinces |
| Nunavut/NWT/Yukon | 5% GST | CRA | No territorial sales tax |

**Simplified rule for OASIS:** If a client has an Ontario address, charge 13%. If another Canadian
province, verify. If unsure of province, default to the customer's billing address — ETA s.144
ties the place of supply to the customer's address for most services.

**Quebec (QST) flag:** `[FUTURE]` If OASIS ever has significant Quebec clients, a separate Revenu
Québec QST registration may be required. The QST small supplier threshold mirrors the GST threshold
at $30,000. At CC's current stage, this is a future-watch item only.

### 6.3 International Customers — Zero-Rated Exports

**ETA Schedule VI, Part V** — Services supplied to non-residents for use outside Canada are
zero-rated at 0%. This is the single most important HST provision for OASIS's growth strategy.

Zero-rated means:
- CC charges the client 0% HST (so zero additional cost to the foreign client)
- The supply still counts toward CC's $30,000 threshold (it is a taxable supply at 0%)
- CC claims full ITCs on all expenses related to the zero-rated activity
- Net position: input tax credit refund on the expense side with no output tax liability

**This creates a structural refund position for OASIS when US/international revenue dominates.**

**Documentation required to support zero-rating (ETA Reg s.4.1):**

| Evidence Type | Purpose |
|--------------|---------|
| Client invoice showing non-Canadian billing address | Establishes non-residency |
| Signed service agreement confirming client is a non-resident | Primary evidence |
| Foreign payment (USD wire, Stripe USD, Wise USD receipt) | Supporting evidence |
| Email correspondence confirming client location | Corroborating evidence |

**Mandatory invoice language for all international sales:**
> "Zero-rated supply of services supplied to a non-resident for use outside Canada — Excise Tax Act,
> Schedule VI, Part V, s.7"

If CRA audits and CC cannot prove non-residency, the zero-rated supply will be reassessed at 13%.
The HST that should have been collected becomes CC's personal liability. Documentation is not
optional.

### 6.4 The Zero-Rated vs Exempt Distinction — Why It Matters

This is one of the most critical distinctions in the ETA. Many advisors conflate them.

| Attribute | Zero-Rated (Sch. VI) | Exempt (Sch. V) |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Tax rate charged | 0% | 0% |
| Counts toward $30K threshold | **YES** — still a taxable supply | NO |
| ITC claimable on related expenses | **YES — full ITCs** | NO — ITCs denied |
| Example | OASIS services to US client | Interest income, crypto trading |

`[OASIS]` International SaaS sales are zero-rated, not exempt. Every dollar of zero-rated export
revenue preserves CC's full ITC entitlement. This is fundamentally different from exempt income
(like trading gains), where ITCs on related expenses are denied.

### 6.5 Digital Services to Foreign Individuals (Non-Business Non-Residents)

A technical nuance: ETA Schedule VI, Part V zero-rating for services requires the recipient to be
a **non-resident** — but the supply must also be for **use outside Canada**. If a non-resident
uses OASIS's SaaS from a Canadian IP address or Canadian location, it may not qualify as
zero-rated (ETA s.143 places of use analysis). For OASIS's B2B client base, this is low risk.
For a future consumer SaaS product, this is a planning consideration.

## 7. Filing Methods: Regular vs Quick Method

### 7.1 Regular Method

Under the regular method, CC:
1. Tracks all HST collected on sales (output tax)
2. Tracks all HST paid on purchases (input tax = ITCs)
3. Remits the difference: HST collected − ITCs = net remittance (or refund if ITCs > collected)

This is the default method and the best choice for businesses with **significant input expenses
relative to revenue**.

### 7.2 Quick Method (ETA s.227, GST/HST Memorandum 4.2)

The Quick Method allows eligible registrants to remit a **prescribed flat percentage** of
HST-included revenue instead of tracking every ITC individually. The tradeoff: simplified
administration but lost ITC claims (with one exception — see below).

**Eligibility (ETA s.227(1.1)):**
- Annual taxable supplies (including associates) ≤ **$400,000**
- Not a financial institution, a non-resident, a charity (for certain supplies), or a public sector body
- Registered for at least one year (election cannot be made in the first fiscal year of registration
  under most circumstances)
- Must elect by filing **Form GST74** with CRA by the due date of the first return for the fiscal
  year the election is to take effect

**Quick Method remittance rates for Ontario service businesses:**

| Supply Type | Remittance Rate | Basis |
|-------------|----------------|-------|
| Services (majority of revenue — >50% from services) | **8.8%** | Of HST-included revenue |
| Goods (majority of revenue is goods) | 1.8% on first $30K, then 4.4% | Different tier |
| Mix (services + goods, Ontario) | 8.8% (service rate applies if primarily services) | |

**First-year credit:** In the first fiscal year of the Quick Method election, CC receives an
additional **1% credit** on the first $30,000 of HST-included revenue. This is a one-time
accelerator — use it in a high-revenue year if possible.

**ITC exception under Quick Method:** CC **cannot claim ITCs** on most purchases under the Quick
Method EXCEPT on the acquisition of **capital property exceeding $30,000** (ETA s.227(3.1)).
A new computer at $3,000? No ITC. A commercial server rack at $50,000? ITC still claimable.
For OASIS at current scale, no capital purchases will exceed $30,000.

### 7.3 Quick Method vs Regular Method: Side-by-Side Calculation

**Scenario: OASIS earns $60,000 in SaaS/dev revenue, all Ontario clients, in 2026**

```
REGULAR METHOD:
  Revenue: $60,000
  HST collected (13%): $7,800
  Estimated ITCs (hardware, software, professional services, home office):
    - MacBook ($2,500 × 13% = $325)
    - Monitors + peripherals ($600 × 13% = $78)
    - Software subscriptions — Canadian ($300 × 13% = $39)
    - Accountant ($800 × 13% = $104)
    - Home office — proportional ($400 × 13% = $52)
    - Phone + internet — proportional ($800 × 13% = $104)
    - Misc office ($300 × 13% = $39)
  Total estimated ITCs: $741
  Net HST remittance (Regular): $7,800 − $741 = $7,059

QUICK METHOD:
  HST-included revenue: $60,000 + $7,800 = $67,800
  Quick Method remittance: $67,800 × 8.8% = $5,966
  Less first-year 1% credit on first $30,000: −$300 (first year only)
  Net HST remittance (Quick Method, Year 1): $5,666
  Net HST remittance (Quick Method, Year 2+): $5,966

COMPARISON:
  Regular method net remittance:          $7,059
  Quick Method net remittance (Year 1):   $5,666   ← SAVE $1,393 in Year 1
  Quick Method net remittance (Year 2+):  $5,966   ← SAVE $1,093 ongoing

QUICK METHOD WINS when ITC claims are low relative to revenue.
```

**Scenario 2: OASIS earns $60,000 but has a high-equipment year ($8,000 in Canadian hardware/software)**

```
REGULAR METHOD:
  HST collected: $7,800
  ITCs on $8,000 equipment: $1,040
  ITCs on other expenses: $400
  Total ITCs: $1,440
  Net remittance (Regular): $7,800 − $1,440 = $6,360

QUICK METHOD:
  Net remittance (Quick Method, Year 2+): $5,966

QUICK METHOD STILL WINS — but the gap narrows.
```

**Breakeven point: Quick Method vs Regular Method**

The Quick Method wins when:
> ITCs < (HST collected − Quick Method remittance)

At 13% HST rate and 8.8% Quick Method rate, the "savings margin" is 4.2% of HST-included revenue.
Quick Method wins when **ITCs < 4.2% of HST-included revenue** (approximately 3.7% of net revenue).

**Rule of thumb:** If CC's total ITCs (Canadian-vendor purchases only, at 13%) are less than
approximately **$0.37 for every $10 in OASIS revenue**, the Quick Method saves money.

For most software-led SaaS businesses where the majority of infrastructure spend goes to US
cloud vendors (no Canadian HST), the Quick Method typically wins year over year. Evaluate annually.

### 7.4 Electing and Revoking the Quick Method

- **Elect:** File **Form GST74** — Election and Revocation of an Election to Use the Quick Method
  of Accounting. Must be filed before or with the first GST/HST return for the fiscal year the
  election applies. Cannot be elected retroactively.
- **Revoke:** Also on Form GST74. Takes effect at the beginning of the next fiscal year. Once
  revoked, CC cannot re-elect for at least one year (ETA s.227(4)).

## 8. Filing Frequency

### 8.1 Annual Reporting Period (Default for Small Businesses)

**ETA s.245(2):** Registrants with annual taxable supplies ≤ **$1,500,000** are assigned an
**annual** reporting period by default. One return per year. Due date: **3 months after the fiscal
year end** (ETA s.238(1)(a)).

For CC (sole proprietor, calendar fiscal year): HST return due **March 31** of the following year
(e.g., 2026 return due March 31, 2027).

**The payment deadline is separate from the filing deadline.** Any net HST owing must be
paid by the filing due date to avoid arrears interest (currently CRA's prescribed rate +
4%). However, if CC is in a **refund position** (zero-rated exports + ITCs > output tax), filing
sooner means CRA issues the refund sooner.

### 8.2 Quarterly Reporting Period

**ETA s.245(4):** A registrant can elect quarterly filing regardless of revenue level.
Returns are due **one month after each quarter end**:

| Quarter | Period | Due Date |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Q1 | Jan 1 – Mar 31 | April 30 |
| Q2 | Apr 1 – Jun 30 | July 31 |
| Q3 | Jul 1 – Sep 30 | October 31 |
| Q4 | Oct 1 – Dec 31 | January 31 |

**When quarterly makes sense:**
- CC is consistently in a **refund position** (e.g., predominantly US/international clients — zero-rated
  revenue with Canadian expense ITCs) and wants quarterly cash recovery instead of annual
- CC has strong quarterly cash discipline and can manage remittances without the annual float
- Revenue exceeds $200,000 and the annual return requires tracking a full year of transactions
  at once — quarterly bookkeeping checkpoints reduce year-end stress

### 8.3 Monthly Reporting Period

Available for businesses consistently in a refund position who want maximum cash flow velocity.
Rarely beneficial for CC at current scale — the administrative cost of 12 returns/year exceeds
the float benefit until revenue is substantial.

### 8.4 Atlas Recommendation on Filing Frequency

| OASIS Revenue Stage | Recommendation | Reason |
|--------------------|----|-----|
| < $100K, primarily Canadian clients | Annual | Simplest. One return. Low cost. |
| < $100K, predominantly US/international clients | Quarterly | Regular refund position — quarterly cash recovery accelerates working capital |
| $100K–$500K, mixed clients | Quarterly | Balance of admin burden and cash flow management |
| > $500K | Quarterly or monthly | Volume justifies more frequent reconciliation |

`[NOW]` For CC: choose today (or the first day of the current quarter) as the effective date.

## 10. Ongoing Compliance

### 10.1 The Remittance Formula

```
Net Tax = HST Collected on Sales (Output Tax)
        − ITCs on Purchases (Input Tax)
        = Amount owed to CRA (positive) OR refund from CRA (negative)
```

If Net Tax is positive: CC remits to CRA by the return due date.
If Net Tax is negative: CRA issues a refund (typically within 4–8 weeks of filing for electronic returns).

### 10.2 Instalment Obligations

For annual filers, if net HST owing in the prior year exceeded **$3,000**, CRA requires quarterly
instalments in the current year (**ETA s.237**). This is similar to income tax instalments. The
instalment schedule:

| Instalment | Due Date |
|-----------|----------|
| Q1 | April 30 |
| Q2 | July 31 |
| Q3 | October 31 |
| Q4 | January 31 (of following year) |

Each instalment is 25% of the prior year's net tax. For OASIS at early revenue stages (sub-$30K
taxable supplies, net tax under $3,000), instalments will not be required. Plan for them as OASIS
scales past $100K.

### 10.3 Late Filing and Non-Filing Penalties

**ETA s.280:** Late filing penalty = 1% of net tax owing + 0.25% for each full month late,
up to a maximum of 12 months (maximum penalty: 4%). Arrears interest on top of the penalty.

**ETA s.280.1:** Where a net tax refund was claimed but return not filed on time, CRA suspends
the refund. File the return — get the refund.

**Non-registrant after threshold crossed:** CRA can assess HST on all past supplies since the
threshold breach date, plus interest and penalties. The maximum look-back is 4 years (6 years if
gross negligence or fraud is found).

### 10.4 The Nil Return Obligation

Even if net tax is zero (or CC had no commercial activity in a period), a registered business must
**file a nil return** by the due date. Failure to file = late filing penalty starting at $250.

## 11. Retroactive ITC Claims

### 11.1 The Transitional ITC Mechanism (ETA s.171)

When CC registers for the first time (prospective registration), certain pre-registration purchases
can generate ITCs. This is called the "transitional ITC" or "opening inventory ITC" mechanism.

**ETA s.171(1) — Capital property transitional ITC:**

Capital property (computers, equipment) held for use in commercial activity at the time of
registration generates an ITC equal to:

```
Transitional ITC = HST paid on original purchase × (FMV at registration date / Purchase price)
```

This accounts for depreciation — CC doesn't get back the full HST on a 3-year-old laptop.

**Example:**
```
MacBook purchased January 2024 for $2,500 + $325 HST = $2,825 total
CC registers for HST in March 2026 (26 months later)
Estimated FMV at registration: $1,200 (depreciated)

Transitional ITC = $325 × ($1,200 / $2,500) = $325 × 0.48 = $156
```

The $156 is claimable on CC's first HST return.

**ETA s.171(1.1) — Inventory and other property:**

Inventory and non-capital business property on hand at registration date: full ITC on the
HST-included cost of the items on hand (no FMV adjustment — full cost ITC for inventory).

### 11.2 The Four-Year Lookback for Operating Expenses (ETA s.225(4))

After registration, CC can claim ITCs on **past operating expenses** within the normal
4-year limitation window. This means:

- If CC registers on April 1, 2026, CC can claim ITCs on operating expenses going back to
  April 1, 2022 (subject to the availability of documentation)
- This applies to recurring expenses like software subscriptions, professional services,
  and supplies — not capital property (which uses the FMV-adjusted s.171 mechanism instead)

**Practical ceiling:** Most software subscriptions (US vendors, Stripe, AWS) don't charge Canadian
HST. The retroactive operating expense ITCs primarily recover HST on Canadian professional services,
accounting fees, and local purchases. Realistically $200–$800 in recoverable retroactive ITCs for
a young OASIS-scale business.

### 11.3 Claiming the Retroactive ITCs

The transitional ITC is claimed on CC's **first HST return** after registration. It is entered
as an ITC (not income) and will reduce the net tax owing for that period. No separate form is
required — it appears as a line on the GST/HST return.

Documentation required: original purchase receipts and invoices (Tier 2 or Tier 3 as applicable
under Section 5). The FMV determination for capital property should be documented (market comps,
eBay/Kijiji listings for similar used equipment, or a simple written estimate with rationale).

## 12. Strategic HST Planning for CC

### 12.1 Time Major Purchases Around Registration

The highest-return move available right now: register for HST before the next significant equipment
or software purchase.

**Action:** Register HST first. Then buy the new laptop, monitor setup, DJ equipment, or any
Canadian-vendor software license. Every qualifying purchase made after registration date generates
an ITC immediately. Purchases made before registration require the FMV-adjusted transitional
mechanism (lower recovery).

**Example — value of timing a $4,000 computer purchase:**
```
Purchase BEFORE registration: $520 HST — recoverable via ETA s.171 transitional ITC,
  adjusted for FMV depreciation. If used for 1 year before registration: recovery ~$390.

Purchase AFTER registration: $520 HST — 100% recoverable ITC on the first return.
  Delta: $130 saved by sequencing the purchase.

For a $10,000 equipment year: ~$325–$500 saved by registering first.
```

### 12.2 International Client Structure — The Net Refund Play

`[OASIS]` If OASIS successfully lands US/international clients (which is the growth trajectory),
the HST math flips in CC's favour. The structure becomes:

```
Revenue side:    US clients pay zero HST (zero-rated) → $0 output tax
Expense side:    Canadian vendors charge 13% HST → $X in ITCs

Net position:    ITCs > Output Tax → CRA OWES CC MONEY EVERY QUARTER
```

This is not a loophole — it is the intended design of the zero-rating system to support Canadian
exports. File quarterly to accelerate the refund cycle. `[NOW]` Set up a Wise USD account for US
client payments and document every transaction as a zero-rated supply (invoice with Schedule VI
language from Section 6.3).

### 12.3 Quick Method Election Timing

Do not elect the Quick Method in the **first year of registration**. In year one:
- The transitional ITC mechanism provides one-time retroactive ITCs that inflate the ITC side
- The 1% first-year Quick Method credit does not fully compensate
- Major equipment purchases in a startup year skew the calculation toward Regular Method

**Election strategy:**
1. Year 1: Regular Method — capture transitional ITCs and any large equipment purchases
2. Year 2: Run the comparison (Section 7.3). Elect Quick Method via Form GST74 if ITC/revenue
   ratio is below the breakeven threshold
3. Evaluate annually — the election can be revoked once per year
