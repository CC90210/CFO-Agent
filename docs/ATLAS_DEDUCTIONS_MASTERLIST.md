# SECTION 1: Obscure Federal Deductions

## 1.1 Moving Expenses (s.62 ITA)

**What it is:** Deduction for costs of moving to a new work location or to attend post-secondary full-time. NOT a credit -- a full above-the-line deduction that reduces net income.

**The 40km rule:** New home must be at least 40km closer (by shortest public route, not straight line) to your new work location or school. CRA uses Google Maps driving distance -- the "as the crow flies" argument was settled in Giannakopoulos v. The Queen (TCC, 2012).

**What qualifies (full list -- most accountants miss half of these):**

| Expense | Deductible? | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| Travel costs (gas, meals, hotel) for you and household | YES | Moving day + reasonable travel days |
| Transportation/shipping of household goods | YES | Includes insurance on goods in transit |
| Temporary living near old AND new home | YES | Up to 15 days each (30 days total) |
| Lease cancellation at old residence | YES | Often missed -- full penalty deductible |
| Cost of selling old home (agent fees, legal, mortgage penalty) | YES | Only if you owned |
| Cost of buying new home (legal fees, transfer tax) | YES | Only if you owned old home |
| Utility hookup/disconnection | YES | Both addresses |
| Mail redirect | YES | Canada Post redirect fee |
| Replacing drivers licence, vehicle registration | YES | Province-specific fees |
| Revising legal documents (wills, trusts) to reflect new address | YES | Lawyer fees for address changes |
| Cost of maintaining old residence while vacant | YES | Property tax, insurance, mortgage interest, utilities -- max until sold or lease ends |
| Moving costs of pets | YES | CRA accepts reasonable pet transport |

**Key limits:**
- Can only deduct against income earned at new location (employment or self-employment income)
- Students: can only deduct against scholarships, bursaries, research grants
- Unused moving expenses carry forward indefinitely (no expiry)
- Reimbursed amounts: reduce deduction by employer reimbursement

**CC relevance:** If CC ever moves for OASIS or a new gig, this is potentially $5K-15K+ in deductions. Keep every receipt from day one of considering the move.

## 1.2 Child Care Expenses (s.63 ITA)

**Future planning item for CC.** When applicable:

**The hidden rules:**
- Lower-income spouse must claim (but exceptions exist for disability, school, prison, separation)
- Overnight camp: max $200/week per child (under 7) or $125/week (7-16)
- Day care, nanny, babysitter, day camp: max $8,000/child (under 7), $5,000 (7-16), $11,000 (DTC-eligible child)
- **Self-employed twist:** You can create child care expenses by hiring a nanny while you work from home -- even if you're physically present. CRA has confirmed this in interpretations. The test is "to enable you to earn income."
- **University student parent:** Full-time students can claim child care for periods of enrollment even if the other parent has higher income (s.63(2)(b))
- Payments to relatives: deductible if the relative is 18+ and not a dependant. Pay your parent/sibling to babysit -- it's deductible for you and income for them (possibly at a lower rate).

## 1.3 Disability Tax Credit (DTC) (s.118.3 ITA)

**Mental health inclusion (since 2021 -- most people don't know):**

The 2021 budget expanded DTC eligibility to explicitly include mental health conditions. "Mental functions necessary for everyday life" is now a standalone category. ADHD, major depression, bipolar disorder, PTSD, severe anxiety disorders, and autism spectrum disorder can all qualify.

**Qualification criteria (one or more):**
- Markedly restricted in a basic activity of daily living (all or substantially all of the time -- CRA interprets as 90%+)
- Need life-sustaining therapy 14+ hours/week (includes time for prep, administration, recovery)
- Cumulative effect of significant restrictions in 2+ categories

**The categories (s.118.4):**
1. Vision
2. Speaking
3. Hearing
4. Walking
5. Eliminating
6. Feeding
7. Dressing
8. **Mental functions necessary for everyday life** (memory, problem-solving, goal-setting, judgement, adaptive functioning)

**Transfer rules:**
- Unused DTC can transfer to supporting spouse, parent, grandparent, child, grandchild, sibling, aunt, uncle, niece, nephew
- Supporting person must have claimed the dependant on their return OR the dependant must have been dependent on them for basic necessities
- Only one person can claim the transferred credit per year

**The retroactive play:**
- DTC approval is retroactive to the onset date the doctor certifies (up to 10 years back via T1-ADJ)
- If approved for 2016-2026, you can refile ALL those years and claim the credit retroactively
- 2025 value: ~$1,525 federal + ~$920 Ontario = ~$2,445/year x 10 years = $24,450+ refund potential

**RDSP unlock:** DTC approval opens the Registered Disability Savings Plan -- $200K lifetime limit, 300% government matching (Canada Disability Savings Grant up to $3,500/year). This is the single most powerful savings vehicle in the Canadian tax code.

## 1.4 Caregiver Credits

### Canada Caregiver Credit (s.118(1)(d), s.118.01)

**For dependants with physical or mental impairment:**
- $7,999 (2025 base amount) for infirm dependant 18+ (parent, grandparent, etc.)
- Reduced by dependant's net income over ~$18,783
- Does NOT require living together (unlike the old caregiver amount)
- Does NOT require DTC approval -- just a medical certification of impairment

**The hidden stacking rule:**
- If you claim the eligible dependant amount (s.118(1)(b)) and the dependant is infirm, you get the enhanced eligible dependant amount ($2,499 additional, 2025) on TOP of the base eligible dependant amount
- Most software doesn't auto-optimize this -- manual check required

### Medical Expense Tax Credit (s.118.2) -- Adjacent Strategies

**Elite deductions most miss:**
- Travel expenses (meals + vehicle + lodging) if medical treatment is 40km+ from home
- Attendant care up to $10,000 (or $25,000 in year of death) without DTC
- Home renovation for medical accessibility (ramps, widened doors, stairlifts) -- no dollar limit
- Service animals (food, grooming, vet care) if certified by a medical practitioner
- Gluten-free food PREMIUM (celiacs -- difference between GF and regular food price)
- Air conditioner if medically required (letter from doctor)
- Tutoring services for learning disabilities (must be supplementary to school)
- Private health plan premiums (not through employer)
- Prescribed cannabis (with Health Canada registration)

**The 3% / $2,635 threshold trick:** Medical expenses are reduced by 3% of net income or $2,635 (2025), whichever is less. For high-income earners, the threshold caps at $2,635. For low-income earners, it's better to have the higher-income spouse claim (counterintuitive) because the 3% threshold is the same and they get more refund value. But if the lower-income spouse has enough tax payable, they should claim because the 3% of their lower income = lower threshold = more net medical expense.

## 1.5 Interest Deductibility Deep Dive (s.20(1)(c) ITA)

**The single most powerful deduction in the Canadian tax code for investors.**

### The Core Rule
Interest on money borrowed for the purpose of earning income from business or property is deductible. Period. Not just against the income it generates -- against ALL income.

### The Singleton Shuffle (Singleton v. Canada, SCC 2001)
- Borrow from your corporation to invest personally
- Corporation borrows to replace the capital
- You deduct the interest on the personal loan because it was used to invest
- SCC confirmed: trace the direct use of funds, not the economic substance

### Compound Interest on Interest (s.20(1)(d))
- Interest you pay on borrowed money used to pay deductible interest is ALSO deductible
- This creates a compounding deduction loop -- you borrow to pay interest, and the interest on THAT borrowing is also deductible
- Most accountants miss this second-layer deduction

### The Disappearing Source Rule (Ludco Enterprises, SCC 2001)
- Even if the investment income is $0 or near-zero, the interest is deductible AS LONG AS there was a reasonable expectation of income at the time of borrowing
- A $1 dividend on a $100K investment is sufficient
- Capital gains alone are NOT income for this purpose -- need business income, interest, dividends, or rent

### The Refinancing Rules (IT-533)
- Refinance your mortgage, use the proceeds to invest, and the NEW interest is deductible
- The Smith Manoeuvre: make regular mortgage payments, reborrow the paid-down amount, invest in dividend-paying assets
- Over 25 years, converts your entire non-deductible mortgage into tax-deductible debt

### Interest Deductibility on Specific Assets

| Asset | Deductible? | Condition |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| Dividend stocks | YES | Must have reasonable expectation of dividends |
| REIT units | YES | REITs distribute income regularly |
| Rental property | YES | Rental income is property income |
| Vacant land held for development | YES | s.18(2) limits to net income from land |
| Bitcoin / crypto | MAYBE | Only if held as inventory (business income), not capital property |
| TFSA contributions | NO | Tax-exempt income = no deduction |
| RRSP contributions | NO | Tax-deferred = no deduction |
| FHSA contributions | NO | Tax-exempt = no deduction |
| Principal residence | NO | Not income-producing |
| Gold bullion | UNLIKELY | No inherent income-producing quality |

### CC Application
- If CC borrows to buy dividend ETFs or REITs, ALL interest is deductible on Line 22100 (carrying charges)
- Even a margin loan from a broker qualifies -- the interest on the margin loan is deductible
- IBKR margin rates (~5.5%) become effectively ~3.6% after tax deduction at 35% marginal rate

## 1.6 Stock Option Deduction (s.110(1)(d) ITA)

### CCPC (Canadian-Controlled Private Corporation) Stock Options
- **Deferral until disposition:** Tax on the benefit is deferred until you actually sell the shares (not when you exercise)
- **50% deduction:** If the exercise price was >= FMV at grant date, you get a 50% deduction on the employment benefit
- Combined effect: exercising CCPC options is almost tax-free until you sell

### Non-CCPC Options (Publicly Traded / Foreign)
- Benefit is taxable at exercise (no deferral)
- 50% deduction available if exercise price >= FMV at grant date AND shares are prescribed shares (common shares of listed companies)

### The $200,000 Annual Vesting Limit (post-2020 budget)
- For non-CCPC options granted after June 2021: only the first $200,000 in FMV of options vesting per year qualifies for the 50% deduction
- CCPC options are exempt from this limit
- Planning: negotiate vesting schedules to stay under $200K/year

### CC Application
- If CC ever receives startup equity (OASIS or a client startup), CCPC rules are critical
- Structure as options, not direct shares, to access the deferral
- Ensure exercise price = FMV at grant (get a business valuation)

## 1.8 Political Contribution Tax Credit (s.127(3))

**The most generous credit in the entire tax code on a percentage basis:**

| Contribution Amount | Credit |
|---------------------|--------|
| First $400 | **75%** = $300 |
| Next $350 ($400-$750) | **50%** = $175 |
| Next $525 ($750-$1,275) | **33.33%** = $175 |
| Total max credit | **$650** |

- Only federal political parties/candidates qualify for the federal credit
- Ontario has its own political contribution credit (see Section 2)
- **The play:** $400 contribution costs you only $100 after the 75% credit. It's the cheapest "deduction" in the tax code.
- Must be a registered federal party or official candidate

## 1.9 Charitable Donations -- Elite Strategies

### First-Time Donor Super Credit
- **Expired after 2017 tax year.** No longer available. Remove from planning.

### Donation of Publicly Listed Securities (s.38(a.1))
- **0% capital gains inclusion rate** on donations of publicly listed securities directly to a registered charity
- You STILL get the full donation tax credit based on FMV
- Example: stock with ACB $5,000, FMV $20,000. Donate directly = $0 capital gains tax + $20,000 donation credit (~46% combined federal/Ontario on amounts over $200)
- If you sell first, then donate cash: ~$3,750 capital gains tax + same donation credit. The direct donation saves $3,750.

### Ecological Gifts (s.110.1(1))
- Donation of ecologically sensitive land (or covenant/easement/servitude)
- 0% capital gains inclusion
- NO 75% of net income limit (normal donations capped at 75%) -- ecological gifts have NO ceiling
- Must be certified by Environment Canada
- Rarely used but extremely powerful for landowners

### Donation Carry-Forward
- Unused donation credits carry forward 5 years (10 years for ecological gifts)
- Strategy: bunch donations into a single high-income year to exceed the $200 threshold (first $200 of donations get 15% federal credit, amounts over $200 get 29% or 33% if income > $235K)

### Donation of Private Corporation Shares / Real Estate
- Since 2024 budget: expanded 0% capital gains treatment to donations of private corp shares and real estate IF the proceeds are donated to charity within 30 days of sale
- Still working its way through legislative implementation -- monitor

## 1.10 Farming and Fishing Losses (s.31 ITA)

### Restricted Farm Loss Rules
- If farming is NOT your chief source of income, losses are restricted to $2,500 + 50% of the next $30,000 = max $17,500/year
- The restricted portion carries forward indefinitely and can only offset future farming income
- Key case: Moldowan v. The Queen (SCC, 1978) -- three classes: full-time farmer, part-time farmer (restricted losses), hobby farmer (no deduction)

### The Farming + Other Income Combination
- If you can show farming IS a chief source (even combined with other income), losses are FULLY deductible
- Craig v. Canada (SCC, 2012): farming + another source of income CAN qualify for full deduction if farming is a chief source viewed in combination
- CC Application: if CC ever buys rural property near Collingwood and runs a small farm operation alongside OASIS, this could unlock full farm loss deductions against business income

## 1.14 Canada Training Credit (s.122.91)

**One of the most underused credits in Canada.**

## 2.4 Ontario Interactive Digital Media Tax Credit (OIDMTC)

- Developed in Ontario by CC
- However: the OIDMTC is designed for digital media companies producing products for commercial sale/licensing
- CC would need to either: (a) sell/license ATLAS to third parties, or (b) sell/license a derivative product
- **If OASIS incorporates and commercializes a trading platform/AI agent framework:** this 40% credit on Ontario labour could be worth tens of thousands per year
- **Recommendation:** flag for CPA review post-incorporation. This credit alone could justify incorporation.

# SECTION 3: Creative Deduction Strategies

## 3.1 Employing Family Members (s.67 ITA Reasonableness Test)

### The Strategy
Pay family members a salary for legitimate services rendered to your business. The salary is deductible to you and potentially taxable at a lower rate for them.

### What CRA Allows

| Family Member | What They Can Do | Reasonable Rate |
|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Parent | Bookkeeping, admin, client liaison, office maintenance | $15-30/hr |
| Sibling | Social media management, data entry, testing | $15-25/hr |
| Spouse (future) | Administrative support, bookkeeping, client communication | $15-40/hr |
| Child (future, 18+) | Any legitimate business task | Market rate for the role |

### The Rules (s.67 + case law)
1. **The work must actually be performed.** Document tasks, hours, deliverables.
2. **The rate must be reasonable.** Pay market rate for the work -- not $100/hr for filing papers.
3. **Actually pay them.** CRA will disallow deductions if no money changed hands. Use e-transfer with clear descriptions.
4. **T4 or T4A required.** If employment: T4, CPP, EI. If contract: T4A over $500.
5. **Keep time records.** Simple spreadsheet is sufficient.

### The Tax Arbitrage
- CC at 29.65% marginal rate pays parent $12,000/year for bookkeeping
- Parent claims the income but uses Basic Personal Amount ($15,705 for 2025) if they have no other income
- Net tax on $12,000 income with BPA: $0
- CC saves: $12,000 x 29.65% = $3,558 in tax
- Net family savings: $3,558

### The CPP Trap
- If structured as employment, employer CPP is owed (5.95% of pensionable earnings over $3,500)
- If structured as independent contractor (T4A), no CPP owed by payer -- but the family member owes self-employment CPP
- **Optimal structure:** T4A up to the Basic Personal Amount to avoid CPP entirely if the family member has no other pensionable income (but they'd need to file as self-employed)
- Better: pay them as employees but keep wages under the CPP exemption ($3,500) or accept the CPP cost as part of the tax play

## 3.2 Management Fees Between Related Entities

### The Strategy
If CC has multiple business activities (OASIS, DJing, trading), structuring one as a corporation and charging management fees to/from the others.

### How It Works Post-Incorporation
- OASIS Corp charges management fee to CC's sole proprietorship (DJing) for admin/IT/financial services
- Or: CC's holding company charges OASIS operating company a management fee
- Deductible to the payer, income to the receiver
- Can be used to shift income to a lower-tax entity or spread income across years

### CRA Red Flags
- Fees must reflect actual services rendered
- Must be documented in a management services agreement
- Transfer pricing rules (s.247) apply to non-arm's length transactions
- Circular fee arrangements (A pays B, B pays A) will be scrutinized

## 3.3 Advisory Fees for Managing Investments

### The Pre-Incorporation Play
- Investment counsel fees (s.20(1)(bb)) are deductible against investment income
- If CC pays a fee to a third party for investment advice, that fee is deductible

### The Post-Incorporation Play
- CC's corporation charges advisory fees for managing CC's personal investments
- The corporation earns the fee income (taxed at small business rate ~12.2% in Ontario)
- CC deducts the fee personally
- **Warning:** this is an aggressive position. CRA may challenge under s.67 (reasonableness) and GAAR (s.245) if the arrangement has no purpose other than tax reduction. Need genuine advisory services with documentation.

## 3.4 Loan Forgiveness Rules (s.80 ITA)

### When Debt Forgiveness Becomes Income
If a commercial debt is forgiven or settled for less than face value, the debtor has a "forgiven amount" that gets applied in this order:
1. Reduce loss carryforwards (non-capital losses, then farm losses, then restricted farm losses)
2. Reduce CCA/UCC of depreciable property
3. Reduce ACB of capital property
4. Reduce allowable capital losses and net capital losses
5. 50% of any remaining amount is taxable income (s.80(13))

### The Planning Angle
- If CC borrows for business and the debt is later forgiven (e.g., negotiated settlement with a creditor), there's a tax cost
- **Strategy:** ensure loss carryforwards exist to absorb the forgiven amount before it becomes taxable
- Related-party debt forgiveness (s.80.4): shareholder loans from a corporation have specific rules -- interest must be imputed at CRA's prescribed rate (currently 5% for Q1 2026)

## 3.5 Bad Debt Deductions (s.20(1)(p) ITA)

### Writing Off Uncollectible Client Invoices
- If CC has invoiced a client and previously included the amount in income, AND the debt becomes uncollectible, CC can deduct it as a bad debt
- Must be established as uncollectible (reasonable collection efforts failed)
- Cannot cherry-pick: must write off the ENTIRE debt to the client, not just a portion

### The Timing Play
- Invoice client in Year 1, include in income
- Client doesn't pay, all collection efforts exhausted in Year 2
- Deduct bad debt in Year 2
- If client later pays in Year 3: include recovered amount in Year 3 income (s.12(1)(i))

### CC Application
- OASIS client invoices that go unpaid -- track them separately
- Send collection notices (emails count), attempt to collect, then write off after reasonable period (6-12 months)
- Keep the documentation trail: invoice, payment reminders, final notice, write-off memo

---

## 3.6 Reserve for Doubtful Debts (s.20(1)(l) ITA)

### The Distinction
- Bad debt (s.20(1)(p)): completely uncollectible, written off permanently
- Doubtful debt (s.20(1)(l)): might be collectible, reserve created against it

### How It Works
- At year-end, assess all outstanding receivables
- Create a reserve for any amount that is doubtful
- Deduct the reserve in Year 1
- Add the reserve back to income in Year 2 (s.12(1)(d))
- Create a new reserve in Year 2 (net effect: only the CHANGE in reserve affects income)

### CC Application
- If OASIS has $20K in receivables at year-end and $5K is doubtful, claim a $5K reserve
- This effectively defers $5K of income by one year
- Repeat annually -- the reserve "rolls" forward

## 3.7 Prepaid Expenses -- Timing Strategies (s.18(9) ITA)

### The General Rule
Prepaid expenses for services extending beyond the end of the tax year are generally not deductible until the period to which they relate (s.18(9)).

### The Exceptions (What IS Deductible When Paid)
1. **Inventory:** prepaid inventory costs are deductible when paid
2. **Amounts under $1,000:** CRA's administrative policy generally allows small prepaid amounts
3. **12-month rule:** if the period covered is 12 months or less and ends before the end of the NEXT tax year, many accountants deduct it immediately (CRA practice, not law)
4. **Insurance premiums:** business insurance for a period starting before year-end can be fully deducted

### The Strategy
- Pre-pay 12 months of deductible business expenses in December (SaaS subscriptions, insurance, professional dues, hosting, domain renewals)
- Accelerates deductions into the current year
- Combined with the 12-month administrative practice, this is low risk

### CC Application
- December: prepay annual software subscriptions (Anthropic API, hosting, domain, professional tools)
- Prepay January rent or co-working space
- Prepay next year's professional association dues

## 3.8 Lease vs. Buy Analysis for Vehicles and Equipment

### Vehicle -- The Math

**Buying (CCA Class 10.1 if >$37,000 before tax):**
- Max CCA cost: $37,000 + tax (2025 threshold)
- Class 10.1: 30% declining balance
- Year 1 (with immediate expensing): potentially 100% of $37,000 cap
- Interest deductible: max $300/month ($3,600/year)

**Leasing:**
- Max deductible lease payment: $950/month ($11,400/year) for 2025
- Business-use percentage applies to both

**When Leasing Wins:**
- Vehicle costs >$37,000 (CCA cap limits the buy deduction)
- Short hold period (2-3 years)
- Low down payment available
- CC wants to preserve cash

**When Buying Wins:**
- Vehicle costs <=$37,000
- Long hold period (5+ years)
- Immediate expensing available (100% Year 1)
- CC has cash or low-interest financing

### Equipment
- Almost always buy (with immediate expensing, 100% deduction in Year 1)
- Leasing only makes sense if cash preservation is critical or the equipment depreciates faster than CCA rates

## 3.10 Club Membership Dues -- What's Deductible

## 3.13 Automobile Expenses -- The Logbook Strategy

### The Overlooked CRA Concession
- CRA accepts a "base year" logbook approach: keep a detailed logbook for ONE full year, then only need to track for a 3-month sample period in subsequent years
- If the 3-month sample is within 10% of the base year, you can use the base year percentage
- Most people either keep no logbook (risky) or keep one forever (unnecessary)

### CC Application
- Track one full year of vehicle usage for business (client meetings, equipment transport, DJ gigs, office supply runs)
- After that, 3-month sample annually
- Business-use percentage applies to: gas, insurance, maintenance, CCA, interest, license, parking

### The Full Rules

| Situation | Deduction Rate |
|-----------|---------------|
| Business meals with clients | 50% |
| Business meals while travelling for business (away 12+ hours) | 50% |
| Long-haul truckers / air transport employees | 80% |
| Fund-raising events for registered charities | 100% |
| Employer-provided meals for all employees (6+ events/year is the threshold for social events) | 100% |
| Company Christmas party / 6 or fewer special events per year | 100% |
| Food and beverages provided on a construction site in a remote work location | 100% |

### The Self-Employed Travel Meal Play
- When CC travels for OASIS (client meetings, conferences, DJ gigs in other cities): meals are 50% deductible
- Use the simplified method: flat rate per meal without receipts ($23/meal or actual costs -- choose per trip)
- CRA flat rates: breakfast $23.00, lunch $23.15, dinner $56.50 (2025 rates vary by province)

## 3.15 Home Office -- Maximizing the Deduction

### Sole Proprietor Method (T2125)

**Eligible expenses (pro-rated by office %):**
- Rent (if renting)
- Mortgage INTEREST (not principal -- never principal)
- Property tax
- Utilities (heat, electricity, water)
- Home insurance
- Internet (business %)
- Maintenance and repairs
- CCA on the home (CAUTION: triggers capital gains on that portion when sold -- usually not recommended)

### The Dedicated Space Play
- "Principally" (>50%) used for business: no time-use restriction needed
- If used <50% for business: must also be used on a "regular and continuous basis" for meeting clients
- CC's home office: if dedicated to OASIS/trading, it's >50% business use
- Optimal %: measure square footage of office vs. total home. If 200 sq ft office in 1,000 sq ft home = 20%

### The CCA Warning
- Claiming CCA on the home office portion converts that % of your home from personal-use to income-producing property
- When you sell: that portion does NOT qualify for the Principal Residence Exemption
- **Rule:** NEVER claim CCA on a home office in a property you plan to sell. The interest/property tax/utilities deduction is almost always sufficient.

# SECTION 4: Tax Calendar Optimization

## 4.1 Month-by-Month Tax Calendar for Canadian Self-Employed Entrepreneur

### JANUARY
- **Jan 1:** New tax year begins. New TFSA room available ($7,000 for 2025, check annual limit).
- **Jan 1:** New FHSA room available ($8,000 annual, $40,000 lifetime).
- **Jan 15:** Quarterly installment due (March 15, June 15, September 15, December 15 -- but CRA says Jan 15 for non-standard installment payers using "no-calculation" option).
- **Jan 31:** T4s and T4As from employers due to be issued.
- **Action:** Organize all receipts from prior year. Pull OASIS invoices, DJ contracts, equipment receipts. Reconcile bank/credit card statements.

### FEBRUARY
- **Feb 28:** Last day for RRSP contributions counting toward PRIOR tax year deduction (60 days after Dec 31).
- **Action:** Max out FHSA and RRSP contributions for prior year if not already done. Calculate optimal RRSP deduction (may be better to carry forward deduction to a higher-income year).
- **Action:** Download all T-slips from CRA MyAccount (T3, T5, T4, T5008).
- **Action:** Calculate crypto ACB for all dispositions in the prior year.

### MARCH
- **Mar 1:** RRSP deadline (in non-leap years, Feb 28; in leap years, Feb 29 -- effectively "March 1" of the year).
- **Mar 15:** Q1 installment due (if required).
- **Action:** Begin preparing T1 return. T2125 for OASIS and DJing. Schedule 3 for capital gains. T5008 reconciliation for investments.
- **Action:** Review all deductions against this masterlist. Check every section.

### APRIL
- **Apr 30:** TAX PAYMENT DEADLINE for everyone (including self-employed). Even though the return isn't due until June 15, taxes OWED are due April 30. Interest accrues from May 1.
- **Apr 30:** T1 filing deadline for employed individuals (CC's Nicky's income).
- **Action:** File early if expecting a refund (get it faster). If owing, file by April 30 and pay what you can -- penalties are worse than interest.
- **Action:** Pay any balance owing. If unable to pay in full, pay as much as possible and contact CRA for a payment arrangement.

### MAY
- **Action:** Review Notice of Assessment when received. Check for errors, missing credits, carryforward balances.
- **Action:** If NOA is wrong, file T1-ADJ within 90 days (though the objection deadline is 90 days, adjustments can be requested within 10 years).

### JUNE
- **Jun 15:** T1 FILING DEADLINE for self-employed individuals and their spouses.
- **Jun 15:** Q2 installment due.
- **Action:** ABSOLUTELY file by June 15 even if you can't pay. Late-filing penalty is 5% + 1%/month (max 12 months). This is BRUTAL and compounds with prior late-filing penalties (10% + 2%/month if repeat offender).

### JULY
- **Jul 1:** Canada Day. Also: new GST/HST credit payments begin (quarterly).
- **Action:** Review mid-year business income. Estimate full-year income for installment planning.
- **Action:** Q3 estimated installment planning -- will you owe >$3,000 in net tax for the year?

### AUGUST
- **Action:** Tax-loss harvesting review #1. Check unrealized losses in non-registered accounts.
- **Action:** Review crypto positions for superficial loss rule planning (need 30 days + 1 between sale and repurchase).

### SEPTEMBER
- **Sep 15:** Q3 installment due.
- **Action:** Back-to-school -- any eligible education credits? Canada Training Credit accumulation check.
- **Action:** Review business expenses year-to-date. Any missing receipts to collect?

### OCTOBER
- **Action:** Accelerate expenses if income is higher than expected (prepay subscriptions, buy equipment).
- **Action:** Defer income if possible (delay invoicing clients until January if cash flow allows).
- **Action:** Charitable donation planning -- donate appreciated securities before year-end.

### NOVEMBER
- **Action:** Tax-loss harvesting execution. Sell losers, wait 31 days before repurchasing (superficial loss rule).
- **Action:** Equipment purchases for immediate expensing (must be acquired and available for use before Dec 31).
- **Action:** FHSA/TFSA/RRSP contribution planning for current year. Max FHSA first ($8K), then TFSA ($7K), then RRSP.

### DECEMBER
- **Dec 15:** Q4 installment due.
- **Dec 31:** Last day for current tax year transactions.
- **Dec 31:** Last day to sell securities for capital gains/losses in the current year (settlement date matters -- for stocks, typically T+1 so trade by Dec 29 or 30 to settle by Dec 31; check exchange settlement rules).
- **Action:** Final expense prepayment push (subscriptions, insurance, professional dues).
- **Action:** Finalize all bookkeeping for the year.
- **Action:** Ensure all invoices sent before year-end are recorded (accrual accounting) or collected (cash accounting).
- **Action:** Year-end inventory of equipment for CCA schedules.
- **Action:** Confirm all charitable donation receipts are in hand.

## 4.2 Quarterly Installment Obligations

### When Installments Are Required
- If your net tax owing (federal + provincial - amounts withheld at source) exceeds **$3,000** in the current year AND either of the two preceding years
- CC: once OASIS income is significant enough that T4 withholding from Nicky's doesn't cover the full tax bill

### The Three Calculation Methods (Choose the Lowest)
1. **Current-year method:** Pay 1/4 of estimated current-year tax each quarter
2. **Prior-year method:** Pay 1/4 of last year's actual tax each quarter
3. **CRA's suggestion method:** Pay the amounts CRA suggests on your installment reminder (uses a blend of 2 prior years)

### Installment Interest
- CRA charges interest on late or insufficient installments at the prescribed rate (currently ~5%)
- BUT: there's a netting mechanism -- overpayments in one quarter offset underpayments in another
- **Strategy:** front-load installments if possible (pay more in Q1 and Q2) to generate offsetting credits against Q3/Q4

### The $3,000 Threshold Planning
- If CC's net tax owing is close to $3,000: consider strategies to stay UNDER (accelerate deductions, maximize RRSP) to avoid the administrative burden of quarterly installments
- Once over: automate the payments through CRA MyAccount

## 4.3 Optimal Timing for Equipment Purchases

### The December vs. January Decision

**Buy in December if:**
- You want the CCA deduction in the CURRENT tax year
- Equipment must be "available for use" before year-end (delivered, set up, functional)
- Current year income is higher than expected next year
- Immediate expensing election is available (100% Year 1)

**Buy in January if:**
- Next year's income will be higher (deduction worth more at a higher marginal rate)
- Cash flow is tight in December
- Equipment won't be set up and available for use before Dec 31 anyway
- You're approaching the $1.5M immediate expensing limit (extremely unlikely for CC)

### The "Available for Use" Rule (s.13(26))
- CCA can only be claimed once property is available for use
- Generally: the earlier of (a) when first used for earning income, and (b) the second tax year after acquisition
- For CC: buy a laptop Dec 20, set it up Dec 22, use it Dec 23 for client work = available for use in the current year

## 4.4 RRSP Contribution Deadline and Strategy

### The 60-Day Rule
- Contributions made in the first 60 days of a year can be deducted in the PRIOR year
- 2025 tax year: contribute by March 1, 2026 (or Feb 28 if not a leap year) to deduct on 2025 return
- Also: you CAN deduct in the current year instead if it's more tax-optimal. You're not forced to deduct in the prior year.

### Deduction Carry-Forward
- Contributing and NOT deducting is a valid strategy
- If CC is in a low tax bracket now but expects higher income later, contribute now (gets the tax-sheltered growth) but carry forward the deduction to a higher-income year
- No limit on how long you can carry forward unused RRSP deductions

### Over-Contribution Buffer
- $2,000 lifetime over-contribution allowed without penalty
- Over-contribution beyond $2,000: 1%/month penalty tax
- **Strategy:** maintain a $2,000 over-contribution to maximize tax-sheltered growth with no penalty

## 4.5 FHSA Contribution Timing

### Key Dates
- $8,000 annual limit, $40,000 lifetime
- Unused room carries forward (max $8,000 carryforward per year, so max $16,000 in one year)
- Account must be open for contributions to count -- open the account EVEN IF contributing $0 to start the clock
- 15-year maximum account life OR end of year you turn 71 (whichever first)
- Must make a qualifying withdrawal (first home purchase) or close within 15 years

### The FHSA Advantage Stack
- Contribution: tax-deductible (like RRSP)
- Growth: tax-free (like TFSA)
- Withdrawal for first home: tax-free (unlike RRSP HBP which must be repaid)
- This is the single best registered account for first-time homebuyers. CC should max this FIRST.

### Transfer Rules
- Can transfer from FHSA to RRSP (no tax, no contribution room impact) if you DON'T buy a home
- Cannot transfer from RRSP to FHSA

## 4.6 T1 Adjustment Deadlines

### Normal Adjustments (T1-ADJ / ReFILE)
- **10 calendar years** from the end of the tax year
- 2015 return: adjustable until end of 2025
- File via CRA MyAccount (ReFILE), by mail (T1-ADJ), or through tax software
- Multiple adjustments to the same year are allowed

### What to Adjust For
- Missed deductions or credits found in this masterlist
- Corrected T-slips received after filing
- Previously overlooked medical expenses (can claim a 12-month period ending in the tax year)
- Unclaimed tuition carryforwards from Bishop's University
- Retroactive DTC approval (amend all eligible years)
- Foreign tax credits not previously claimed

### Limitation
- CRA can reassess you within 3 years of the original Notice of Assessment (normal reassessment period)
- For fraud/misrepresentation: no time limit on reassessment
- Voluntary disclosure: separate program with its own rules if you have unreported income

---

## 4.7 Notice of Objection Deadline

### The 90-Day Rule
- **90 days from the date on the Notice of Assessment/Reassessment**
- File by mail or through CRA MyAccount
- Must clearly state the facts and reasons for the objection
- CRA assigns to Appeals division (separate from Audit)

### What Happens After
1. CRA Appeals reviews independently
2. They may: confirm, vary, or vacate the assessment
3. If you disagree with Appeals: Tax Court of Canada (informal procedure for amounts under $25,000 federal tax -- no lawyer needed)

### The Extended Deadline
- If you missed the 90 days: you can apply to CRA for an extension up to ONE YEAR after the 90-day deadline (s.166.1)
- Must demonstrate: reasonable grounds AND a genuine intention to object within the 90 days

### CC Application
- ALWAYS review NOAs within 2 weeks of receiving them
- If CRA disallows a legitimate deduction: object within 90 days. Don't let it slide.

# APPENDIX B: CRA Audit Risk Factors
