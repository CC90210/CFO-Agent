## Section 1: Insurance Strategy (Tax-Advantaged)

### 1.1 Life Insurance — Term vs Whole vs Universal

| Feature | Term Life | Whole Life | Universal Life (UL) |
|---------|-----------|------------|---------------------|
| **Duration** | Fixed period (10, 20, 30 years) | Lifetime (to age 100+) | Lifetime (flexible) |
| **Premiums** | Lowest initially; increase at renewal | Level, highest initially | Flexible (min to max) |
| **Cash Surrender Value (CSV)** | None | Yes — guaranteed + dividends | Yes — investment component |
| **Tax-sheltered growth** | No | Yes (exempt policy) | Yes (exempt policy) |
| **Best for** | Pure death benefit, young families, cheap coverage | Forced savings, estate planning, COLI | Maximum tax-sheltered investment room, sophisticated planning |
| **CC relevance now** | High — cheap coverage while building wealth | Low — premiums too expensive at current income | Low — same reason |

**When each makes sense for CC:**

- **Now (age 22, sole prop):** Term 20 or Term 30. Cheapest way to cover business debts, protect family if applicable, and lock in insurability while healthy. A healthy 22-year-old male can get $500K term-20 for ~$20-30/month.
- **After incorporation (revenue > $80K):** Consider converting a portion to whole/UL inside the corporation for COLI strategy (see below).
- **Age 35-45 with established corp:** Prime time for corporate-owned permanent insurance as a tax-sheltered retirement vehicle.

### 1.2 Corporate-Owned Life Insurance (COLI) — The Insured Retirement Strategy

This is the single most powerful legal tax strategy available to Canadian private corporation owners. It turns life insurance into a tax-free retirement income stream.

#### How It Works — Step by Step

1. **Corporation purchases permanent life insurance** (whole or UL) on the shareholder's life.
2. **Premiums paid with corporate after-tax dollars** — not deductible, but paid from the corp's lower-taxed income (12.2% SBD rate in Ontario vs 53.53% personal top rate).
3. **Cash surrender value (CSV) grows tax-free** inside an exempt policy — no annual taxation on investment gains within the policy.
4. **At retirement, shareholder borrows against the CSV** from a third-party lender (bank) using the policy as collateral. This is NOT a policy disposition — no tax triggered (s.148(1) ITA).
5. **Loan proceeds are not income** — borrowing is not a taxable event in Canada.
6. **Interest on the loan can be deductible** if loan proceeds are used for income-producing purposes (21-year interest deductibility strategy).
7. **At death, the death benefit pays off the loan** and the remainder flows through the **Capital Dividend Account (CDA)** — extracted to the estate/beneficiaries **completely tax-free**.

#### Exempt Policy Limits — Maximum Tax Actuarial Reserve (MTAR)

The ITA (s.306, Reg. 306) defines the maximum amount of investment that can accumulate tax-free inside a life insurance policy. Key rules:

- **Exempt test policy (ETP):** CRA tests whether the policy's CSV exceeds what a notional reference policy would accumulate. If it exceeds the limit, the policy loses its exempt status and all growth becomes annually taxable.
- **MTAR (Maximum Tax Actuarial Reserve):** The ceiling on tax-sheltered accumulation. Calculated actuarially based on age, face amount, and policy type.
- **Practical impact:** You cannot over-fund a policy. The insurance company structures premiums to stay within exempt limits. Pay the maximum allowed premium to maximize tax-free growth.
- **2017 rule changes:** Reduced exempt room for policies issued after 2016. Policies issued before 2017 have more generous limits (grandfathered).

#### Collateral Borrowing Against CSV

- The shareholder (or corporation) pledges the policy's CSV as collateral for a bank loan.
- **Key legal point:** Assigning a policy as collateral is NOT a disposition under s.148(1)(b) ITA — no taxable event.
- **Banks that do this:** Major Canadian banks (RBC, TD, BMO) and specialized lenders (B2B Bank, Manulife Bank) all offer collateral insurance loans.
- **Loan-to-CSV ratio:** Typically 75-90% of CSV.
- **Interest:** Prime + 0.5% to Prime + 1.5%. Interest accrues and is added to the loan balance (no payments required if structured correctly).
- **At death:** Death benefit pays loan first, remainder goes to beneficiary/estate via CDA.

#### CDA — Capital Dividend Account

The CDA is a notional account that tracks amounts a private corporation can pay out as **tax-free capital dividends**. The life insurance death benefit (minus the policy's ACB) is credited to the CDA.

- **s.89(1) ITA:** Death benefit received by corporation, less the ACB of the policy, is added to the CDA.
- **s.83(2) ITA:** Corporation can elect to pay capital dividends from CDA — received tax-free by shareholders.
- **Estate benefit:** The death benefit → CDA → tax-free capital dividend to estate → bypasses both corporate and personal tax.

#### Numerical Example — CC's COLI Strategy

**Assumptions:**
- CC incorporates OASIS at age 25 (revenue > $80K trigger)
- Corporation purchases UL policy on CC's life at age 30
- Face amount: $1,000,000
- Annual premium: $20,000/year (paid from corporate after-tax income)
- Policy CSV growth: 5% net annual (conservative for equity-indexed UL)
- CC retires at age 60 (30 years of premium payments)
- Collateral loan at Prime + 1% (assume 5.5% blended)
- CC dies at age 85

**Accumulation Phase (Age 30-60):**

| Year | Age | Cumulative Premiums | Est. CSV | Death Benefit |
|------|-----|-------------------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | 30 | $20,000 | $12,000 | $1,000,000 |
| 10 | 40 | $200,000 | $185,000 | $1,000,000 |
| 20 | 50 | $400,000 | $520,000 | $1,050,000 |
| 30 | 60 | $600,000 | $1,050,000 | $1,200,000 |

**Retirement Phase (Age 60-85):**
- CC borrows $80,000/year against CSV as collateral loan.
- Over 25 years: $2,000,000 in tax-free retirement income drawn.
- Loan balance at death (with accrued interest at 5.5%): ~$3,200,000.
- Death benefit at age 85 (assuming participating/increasing): ~$1,800,000-$2,500,000.

**At Death:**
- Death benefit pays off loan balance.
- If structured with sufficient face amount, net proceeds flow through CDA tax-free.
- **Total tax-free income received during retirement: ~$2,000,000.**
- **Compared to taxable withdrawals:** At 50% marginal rate, CC would need $4,000,000 in pre-tax corporate income to net $2,000,000. The COLI strategy effectively doubles the spending power.

**Important caveats:**
- The numbers above are illustrative. Actual returns depend on the policy's investment performance.
- If CSV growth underperforms, the collateral loan may exceed death benefit — requiring additional premiums or reduced borrowing.
- Work with an insurance actuary and tax advisor to model specific scenarios.

### 1.3 Key Person Insurance

**What it is:** Life or disability insurance on a person critical to the business (CC, for OASIS).

**Tax treatment:**
- **Premiums:** Generally NOT deductible as a business expense (CRA's position: capital expenditure, not current expense). Exception: if required by a lender as a condition of a loan, the portion related to the loan may be deductible.
- **Death benefit:** Tax-free to the corporation (s.148(1) ITA). Credited to CDA if the corporation is the beneficiary.
- **Why it matters for OASIS:** If CC is the sole operator and something happens, key person insurance provides the corporation with funds to hire replacements, cover contracts, or wind down operations.

**Recommendation for CC:**
- Not a priority until OASIS has employees or contractual obligations that require continuity.
- Revisit when OASIS revenue exceeds $200K or CC hires first full-time employee.

### 1.4 Disability Insurance — Critical for Self-Employed

CC has **zero** disability coverage. No employer benefits. No EI (self-employed must opt in, and the benefit is minimal). This is the single biggest insurance gap.

#### Individual Disability Policy

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **What it covers** | Replaces income if CC cannot work due to illness or injury |
| **Benefit amount** | Typically 60-70% of pre-disability income (max ~$10K-15K/month) |
| **Waiting period** | 30, 60, 90, or 120 days. Longer wait = lower premium. |
| **Benefit period** | 2 years, 5 years, or to age 65. Always choose "to age 65" — anything shorter is inadequate. |
| **Definition of disability** | "Own occupation" (can't do YOUR job) vs "any occupation" (can't do ANY job). ALWAYS get own-occ for at least 2 years. |
| **Cost for a 22-year-old male, non-smoker** | ~$80-150/month for $3,000-5,000/month benefit to age 65, 90-day wait |

#### Tax Treatment of Disability Insurance

This is critical and often misunderstood:

| Who Pays Premium? | Premium Deductible? | Benefit Taxable? |
|-------------------|---------------------|------------------|
| CC personally | No | **No — benefits are tax-free** |
| Corporation | Yes (as employee benefit) | **Yes — benefits are taxable income** |
| Corporation (not deducted) | No (choose not to deduct) | **No — benefits are tax-free** |

**Atlas recommendation:** Pay disability premiums personally (or have corp pay but don't deduct). Tax-free benefits when you need them most are worth far more than the small deduction on premiums.

#### Business Overhead Expense (BOE) Disability

- Separate policy that covers fixed business expenses (rent, software subscriptions, salaries for staff) if CC is disabled.
- **Premiums ARE deductible** as a business expense.
- **Benefits ARE taxable** but offset by the deductible expenses they cover — net neutral.
- Not needed until OASIS has significant fixed overhead.

### 1.5 Critical Illness Insurance

- Pays a **tax-free lump sum** upon diagnosis of a covered condition (cancer, heart attack, stroke — typically 25+ conditions).
- Not a replacement for disability insurance — complementary.
- Can be used for treatment, experimental procedures, lifestyle changes, mortgage payoff.
- **Cost for 22-year-old male:** ~$40-80/month for $100K coverage.
- **Return of premium (ROP) rider:** Get all premiums back if you never claim. Costs more but eliminates the "wasted premium" concern.
- **Atlas recommendation:** Lower priority than disability insurance. Consider adding once income exceeds $100K or after incorporation.

### 1.6 Health Spending Account (HSA) — For Incorporated Businesses

Once CC incorporates OASIS, an HSA becomes one of the best small-business tax tools available.

**How it works:**
1. Corporation establishes an HSA (also called a Private Health Services Plan — PHSP).
2. CC submits eligible medical expenses (dental, vision, prescriptions, paramedical, mental health, etc.).
3. Corporation reimburses CC through the HSA.
4. **Corporation deducts the reimbursement as a business expense** (s.18(1)(a) ITA).
5. **CC receives the reimbursement tax-free** (s.6(1)(a) exception for PHSP).

**Key rules:**
- Must cover a "class of employees" — if CC is the only employee, CC is the class. That's fine.
- Annual maximum: $15,000 for individual, $30,000 for family (CRA administrative guidelines, not legislated — some plans allow more).
- Cannot cover cosmetic procedures, gym memberships, or non-medical expenses.
- Third-party administrator required (cost: ~$200-500/year). Providers: Olympia Benefits, Benecaid, Health Spending Account Solutions.

**Example:** CC has $5,000/year in dental, vision, and therapy costs. Without HSA, these are paid with after-tax personal dollars (cost: ~$10,000 pre-tax at 50% marginal rate). With HSA, the corporation deducts $5,000 at the 12.2% SBD tax rate, saving CC ~$4,400/year in combined tax.

### 1.7 Professional Liability / E&O Insurance for OASIS

**Errors & Omissions (E&O) / Professional Liability Insurance:**

- Covers claims that OASIS's AI consulting services caused financial harm to a client (bad advice, software bug, data breach, missed deadline).
- **Essential for AI consulting** — if an AI system CC builds causes a client financial loss, E&O insurance covers legal defense and settlements.
- **Cost:** ~$500-2,000/year for $1M-2M coverage (varies by revenue and services).
- **Premiums are 100% deductible** as a business expense.
- **Providers:** Federated Insurance, Intact, Trisura (tech-specific), Coalition (cyber + E&O combo).

**Cyber liability insurance:**
- Covers data breaches, ransomware, client data exposure.
- Often bundled with E&O for tech companies.
- **Strongly recommended** once OASIS handles client data or deploys AI systems that process sensitive information.

## Section 2: Estate Planning for Entrepreneurs

### 2.1 Will and Powers of Attorney — The Non-Negotiable Basics

Every adult needs three documents. CC has none of these yet (assumption — confirm).

#### 1. Last Will and Testament

- Directs how assets are distributed after death.
- Names an executor (estate trustee) to administer the estate.
- Without a will (dying "intestate"), Ontario's Succession Law Reform Act distributes assets by formula — likely not what CC wants.
- **Cost:** $500-1,500 for a basic will through a lawyer. Online options (Willful, LegalWills.ca) are $100-300 but may not handle business assets properly.
- **For a business owner:** The will MUST address what happens to OASIS (wind down, sell, transfer), trading accounts, crypto wallets, and digital assets.

#### 2. Power of Attorney for Property (Continuing)

- Appoints someone to manage CC's finances if incapacitated (pays bills, manages investments, runs the business).
- "Continuing" means it survives incapacity — this is the whole point.
- Without one, someone must apply to the court to become CC's "guardian of property" — expensive ($5K-15K), slow (months), and the court chooses who.

#### 3. Power of Attorney for Personal Care

- Appoints someone to make health and medical decisions if CC cannot.
- Includes end-of-life wishes, treatment preferences.
- Without one, the Health Care Consent Act hierarchy applies (spouse → parent → sibling) but this can cause disputes.

**Atlas recommendation:** Get all three done by a lawyer. Budget $1,500-2,500 total. This is the single highest-ROI legal spend CC can make right now.

### 2.2 Beneficiary Designations — Bypass Estate, Avoid Probate

Assets with named beneficiaries pass **directly** to the beneficiary outside the estate. They are not subject to probate fees and are not governed by the will.

| Account | Can Name Beneficiary? | Probate Avoided? | Notes |
|---------|----------------------|------------------|-------|
| **TFSA** | Yes (successor holder or beneficiary) | Yes | Successor holder = spouse continues TFSA tax-free. Beneficiary = collapses to cash. |
| **RRSP/RRIF** | Yes | Yes | Spouse/CLP as beneficiary = tax-deferred rollover. Anyone else = fully taxable to deceased. |
| **FHSA** | Yes | Yes | Similar to RRSP treatment. Spouse can receive tax-deferred rollover. |
| **Life insurance** | Yes | Yes | Beneficiary receives death benefit directly. Not part of estate. |
| **RESP** | No (subscriber's estate) | No | Subscriber's estate gets refund of contributions. Government grants returned. EAP to beneficiary. |
| **Non-registered accounts** | No | No | Pass through estate/will. |
| **Corporation shares** | No | No | Pass through estate/will (unless structured otherwise). |

**Key action:** Ensure every registered account and insurance policy has an up-to-date named beneficiary. Review annually.

### 2.3 Ontario Probate Fees — Estate Administration Tax

Ontario charges the highest probate fees in Canada:

| Estate Value | Tax Rate | Fee |
|-------------|----------|-----|
| First $50,000 | 0.5% | $250 |
| Amount over $50,000 | **1.5%** | Calculated on excess |

**Example:** $1,000,000 estate → $250 + ($950,000 x 1.5%) = **$14,500** in probate fees.

These fees are based on the **gross value** of assets that pass through the estate (not net of debts). Assets with named beneficiaries, jointly-held assets with right of survivorship, and assets in certain trusts are excluded.

### 2.4 Probate Avoidance Strategies

#### Strategy 1: Named Beneficiaries on Registered Accounts + Insurance
- Already covered above. Simplest and most effective.
- **Saves:** 1.5% of the value of all registered accounts and insurance.

#### Strategy 2: Joint Ownership with Right of Survivorship (JTWROS)

- When one joint owner dies, the asset passes automatically to the surviving owner(s) — outside the estate.
- **Works for:** Bank accounts, investment accounts, real estate.
- **Risks:** The other joint owner has full access to the asset during your lifetime. Creditor claims against the other owner can attach to the asset. Potential deemed disposition at fair market value for tax purposes.
- **Not recommended for:** Unmarried individuals adding parents as joint owners purely for probate avoidance (CRA may treat this as a taxable gift — see Pecore v. Pecore SCC 2007).

#### Strategy 3: Inter Vivos (Living) Trust

- A trust created during CC's lifetime to hold assets outside the estate.
- **How it works:** CC transfers assets to a trust. The trust holds and manages them. At CC's death, the trust distributes according to its terms — no probate needed because the assets are not in CC's estate.
- **Costs:** $2,000-5,000 to establish. Annual tax filing (T3 return). Ongoing trustee duties.
- **Drawback:** The **21-year deemed disposition rule** (see Section 2.8) means the trust must dispose of (or be deemed to dispose of) all capital property at FMV every 21 years — triggering capital gains tax.
- **Best for:** Holding private company shares, investment portfolios, real estate.

#### Strategy 4: Multiple Wills Strategy (Ontario-Specific)

This is the most powerful probate avoidance tool for Ontario business owners. Validated by Granovsky Estate v. Ontario (2000).

**How it works:**
- **Primary Will:** Covers assets that REQUIRE a Certificate of Appointment of Estate Trustee (probate) — bank accounts, publicly traded securities, real estate.
- **Secondary Will:** Covers assets that do NOT require probate — **private company shares**, personal property, household items, jewelry, vehicles.

**Why it works:**
- Only the Primary Will is submitted for probate. Probate fees are calculated only on assets covered by the Primary Will.
- Private company shares (OASIS shares after incorporation) do NOT require probate to transfer — the corporation's share register is updated by the estate trustee under the Secondary Will.

**Example for CC after incorporation:**
- OASIS shares worth $500,000 → Secondary Will (no probate).
- Bank accounts, TFSA, real estate worth $300,000 → Primary Will.
- **Probate fee:** Calculated on $300,000 (minus any beneficiary-designated amounts), not $800,000.
- **Savings:** Up to $7,500 in probate fees avoided on the OASIS shares alone.

**Requirements:**
- Both wills must be drafted by the same lawyer at the same time, with careful language ensuring they don't accidentally revoke each other.
- Each will must clearly define which assets it covers.
- Cost: ~$2,000-3,500 for the pair.

### 2.5 Alter Ego Trust (Age 65+)

Not relevant to CC now, but important for long-term planning:

- Available to individuals age 65+ (s.73(1.01)(c) ITA).
- CC transfers assets to the trust at cost (no immediate tax). CC is the sole beneficiary during lifetime. At CC's death, assets pass according to trust terms — outside the estate, no probate.
- **Advantages over living trust:** No deemed disposition on transfer (at-cost rollover). No 21-year deemed disposition while CC is alive.
- **Disadvantage:** Only available at 65+. CC has 43 years before this is relevant.

### 2.6 Estate Freeze (s.85/s.86 ITA)

Already covered in `docs/ATLAS_INCORPORATION_TAX_STRATEGIES.md`. Key points for estate planning:

**Purpose:** Lock the current value of the corporation in CC's hands (freeze shares) and have all future growth accrue to the next generation (new common shares held by family trust or children directly).

**How it works (s.86 — simpler method):**
1. CC exchanges existing common shares of OASIS HoldCo for preferred shares with a fixed redemption value equal to current FMV.
2. New common shares (nominal value) are issued to a family trust or CC's children.
3. All future corporate growth accrues to the new common shares.
4. At CC's death, only the frozen preferred share value is included in the estate.

**When to do it:**
- After incorporation, once the corporation has significant value ($500K+).
- Earlier is better — the lower the current FMV, the less tax at death.
- Can be "re-frozen" if the corporation's value drops (redo the freeze at a lower FMV).

**Tax at death without freeze:** Full FMV of shares is a deemed disposition. If OASIS is worth $5M at death, capital gains tax on $5M.
**Tax at death with freeze done at $500K:** Capital gains tax only on $500K (freeze value). The remaining $4.5M of growth is in the hands of the next generation at nominal cost.

### 2.7 21-Year Deemed Disposition Rule on Trusts

Every 21 years from the creation of a trust (other than alter ego, joint partner, or qualified disability trusts while the relevant beneficiary is alive), the trust is deemed to dispose of all capital property at FMV (s.104(4) ITA).

**Impact:** Capital gains tax is triggered even though no actual sale occurs. This can be devastating for trusts holding appreciated shares or real estate.

**Planning strategies:**
1. **Distribute assets to beneficiaries before the 21-year mark.** Transfer at cost to Canadian-resident beneficiaries under s.107(2).
2. **Wind up the trust** at year 20 and distribute to beneficiaries.
3. **Use a series of shorter-term trusts** — though CRA may challenge this as aggressive planning.
4. **For family trusts holding OASIS shares:** Ensure the trust's terms allow distribution to beneficiaries before the 21st anniversary.

### 2.8 Graduated Rate Estate (GRE)

For the first **36 months** after death, a qualifying estate is taxed at **graduated rates** (the same progressive brackets as individuals) rather than the top marginal rate.

**Requirements:**
- Must be designated as a GRE in the first T3 return.
- Only ONE estate per individual can be a GRE.
- Must arise as a consequence of death (i.e., the person's actual estate, not a pre-existing trust).

**Benefits:**
- Income earned in the estate during the 36-month window is taxed at graduated rates. First ~$55K of income taxed at lower brackets — significant savings vs top rate.
- GRE can claim charitable donation tax credits (donations made by will).
- GRE has a flexible tax year-end (doesn't have to be December 31).
- Losses in the estate's first year can be carried back to the deceased's final return.

**Planning:** Don't rush to distribute all estate assets immediately. Keeping income-producing assets in the GRE for up to 36 months can save significant tax through the graduated brackets.

### 2.9 Pipeline Planning — Extracting Corporate Value After Death

**The double-tax problem:**
When a shareholder dies, they have a deemed disposition of their shares at FMV, triggering capital gains tax. Then, when the estate winds up the corporation, the same value is taxed again — once as a capital gain on the shares, and again as a taxable dividend when corporate assets are distributed.

**Pipeline strategy:**
1. After death, the estate's cost base in the shares is stepped up to FMV (because capital gains tax was paid on the deemed disposition).
2. The estate sells its shares to a related "pipeline" corporation (NewCo) for a promissory note equal to FMV.
3. The original corporation (OldCo) is amalgamated with NewCo or wound up into NewCo.
4. NewCo repays the promissory note to the estate over 1-5 years.
5. The estate receives cash — not a dividend — in repayment of the note. No additional tax.

**CRA's position:** CRA has challenged aggressive pipeline transactions under s.84.1 (non-arm's length surplus stripping). The 2024 amendments to s.84.1 have tightened rules. Pipeline planning remains viable but must be structured carefully with tax counsel.

**Atlas note:** This is post-mortem planning. CC's estate trustee and tax advisor will handle this. CC's role now is to ensure the will names a competent estate trustee and references a tax advisor.

## Section 3: Asset Protection for Canadian Entrepreneurs

### 3.1 What Creditors Can and Cannot Seize in Ontario

| Asset | Protected? | Legal Basis | Key Exceptions |
|-------|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **RRSP/RRIF** | **YES** | s.67(1)(b.3) BIA + Ontario Pension Benefits Act | Contributions made in 12 months before bankruptcy are clawable |
| **TFSA** | **YES** | s.67(1)(b.3) BIA | Same 12-month clawback on contributions before bankruptcy |
| **FHSA** | **YES** | Same treatment as RRSP | Same 12-month clawback |
| **Life insurance (CSV) with designated beneficiary** | **YES** | Ontario Insurance Act, s.196 | Beneficiary must be a "preferred beneficiary" (spouse, child, parent, grandchild) or irrevocably named |
| **RESP** | **Generally YES** | s.67(1)(b.3) BIA | Subscriber's contributions may be clawable. Government grants/income go to beneficiary. |
| **Principal residence** | **NO** | Ontario has NO homestead exemption | Can be seized, sold by creditors, or by trustee in bankruptcy |
| **Locked-in pension (LIRA/LIF)** | **YES** | Ontario Pension Benefits Act | Cannot be seized |
| **Personal property** | **Partial** | Ontario Execution Act, s.2 | Exemptions: $6,500 tools of trade, $13,150 household furnishings, $7,117 motor vehicle |
| **Corporate assets** | Behind corporate veil | OBCA | Veil can be pierced (see 3.3) |
| **Bank accounts** | **NO** | No protection | Fully seizable |
| **Non-registered investments** | **NO** | No protection | Fully seizable |
| **Real estate (non-principal)** | **NO** | No protection | Fully seizable |

**Critical takeaway for CC:** Ontario is one of the worst provinces for asset protection. No homestead exemption. Bank accounts and non-registered investments are fully exposed. The only truly safe assets are registered accounts (RRSP, TFSA, FHSA) and insurance with named beneficiaries.

### 3.2 The Ontario Homestead Exemption — There Isn't One

Unlike Alberta ($40,000 exemption), Saskatchewan ($32,000), and US states like Florida or Texas (unlimited), **Ontario provides ZERO homestead protection**.

- A creditor with a judgment can register a lien against CC's home.
- In bankruptcy, the trustee can force a sale of the home.
- The equity in the home is fully available to creditors.

**Implication:** Once CC buys a home, it is NOT a protected asset. This makes the OpCo/HoldCo structure (Section 3.4) even more important — keep liability-generating activities in OpCo, and accumulate wealth in HoldCo or registered accounts.

### 3.3 Corporate Veil — When It Protects, When It's Pierced

**The general rule:** A corporation is a separate legal person. CC is not personally liable for the corporation's debts (limited liability).

**When the veil protects CC:**
- Arm's-length commercial contracts (client doesn't pay, vendor sues — they can only go after OASIS Inc., not CC personally).
- General business debts (rent, subscriptions, equipment financing — assuming CC didn't personally guarantee).
- Tort claims against the business (if someone sues the business for negligence, CC's personal assets are generally protected).

**When the veil is pierced (CC IS personally liable):**
- **Personal guarantees:** If CC personally guaranteed a lease, loan, or credit line (banks almost always require this for small businesses).
- **Director liability:** Directors are personally liable for up to 6 months of unpaid employee wages (OBCA s.131), unremitted HST/GST (ETA s.323), unremitted payroll deductions (ITA s.227.1), and environmental cleanup orders.
- **Fraud or misrepresentation:** Using the corporation to commit fraud or as a "mere facade" can result in piercing.
- **Commingling funds:** If CC treats corporate and personal funds interchangeably, courts may disregard the corporate form.
- **Thin capitalization:** Incorporating with minimal capital and immediately loading the corp with debt can suggest the corporation was a sham.

**Best practices for CC:**
1. Never commingle personal and corporate funds. Separate bank accounts, always.
2. Maintain corporate records (minutes, resolutions, annual filings).
3. Avoid personally guaranteeing corporate obligations when possible.
4. Pay directors' liability items (HST, payroll) on time — always.
5. Maintain adequate E&O and general liability insurance.

### 3.4 OpCo/HoldCo Structure for Asset Protection

The gold standard for Canadian small business asset protection.

```
         CC (Individual)
              |
         [HoldCo] ← Holds: investments, excess cash, real estate, insurance policies
              |
         [OpCo = OASIS] ← Holds: client contracts, equipment, receivables, RISK
```

**How it works:**
1. **OpCo (OASIS)** conducts all business operations. It earns revenue, signs contracts, employs people, and carries all business risk.
2. **OpCo pays dividends to HoldCo** (tax-free intercorporate dividends under s.112 ITA — Part IV refundable tax may apply but is recoverable).
3. **HoldCo accumulates wealth:** Investments, retained earnings, real estate, insurance policies — all held by a corporation with no operational risk.
4. **If OpCo is sued or goes bankrupt:** HoldCo's assets are not reachable by OpCo's creditors (they are separate legal entities).

**Key benefits:**
- **Liability isolation:** Operational risk stays in OpCo. HoldCo's assets are protected.
- **Tax-deferred investment:** Investment income in HoldCo is subject to refundable taxes (Part I and Part IV) but integrates with the personal tax system upon dividend payout.
- **Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE):** If OpCo qualifies as a QSBC, HoldCo holding OpCo shares can facilitate purification and access the $1.25M LCGE on sale.
- **Estate freeze:** HoldCo is the ideal vehicle for an estate freeze (Section 2.6).
- **Insurance:** Corporate-owned life insurance in HoldCo, not OpCo. If OpCo fails, the insurance is safe.

**Cost:** ~$2,000-3,500 to incorporate HoldCo. Annual filing: ~$300-500. Annual T2 return: ~$1,000-1,500 if using an accountant.

**When to implement:** At or shortly after incorporating OASIS (revenue > $80K trigger). The HoldCo costs are minimal and the protection is significant.

### 3.5 Spousal Trust for Asset Protection

- Assets held in a spousal trust are owned by the trust, not by CC or CC's spouse.
- Provides creditor protection for the beneficiary spouse (creditors of the spouse generally cannot reach trust assets).
- Also provides family law protection — assets in a trust are NOT "net family property" for equalization purposes on marriage breakdown (Succession Law Reform Act, Family Law Act interaction).
- **Relevance for CC now:** Low — CC is 22 and presumably unmarried. File this for future reference.

### 3.6 Directors & Officers (D&O) Liability Insurance

- Covers legal costs and settlements when directors or officers are personally sued for decisions made in their corporate capacity.
- **Covers:** Breach of fiduciary duty, mismanagement allegations, employment disputes, regulatory investigations, shareholder lawsuits.
- **Does NOT cover:** Fraud, criminal acts, intentional misconduct.
- **Cost:** $1,500-5,000/year for small private corporations.
- **When CC needs it:** After incorporating OASIS, especially if CC brings on co-directors, investors, or advisory board members.

### 3.7 Shareholder Agreements — Essential Clauses

If CC ever takes on a co-founder, investor, or partner in OASIS, a **unanimous shareholder agreement (USA)** is critical.

**Key clauses:**

| Clause | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| **Shotgun (buy-sell)** | Either party can make an offer to buy the other out. The other party must either accept or buy the offerer's shares at the same price. Forces fair valuation. |
| **Drag-along** | If a majority shareholder wants to sell to a third party, they can force minority shareholders to sell on the same terms. Prevents minority holdout. |
| **Tag-along** | If a majority shareholder sells, minority shareholders can "tag along" and sell their shares on the same terms. Prevents majority from selling and leaving minority stuck. |
| **Right of first refusal (ROFR)** | Before selling shares to an outsider, the selling shareholder must first offer them to existing shareholders. |
| **Vesting schedule** | Shares vest over time (e.g., 4 years with 1-year cliff). Prevents a co-founder from leaving after 3 months with 50% of the company. |
| **Non-compete / non-solicit** | Departing shareholders cannot compete or poach clients for a defined period. |
| **Valuation methodology** | Pre-agreed formula for valuing shares (book value, earnings multiple, independent appraisal). Prevents disputes. |
| **Insurance-funded buy-sell** | Life insurance funds the buyout if a shareholder dies. The surviving shareholders use the death benefit to purchase the deceased's shares. |

**Cost:** $3,000-8,000 for a comprehensive USA drafted by a corporate lawyer.

### 3.8 Business Continuity Planning — What Happens to OASIS if CC Is Incapacitated

**Current risk:** CC is the sole operator of OASIS. If CC is hit by a bus tomorrow, OASIS has:
- No one to deliver client work
- No one to manage trading accounts
- No one to access business accounts
- Active trading bots running with real money

**Minimum continuity plan:**

1. **Power of Attorney for Property** (see Section 2.1) must explicitly authorize the attorney to manage business operations, access corporate bank accounts, and make decisions about OASIS.

2. **Emergency access document** (stored securely, NOT in the will itself):
   - List of all business accounts and access credentials
   - Trading account details (Kraken, OANDA) with instructions to CLOSE ALL POSITIONS immediately
   - Client list with contact information and contract status
   - Server/infrastructure access (hosting, domains, API keys)
   - Crypto wallet locations and seed phrase access procedure

3. **Emergency shutdown procedure for trading systems:**
   - How to kill all trading daemons
   - How to close all open positions
   - Who to contact at each broker
   - Where API keys are stored

4. **Designated person** who knows the plan exists and how to access it. This should be the attorney named in the Power of Attorney for Property.

- OANDA: Forex/gold positions (regulated by IIROC, covered by CIPF up to $1M)

**Rules:**
1. **Never store more than your active trading allocation on an exchange.** Excess goes to cold storage.
2. **Use exchanges regulated in your jurisdiction.** Kraken is registered with FINTRAC (Canada). Good.
3. **Enable all security features:** 2FA (hardware key, NOT SMS), withdrawal address whitelisting, API key IP restrictions.
4. **Diversify across exchanges** if holding significant amounts.

### 4.2 Hardware Wallet Security — Cold Storage Best Practices

**Recommended hardware wallets:**

| Wallet | Price | Key Features |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| **Ledger Nano X / Stax** | $150-400 | Bluetooth, wide coin support, Ledger Live app |
| **Trezor Model T / Safe 3** | $150-250 | Open source firmware, touchscreen, Shamir backup |
| **Coldcard Mk4** | $150 | Bitcoin-only, air-gapped, most paranoid security |
| **Keystone Pro** | $170 | Air-gapped (QR codes only), open source |

**Best practices:**

1. **Seed phrase storage:**
   - Write on metal (Cryptosteel, Billfodl) — survives fire and flood.
   - NEVER store digitally (no photos, no cloud, no password manager, no email).
   - Store in a fireproof safe or safety deposit box.
   - Consider splitting across two locations (e.g., first 12 words in safe at home, last 12 words in safety deposit box).

2. **Passphrase (25th word):**
   - Adds an extra layer of security. Even if someone gets your 24-word seed, they cannot access funds without the passphrase.
   - Store the passphrase SEPARATELY from the seed phrase.

3. **Test recovery:**
   - Before loading significant funds, test the recovery process with a small amount.
   - Verify the seed phrase restores correctly on a different device.

4. **Firmware updates:**
   - Only update from official sources (manufacturer's website/app).
   - Verify firmware signatures.

### 4.3 Multi-Sig Wallet Arrangements

**What it is:** A wallet that requires M-of-N signatures (keys) to authorize a transaction. Example: 2-of-3 means any 2 out of 3 keys must sign.

**Use cases:**
- **Personal security (2-of-3):** One key on hardware wallet at home, one in safety deposit box, one with trusted family member or lawyer. Losing one key doesn't lose funds.
- **Business treasury (3-of-5):** Multiple directors each hold a key. No single person can move corporate crypto.
- **Estate planning:** Include one key in estate plan so heirs can access with cooperation of another keyholder.

**Providers:**
- **Casa:** Managed multi-sig for individuals. 2-of-3 or 3-of-5. They hold one key as a "recovery key." Premium service includes inheritance planning.
- **Unchained Capital:** Collaborative custody. 2-of-3 with Unchained holding one key.
- **Gnosis Safe (now Safe):** For Ethereum/EVM chains. Open source, widely used for DAOs and business treasuries.
- **Sparrow Wallet + Coldcard:** DIY multi-sig for Bitcoin. Most private but requires technical knowledge.

**Atlas recommendation for CC:** At current portfolio size (~$136), multi-sig is overkill. Implement when crypto holdings exceed $10,000. Start with a 2-of-3 setup via Casa or DIY with Sparrow Wallet.

### 4.4 Crypto in Estate Planning — Passing Crypto to Heirs

**The problem:** If CC dies and no one can access the crypto, it's gone forever. Unlike a bank account, there is no "estate department" to call.

**What NOT to do:**
- Do NOT put seed phrases in your will. Wills become **public documents** when submitted for probate. Anyone can read them. Your crypto would be immediately stealable.
- Do NOT give seed phrases to your lawyer without protection. Lawyers can be hacked or compromised.
- Do NOT store seed phrases only in your head.

**Recommended approaches (from simplest to most sophisticated):**

#### Option 1: Sealed Envelope with Lawyer (Budget)
- Write detailed instructions (wallet locations, seed phrases, PINs) on paper.
- Place in a sealed, tamper-evident envelope.
- Give to your estate lawyer with instructions to release ONLY to your named executor upon proof of death.
- **Cost:** Minimal (lawyer's storage fee, ~$100-300/year).
- **Risk:** Lawyer's office could be broken into. Envelope could be opened by a dishonest party.

#### Option 2: Safety Deposit Box + Instructions in Will (Moderate)
- Store seed phrases (on metal backup) in a safety deposit box.
- The will names the executor and instructs them to access the safety deposit box.
- Include a separate, non-public letter of wishes that explains what the crypto is and how to access it.
- **Cost:** Safety deposit box (~$50-200/year).
- **Risk:** Bank may freeze the box upon death notification. Executor needs death certificate and probate to access.

#### Option 3: Casa Collaborative Custody (Recommended)
- Casa's 3-of-5 multi-sig includes an inheritance protocol.
- CC holds 2 keys, Casa holds 1 key (recovery), and 1-2 keys are with designated heirs or in secure storage.
- Upon death, the heir contacts Casa with proof of death. Casa facilitates key recovery to allow 3-of-5 signing.
- **Cost:** Casa Premium ~$250/year.
- **Advantage:** No single point of failure. No seed phrases exposed in legal documents. Professional process.

#### Option 4: Dead Man's Switch Services
- Automated systems that trigger after a period of inactivity.
- CC must "check in" periodically (e.g., monthly email confirmation). If CC fails to check in after N attempts, the service delivers a pre-written message (with instructions/partial keys) to designated recipients.
- **Providers:** Google Inactive Account Manager (free, but limited), Sarcophagus (decentralized, crypto-native), Safe Haven (crypto inheritance protocol).
- **Risk:** False triggers if CC forgets to check in. Service could shut down.

#### Option 5: Shamir's Secret Sharing (Technical)
- The seed phrase is mathematically split into N shares, where any M shares can reconstruct the original.
- Example: Split into 5 shares, any 3 can recover the seed. Give shares to: executor, parent, sibling, lawyer, safety deposit box.
- **Implementation:** Trezor natively supports Shamir backup (SLIP-0039). For other wallets, use `iancoleman/shamir39` or `dark-pool/shamir`.
- **Advantage:** No single party can access the crypto. Tolerates loss of up to N-M shares.
- **Disadvantage:** Requires technical setup and education of shareholders on how to use their shares.
