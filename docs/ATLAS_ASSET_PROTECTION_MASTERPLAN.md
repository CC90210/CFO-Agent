## 1. Canadian Corporate Asset Protection

### 1.1 OpCo/HoldCo Structure

The most powerful domestic asset protection tool available to CC before any offshore move.
The core mechanic: separate the entity that earns and faces liability (OpCo) from the entity that holds
accumulated wealth (HoldCo). When a judgment is obtained against OpCo, HoldCo assets are not reachable.

**How it works:**

| Entity | Role | What It Holds | Tax Rate |
|--------|------|---------------|----------|
| OpCo (OASIS AI Solutions Inc.) | Active operations | Contracts, employees, liability, day-to-day cash | 12.2% SBD (Ontario, first $500K) |
| HoldCo (CC's holding CCPC) | Passive wealth | Surplus cash, investments, real estate, COLI, seg funds | 50.17% on investment income (refundable via RDTOH) |

**The sweep mechanic:**
- OpCo earns revenue at 12.2% corporate tax
- Surplus profits flow to HoldCo as intercorporate dividends
- Intercorporate dividends are tax-free under s.112 ITA (deduction for dividends received)
- HoldCo holds accumulated wealth beyond reach of OpCo's creditors
- CC takes salary + dividends from HoldCo at personal tax rates — on CC's schedule

**Tax deferral at $400K projected revenue:**

| Scenario | Tax Paid This Year |
|----------|--------------------|
| Sole proprietor, $400K income | ~$180K (53.53% marginal, Ontario) |
| OpCo CCPC, $400K retained | ~$48K (12.2% SBD) |
| Annual deferral | ~$132K stays in the corporation, compounding |

That deferral funds future investments, pays the structure costs many times over within year one.

**Setup costs:**

| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| OpCo incorporation (Ontario) | $1,200–$2,500 |
| HoldCo incorporation (Ontario) | $1,200–$2,500 |
| Legal (shareholder agreements, articles) | $2,000–$4,000 |
| Annual accounting (2x T2, bookkeeping) | $8,000–$18,000/year |
| **Total first year** | **$12,400–$27,000** |

At $400K revenue, this structure pays for itself in the first month through tax deferral alone.

**Legal authority:** ITA s.112 (intercorporate dividend deduction), OBCA (Ontario Business Corporations Act),
CRA IT-528 (transfers of funds between related corporations).

### 1.2 Piercing the Corporate Veil — How to Prevent It

The corporate shield is only as strong as CC's adherence to corporate formalities. Canadian courts have
pierced the corporate veil in the following circumstances (reference: *Salomon v. Salomon & Co.* [1897]
AC 22; *642947 Ontario Ltd v. Fleischer* (2001) ONCA; *Shoppers Drug Mart v. 6470360 Canada Inc.*):

**Triggers that allow courts to pierce the veil:**

| Trigger | Description | Risk Level for CC |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Fraud or improper conduct | Using corp to commit fraud, evade legal obligation | LOW if operating legitimately |
| Commingling of funds | Treating corp bank account as personal wallet | MODERATE — common mistake |
| Failure to observe formalities | No board minutes, resolutions, proper records | MODERATE — easy to overlook |
| Undercapitalization | OpCo left with no assets to meet foreseeable obligations | LOW-MODERATE |
| Alter ego doctrine | Company is mere extension of individual, no separate existence | MODERATE without proper setup |
| Sham/puppet theory | Company exists only to hide assets, no genuine business | LOW if OASIS operates genuinely |

**How CC maintains the shield:**

1. **Separate bank accounts** — OpCo has its own RBC/Wise business account. HoldCo has a separate account.
   Never deposit client payments into a personal account.

2. **Annual board meetings** — Hold them, even if CC is the sole director. Document resolutions in writing.
   Key resolutions: salary declaration, dividend declaration, fiscal year-end approval.

3. **Formal intercompany agreements** — Management services agreement (OpCo pays HoldCo a management fee),
   IP licence agreement (OpCo pays IP HoldCo royalties), all at documented arm's length rates.

4. **Keep OpCo capitalized** — Maintain enough working capital in OpCo to meet foreseeable obligations.
   Do not sweep OpCo to zero if contracts are outstanding.

5. **Sign contracts as corporation** — All client agreements signed "OASIS AI Solutions Inc., per: CC, Director."
   Never sign personally for corporate obligations.

6. **Expense reimbursement** — Personal expenses paid from personal accounts, then reimbursed via proper
   expense claim. Never run personal expenses directly through OpCo.

### 1.3 Director Liability (s.227.1 ITA + OBCA)

Even with a corporation, CC as director faces personal liability in specific statutory situations.
This is non-negotiable — the ITA imposes joint and several liability on directors regardless of
corporate structure.

**Federal exposures (ITA):**

| Provision | Liability | Amount |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| s.227.1 ITA | Unremitted employee source deductions (CPP, EI, income tax) | Full amount + interest + penalties |
| s.323 ETA | Unremitted HST | Full amount + interest + penalties |

**Ontario exposures (OBCA):**

| Provision | Liability | Amount |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| s.131 OBCA | Employee wages (if corp insolvent) | Up to 6 months unpaid wages |
| Environmental liability | Remediation orders | Potentially unlimited |
| Pension fund contributions | Underfunded defined benefit plans | Full shortfall |

**Limitation period:** 2 years from the date CC ceases to be a director (s.227.1(4) ITA).
Resigning as director does not eliminate prior liability — it starts the clock.

**Due diligence defence (s.227.1(3) ITA):**
Directors avoid liability if they exercised the degree of care, diligence, and skill that a reasonably
prudent person would exercise in comparable circumstances. Defence is strongest when CC:
- Uses a payroll service (Ceridian, ADP) for source deduction remittances
- Reviews remittance schedules monthly
- Documents all oversight activities
- Acts immediately when remittances are missed

**Mitigation:** D&O insurance (Section 5.2) covers defence costs and settlements arising from director
liability claims. Essential after incorporation.

### 1.4 Three-Entity Structure (OpCo + HoldCo + IP HoldCo)

At $280K+ revenue, adding an IP HoldCo separates the most valuable asset — OASIS software, brand, and
methods — from both operational creditors (OpCo) and personal creditors (CC).

```
CC (Personal)
    |
    v
HoldCo (Ontario CCPC)
    |--- Owns 100% of OpCo shares
    |--- Owns 100% of IP HoldCo shares (or directly holds IP)
    |--- Holds: surplus cash, investment portfolio, real estate, COLI
    |--- Receives: tax-free intercorporate dividends from OpCo (s.112)
    |
    +---> OpCo (Ontario CCPC) "OASIS AI Solutions Inc."
    |         |--- All client contracts, employees, liability
    |         |--- Licenses IP from IP HoldCo (deductible royalty expense)
    |         |--- SBD rate: 12.2% on first $500K active business income
    |         |--- Annual surplus swept to HoldCo via tax-free dividend
    |
    +---> IP HoldCo (Ontario CCPC or later offshore company)
              |--- Owns: OASIS software, algorithms, brand, domain, methods
              |--- Licenses IP to OpCo at arm's length royalty rate
              |--- IP is unreachable by OpCo creditors (separate legal entity)
              |--- If OpCo faces catastrophic judgment: IP survives in IP HoldCo
              |--- Future: transfer IP HoldCo offshore (Isle of Man) post-departure
```

**Why IP HoldCo is critical for OASIS:**
- Software and AI models are the entire business value
- An E&O claim that exceeds insurance and bankrupts OpCo does NOT reach IP HoldCo
- IP HoldCo can license to a new OpCo immediately — business continues
- Pre-positions IP for offshore transfer at departure (avoids inflated FMV trigger)
- SR&ED credits claimed at OpCo level; IP ownership at IP HoldCo level (structure royalty properly)

**Transfer pricing requirement:** Royalty rate between OpCo and IP HoldCo must be arm's length.
Use a third-party comparable licence or obtain a formal transfer pricing study ($5K-$15K one-time).
CRA Advance Pricing Agreement (APA) available for certainty at larger revenues.

### 1.5 CRA Transfer Pricing (s.247 ITA) — 2025 Reforms

Bill C-59 (2024 Royal Assent) and CRA administrative updates have tightened transfer pricing rules.
CC's intercompany transactions must withstand scrutiny.

**Key 2025 changes:**

| Change | Old Rule | New Rule (2025) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Recharacterization power | Rarely used | Expanded — CRA can disregard transactions lacking economic substance |
| Penalty | 10% of gross revenue if non-compliant | Same, but now more aggressively applied |
| Documentation deadline | 90 days after CRA request | 30 days after request (Bill C-59) |
| GAAR interaction | Separate analysis | Combined — transfer pricing + GAAR can both apply |
| Contemporaneous documentation | Required for amounts > $1M | CRA increasingly expects it below $1M for related-party royalties |

**What this means for the three-entity structure:**

- Intercompany management fee (OpCo → HoldCo): document the services provided, hours, comparable rates
- IP licence (OpCo → IP HoldCo): set royalty as % of revenue or per-user — benchmark against commercial SaaS licences
- Domestic structures are lower risk than international; CRA focuses enforcement on cross-border transactions
- Even domestic: keep a one-page transfer pricing memo explaining the rationale

**Domestic IP HoldCo still provides full asset protection benefit even if CRA limits tax benefit.**
The protection rationale is independent of tax efficiency — structure survives even if royalty is adjusted.

## 2. Crown Dependencies Asset Protection

### 2.1 Guernsey Trusts — Among the Strongest in the World

The Trusts (Guernsey) Law 2007 (as amended 2021) contains firewall provisions that make Guernsey
discretionary trusts among the most creditor-resistant structures available to a British citizen.

**Statutory firewall provisions (s.14, Trusts (Guernsey) Law 2007):**

| Protection | Detail |
|------------|--------|
| Foreign matrimonial law | A Guernsey trust cannot be voided, varied, or set aside by foreign matrimonial law |
| Forced heirship | Foreign forced heirship rules (e.g., French, Quebec civil law) have no effect |
| Foreign judgments | Judgments obtained outside Guernsey against trust assets are generally NOT enforceable |
| Choice of law | All questions about the trust are determined solely under Guernsey law |
| Foreign creditors | Cannot enforce claims in Guernsey courts based on foreign judgments alone |

**Key limitation:** Fraudulent conveyance. If a trust is settled after a creditor claim exists
(or is foreseeable), it may be challenged under Guernsey's fraudulent disposition provisions.
Settlement must occur BEFORE any claim exists to obtain full protection.

**Guernsey Purpose Trusts (Trusts (Guernsey) Law 2007, s.12):**
- No identified beneficiaries required
- Used for "orphan structures" — trust holds shares in an SPV/company
- Requires an enforcer (a named person or company to enforce the trust's purpose)
- CC's use case: purpose trust holds IoM company shares, purpose = "long-term wealth preservation"
- Effective for asset protection and succession planning without naming individual beneficiaries

**2025 licensing update:** Private trust companies (PTCs) require a fiduciary licence or a limited
permission from the Guernsey Financial Services Commission. Annual fee: approximately £1,420.
Use a licensed Guernsey trust company as trustee (cost: £5,000-£15,000/year in trustee fees).

**Cost estimate:**

| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| Trust deed drafting | $5,000–$10,000 |
| Initial setup (trustee, registration) | $3,000–$5,000 |
| Annual trustee fees | $5,000–$15,000/year |
| Annual accounting/administration | $2,000–$5,000/year |
| **Total first year** | **$15,000–$35,000** |

### 2.2 Jersey Trusts

Jersey offers equivalent protections under the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984, with the March 2026 Eighth
Amendment further strengthening the statutory framework.

**Key provisions (Article 9, Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984):**

| Protection | Detail |
|------------|--------|
| Choice of law firewall | Jersey law governs — foreign law cannot override |
| Foreign judgment enforcement | Judgments seeking to defeat the trust are unenforceable in Jersey |
| Matrimonial property | Foreign matrimonial property rights do not affect Jersey trusts |
| Heirship rights | Foreign forced heirship rights have no effect |

**March 2026 Eighth Amendment additions:**
- Strengthened protections for digital assets held in trust
- Clarified trustee duties regarding AI-managed investment portfolios
- Enhanced firewall against EU regulatory orders post-Brexit

**Jersey vs. Guernsey for CC:**
- Both offer essentially equivalent firewall protection
- Jersey has a larger financial services industry (more trustee options, more competitive pricing)
- Guernsey has slightly more established case law on the firewall provisions
- Choice is largely operational — which fiduciary relationships CC develops first

### 2.3 Guernsey Trust + IoM Company Structure

The gold standard offshore structure for CC post-departure, combining Guernsey's trust firewall with
Isle of Man's 0% corporate tax environment.

```
Guernsey Discretionary Trust (or Purpose Trust)
    |--- Trustee: Licensed Guernsey fiduciary
    |--- Settlor: CC (settled pre-departure, pre-appreciation)
    |--- Beneficiaries: CC, CC's descendants (discretionary — trustee decides distributions)
    |--- Guernsey firewall: protects all assets from foreign creditors
    |
    +--- IoM Company (100% owned by Guernsey Trust)
              |--- Registered in Isle of Man
              |--- Holds: OASIS IP, SaaS revenue, investment portfolio
              |--- Corporate tax rate: 0% (IoM Income Tax Act 1970, as amended)
              |--- No CGT in Isle of Man
              |--- Pays dividends UP to Guernsey Trust (0% at trust level)
              |--- CC receives distributions from Guernsey Trust
```

**While CC is Canadian resident:**
- FAPI (Foreign Accrual Property Income, s.91 ITA) attributes passive income back to CC
- Trust can still serve asset protection purposes (beyond Canadian creditor reach)
- Tax benefit is limited until departure — structure for protection now, tax efficiency post-departure

**After CC establishes IoM residency:**
- FAPI ceases to apply — no Canadian tax jurisdiction
- IoM corporate tax: 0% on trading income (banking, retail income taxed at 10%)
- IoM personal tax: 10% on first £14,000; 20% on balance; £220,000 cap
- Guernsey trust firewall fully operational — creditors cannot reach trust assets
- Dividends from IoM company to trust: 0%; distributions from trust to CC: IoM personal rates

### 2.4 Interaction with Departure Tax (s.128.1 ITA)

Settling a trust before departure is the critical timing element. Departure tax applies to deemed
disposition at fair market value on the day CC ceases Canadian residency. Assets inside the trust
at that date may be subject to deemed disposition.

**Planning sequence:**

| Step | Timing | Purpose |
|------|--------|---------|
| Settle Guernsey trust | 2+ years before departure | Trust assets begin creditor protection immediately |
| Settle with nominal assets first | Same time | Establish trust structure before assets appreciate |
| Transfer IP to IoM company (inside trust) | Controlled transfer — arm's length price | IP locked in; growth accrues offshore |
| Departure | When ready | FMV of trust interest potentially excluded under s.54.2 ITA |

**s.128.1(10) ITA — Excluded rights or interests:**
An interest in a non-resident trust may qualify as an "excluded right or interest" if it was acquired
for fair market value consideration. Legal analysis required (consult a departure tax CPA, $5K-$10K).

**Key principle:** The trust does not eliminate departure tax on the assets inside it at time of departure.
It protects future appreciation from Canadian tax AND protects trust assets from Canadian creditors.
Settle early, transfer assets before they appreciate significantly, to minimize the departure tax hit.

## 3. Shipping Structures — Honest Assessment

### 3.1 Tonnage Tax Regimes

For completeness, here are the tonnage tax regimes that exist globally.

| Jurisdiction | Rate | Requirement | Registration Body |
|---|---|---|---|
| UK | Notional profit based on ship tonnage × days | Must manage ships in UK | Maritime and Coastguard Agency |
| Isle of Man | Favorable tonnage rates | Red Ensign Group compliance | Isle of Man Ship Registry |
| Malta | EU-approved scheme, effectively ~10% | EU/EEA flagged or managed vessels | Malta Maritime Authority |
| Marshall Islands | 0% on international shipping income | Ships registered in Marshall Islands | MIETA (world's 3rd largest registry) |
| Cyprus | EU-approved tonnage | Cyprus-flagged or managed vessels | Department of Merchant Shipping |
| Greece | Special tonnage regime | Greek-owned/managed vessels | Ministry of Shipping |

### 3.2 Can SaaS Use Shipping Structures?

**Hard no. This bears full clarity.**

Tonnage tax regimes are exclusively available to companies that own and operate qualifying ships.
The income must be derived from the use or operation of qualifying ships in international transport.

A SaaS/AI company like OASIS AI Solutions:
- Does not own ships
- Does not operate ships
- Has no qualifying shipping income
- Cannot satisfy any eligibility criterion of any tonnage regime in any jurisdiction

Attempting to use a shipping structure for SaaS income is not aggressive tax planning.
It is misrepresentation of the nature of income — tax fraud.
The consequence is not reassessment. It is potential criminal prosecution under s.239 ITA.

**Marshall Islands LLC note:** Marshall Islands LLCs are legitimate holding vehicles (0% tax,
strong privacy, low cost). The Marshall Islands registry is for SHIPS. An IoM LLC or Guernsey
company provides equivalent or better holding structure without any shipping pretense.

### 3.3 What the "Layered Entity" Concept Actually Means

The concept CC is likely thinking of — income flowing through multiple entities, with liability
contained in one place and assets protected in another — is correct and legal. The implementation is:

- **Liability entity:** OpCo (contracts, operations, creditor exposure)
- **Domestic wealth entity:** HoldCo (surplus cash, investments, COLI)
- **IP entity:** IP HoldCo (software, brand, algorithms — isolated from operational risk)
- **Offshore holding entity:** IoM Company inside Guernsey Trust (post-departure)

This achieves every objective that someone mistakenly associates with shipping structures, through
legitimate, court-tested legal frameworks.

## 4. LLC Structures (US and Offshore)

### 4.1 Comparison Matrix

| Feature | Wyoming LLC | Delaware LLC | Nevis LLC | Cook Islands LLC |
|---------|-------------|--------------|-----------|------------------|
| Charging order protection | Yes (statutory) | Yes (limited) | Yes + expires 3 years | Yes + expires 5 years |
| Foreign judgment recognition | Yes | Yes | NO — foreign judgments not recognized | NO — must re-litigate in Cook Islands |
| Bond requirement to sue | No | No | $25K-$100K bond required | Yes |
| Fraudulent transfer statute of limitations | Varies (4-6 years) | 3 years | 1 year from transfer | 2 years from transfer |
| Single-member protection | Yes (statutory since 2017) | Case-by-case | Yes (statutory) | Yes (statutory) |
| Annual maintenance cost | ~$100-$300 | ~$300-$500 | ~$1,500-$3,000 | ~$2,000-$5,000 |
| Setup cost | ~$500-$1,000 | ~$500-$1,000 | ~$5,000-$15,000 | ~$10,000-$25,000 |
| Privacy (member names) | No public filing | No public filing | Strong | Gold standard |
| Litigation-tested | Moderate | Moderate | Strong (30+ years) | Gold standard (35+ years) |
| Best use case | Domestic US holding | VC/startup investment vehicles | Offshore holding + protection | Maximum offshore protection |

**Charging order protection explained:**
A charging order is a creditor's only remedy against an LLC member's interest in most strong-protection
jurisdictions. The creditor cannot: force a distribution, take over management, or liquidate the LLC.
They can only receive distributions IF the manager (CC) chooses to make them. In practice, this means
a creditor gets nothing unless CC decides to pay out. Nevis and Cook Islands take this further — the
charging order expires, leaving the creditor with nothing after the statutory period.

### 4.2 CRA Treatment of Foreign LLCs — The FAPI Dealbreaker

CRA's position on foreign LLCs (confirmed in multiple technical interpretations and the *McFadyen v. MNR*
line of cases):

- CRA treats single-member LLCs as corporations for Canadian tax purposes
- While CC is a Canadian resident, ALL income earned by the LLC is subject to FAPI (s.91 ITA)
- FAPI is attributed to CC in the year it is earned by the foreign affiliate
- T1134 (foreign affiliate return) required — failure to file: $25/day, max $2,500/form
- T1135 (foreign income verification, if cost > $100K CAD) — failure to file: $25/day, max $2,500

**Result while CC is in Canada:**
- No tax savings whatsoever — FAPI eliminates the offshore benefit
- Only benefit is asset protection (foreign creditors still face barriers)
- Compliance costs ($3K-$8K/year) may exceed the protection benefit at current revenue

**Result after CC establishes non-Canadian residency:**
- FAPI ceases — no Canadian tax jurisdiction
- LLC/offshore company income taxed at IoM rates (0-10%)
- Full offshore benefit realized

### 4.3 Recommendation — When to Add Each Structure

| Phase | Recommended Structure | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Now ($0-$80K) | Ontario sole proprietor + insurance | FAPI negates offshore benefit; insurance is first dollar |
| Incorporation ($80K+) | OpCo + HoldCo (Ontario CCPCs) | Domestic corporate shield, massive tax deferral |

## 5. Insurance as Asset Protection

Insurance is the FIRST layer of protection — it pays before any corporate shield is tested.
It is also the cheapest and fastest layer to deploy. Every dollar of insurance limits CC pays is
a dollar a creditor collects from a third party, not from CC's assets.

### 5.1 E&O Insurance — Buy Immediately

Errors and Omissions (E&O), also called Professional Liability insurance, is the single most
important purchase CC can make as an AI/software service provider.

**What it covers:**
- AI or software causes client financial damage (incorrect outputs, system failures)
- Negligent delivery of professional services
- Missed project deadlines causing client losses
- Failure to deliver promised functionality
- Defense costs for covered claims (even fraudulent ones)

**What it does NOT cover:**
- Intentional fraud or criminal acts
- Bodily injury or property damage (requires GL policy)
- Employee claims (requires EPLI)

**Market pricing (Ontario, AI/SaaS, $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate):**

| Provider type | Annual premium |
|---|---|
| Lloyd's / Beazley (AI-specific) | $1,200–$1,800/year |
| Intact / Aviva (standard tech E&O) | $800–$1,200/year |
| BrokerLink / licensed broker | $756–$1,056/year (smaller policies) |

**Atlas recommendation:** $1M/$2M coverage, applied for immediately as sole proprietor.
Transfers automatically to OASIS Inc. upon incorporation with endorsement.

**Legal relevance:** AIDA (Artificial Intelligence and Data Act, Bill C-27) is progressing through
Parliament. Liability framework for AI systems is crystallizing. E&O is mandatory for AI SaaS operators.

### 5.2 D&O Insurance — After Incorporation

Directors and Officers insurance protects CC personally from claims arising from decisions made
as a corporate officer or director.

**Coverage structure:**
- **Side A:** Protects CC directly when the corporation cannot or will not indemnify (insolvency, derivative suits)
- **Side B:** Reimburses the corporation for indemnifying CC after a covered claim
- **Side C (entity coverage):** Protects the corporation itself from securities claims (less relevant at this stage)

**Covers:**
- Regulatory investigations (CRA, CRTC, Competition Bureau)
- Employment practices claims (wrongful dismissal, harassment) — where EPLI not separate
- Shareholder derivative actions (if CC takes on investors later)
- Breach of fiduciary duty claims
- Defense costs for s.227.1 ITA director liability claims (partial)

**Market pricing (Ontario, small private company, $1M limit):**

| Profile | Annual premium |
|---|---|
| Single-director, low-risk tech company | $400–$600/year |
| Small company, < 5 employees | $600–$900/year |
| With investors/shareholders | $1,000–$2,500/year |

**Note:** D&O does NOT cover unremitted source deductions in most policies (s.227.1 ITA claims).
That is specifically excluded. Use payroll services + diligent remittance to manage that risk separately.

### 5.3 Umbrella Liability

Excess liability / personal umbrella policy sits above CC's existing policies (auto, homeowners/tenants,
or boat) and provides catastrophic coverage for personal liability scenarios.

**What it covers:**
- Libel, slander, defamation (critical for any public-facing business owner)
- Privacy invasion, false arrest
- Excess auto liability beyond standard policy limits
- Personal injury lawsuits above underlying policy limits

**Cost:**

| Coverage | Annual Cost |
|---|---|
| $1M umbrella | ~$300–$400/year |
| $2M umbrella | ~$400–$500/year |
| $5M umbrella | ~$500–$700/year |

At $1/day, this is non-negotiable. Buy immediately, regardless of corporate structure.

**Carrier requirement:** Umbrella typically requires underlying auto + homeowners/tenants policy with
the same carrier, or an approved carrier. Contact current insurer first.

### 5.4 Life Insurance — Ontario Insurance Act s.196

Life insurance with a designated beneficiary (spouse, child, grandchild, or parent) is exempt from
seizure by creditors of the insured under the Ontario Insurance Act s.196.

**Asset protection applications:**

| Structure | Protection Level |
|---|---|
| Personal life insurance with qualifying beneficiary | Full — exempt from creditor seizure under Insurance Act |
| COLI (Corporate-Owned Life Insurance) in HoldCo | Full — HoldCo creditors do not reach COLI; COLI not reachable by OpCo creditors |
| COLI proceeds flow to Capital Dividend Account (CDA) | Tax-free extraction to CC via capital dividend |

**COLI in HoldCo — triple benefit:**
1. Asset protection from creditors of both CC and OpCo
2. Tax-sheltered investment growth inside policy
3. Death benefit flows into CDA → tax-free capital dividend to estate

Minimum insurable interest: no minimum for own-life policy. COLI requires insurable interest in
the key person (CC is both shareholder and key person — satisfies requirement).

**Products to explore:** Participating whole life (Sun Life, Manulife, Canada Life), Universal Life
with investment component. Engage an independent life broker for comparison.

### 5.5 Segregated Funds

Segregated funds are insurance contracts that function like mutual funds but are issued by life
insurance companies. The insurance wrapper provides statutory creditor protection.

**Protection source:** Ontario Insurance Act (same s.196 analysis as life insurance).
Segregated fund contracts with a qualifying beneficiary designation are exempt from creditor seizure.

**Protection hierarchy:**
- Full protection in bankruptcy and non-bankruptcy proceedings (if beneficiary is qualifying)
- Non-bankruptcy protection requires irrevocable beneficiary OR qualifying beneficiary AND policy must not be a sham

**Tradeoff:**

| Feature | Mutual Fund | Segregated Fund |
|---|---|---|
| MER (annual cost) | 1.0–2.0% | 2.0–3.5% |
| Creditor protection | None | Full (with qualifying beneficiary) |
| Maturity guarantee | None | 75%–100% after 10 years |
| Death benefit guarantee | None | 75%–100% |
| CDIC coverage | No | No (insurance company, not bank) |
| Reset options | No | Yes (lock in gains, reset 10yr clock) |

**Atlas recommendation:** Use segregated funds ONLY for non-registered assets where creditor protection
is the priority (e.g., investment savings beyond RRSP/TFSA room). For registered accounts (RRSP, TFSA),
use low-cost ETFs — segregated funds' high MER is not justified when the account itself provides protection.

### 5.6 RRSP/TFSA Creditor Protection Summary

| Account Type | Bankruptcy Protection | Non-Bankruptcy Creditor Protection |
|---|---|---|
| RRSP (investment form, bank) | Yes — BIA s.67(1)(b.3), EXCEPT contributions in last 12 months | NO — fully reachable by creditors |
| RRSP (insurance form / seg fund) | Full, including last 12 months | Full (Insurance Act s.196 with qualifying beneficiary) |
| TFSA (investment form, bank) | NO — NOT protected under BIA | NO — fully reachable by creditors |
| TFSA (insurance form / seg fund) | Yes (if qualifying beneficiary) | Yes (if qualifying beneficiary) |
| FHSA | Same as RRSP (investment form) | NO |
| LIRA/LRSP (locked-in) | Full (pension legislation) | Full (pension legislation) |
| Company RRSP / group plan | Full | Full (while in group plan) |

**KEY INSIGHT — TFSA has ZERO creditor protection in non-bankruptcy scenarios.**

This is counterintuitive and widely misunderstood. A judgment creditor can garnish a TFSA at a bank.
Converting to a segregated fund TFSA (with qualifying beneficiary) adds creditor protection at the cost
of higher MER.

At CC's projected income, TFSA room (~$95,000 cumulative by 2026) is real money. Consider:
- Keeping TFSA in insurance/seg fund form if creditor risk is elevated
- Running business through corporation (major creditors are commercial, not personal)
- Post-incorporation, personal creditor risk drops significantly

## 6. SaaS Lawsuit Scenarios

### 6.1 AI/Software Causes Client Damage

**Risk level: HIGH** — and increasing as OASIS scales revenue and client count.

### 7.4 The Tax Havens Most People Forget

Commonly overlooked jurisdictions that deserve analysis given CC's profile:

**Cyprus — 60-Day Residency Rule (the hidden gem):**
Under Cyprus tax law, an individual can establish Cyprus tax residency with ONLY 60 days/year
(s.2(1), Income Tax Law 118(I)/2002, as amended). Requirements:
- Present in Cyprus for at least 60 days in the tax year
- Not a tax resident of another country
- Maintain a permanent home in Cyprus (rent or own)
- Conduct business or hold a position in Cyprus
As a non-domiciled Cyprus tax resident: 0% CGT, 0% dividends, 0% foreign source income.
Corporate income tax rose to 15% in 2026, but for a company held through IoM/Guernsey, the effective
rate can remain near 0%. Cyprus non-dom status lasts 17 years.
**Bottom line:** 60 days in Cyprus, plus IoM/Guernsey company = 0% effective tax. Best optionality.

**Bermuda — Often Dismissed Due to Cost of Living:**
Bermuda has 0% income tax, 0% CGT, 0% corporate tax — permanently enshrined in the Companies Act.
Cost of living is extremely high ($5K-$8K USD/month). For CC at $400K+ CAD, the tax saving
($130K-$180K/year) vastly exceeds the premium cost of living. Worth revisiting at $500K+ revenue.

**Liechtenstein — Foundations for Maximum Anonymity:**
The Liechtenstein Stiftung (foundation) is the most private and legally robust asset protection
structure in the world. Foundation assets are not owned by the founder — they are dedicated to a
purpose. No beneficial ownership register visible to public. Court-ordered disclosure is extremely
difficult (treaty-limited). Corporate tax: 12.5% (effective often 8-10% with participation exemption).
Setup: $20K-$50K. Annual maintenance: $10K-$25K. Appropriate for very high net worth (>$5M).

**Andorra — 90 Days + 10-Year CGT Holiday:**
Often overlooked because it is landlocked and requires actually living there (no digital-only option).
But: Andorra has no CGT for the first 10 years of residency. 10% flat personal tax after that.
10% corporate tax. No inheritance tax, no wealth tax. Passive income residency permit available
with €50K deposit or €400K real estate investment. No Irish/British passport advantage, but
accessible to any nationality.

**Estonia e-Residency — EU Company Without Physical Presence:**
Estonian e-Residency provides a EU-registered company without living in Estonia. Key feature:
0% corporate tax on retained profits (only taxed at 22% when distributed). For a business that
reinvests most profits, this provides indefinite tax deferral while maintaining EU company status
for EU client billing. Does NOT provide personal residency or personal tax benefits. Best used as
a billing entity for EU clients alongside a primary residency in IoM or Guernsey.

## 8. GAAR (2024-2025 Amendments)

### Bill C-59 — General Anti-Avoidance Rule Amendments (Royal Assent June 2024)

The 2024 GAAR amendments are the most significant change to Canadian anti-avoidance law since 1988.
Any offshore structure CC builds must be designed to withstand the new framework.

**Key changes:**

| Change | Pre-Bill C-59 | Post-Bill C-59 |
|---|---|---|
| Primary purpose test | "The primary purpose" (singular) | "One of the main purposes" — catches more transactions |
| Penalty on avoidance | No automatic penalty | **25% penalty** on denied tax benefit (s.245(5.1) ITA) |
| Extended reassessment | Standard 3-year normal reassessment period | **+3 years** extended period unless transaction was disclosed |
| Economic substance | Not a statutory requirement | Now codified — CRA considers whether transaction has economic substance beyond tax |
| Retroactivity | Not retroactive | Applies to transactions closed after June 2024; CRA has signalled they will apply to pre-existing arrangements |

**What this means for CC's planning:**

| Planning Action | GAAR Risk | Analysis |
|---|---|---|
| Incorporation + OpCo/HoldCo | **None** | Long-established, CRA-blessed structure. IT-528, numerous technical interpretations confirm tax-free intercorporate dividends are not GAAR |
| IP HoldCo with arm's length royalties | **Low** | Genuine business purpose (asset protection); royalty at arm's length — economic substance present |
| Guernsey trust settled while CC in Canada | **Low-Moderate** | Asset protection purpose is legitimate non-tax purpose. FAPI means no tax benefit while in Canada anyway. GAAR requires a "tax benefit" — hard to apply when FAPI eliminates it. |
| IoM company structure (post-departure) | **None** (if genuinely departed) | CC is no longer a Canadian resident. Canada has no jurisdiction over a non-resident's foreign income. |
| IoM company used WHILE CC is in Canada | **HIGH** — GAAR + FAPI | Attempting to earn income offshore while remaining in Canada is precisely what GAAR targets. DO NOT. |
| Shipping structures for SaaS | **Criminal** | Not GAAR — fraud. See Section 3.2. |

**The GAAR defence — Economic Substance:**
The 2025 draft GAAR guidelines emphasize that transactions with genuine economic substance beyond
tax benefits are not abusive avoidance. CC's structure has genuine non-tax purposes at every layer:
- OpCo/HoldCo: operational separation, liability management
- IP HoldCo: IP protection from operational creditors
- Guernsey trust: creditor protection, succession planning
- IoM company (post-departure): actual business operations in the jurisdiction

Document these purposes contemporaneously. A one-page business purpose memo for each structure,
dated at the time of implementation, is the cheapest insurance against a GAAR challenge.

**Voluntary disclosure (proactive filing):**
Disclosing reportable transactions (s.237.3 ITA) eliminates the +3 year extended reassessment period.
This is worth doing for any offshore structure — the compliance cost ($500-$1,500 legal) far outweighs
the exposure from extended reassessment.
