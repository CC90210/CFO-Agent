## 1. SR&ED for AI/ML Development — CC's HIGHEST VALUE STRATEGY

**Tier:** NOW (sole prop) + AFTER INCORPORATION (maximum value)
**ITA Sections:** s.37, s.127(5), s.127(9), s.127.1, Reg. 2900

### 1.1 What It Is

The Scientific Research and Experimental Development (SR&ED) program is Canada's largest federal tax incentive — $3.8B+ distributed annually. It rewards companies that take technological risks attempting to advance knowledge. For AI/ML developers, it is the single highest-yield tax strategy available. CRA does NOT care if the project succeeded. Failed experiments qualify.

### 1.2 Rates at CC's Income Levels

| Entity | ITC Rate | Refundability | Expenditure Limit |
|--------|----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Sole proprietor | 15% | 40% of ITC is refundable | Unlimited |
| CCPC (enhanced) | **35%** | **100% refundable** | $6M (Budget 2025 — increased from $3M) |
| CCPC above limit | 15% | Non-refundable | Above $6M |
| Public corporation | 15% | Non-refundable | N/A |

**Budget 2025 SR&ED Enhancements (Bill C-15, effective Jan 1, 2025):**
- Expenditure limit increased from $3M → $4.5M, proposed further to $6M
- Capital expenditures RESTORED — equipment purchased for SR&ED qualifies again
- Pre-claim approval process (elective) — request upfront technical validation before filing
- CRA piloting AI-assisted low-risk claim fast-tracking (target: 45-day refunds)
- 10% investment tax credit for clean technology/R&D facility expansion

### 1.3 What Qualifies for AI/ML

**ELIGIBLE:**
- Developing novel ML architectures where existing approaches demonstrably fail
- Solving technological uncertainty in model accuracy, latency, or generalization
- Creating new data preprocessing pipelines overcoming specific technical unknowns
- Performance optimization where known techniques are insufficient
- Custom training loop modifications resolving identified failure modes
- Novel inference optimization techniques (quantization, distillation of uncertain outcome)
- Experimental prompt frameworks that constitute a new methodology (rare — must be systematic)
- Custom embedding strategies for domain-specific tasks with uncertain outcomes

**NOT ELIGIBLE (routine development):**
- Calling existing APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) — standard integration
- Prompt engineering in most forms — not technological advancement
- Using off-the-shelf ML frameworks (PyTorch, TensorFlow) with standard configuration
- Fine-tuning pre-trained models without novel architectural modification
- Bug fixing, standard testing, routine maintenance
- Deploying known algorithms to solve known problems
- Software that advances business practices but not technology itself

**The Line:** Technological uncertainty = no known solution exists in the field (not just unknown to CC). If a Stack Overflow answer solves it, it's not SR&ED.

### 1.4 The Technological Uncertainty Test (Reg. 2900(1))

CC must document ALL four elements for each project:

1. **Technical problem:** Clearly defined problem with no known solution in existing literature
2. **Systematic investigation:** Scientific method — hypothesis → experiment → analysis → conclusion
3. **Technological uncertainty at the start:** Outcome was genuinely uncertain (even to experts)
4. **New knowledge/advancement resulted:** Even a negative result (confirming an approach fails) qualifies

**Documentation Atlas Recommends:**
- Git commit logs with technical rationale in messages
- Technical design documents dated contemporaneously
- Experiment tracking (MLflow, Weights & Biases, or even spreadsheets)
- Meeting notes / Slack messages discussing technical challenges
- Code comments explaining WHY an approach was attempted
- Failure reports — CRA loves these (proves uncertainty)

### 1.5 Form T661 Filing Strategy

**Proxy Method vs. Traditional:**
- **Proxy method:** Claim 55% of qualifying salary expenditures as overhead — simpler, no receipts needed for overhead
- **Traditional method:** Claim actual overhead — requires detailed records

Atlas recommends proxy method for sole proprietors.

**T661 Section 3 (Project Descriptions) — Critical Rules:**
- Each project requires its own technical narrative
- Use structured format: Background → Objective → Technological Uncertainty → Hypothesis → Experimental Work → Results
- Describe what was UNKNOWN at the start, not what was DONE
- Avoid business language ("improve efficiency") — use technical language ("reduce inference latency below 50ms while maintaining >92% accuracy on OOD test set")
- Never copy-paste descriptions between projects
- Never use boilerplate — CRA's SR&ED reviewers see thousands of claims; vague language triggers Technical Review

**CRA Audit Triggers for SR&ED:**
1. Year-over-year claim increases unexplained by revenue growth
2. High contractor-to-salary expenditure ratio (contractors harder to verify)
3. Vague or generic project descriptions
4. Claims in high-volume sectors (software represents ~40% of all claims — higher scrutiny)
5. First-time claimants (higher review probability)
6. Post-dated or reconstructed documentation (fatal — claim denied)
7. Overly broad project scope (break into discrete projects)

## 2. Immediate Expensing — RIIP 2025-2029

**Tier:** NOW
**ITA Sections:** s.13(1), s.20(1)(a), Reg. 1100, Reg. 1101; Budget 2025 RIIP provisions

### 2.1 What It Is

The $1.5M Immediate Expensing for CCPCs and individuals expired December 31, 2024. Budget 2025 replaced it with the **Reaccelerated Investment Incentive Property (RIIP)** — a first-year CCA multiplier for eligible property acquired 2025-2029.

### 2.2 RIIP Rates by Period

| Acquisition Period | First-Year CCA Rate | Half-Year Rule |
|-------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Jan 1, 2025 – Dec 31, 2029 | **3× normal CCA rate** | **Suspended** |
| Jan 1, 2030 – Dec 31, 2033 | 1.5× normal CCA rate | Suspended |
| Jan 1, 2034+ | Normal CCA rate | Applies |

No dollar cap on RIIP (unlike the old $1.5M DIEP). Applies to both sole proprietors and CCPCs.

### 2.3 Key CCA Classes for OASIS

| Class | Normal Rate | RIIP Rate (2025-2029) | Typical Assets |
|-------|------------|----------------------|----------------|
| **Class 50** | 55% | **165% (= full write-off, capped at cost)** | Computers, laptops, servers, hardware |
| **Class 12** | 100% | 100% (already immediate) | Software licenses, tools <$500, uniforms |
| **Class 10** | 30% | 90% | General-purpose vehicles, equipment |
| **Class 8** | 20% | 60% | Furniture, fixtures, miscellaneous equipment |
| **Class 14.1** | 5% | 15% | Goodwill, customer lists (eligible capital) |
| **Class 43.1** | 30% | 90% | Clean energy equipment |
| **Class 55** | 40% | 120% (= full write-off) | Zero-emission light vehicles |

### 2.4 Dollar Impact for CC

**Example — MacBook Pro M4 ($3,500) purchased January 2025:**
- Class 50 RIIP rate: 165% → write off full $3,500 in Year 1
- At $50K income (~33% combined marginal rate): saves $1,155 in taxes
- Without RIIP: first year deduction = $3,500 × 55% × 50% (half-year) = $962 → saves $317

**Example — Home office equipment bundle ($8,000):**
- Full write-off Year 1 under RIIP
- Tax savings: $8,000 × 33% = **$2,640 vs. ~$880 under old rules**

### 2.5 Atlas Recommendation

Purchase qualifying equipment before December 31 of each tax year through 2029. Keep ALL receipts and track class. For OASIS, Class 50 is the primary class — computers, monitors, external drives, servers. Do not use the simplified Class 12 for items >$500 that should be Class 50 — Class 50 gives the same result but preserves future CCA pool if assets aren't disposed.

## 3. Home Office Advanced Deductions

**Tier:** NOW
**ITA Sections:** s.18(12), s.20(1)(a), Reg. 1100; IT-514 (archived), CRA Guide T4002

### 3.1 What It Is

Home office deductions under s.18(12) allow self-employed individuals to deduct the business-use percentage of home operating costs. This is not limited to the simplified "flat rate" method — detailed method captures significantly more.

### 3.2 Eligible Expenses

**For Owners (mortgage situation):**

| Expense | Deductible (× business %) | Notes |
|---------|--------------------------|-------|
| Mortgage INTEREST | YES | Not principal — interest only |
| Property tax | YES | Full annual amount × % |
| Home insurance | YES | Contents + structure |
| Utilities (heat, electricity, water) | YES | All eligible |
| Internet | YES — often higher % | If dedicated line: 100% |
| Maintenance and repairs | YES | Specific to workspace: 100% |
| Snow removal / landscaping | YES | Partial — access maintenance |
| Capital Cost Allowance on home | **AVOID** | Destroys Principal Residence Exemption |

**For Renters:**

| Expense | Deductible (× business %) | Notes |
|---------|--------------------------|-------|
| Rent | YES | Primary deduction |
| Utilities | YES | If not included in rent |
| Renter's insurance | YES | Pro-rated |
| Internet | YES — often 80-100% | Based on business use |

### 3.3 Business Percentage Calculation

**Method 1 — Square Footage (standard):**
```
Business % = Workspace sq ft ÷ Total finished home sq ft
Example: 120 sq ft office ÷ 800 sq ft home = 15%
```

**Method 2 — Number of Rooms (CRA accepts for multi-room homes):**
```
Business % = Rooms used for business ÷ Total rooms
Example: 1 office ÷ 5 rooms = 20%
```

Use whichever produces the higher result — CRA accepts both methods.

### 3.4 The CCA-on-Home Warning

Claiming CCA on the home under s.13(7)(i) allocates a portion of the home to business use. This is IRREVERSIBLE while the home is owned. Upon sale, that portion is NOT protected by the Principal Residence Exemption (s.40(2)(b)) and triggers a capital gain. On a $500K home, the math almost never favours it. Do not claim CCA on the home.

### 3.5 Dollar Impact

| Annual Home Cost | Business % | Annual Deduction | Tax Savings (33% marginal) |
|-----------------|-----------|------------------|---------------------------|
| $18,000 (rent + utils) | 15% | $2,700 | $891 |
| $18,000 | 20% | $3,600 | $1,188 |
| $24,000 (mortgage int + utils + tax) | 15% | $3,600 | $1,188 |
| $24,000 | 20% | $4,800 | **$1,584** |

**Practical tip:** CRA scrutinizes home office claims but does not disallow them when they are reasonable and documented. Keep a floor plan screenshot, utility bills, and a note explaining business use. Required condition: business income > 0 AND no "loss multiplication" rule breach (s.18(12)(b) — cannot create a loss from home office alone; carry forward excess).

### 3.6 Atlas Recommendation

Use detailed method, not flat rate. Calculate sq footage. Deduct mortgage interest (NOT principal), property tax, utilities, insurance, and internet proportionally. Document with a dated floor plan. Value: $1,000-$5,000/year depending on housing costs.

## 4. Automobile Deductions

**Tier:** NOW
**ITA Sections:** s.13(7)(g), s.18(1)(r), s.67.2, s.67.3, Reg. 7306, 7307; IT-521R (archived)

### 4.1 2025 Statutory Limits

| Item | 2025 Limit |
|------|-----------|
| Class 10.1 CCA ceiling (before HST) | $38,000 |
| Lease deduction ceiling | $1,050/month |
| Interest on automobile loan | $350/month |
| Prescribed per-km operating benefit (employee) | $0.33/km |
| Ontario EHT threshold | N/A (sole prop) |

### 4.2 Business vs. Personal Split

Business-use percentage = business km ÷ total km driven in year.

**Required records:** Contemporaneous driving log with:
- Date
- Destination
- Business purpose
- Odometer start/end
- Business km

CRA will deny 100% of automobile claims without a log. Apps: MileIQ, TripLog, or manual spreadsheet.

### 4.3 What Can Be Deducted (× business %)

- Gas and oil
- Maintenance and repairs
- Insurance
- License and registration
- Parking (business trips — not commuting)
- Lease payments (capped at $1,050/month)
- CCA on vehicle (capped at $38,000 cost)
- Loan interest (capped at $350/month)

### 4.4 Zero-Emission Vehicle Advantage

If CC acquires a ZEV (electric/hydrogen) — Class 54 or 55 — the CCA ceiling is $61,000 (Class 54) and RIIP applies (2025-2029). At 100% business use, full write-off in Year 1 on up to $61,000.

### 4.5 Dollar Impact

**Example — $30,000 vehicle, 70% business use:**
- Annual costs: gas $3,000 + insurance $2,400 + maintenance $800 = $6,200
- Deductible: $6,200 × 70% = $4,340
- CCA (Class 10, 90% RIIP): $30,000 × 90% × 70% = $18,900 Year 1
- Total Year 1 deduction: **$23,240**
- Tax savings at 33%: **$7,669**

## 5. Loss Utilization Strategies

**Tier:** NOW
**ITA Sections:** s.3(d), s.31, s.38(c), s.74.1, s.74.2, s.111(1)(a)(b)(c), s.112, Part I; Form T1A

### 5.1 Three Types of Losses

| Loss Type | Carryback | Carryforward | Applies Against |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Non-capital losses (business) | 3 years | 20 years | Any income |
| Net capital losses | 3 years | Indefinite | Capital gains only |
| ABIL (Allowable Business Investment Loss) | 3 years | 10 years* | ANY income |

*After 10 years, unused ABIL converts to net capital loss — loses the ability to apply against income.

### 5.2 ABIL — The Hidden Gem (s.38(c))

An ABIL arises when CC disposes of shares or debt of a **Small Business Corporation (SBC)** at a loss. An SBC is a CCPC with 90%+ of assets used in active business.

**Why it is powerful:** Capital losses normally only offset capital gains. ABILs offset ANY income — employment, business, rental, other. At CC's marginal rate, a $20,000 ABIL saves $6,600 in real cash.

**How to trigger:**
- Lend money to a qualifying small business (friend's startup, own CCPC) — if it becomes uncollectible under s.50(1), you have a deemed disposition
- Invest in SBC shares that go to zero — elect s.50(1) disposition
- The loss must be real — not circular or non-arm's length wash transactions

**ITA s.50(1) Election:** File election on the T1 in the year the shares/debt become worthless. No proceeds required — ACB becomes the loss automatically.

### 5.3 Net Capital Loss Strategy

Crypto tax-loss harvesting is the most accessible source of net capital losses for CC.

**Rules:**
- Superficial loss rule (s.54): If CC (or affiliated person) buys the same asset within 30 days before or after the sale, the loss is denied and added back to ACB
- Affiliated persons: CC, CC's spouse, corporation controlled by CC, partnership CC is majority of
- "Identical property" for crypto = same coin/token (BTC ≠ ETH, ETH ≠ WETH in most cases, but stablecoins may be identical)

**Q4 Harvest Protocol:**
1. Review unrealized losses in crypto portfolio by Nov 15
2. Calculate superficial loss exposure
3. Sell positions at a loss — book loss
4. Do NOT rebuy same asset for 30+ days
5. Alternative: rebuy a correlated but non-identical asset (ETH instead of BTC) to maintain exposure
6. File Form T1A to carry back 3 years if no current-year gains

### 5.4 Business Loss Creation (Pre-Year-End)

If OASIS has a good income year with large capital gains, consider:
- Prepaying deductible business expenses before Dec 31
- Purchasing capital equipment (RIIP write-off)
- Contributing to RRSP
- Creating timing differences to smooth income across years

**Form T1A (Loss Carryback):** File with original T1 or amended T1 to carry losses back 3 years for immediate refund. Processing time: 2-4 months. Worth filing for any loss >$5,000.

### 5.5 Dollar Impact

**ABIL Example — $30,000 loss on SBC shares:**
- Allowable ABIL: $30,000 × 50% = $15,000
- Applied against $50K business income
- Tax reduction at 33% marginal: **$4,950 cash savings**

**Net Capital Loss Harvest — $15,000 crypto loss:**
- Applied against $15,000 capital gains in same year → zero capital gains tax
- Savings: $15,000 × 50% (inclusion) × 33% = **$2,475**

## 6. Statute-Barred Years — The 3/6 Year Window

**Tier:** NOW — awareness and strategy
**ITA Sections:** s.152(3.1), s.152(4), s.152(4.01), s.152(4.1), s.152(9); Form T2029, Form T652

### 6.1 Normal Reassessment Period (s.152(3.1))

| Taxpayer Type | Normal Reassessment Period |
|--------------|---------------------------|
| Individual (including self-employed) | **3 years** from Notice of Original Assessment |
| CCPC eligible for SBD | 3 years |
| Other corporations | 4 years |
| Specified foreign property errors (T1135) | **Indefinite** |

After the normal reassessment period expires, the year is **statute-barred** — CRA cannot reassess regardless of errors or underpayments. This is YOUR protection.

### 6.2 Extended Reassessment Triggers (s.152(4)(a)(i))

CRA CAN reassess beyond 3 years if:
1. **Misrepresentation attributable to neglect, carelessness, or willful default** — any understatement on return
2. **Fraud** — no time limit
3. **Tax shelter transactions** — 3 additional years beyond normal
4. **Specified foreign property (T1135 required but not filed)** — indefinite reassessment period

**Key:** "Neglect or carelessness" is a LOW bar. A mathematical error, forgotten T4, or undeclared freelance income can re-open statute-barred years. CRA must only show the misrepresentation was there — not that it was intentional.

### 6.3 Strategic Use for CC

**Aggressive-but-defensible positions:** Under the 3-year window, any position that is arguable and documented will either:
- Be accepted as filed (most common)
- Be reassessed within 3 years with a proposed adjustment
- After 3 years: permanently statute-barred even if CRA thinks you were wrong

**The playbook:**
1. Take defensible positions on grey-area deductions (home office %, business meal %, vehicle %)
2. Document every position with contemporaneous records
3. Respond to any CRA review within the 3-year window with your documentation
4. After 3 years: that year is locked

### 6.4 Form T2029 — NEVER Sign Without Analysis

Form T2029 is a Waiver of Reassessment — CC voluntarily extends CRA's right to reassess beyond the normal period. CRA will send this if they are investigating but running out of time.

**Rules:**
- Never sign a waiver unless your accountant confirms you benefit (rare)
- If waiver was signed: file Form T652 to revoke it
- T652 revocation takes effect **6 months after CRA receives it**
- Once revoked, CRA has 6 months to complete their assessment

### 6.5 Record Retention Requirements

| Record Type | Minimum Retention |
|------------|------------------|
| Income tax records (general) | **6 years** from end of tax year |
| T1135 and foreign property records | 6 years + indefinite risk |
| Property records (ACB) | 6 years after DISPOSITION of property |
| GST/HST records | 6 years from end of relevant period |
| Employment records | 6 years from end of year payroll relates to |

**For crypto ACB:** Records must trace back to ORIGINAL acquisition date, potentially years prior. Never delete transaction history.

## 7. Capital Dividend Account (CDA)

**Tier:** AFTER INCORPORATION
**ITA Sections:** s.83(2), s.89(1) "capital dividend account", s.83(2.1), Part III; Form T2054

### 7.1 What It Is

The Capital Dividend Account (CDA) is a notional memorandum account maintained by a CCPC tracking tax-free amounts the corporation can distribute to shareholders as capital dividends. Capital dividends are 100% tax-free to the recipient shareholder — no income inclusion, no dividend tax credit needed.

### 7.2 What Flows Into the CDA (s.89(1))

| Source | Amount Added to CDA |
|--------|-------------------|
| Capital gains realized by corporation | Non-taxable portion (50% of gain) |
| Life insurance death benefit | Proceeds minus ACB of policy |
| Capital dividends RECEIVED from other CCPCs | 100% |
| Taxable dividends paid (negative adjustment) | Reduces CDA |

**Example:** Corporation realizes $200,000 capital gain.
- Taxable portion (50%): $100,000 — taxed at ~25% corporate rate
- CDA addition (50%): **$100,000 — can be paid to CC tax-free**

### 7.3 Crypto Gains in a CCPC — Classification Is Critical

**If gains are CAPITAL (not business income):**
- 50% included in corporate income for tax
- 50% credited to CDA → distributable tax-free

**If gains are BUSINESS INCOME:**
- 100% included in corporate income
- NOTHING flows to CDA
- ALL income taxed at 12.2% (SBD rate) first, then dividend withholding on distribution

The capital vs. business income distinction (s.9(1), IT-479R) is determined by:
- Frequency of transactions
- Length of holding period
- Intent at time of acquisition
- Integration with other business activities

For OASIS trading through a CCPC: long-term crypto holdings with documented investment intent = capital gains. Active trading executed as part of ATLAS operations = business income. Separate the accounts and document intent at acquisition.

### 7.4 COLI Strategy — Extracting Value Tax-Free

The Corporate-Owned Life Insurance (COLI) structure leverages the CDA for maximum extraction:

1. CCPC purchases permanent life insurance (whole life/universal life) on CC
2. Corporation pays premiums — partially deductible (Net Cost of Pure Insurance component)
3. Cash value grows tax-sheltered inside the corporation
4. At CC's death: death benefit minus ACB of policy → credited to CDA
5. Corporation elects s.83(2) capital dividend to estate
6. Estate receives proceeds **100% tax-free**

**Alternative (living benefit):** Use policy as collateral for corporate loans. Borrow against cash value — loan is not income. "Buy, Borrow, Die" at the corporate level.

### 7.5 Form T2054 — Filing Requirements

CDA dividends require:
- Director's resolution authorizing the capital dividend
- Form T2054 — Election in Respect of a Capital Dividend
- Must be filed **on or before the payment date** — retroactive elections not permitted
- Include Schedule 89 (CDA balance calculation)

**Part III Tax Risk:** If CDA balance is overstated and a dividend is paid in excess of the actual CDA balance, the excess is taxed at 60% (Part III tax). This is one of the most punishing errors in corporate tax — always verify balance before filing.

### 7.6 Dollar Impact at Incorporation

**Scenario — CCPC with $50,000 capital gain on crypto:**
- Corporate tax on $25,000 (50% inclusion) at 12.2%: $3,050
- CDA credit: $25,000
- Capital dividend paid to CC: $25,000 **tax-free**
- Total tax on $50K gain: $3,050 (6.1% effective)
- vs. personal capital gains at 33% on $25,000 inclusion: $8,250

**Saving: $5,200 on one transaction.**

## 8. Salary vs Dividend Optimization

**Tier:** AFTER INCORPORATION
**ITA Sections:** s.15(1), s.82, s.83(2), s.84.1, s.89(1), s.121, s.125, s.129; T2 Schedule 7; s.20(1)(oo) for eligible dividends

### 8.1 Ontario 2025 Corporate Rates

| Rate Type | Federal | Ontario | Combined |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|
| CCPC — Small Business Deduction (≤$500K) | 9.0% | 3.2% | **12.2%** |
| CCPC — General (>$500K or SBD ground down) | 15.0% | 11.5% | **26.5%** |

### 8.2 Dividend Types

| Type | Origin | Gross-Up | DTC | Effective Rate |

| Non-eligible dividends | SBD-taxed income | 15% | 9.03% | Higher personal tax |
| Eligible dividends | General-rate income pool (GRIP) | 38% | 15.02% | Approach capital gains rate |

### 8.3 Integration Analysis — Ontario 2025

**At $80K Corporate Income:**

| Extraction Method | Corporate Tax | Personal Tax | CPP | **Total to CC** |
|------------------|--------------|-------------|-----|----------------|
| All salary ($80K T4) | None | ~$19,300 | ~$4,600 | **~$56,100** |

| Hybrid: $65K salary + $15K non-elig div | None + $1,830 | ~$17,100 | ~$3,500 | **~$57,570** |
| Leave in corp (reinvest) | $9,760 | None | None | **$70,240 in corp** |

**Notes:** CPP column reflects employer + employee combined contribution at current rates. Numbers approximate — engage accountant for actual optimization.

### 8.4 RRSP Room — The Salary Argument

Non-eligible dividends do NOT generate RRSP contribution room. Salary (T4) generates 18% of T4 income in new RRSP room annually (maximum: $32,490 in 2025).

At 22, every year without RRSP contributions is a permanent loss of tax-sheltered compounding. The hybrid salary strategy preserves this.

| Salary Amount | New RRSP Room | Tax Savings if Invested (33%) |
|--------------|--------------|------------------------------|
| $50,000 | $9,000 | $2,970/year |
| $65,000 | $11,700 | $3,861/year |
| $80,000 | $14,400 | $4,752/year |

### 8.5 The Refundable Dividend Tax on Hand (RDTOH)

When a CCPC pays dividends, it triggers a **dividend refund** from its RDTOH account — $38.33 refunded per $100 of non-eligible dividends paid. This means deferred corporate tax is partially recovered when profits are distributed. Track this on T2 Schedule 43.

## 9. Individual Pension Plan (IPP)

**Tier:** MUCH LATER (age 40+, $100K+ T4)
**ITA Sections:** s.147.1, s.147.2, Reg. 8300-8517; s.8(1)(m) (employee deduction); Form T1004

### 9.1 What It Is

An IPP is a defined benefit (DB) registered pension plan for a single employee-shareholder. The corporation contributes to the IPP trust on behalf of CC. Contributions are deductible to the corporation and not taxable to CC until retirement. Contribution limits exceed RRSP limits and INCREASE with age.

### 9.2 IPP vs RRSP Comparison

| Feature | RRSP | IPP |
|---------|------|-----|
| 2025 annual limit | $32,490 | ~$31,500 (age 40) → ~$42,900 (age 50) |
| Past service buyback | No | Yes — fund years back to 1991 or date of incorporation |
| Terminal funding at 65 | No | Yes — large deductible lump-sum contribution |
| Investment losses | Your problem | Corporation must top up (additional deduction) |
| Locked-in until retirement | Yes | Yes (LIF on wind-up) |
| Creditor protection | Provincial (varies) | **Full federal protection** — PBSA |
| Admin cost | None | $1,500-$3,000/year actuarial + admin |
| RRSP room consumed | Generates room | Consumes existing room via Pension Adjustment |

### 9.3 Why IPP Beats RRSP at Age 40+

At age 40, IPP contribution limits exceed RRSP limits by ~$8,000/year. By age 50: ~$10,400/year excess. Over a 20-year period, this differential compounding at 7% = **additional $400,000** in tax-sheltered growth.

Past service buyback can credit years worked for the company going back to date of incorporation. If OASIS incorporates in 2027 and CC is 40 in 2043, they could buy back 16 years of past service — large one-time deduction.

## 10. Retirement Compensation Arrangement (RCA)

**Tier:** FAR FUTURE ($200K+ T4, IPP and RRSP maxed)
**ITA Sections:** s.207.5-207.7, Part XI.3; s.153(1)(s) (withholding); s.32.1 (trust)

### 10.1 What It Is

An RCA is an employer-funded arrangement to provide retirement or severance benefits to key employees (including owner-employees). Contributions are 100% deductible to the corporation. However, 50% of all contributions are remitted to CRA into a Refundable Tax Account (RTA) — this 50% is returned when distributions are made to the beneficiary.

### 10.2 The 50% Refundable Tax Mechanism

| Event | Cash Flow |
|-------|----------|
| Corp contributes $100,000 to RCA | $50,000 to CRA (RTA), $50,000 to RCA trust |
| Investment income earned: $10,000 | $5,000 to CRA, $5,000 stays in trust |
| Distribution to CC at retirement: $100,000 | CRA refunds $50,000 from RTA |
| CC pays personal tax on $100,000 | At marginal rate in retirement (likely lower) |
| **Net tax on Corp contribution** | **Corp deduction × marginal rate savings** |

The RCA is most powerful when:
- Corporate tax rate is high (general rate 26.5% > SBD 12.2%)
- CC's marginal rate in retirement is materially lower than during contribution years
- The 50% in trust earns returns that exceed the cost of the refundable tax drag

### 10.3 RCA + Life Insurance Strip (s.207.5)

1. RCA trust is structured to purchase Universal Life insurance on CC
2. Corp contributions fund premiums (deductible at 50% net of refundable tax)
3. Cash value inside UL policy grows tax-deferred
4. At retirement: policy is surrendered or annuitized — distributions trigger 50% RTA refund
5. At death: death benefit paid to RCA trust → distributed to beneficiaries with 50% RTA refund

**Net result:** Roughly equivalent to RRSP for individual, but with full corporate deduction (not individual deduction) and creditor protection.

### 10.4 When RCA Makes Sense for CC

- T4 income exceeds $200,000 consistently
- RRSP contribution room is fully utilized
- IPP contributions are maximized
- Need for additional creditor-protected savings beyond registered limits
- Estate planning motivation for life insurance component

**Not applicable today.** Earmark for review when OASIS T4 income sustained above $200K.

## 11. Estate Freeze & LCGE Multiplication

**Tier:** LATER (post-incorporation, before significant value accretion)
**ITA Sections:** s.84.1, s.86, s.89, s.110.6, s.120.4, Reg. 6205; Capital Gains Exemption Regulations

### 11.1 Current LCGE

The Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE) shelters capital gains on Qualified Small Business Corporation (QSBC) shares:

| Tax Year | LCGE Amount |
|---------|------------|
| 2025 | **$1,250,000** |
| 2026+ | Indexed to CPI annually |

The LCGE is a per-person exemption. Each individual can claim their own $1.25M. With proper structuring, a family of four can shelter $5M in gains. Eight beneficiaries: $10M.

### 11.2 QSBC Requirements (s.110.6(1))

CC's OASIS shares must meet ALL of the following to qualify:

| Test | Requirement |
|------|------------|
| Canadian-Controlled Private Corporation | Must be CCPC at time of sale |
| Asset test at sale | **90%+ of FMV of assets** used in active business in Canada |
| Asset test — 24 months prior | **50%+ of FMV of assets** used in active business for entire 24-month period |
| Holding period | Shares must be owned by CC or related person for **24+ consecutive months** prior to sale |
| Not purification issue | Excess cash can disqualify — must be actively managed |

**Purification Warning:** If OASIS accumulates excess cash from trading profits or retained earnings beyond active business needs, the 90% asset test fails. Solution: pay dividends, make RRSP contributions, invest in active business assets, or distribute CDA balance to bring asset ratio back above 90%.

### 11.3 Estate Freeze Mechanics (s.86)

**Step 1 — Exchange (s.86):**
- CC exchanges current common shares for new fixed-value preferred shares equal to current FMV of the company
- This is a "freeze" — CC's shares are now worth their current value, permanently
- Example: OASIS worth $200,000 → CC gets $200,000 of preferred shares

**Step 2 — Family Trust (new growth):**
- Family trust subscribes for new common shares at nominal value ($1-$100)
- All future growth in OASIS value accrues to the common shares (held by trust)
- Trust beneficiaries: CC, CC's parents, siblings, future spouse, children

**Step 3 — LCGE on Sale:**
- When OASIS is eventually sold for $5M, the preferred shares ($200K frozen value) are CC's
- The $4.8M growth is in the trust → allocated to beneficiaries
- Each beneficiary claims their own $1.25M LCGE
- Total sheltered: number of adult beneficiaries × $1.25M

**Step 4 — Capital gains allocation:**
- Trust resolution allocates gains to beneficiaries before December 31 of sale year
- Beneficiaries report on personal T1, claim LCGE on Schedule 3
- After LCGE: remaining gains taxed at personal capital gains rates

### 11.4 TOSI Risk — Tax on Split Income (s.120.4)

Since Budget 2018, trust allocations can be subject to TOSI — top-rate tax on "split income" regardless of recipient's actual tax bracket.

**TOSI applies to trust allocations UNLESS beneficiary meets an exception:**

| Exception | Requirement |
|-----------|------------|
| Excluded business | Actively engaged 20+ hours/week in business in current year or any 5 prior years |
| Excluded shares (CCPC, 10%+ of each class) | CC family member holds 10%+ of a share class, excluded if NOT a professional business |
| Arm's length | Not applicable in family scenarios |

**Planning implication:** Adult children or family members who work for OASIS 20+ hours/week are exempt from TOSI. Document their hours and roles meticulously.

### 11.5 Atlas Recommendation

**Perform the estate freeze NOW, while OASIS value is low ($50K-$100K).**

If frozen at $100K and company sells for $5M later:
- Only $100K is in CC's preferred shares (no LCGE needed on freeze value)
- $4.9M in trust → $1.25M × multiple beneficiaries exempt

If frozen at $2M instead:
- $2M in CC's preferred shares (CC uses $1.25M LCGE on their portion, pays tax on $750K)
- Less room for multiplication

**Cost:** $2,000-$5,000 legal (corporate reorganization). **Lifetime tax saving potential:** $500,000-$2,000,000+ depending on exit value.

## 12. Prescribed Rate Loan Strategy

**Tier:** LATER (requires spouse/partner or family members with lower income)
**ITA Sections:** s.74.1, s.74.2, s.74.5(2), s.74.5(3); Reg. 4301; IT-511R (archived)

### 12.1 What It Is

The attribution rules (s.74.1, s.74.2) normally attribute investment income back to the higher-income spouse — preventing income splitting through direct gifts of investments. The Prescribed Rate Loan (PRL) exception in s.74.5(2) carves out a loophole: if CC lends money to a lower-income family member at the CRA prescribed rate, income earned on those funds is taxed in the borrower's (lower) hands.

### 12.2 Current Prescribed Rate

| Quarter | Prescribed Rate |
|---------|----------------|
| Q1 2026 | 3% |
| Q2 2026 | 3% |

**The critical advantage:** Once the loan is made, the rate is LOCKED FOREVER at the rate in effect when the loan was established, even if prescribed rates rise. Loans made during the 1% era (2020-2021) are still at 1% today.

### 12.3 Mechanics

1. CC documents a formal loan to lower-income recipient (spouse, adult child, family trust)
2. Loan must be at prescribed rate or higher at time of advance
3. Borrower invests proceeds in income-producing assets
4. Borrower pays interest to CC by January 30 of the following year — EVERY year without exception
5. Investment income taxed in borrower's hands at their (lower) rate
6. Interest CC receives is taxable to CC — but at lower rate than the investment income would have been

### 12.4 The One-Miss Destruction Rule

If the borrower MISSES a single interest payment (even by one day, even for one year), the attribution rules immediately and permanently re-apply. ALL future income is attributed back to CC as if the loan never existed. There is no cure.

**Automation requirement:** Pre-authorize interest payments January 1-30 of each year. Never leave this to memory.

### 12.5 Dollar Impact (when applicable)

**Example — $100K PRL to spouse in 15% tax bracket, generating 7% return:**
- Investment income: $7,000
- Interest paid to CC: $3,000 (3%)
- CC pays tax on interest: $3,000 × 40% = $1,200
- Spouse pays tax on $7,000 at 15% = $1,050
- Total family tax: $2,250
- Without PRL (all income to CC at 40%): $7,000 × 40% = $2,800
- **Annual saving: $550 per $100K lent**

At $500K lent: $2,750/year. Not spectacular but permanent, risk-free, and accumulates indefinitely.

### 12.6 Atlas Recommendation

Not relevant today. Bookmark for when CC has a partner with materially lower income, or when a family trust is established with lower-income beneficiaries. At that time, establish loans at the prevailing prescribed rate. If rates drop below 3%, establish new loans at the lower rate.

## 13. Employee Ownership Trust (EOT)

**Tier:** FAR FUTURE (business sale/transition scenario)
**ITA Sections:** s.248(1) "employee ownership trust", s.110.6(1) "qualifying business transfer"; Budget 2024 amendments

### 13.1 What It Is

An EOT is a trust that holds shares of a business for the benefit of employees. Budget 2024 introduced a $10M capital gains exemption for qualifying business transfers to EOTs — layerable with the $1.25M LCGE for a potential $11.25M+ shelter.

### 13.2 Qualifying Business Transfer Requirements

| Requirement | Rule |
|------------|------|
| Seller ownership | CC must have owned shares for 24+ consecutive months |
| Business assets | 50%+ used in active business for 24 months |
| Canadian beneficiaries | 75%+ of beneficiaries are Canadian employees |
| Employment continuation | Business must continue operating post-transfer |
| Sunset clause | Transfer must occur **before end of 2026** (current sunset) |
| Consideration holdback | Up to 10-year promissory note from EOT permitted |

### 13.3 $10M Exemption Details

- Capital gains up to $10M sheltered from CC's perspective
- Stacks with LCGE ($1.25M) = $11.25M potential shelter
- Reserve can be claimed over 10 years
- EOT is exempt from 21-year deemed disposition rule (unlike most trusts)
- Management can remain with CC post-transfer (unlike some sale structures)

### 13.4 Practical Consideration

EOTs are complex, require legal structuring ($15K-$40K), and are most relevant for businesses with significant employee base. Sunset clause (before 2026 year-end) creates urgency but only if OASIS is at the right stage.

At current scale, this is a "know it exists" strategy. If OASIS grows to 10+ employees and CC is considering an exit by December 2026, engage a mergers/acquisitions tax lawyer immediately.

## 14. Flow-Through Shares

**Tier:** LATER ($150K+ income, high risk tolerance)
**ITA Sections:** s.66(12.6), s.66.1(6) "Canadian exploration expense", s.127(9) "investment tax credit"; s.127.1 (refundability)

### 14.1 What They Are

Resource companies (mining, oil/gas) can "flow through" their Canadian Exploration Expenses (CEE) and Canadian Development Expenses (CDE) to investors by issuing flow-through shares. The investor deducts 100% of the renounced expenses against any income.

**Stacked incentives for critical minerals (2025-2026):**
- 100% CEE deduction
- Mineral Exploration Tax Credit (METC): 15% ITC (extended to March 31, 2027)
- Critical Mineral Exploration Tax Credit (CMETC): **30% ITC** for lithium, cobalt, graphite, nickel, copper, etc.
- Ontario Focused Flow-Through Share Tax Credit: additional 5% provincial

**Total stacked benefit on critical mineral flow-through: 100% deduction + 30% CMETC + 5% Ontario = up to 135% effective deduction equivalent.**

### 14.2 Risk Assessment

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Underlying share risk | Speculative junior miners — frequently lose 50-80% of value |
| Liquidity | Typically 4-month hold restriction post-issuance |
| Minimum investment | $5,000-$25,000 typical |
| Arrangement fees | 10-15% of investment (dealer compensation) |
| Tax shelter registration | CRA scrutiny on aggressive structures |

### 14.3 Break-Even Analysis

**At $150K income (53.53% Ontario marginal):**
- $25,000 investment
- Deduction saves: $25,000 × 53.53% = $13,383
- CMETC credit (30%): $7,500
- Total tax benefit: $20,883
- Break-even share loss: 83.5% of investment

For the shares to produce a net LOSS, they must decline more than 83.5%. Given typical 50-70% losses on juniors, this often still generates profit after tax.

**At $50K income (33% marginal):**
- Same $25,000 investment
- Tax benefit: $25,000 × 33% + CMETC = $8,250 + $7,500 = $15,750
- Break-even share loss: 63%
- Risk/reward is much less attractive

### 14.4 Atlas Recommendation

NOT recommended at $50K income. Tax savings insufficient to offset speculative risk. Revisit when OASIS income exceeds $150K and CC has capital available for high-risk allocation. When viable: use only through a registered dealer, choose CMETC-qualifying critical mineral projects, and never allocate more than 5% of investable assets.
