## 1. CRA Audit Process (Step by Step)

### 1.1 How CRA Selects Files for Audit

CRA does not audit randomly in the way most taxpayers assume. Selection is overwhelmingly data-driven:

| Selection Method | How It Works | Frequency |
|-----------------|--------------|-----------|
| **Computer risk scoring** | CRA's Risk Assessment System (RAS) flags returns that deviate from statistical norms for your industry, income bracket, and filing history. Deductions far above your income class average trigger flags. | ~75% of audits |
| **Matching & cross-referencing** | CRA matches T-slips (T4, T4A, T5, T5008, T3) against your return. If a slip says you earned $50K but you reported $40K, automatic flag. | ~15% of audits |
| **Industry targeting (compliance projects)** | CRA runs sector-specific compliance campaigns. Recent targets: crypto traders (2024-26), sharing economy (Airbnb/Uber), construction cash economy, restaurants, real estate. Self-employed individuals are audited at 3-5x the rate of T4 employees. | Varies by year |
| **Informant tips** | Anyone can report suspected tax evasion to CRA via the Informant Leads Program. CRA pays up to 15% of tax recovered under the Offshore Tax Informant Program (OTIP) for offshore evasion. | ~5% of audits |
| **Random selection** | Small percentage of returns are randomly selected to assess overall compliance. There is nothing you can do to avoid these. | ~2-3% of audits |
| **Related-file audits** | If your business partner, spouse, or corporation is audited, you get pulled in too. | Common |
| **Lifestyle/net worth triggers** | CRA may compare your reported income against publicly visible spending — property purchases, vehicle registrations, social media posts of luxury items. If you report $30K but bought a $60K car, expect a call. | Targeted |

**CC-specific risk factors:**
- Self-employed (T2125) — highest audit rate category
- Crypto trading income — CRA dedicated crypto audit team (35 auditors, 230+ files)
- Multiple income sources (OASIS, PropFlow, DJ, Nicky's) — cross-referencing complexity
- Home office deduction — always scrutinized
- Young taxpayer with business losses — CRA tests "reasonable expectation of profit"

### 1.2 Types of Audits

#### Desk Audit (Most Common)
- Conducted **entirely by mail/phone** from a CRA office
- CRA requests specific documents to verify specific claims (e.g., receipts for a deduction)
- Usually focused on 1-3 line items
- Timeline: 2-6 months
- Least invasive. Most audits start and end here.

#### Office Audit
- You (or your representative) attend a CRA office with your records
- More thorough than desk audit — auditor reviews multiple areas
- Timeline: 3-9 months

#### Field Audit
- CRA auditor **comes to your home or business premises**
- Most thorough regular audit type — reviews books, bank statements, invoices, contracts
- Auditor may inspect your home office (measure it, photograph it)
- May interview you, employees, suppliers
- Timeline: 6-18 months (sometimes longer)
- **CC risk:** If claiming home office on T2125, a field audit means an auditor in your house

#### Lifestyle / Net Worth Assessment (s.152(7))
- CRA reconstructs your income based on your spending and assets rather than your reported income
- Used when CRA suspects significant unreported income
- CRA totals your known expenditures + asset increases, subtracts known non-taxable sources, and the remainder is deemed unreported income
- **Burden of proof shifts to the taxpayer** — you must explain the gap
- Extremely aggressive. Often used in cash businesses, crypto, and suspected fraud.

#### Compliance Audit (Payroll/GST/HST)
- Focused specifically on source deductions, GST/HST remittances
- Common for businesses with employees or $30K+ revenue (mandatory GST registration)

### 1.3 CRA's Audit Powers

CRA has sweeping statutory powers under the Income Tax Act (ITA):

#### Section 231.1 — Inspection Power
- CRA can **enter any premises** where business is carried on (not a private dwelling without consent or warrant)
- Can inspect, audit, or examine **any document** or property
- Can require the owner/occupant to give "all reasonable assistance"
- **Limitation:** Cannot enter a private dwelling without consent. If you work from home, CRA needs either your consent or a warrant to enter — but refusing consent looks terrible and will escalate things

#### Section 231.2 — Requirement for Information
- CRA can issue a **formal requirement** (like a subpoena) for any document or information
- Can be issued to **you or any third party** (bank, exchange, accountant, employer)
- Non-compliance is a criminal offence (s.238) — fine of $1,000-$25,000 and/or up to 12 months imprisonment
- CRA routinely issues s.231.2 requirements to banks, crypto exchanges, and payment processors
- **This is the power behind exchange data orders** (Kraken, Coinsquare, etc.)

#### Section 231.4 — Search Warrant
- When CRA suspects a criminal offence (tax evasion, fraud), it can obtain a search warrant from a judge
- Allows CRA to **search and seize** documents, computers, phones, hard drives
- If you see a search warrant, **stop talking immediately and call a lawyer**
- At this point, this is a criminal investigation — your Charter s.7 and s.8 rights apply

#### Section 231.6 — Foreign-Based Information
- CRA can demand production of documents located **outside Canada**
- If you fail to produce within 90 days (extendable to 180), the foreign documents are **inadmissible** in any later appeal — devastating for your defense
- Relevant for CC: Any crypto exchange records, Wise US account records

#### What CRA CANNOT Do:
- Cannot enter your private dwelling without consent or a warrant
- Cannot demand solicitor-client privileged documents (but you must assert the privilege)
- Cannot arrest you (CRA auditors are not police — only RCMP/CRA Criminal Investigations can arrest)
- Cannot garnish wages or seize assets until a Notice of Assessment/Reassessment is issued
- Cannot audit beyond the statute of limitations (see below) without specific grounds
- Cannot force you to incriminate yourself in a criminal investigation (Charter s.7) — but note: civil audits have NO right to silence; you MUST comply with s.231.2 requirements

### 1.4 Statute of Limitations

| Situation | Limitation Period | ITA Section |
|-----------|------------------|-------------|
| **Normal reassessment** | **3 years** from date of original Notice of Assessment (4 years for CCPCs) | s.152(3.1) |
| **T1135 (foreign property) non-filing or misstatement** | **6 years** from original assessment | s.152(4)(b.2) |
| **FAPI (foreign accrual property income)** | **6 years** | s.152(4)(b.3) |
| **Carelessness, neglect, or wilful default** | **No time limit** — CRA can reassess any year, ever | s.152(4)(a)(i) |
| **Fraud** | **No time limit** | s.152(4)(a)(i) |
| **Taxpayer filed a waiver** | Period extended per waiver terms | s.152(4)(a)(ii) |

**Critical point:** The "misrepresentation due to neglect, carelessness, or wilful default" exception (s.152(4)(a)(i)) is CRA's favourite tool to blow open old years. If CRA can show you were careless (forgot to report a T-slip, missed crypto income), they can go back indefinitely. This is NOT the same as fraud — mere carelessness is enough.

**CC action:** File accurately every year. A single forgotten T5008 slip can open every prior year to reassessment.

### 1.5 The Audit Letter — How to Respond

When CRA initiates an audit, you receive a letter (usually via mail, sometimes via My Account) that includes:
- The **tax years** under review
- The **areas being examined** (e.g., business income, capital gains, home office)
- A **list of documents requested** (bank statements, receipts, contracts, etc.)
- A **deadline to respond** (usually 30 days)
- The name and contact information of the assigned auditor

**What to do when you receive the letter:**

1. **Do NOT panic.** Most audits are resolved at the desk level with no additional tax owing.
2. **Do NOT ignore it.** Non-response = CRA proceeds with the information they have, which is always worse for you.
3. **Note the deadline.** You can request an extension (call the auditor directly — they almost always grant 2-4 more weeks).
4. **Engage a tax professional immediately.** For self-employed with crypto, this means a CPA or tax lawyer, not H&R Block. Budget $500-$2,000 for audit representation (desk audit), $2,000-$10,000+ for field audit.
5. **Authorize your representative.** File a T1013 (Authorizing or Cancelling a Representative) so CRA communicates with your accountant, not you directly. This is critical — you do not want to be the one answering CRA's questions.
6. **Gather ONLY what was requested.** Do not volunteer extra documents. Do not provide more than what is asked. Every extra document is a potential new line of inquiry.
7. **Organize documents clearly.** Labelled folders, cross-referenced to your return. A well-organized response signals competence and reduces auditor suspicion.
8. **Keep copies of everything you submit.** Photograph or scan before mailing.
9. **Respond in writing, never verbally.** Even if the auditor calls, follow up with a written confirmation of anything discussed.

## 2. Your Rights During an Audit

### 2.1 Taxpayer Bill of Rights

CRA is bound by the **Taxpayer Bill of Rights**, which contains 16 enumerated rights. The most audit-relevant ones:

| # | Right | What It Means in Practice |
|---|-------|--------------------------|
| 1 | Right to receive **entitlements** and pay no more/less than required | CRA must apply the law correctly — not just maximize revenue |
| 2 | Right to **service in both official languages** | Request English or French communications |
| 4 | Right to **privacy and confidentiality** | CRA cannot disclose your tax information to third parties without authority |
| 5 | Right to a **formal review** and subsequent **appeal** | You can object to any assessment, and if unsatisfied, go to Tax Court |
| 6 | Right to **complete, accurate, clear, and timely information** | CRA must explain what they're doing and why |
| 9 | Right to **lodge a service complaint** and be given an explanation | If the auditor is abusive, slow, or unfair, you can complain |
| 10 | Right to have the **costs of compliance taken into account** | CRA should not impose unreasonable compliance burdens |
| 13 | Right to **expect CRA to be accountable** | CRA must follow its own procedures |
| 15 | Right to be **represented** | You can have an accountant, lawyer, or other representative at all times |
| 16 | Right to **lodge a service complaint** and request a formal review if not satisfied | Escalation path: service complaint -> Taxpayers' Ombudsperson |

### 2.2 Right to Representation

- You have an **absolute right** to have a representative (CPA, tax lawyer, or enrolled agent) at every stage of the audit
- File **Form T1013** to authorize your representative — CRA will then communicate only with them
- Your representative can attend any meeting, respond to any request, and negotiate on your behalf
- **Pro tip:** Never speak to CRA directly during an audit without your representative. Even innocent statements can be used against you. Auditors are trained to ask questions in ways that can produce harmful admissions.
- If you cannot afford a representative, free tax clinics exist but they generally do not handle audit representation. Budget $500-$2,000 minimum for desk audit representation.

### 2.3 Solicitor-Client Privilege

- Documents exchanged between you and your **lawyer** for the purpose of obtaining legal advice are **absolutely privileged** and CRA cannot compel their production
- This privilege does NOT extend to your accountant — CRA can require production of accountant work papers
- If CRA requests documents that include legal advice, you must **assert the privilege** explicitly. If you hand them over without asserting privilege, you've waived it.
- **Litigation privilege** also applies: documents prepared in contemplation of litigation (e.g., a tax opinion commissioned when you know an audit is coming) are privileged
- **Tax tip:** If you anticipate a serious audit, get a lawyer to retain an accountant. The accountant's work papers are then protected under solicitor-client privilege because the accountant is acting as an agent of the lawyer. This is standard practice in complex tax disputes.

### 2.4 Service Complaints

If the auditor is unreasonable, you can escalate:

1. **Talk to the auditor's team leader** — Most issues resolve here
2. **File a service complaint with CRA** — CRA must respond within 30 business days
3. **Contact the Taxpayers' Ombudsperson** — Independent office that reviews CRA service complaints. Cannot change your tax assessment but can pressure CRA to follow its own rules
4. **File a complaint with the Office of the Privacy Commissioner** — If CRA has disclosed your information improperly

### 2.5 Taxpayer Relief Provisions (s.220(3.1))

CRA has discretion to **cancel or waive penalties and interest** in certain circumstances:

**Eligible grounds:**
- **Extraordinary circumstances:** Natural disaster, serious illness, death in family, imprisonment, emotional/mental distress
- **CRA actions:** Errors or delays caused by CRA itself, incorrect information provided by CRA, processing delays
- **Financial hardship:** Inability to pay would cause undue hardship (CRA considers income, assets, expenses, dependents)

**How to apply:**
- Submit Form **RC4288** (Request for Taxpayer Relief) or a detailed letter
- Include all supporting documentation (medical records, financial statements, correspondence showing CRA error)
- CRA reviews and responds — if denied, you can request a **second-level review**
- If still denied, you can apply for **judicial review** in Federal Court (not Tax Court)

**Limitations:**
- Only applies to penalties and interest — **not to the underlying tax itself**
- CRA can only grant relief for the **10 calendar years preceding** your request
- CRA's discretion is broad, but they are not required to grant relief
- Interest relief is rarely granted in full — partial relief is more common

**CC context:** If you ever file late or get hit with penalties, this is the first line of defense. A well-documented RC4288 citing extraordinary circumstances has a 40-60% success rate for penalty relief.

### 2.6 Voluntary Disclosures Program (VDP)

The VDP allows taxpayers to come forward and correct errors in prior returns **before CRA contacts them**. This is the "confess before you're caught" program.

#### When to Use It
- You have unreported income from prior years (e.g., forgot to report crypto gains)
- You failed to file a required return (T1, T1135, T2)
- You claimed a deduction/credit you were not entitled to
- **Critical timing:** You must apply BEFORE CRA contacts you about the issue. Once CRA sends an audit letter, it's too late for VDP.

#### Two Tracks

| Feature | General Track | Limited Track |
|---------|--------------|---------------|
| **Penalty relief** | Full relief from gross negligence penalties (s.163(2)) and late-filing penalties (s.162) | Full relief from gross negligence penalties only |
| **Interest relief** | Partial — 50% of interest for first 3 years of disclosure period | **No interest relief** |
| **Criminal prosecution relief** | Not referred for prosecution | Not referred for prosecution |
| **Eligibility** | Most disclosures | Disclosures involving intentional conduct, large amounts, multiple years, sophisticated taxpayers |
| **Typical use** | Forgot to report a T-slip, overlooked income source | Deliberately hid offshore income, complex tax schemes |

#### VDP Application Process

1. **Pre-disclosure consultation (optional but recommended)**
   - Contact CRA VDP at 1-800-959-8281 or submit a **no-name** letter describing the situation in general terms
   - CRA will confirm whether VDP applies to your situation
   - No-name letters do NOT identify you — fully anonymous at this stage

2. **Formal application**
   - Submit Form **RC199** or a detailed letter
   - Must include: taxpayer name, SIN, all years affected, nature and amount of unreported income, corrected returns (T1-ADJ or new T1 for each year)
   - Must include **full payment** or a payment arrangement proposal
   - Application must be **complete** — CRA will reject incomplete applications

3. **CRA review (60-90 days typical)**
   - CRA verifies the disclosure is voluntary (they haven't already started an audit)
   - CRA determines whether General or Limited track applies
   - CRA may request additional information

4. **Acceptance or rejection**
   - If accepted: penalties waived per track, interest adjusted, no criminal referral
   - If rejected: you're in the same position as if you hadn't applied — CRA uses the information to reassess you with full penalties

**CC application:** If you discover unreported crypto income from prior years, VDP is the first call. The penalty savings alone (gross negligence penalty = 50% of tax owing) make it worthwhile. But you MUST apply before CRA reaches out.

## 3. Notice of Reassessment — What to Do

### 3.1 Understanding the Notice of Reassessment

After an audit, CRA issues a **Notice of Reassessment (NOR)** if they determine you owe additional tax. The NOR specifies:
- The original assessment vs. the reassessed amounts
- The additional tax, interest, and penalties owing
- The **basis** for the reassessment (though often frustratingly vague)
- Your right to object

**Types of adjustments:**
- **Income inclusion:** CRA adds income you didn't report
- **Deduction denial:** CRA disallows a deduction you claimed (home office, business expenses, etc.)
- **Reclassification:** CRA reclassifies income (e.g., capital gains reclassified as business income — affects crypto traders)
- **Penalty assessment:** Gross negligence (50% of tax), late filing (5% + 1%/month), repeated failure (10% + 2%/month)

### 3.2 The 90-Day Deadline (s.165)

**This is the most critical deadline in Canadian tax law.**

- You have **90 days from the date of the Notice of Reassessment** to file a Notice of Objection
- If you miss this deadline, you lose your right to dispute the reassessment (with very limited exceptions)
- The 90-day clock starts from the date on the NOR, NOT the date you received it
- **Exception:** If you miss the 90 days, you can apply to CRA for a **one-year extension** (s.166.1) — but this is discretionary and CRA often refuses
- If both the 90 days and the one-year extension pass, you are **permanently locked out** of disputing the assessment

**CC action:** If you ever receive a Notice of Reassessment, calendar the 90-day deadline IMMEDIATELY. This is not optional.

### 3.3 How to File a Notice of Objection

#### Method 1: CRA My Account (Online — Fastest)
1. Log in to CRA My Account
2. Navigate to "Register a formal dispute (Notice of Objection)"
3. Select the tax year and complete the form
4. Upload supporting documents
5. You receive immediate confirmation

#### Method 2: Paper Filing
1. Complete **Form T400A** (Notice of Objection) or write a letter
2. Include: your name, SIN, tax year, the specific items you disagree with, the facts and reasons for your objection
3. Mail to:
   - **Chief of Appeals, Canada Revenue Agency**
   - Address on your Notice of Reassessment
4. Send by **registered mail** to prove the filing date
5. Keep copies of everything

#### What to Include in Your Objection
- Identify every line item you dispute — do not file a blanket objection
- State the **facts** supporting your position (e.g., "The home office was used exclusively for business as documented by the attached floor plan and photos")
- State the **legal basis** for your position (e.g., "Subsection 18(12) permits deduction of home office expenses where the home is the principal place of business")
- Attach supporting documents that were not previously provided or that CRA overlooked
- If you agree with some adjustments but not others, say so — partial agreement speeds resolution

### 3.4 What Happens During the Objection Review

Once you file a Notice of Objection, your file is transferred from the Audit Division to the **Appeals Division** — a separate, quasi-independent branch of CRA.

**Key points:**
- The Appeals officer is NOT the same person who audited you — they review the file fresh
- Appeals officers have authority to **confirm, vary, or vacate** the reassessment
- You will usually be contacted by the Appeals officer for additional information or discussion
- The process takes **6-18 months** (often longer for complex files)
- **Collection action is suspended** while your objection is pending — CRA cannot garnish, seize, or collect the disputed amount (s.225.1). However, you continue to accrue interest.
- You can request a **meeting** with the Appeals officer — in-person or by phone

### 3.5 Settlement Negotiations

The Appeals Division actively negotiates settlements. This is the most pragmatic stage of the process:

- Appeals officers have significant discretion to **compromise on facts** (e.g., allowing 60% of a disputed deduction)
- They can split the difference on valuation disputes (e.g., fair market value of property)
- They can accept alternative characterizations if reasonable (e.g., capital gain vs. business income)
- **Settlement conferences** are common — your representative negotiates directly with the Appeals officer

**Negotiation tactics:**
- Present your strongest arguments first — the Appeals officer is assessing whether CRA would win at Tax Court
- Show you have **documentation** even if it's imperfect — a reasonable record is better than none
- Demonstrate **reasonableness** — if you concede obvious errors, CRA is more likely to concede debatable points
- Mention Tax Court explicitly — Appeals officers know that Tax Court costs CRA resources and creates risk of adverse precedent

### 3.6 Does CRA Accept Offers in Compromise?

**No.** Unlike the IRS (which has the Offer in Compromise program allowing taxpayers to settle for less than full tax owing), CRA has **no formal mechanism** to reduce the underlying tax debt based on ability to pay.

CRA can:
- Waive penalties and interest (Taxpayer Relief, s.220(3.1))
- Accept payment arrangements (installment plans)
- Declare a debt "uncollectible" and write it off administratively (but it remains technically owing)

CRA **cannot** (or will not):
- Accept less than 100% of the tax owing as a settlement
- Forgive the underlying tax liability based on financial hardship

**The only ways to reduce the underlying tax are:**
1. Successfully objecting (Appeals Division)
2. Winning at Tax Court
3. CRA administrative write-off (rare, taxpayer has no control over this)

## 4. Tax Court of Canada

### 4.1 When to Escalate to Tax Court

You can appeal to Tax Court after:
- CRA **confirms** the reassessment following your Notice of Objection, OR
- **90 days have passed** since you filed your objection without CRA responding (deemed confirmation)

You have **90 days from the date of CRA's decision** on your objection to file a Tax Court appeal.

**File at Tax Court when:**
- CRA refused to budge on a material amount at the Appeals stage
- The legal issue is clearly in your favour (CRA sometimes takes positions contradicted by their own case law)
- The amount at stake justifies the cost and time
- You want to establish a precedent

**Do NOT file at Tax Court when:**
- The amount is trivial relative to the cost of litigation
- Your position is factually weak (you lack documentation)
- You're fighting penalties only — negotiate these through Taxpayer Relief instead

### 4.2 Informal Procedure

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Eligibility** | Disputes of **$25,000 or less** in federal tax per year, OR **$50,000 or less** in losses per year |
| **Lawyer required?** | **No** — you can represent yourself |
| **Filing fee** | **$100** (or free if filing electronically) |
| **Rules of evidence** | Relaxed — hearsay is admissible, technical rules relaxed |
| **Discovery process** | None or minimal |
| **Hearing** | Usually 1-2 days, informal setting |
| **Cost awards** | Generally **no costs awarded against the losing party** — you won't pay CRA's legal fees if you lose |
| **Decision** | Written reasons, usually within 60-120 days of hearing |
| **Appeal** | Limited — can only appeal on questions of law to Federal Court of Appeal |
| **Timeline** | Filing to hearing: 6-18 months |

**This is the go-to for CC.** Self-represented taxpayer, disputes under $25,000, relaxed rules, no cost risk. The informal procedure is intentionally designed for regular people to use without lawyers.

### 4.3 General Procedure

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Eligibility** | Any amount. Mandatory for disputes over $25,000 per year. |
| **Lawyer required?** | Not legally required, but **practically essential** for amounts over $25,000 |
| **Filing fee** | **$250** |
| **Rules of evidence** | Full rules of evidence apply (Canada Evidence Act) |
| **Discovery process** | Full discovery — document production, examinations for discovery (depositions) |
| **Hearing** | Multiple days, formal courtroom |
| **Cost awards** | **Loser typically pays partial costs** to the winner (Tariff B, typically 50-75% of actual fees) |
| **Timeline** | Filing to hearing: 12-36 months |
| **Legal costs** | $10,000-$100,000+ depending on complexity |

### 4.4 Success Rates and Winning Arguments

Based on published Tax Court decisions (2020-2025):

**Taxpayer success rates by issue type:**
- **Business expense deductions:** ~45% full or partial win (CRA often over-disallows)
- **Capital gains vs. business income:** ~40% win for taxpayer position (CRA aggressively reclassifies)
- **Home office deductions:** ~50% partial win (court often splits the difference)
- **Vehicle expenses:** ~35% partial win (mileage logs are decisive)
- **Employment vs. contractor status:** ~55% taxpayer win (CRA frequently overclassifies as employee)
- **Gross negligence penalties:** ~60% taxpayer win (CRA must prove knowledge or wilful blindness — a high bar)

**What makes taxpayers WIN at Tax Court:**
1. **Documentation** — receipts, contracts, bank statements, logs. The #1 factor.
2. **Consistent record-keeping** — if you kept records contemporaneously (at the time), courts give them strong weight
3. **Credible testimony** — if you testify, be truthful, organized, and specific
4. **Expert evidence** — appraisals, industry expert testimony for valuation disputes
5. **CRA procedural errors** — CRA failed to follow its own rules, missed limitation periods, etc.

**What makes taxpayers LOSE:**
1. **No documentation** — "I lost the receipts" is the most common reason for loss
2. **Inconsistent testimony** — contradicting your own return or prior statements
3. **Reconstructed records** — records created after the audit started have low credibility
4. **Unreasonable positions** — claiming 100% business use of a personal vehicle, for example

### 4.5 Key Case Law Every Canadian Taxpayer Should Know

| Case | Citation | Principle |
|------|----------|-----------|
| **Stewart v. Canada** | 2002 SCC 46 | Replaced "reasonable expectation of profit" (REOP) test with a two-stage inquiry: (1) Is there a source of income? (2) Is there a personal element? If no personal element, CRA cannot deny losses based on profitability. **Key for CC:** Trading losses are deductible if the activity has no personal benefit element. |
| **Tonn v. Canada** | [1996] 2 FC 73 (FCA) | CRA cannot second-guess honest business decisions with hindsight. A business that loses money is not automatically a hobby. |
| **Njenga v. Canada** | [1996] TCC | Taxpayer's own testimony about expenses can be sufficient if credible, even without every receipt. But the testimony must be specific and consistent. |
| **Venne v. Canada** | [1984] FC | Gross negligence requires more than simple negligence — CRA must show wilful blindness or intentional disregard. Mere errors are not gross negligence. **Key for penalties.** |
| **Panini v. Canada** | 2006 FCA 224 | Taxpayer's subjective intention matters for business vs. hobby. If you intend to make profit and take steps toward it, CRA cannot easily deny business status. |
| **Baxter v. Canada** | 2007 TCC 221 | Crypto/day-trading: Court considered trading frequency, knowledge, time spent, and intention to determine whether gains were capital or business income. |
| **Friedberg v. Canada** | [1993] FCA | Commodity trading gains — the factors for capital vs. income include: frequency of transactions, period of ownership, knowledge of markets, and whether the taxpayer's intention was to trade or invest. **Key for CC's crypto characterization.** |
| **Fourney v. Canada** | 2011 TCC 520 | Home office: The "principal place of business" test is met if more than 50% of work time is spent there, even if client meetings happen elsewhere. |
| **Dao v. Canada** | 2010 TCC 84 | CRA must prove the factual basis for reassessment on a balance of probabilities. If CRA's assumptions are wrong, reassessment fails even if taxpayer's records are poor. |
| **Farm Business Consultants v. Canada** | [1994] TCC | CRA's internal policies and interpretation bulletins are not law. If the ITA says one thing and CRA's policy says another, the ITA wins. |

## 5. CRA Collections

### 5.1 How CRA Collects Unpaid Tax

Once a Notice of Assessment or Reassessment is issued and not objected to (or the objection/appeal is resolved), CRA has powerful collection tools:

#### Requirement to Pay (s.224)
- CRA issues a requirement to your **bank, employer, or any third party** who owes you money
- The third party must immediately remit funds to CRA
- Your bank account can be **frozen** with no advance warning
- Your employer diverts your paycheque to CRA
- CRA can garnish up to **100%** of amounts owed to you by third parties (unlike provincial garnishment limits)

#### Lien / Certificate (s.223)
- CRA registers a **certificate of debt** in Federal Court, which then has the force of a court judgment
- CRA can then register a lien against your **real property** (house, land)
- The lien prevents you from selling or refinancing without paying CRA first
- CRA can also use the certificate to pursue **seizure and sale** of assets

#### Seizure and Sale (s.225)
- CRA can seize and sell any of your property (vehicles, equipment, investments, real estate)
- CRA must give **30 days written notice** before seizure
- CRA sells at auction — typically at well below market value (you get the worst price)
- Proceeds go first to CRA's claim, then other creditors, then you

#### Set-Off (s.224.1)
- CRA intercepts **any amount the government owes you** — tax refunds, GST/HST credits, CCB, OTB, etc.
- Automatic — no notice required
- This is the first collection action CRA takes, and they do it silently

**Timeline for collections:**
- CRA generally cannot collect during the first **90 days** after assessment (objection period) (s.225.1)
- If you file an objection, **collection is suspended** for the disputed amount until the objection is resolved
- Once the objection is resolved or the 90-day period expires without objection, CRA can collect immediately
- CRA has **10 years** from the date of assessment to collect (s.222), with various extensions

### 5.2 Payment Arrangements

CRA will negotiate payment plans. Here's how:

1. **Call CRA Collections** at 1-888-863-8657 (or the number on your statement)
2. **Propose a specific plan:** "I can pay $X per month for Y months"
3. **CRA will assess your ability to pay** based on income, expenses, and assets
4. **Be prepared to disclose:** Full income and expense breakdown, bank statements, asset list
5. **CRA expects the maximum you can reasonably pay** — they are not generous

**What CRA typically accepts:**
- Monthly instalments over 6-24 months for small amounts ($1,000-$10,000)
- Longer arrangements for larger amounts if you demonstrate inability to pay faster
- Interest continues to accrue during the payment arrangement (CRA does not freeze interest)

**What improves your chances:**
- Proposing a specific, realistic plan (not "I'll pay when I can")
- Demonstrating a track record of compliance (filed all returns, no other debts)
- Offering to set up pre-authorized debit (shows commitment)
- Starting payments before even calling (shows good faith)

### 5.3 Hardship Provisions

CRA has an internal policy (not codified in law) that it will not collect when collection would cause **undue hardship:**

- Cannot leave you unable to meet **basic living expenses** (food, shelter, medical)
- Cannot force the sale of a **principal residence** if it would leave you homeless (though they can lien it)
- Will consider **medical conditions** that prevent earning income
- Will consider **family circumstances** (dependents, single parent, etc.)

To invoke hardship:
- Write to CRA Collections with a detailed financial statement
- Include proof of income, expenses, medical conditions
- Request that collection action be suspended or that a minimal payment arrangement be accepted
- If CRA refuses, you can request a review by the Collections Fairness Committee

### 5.4 Director's Liability (s.227.1)

If CC ever incorporates (OASIS AI Solutions incorporation threshold: $80K CAD revenue):

- Directors are **personally liable** for unremitted payroll deductions (CPP, EI, income tax) and unremitted GST/HST
- This liability survives resignation — you remain liable for amounts that became due during your directorship, plus **2 years after resignation**
- CRA must first attempt to collect from the corporation and fail (or the corporation must be insolvent)
- **Due diligence defense:** You are not liable if you "exercised the degree of care, diligence and skill to prevent the failure that a reasonably prudent person would have exercised in comparable circumstances"
- **CC action:** If you incorporate, always remit source deductions and GST/HST on time. This is the single largest personal liability risk of incorporation.

### 5.5 Trust Exam

CRA can hold you liable for **someone else's tax debt** in specific situations:

- **Non-arm's length transfers (s.160):** If you receive property from a tax debtor for less than fair market value, you become liable for their tax debt up to the value transferred. Common between spouses.
- **No limitation period.** CRA can assess under s.160 at any time.
- **Example:** Your spouse owes $20K in taxes. They transfer their car (worth $15K) to you for $1. CRA can assess you for $15K of the spouse's debt.
- **CC context:** Be careful accepting property from anyone who might owe CRA. Joint accounts with family members can trigger s.160 assessments.

### 5.6 Bankruptcy and Tax Debt

| Type of Tax Debt | Survives Bankruptcy? |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Income tax, GST/HST** | **Discharged** in personal bankruptcy (with exceptions) |
| **Tax debt from fraud or misrepresentation** | **Survives** — not dischargeable (s.178(1)(e) BIA) |
| **Student loans < 7 years** | **Survives** |
| **Director's liability (s.227.1)** | **Discharged** unless arising from fraud |
| **s.160 non-arm's length transfer liability** | Debatable — courts have gone both ways |

**Consumer Proposal alternative:**
- Negotiate with CRA (as a creditor) to pay a portion of the debt over up to 5 years
- CRA votes as a creditor — they sometimes accept 30-50% of the debt
- No bankruptcy on your record, but a consumer proposal note stays for 3 years after completion
- Often better than bankruptcy for tax debts under $100K

## 6. Proactive Audit-Proofing (Prevention)

### 6.1 Record-Keeping Requirements

Under s.230 ITA, you must keep **adequate books and records** for **6 years from the end of the tax year** they relate to.

**What specifically to keep:**

| Record Type | What to Keep | Format |
|-------------|-------------|--------|
| **Income records** | All T-slips (T4, T4A, T5, T5008), invoices issued, contracts, payment confirmations | Paper or digital |
| **Expense receipts** | Every business expense receipt — no minimum amount. CRA can deny any expense without a receipt. | Original paper preferred; digital scan accepted if legible with date/amount/vendor |
| **Bank statements** | All business and personal bank/credit card statements — 6 years | PDF or paper |
| **Crypto records** | Every trade: date, pair, quantity, price, exchange, fees. Export CSV from each exchange annually. On-chain tx hashes for DeFi. Wallet addresses you control. | CSV exports + blockchain records |
| **Contracts** | Client contracts, leases, partnership agreements, loan agreements | Paper or digital |
| **Vehicle logs** | Mileage log with date, destination, purpose, km driven for EACH business trip | See section 6.5 |
| **Home office records** | Floor plan, measurements, utility bills, property tax, insurance, mortgage interest/rent receipts | See section 6.6 |
| **CCA schedules** | Asset cost, date acquired, UCC schedule, CCA claimed per year | Spreadsheet |
| **Investment records** | Purchase confirmations, ACB calculations, disposition records | Broker statements + calculations |

**Digital records:** CRA accepts digital records if they are (1) legible, (2) complete, (3) backed up, and (4) you can produce them within a reasonable time. Store receipts in a dedicated folder (Google Drive, Dropbox, or local). Photograph paper receipts immediately — thermal paper fades.

### 6.2 Bank Account Separation

**This is the #1 audit trigger for self-employed individuals and the #1 thing CC should do.**

| Account | Purpose | Current (CC) |
|---------|---------|--------------|
| **Personal chequing** | Living expenses, personal spending | RBC |
| **Business chequing** | ALL business income deposits, ALL business expense payments | Should be separate — Wise business or RBC business |
| **Trading/investing** | Kraken, OANDA, brokerage funding | Currently commingled? |

**Why separation matters:**
- When CRA audits, they request **all bank statements** for accounts where business transactions occur
- If business and personal are mixed, CRA reviews **all** your personal spending too
- Mixed accounts make it nearly impossible to prove which expenses are business vs. personal
- CRA auditors are trained to flag commingled accounts as a risk indicator

**CC action items:**
1. Open a dedicated business account (Wise Business account is free and already in the ecosystem)
2. Route ALL OASIS/PropFlow/DJ income through the business account
3. Pay ALL business expenses from the business account
4. Transfer a "salary" from business to personal monthly
5. Fund trading accounts only from the business account (if trading is a business activity)

### 6.3 Receipt Organization System

**Recommended system (15 minutes/week max):**

1. **Physical receipts:** Photograph immediately with your phone. Use an app like Genius Scan or just the camera.
2. **Digital receipts:** Forward email receipts to a dedicated folder (e.g., "2026-Tax-Receipts" in Gmail)
3. **Folder structure:**
   ```
   2026-Tax/
   ├── Income/
   │   ├── OASIS-invoices/
   │   ├── PropFlow-invoices/
   │   ├── DJ-income/
   │   └── T-slips/
   ├── Expenses/
   │   ├── Office-supplies/
   │   ├── Software-subscriptions/
   │   ├── Equipment/
   │   ├── Home-office/

   │   ├── Meals-entertainment/
   │   ├── Professional-fees/
   │   └── Other/

   ```
4. **Monthly reconciliation:** Last Sunday of each month, reconcile bank statements against receipts. Flag any missing receipts. This takes 20-30 minutes if you've been photographing receipts weekly.

### 6.4 Mileage Log Requirements

CRA requires a **contemporaneous mileage log** to claim vehicle expenses. "Contemporaneous" means recorded at or near the time of the trip — not reconstructed at year-end.

**Required fields per trip:**
- Date
- Destination (address or business name)
- Purpose of trip (client meeting, supply pickup, etc.)
- Kilometres driven (odometer start/end, or GPS-tracked)
- Total km for the year (business + personal)

**CRA's simplified method (2024+):**
- For the first full year, keep a **complete log** for the entire year
- In subsequent years, you can keep a **3-month sample period** and extrapolate — but only if your driving pattern is consistent
- If CRA audits, the full-year log is your best defense

**Apps:** MileIQ, Driversnote, or a simple spreadsheet. The key is consistency.

**Without a mileage log:** CRA will deny 100% of vehicle expenses. Courts consistently uphold this denial. There is no workaround — estimation is not accepted.

### 6.5 Home Office Documentation

**What CRA wants to see:**

1. **Floor plan** with measurements — total home square footage and dedicated office square footage
2. **Photos** of the home office space (showing it is set up as an office, not a bedroom with a laptop)
3. **Calculation worksheet** showing the business-use percentage:
   - Method 1: Square footage — office sq ft / total sq ft
   - Method 2: Rooms — 1 office room / total rooms (less precise, CRA prefers sq ft)
4. **Utility bills** — hydro, heat, water, internet (12 months)
5. **Property tax** bill (if homeowner)
6. **Insurance** premium receipt
7. **Rent receipts** (if renting)
8. **Mortgage interest** statement (if homeowner — note: only interest, not principal)

**T2125 home office requirements (s.18(12)):**
- The home office must be your **principal place of business** (>50% of work time), OR
- You use the space **exclusively** for earning business income AND you use it on a **regular and continuous basis** for meeting clients/customers

**CC's situation:** Working from home as sole proprietor of OASIS — almost certainly qualifies as principal place of business. Document it once with measurements and photos, update annually.

**Common audit adjustments:**
- CRA reduces the percentage (you claimed 25%, they say 15%)
- CRA denies because space is not "exclusive" (it's also a guest bedroom)
- CRA denies because no floor plan was provided

### 6.6 Business vs. Hobby — The Reasonable Expectation of Profit Test

If CRA argues your trading (or DJ work, or any venture) is a hobby rather than a business, they can deny all losses and deductions.

**Post-Stewart (2002 SCC 46) test:**
1. Is there a **source of income** (business or property)?
2. Is there a **personal element** to the activity?

If there is NO personal element → it is a business. Period. CRA cannot use profitability to deny business status.

If there IS a personal element → CRA can examine whether the activity is carried on with a "sufficient degree of commerciality" — which includes:
- Profit and loss history
- Training and expertise
- Time and effort devoted
- Business plan or strategy
- Ability to show a profit

**How to defend business status for trading:**
- Maintain a **written trading plan** (ATLAS_ALGORITHM.md — already exists)
- Track **time spent** on trading activities (research, analysis, execution)
- Document **education and training** (courses, books, certifications)
- Show a **progression** — backtesting → paper trading → live trading
- Keep **professional records** — trade journal, P&L statements, risk metrics
- **CC is well-positioned:** Algorithmic trading system, documented strategies, systematic approach, professional tools. This is clearly commercial activity.

### 6.7 Capital Gains vs. Business Income — Crypto

CRA's position: **No bright-line rule.** They examine the "badges of trade":

| Factor | Capital (Investor) | Business (Trader) |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Frequency** | Infrequent trades, buy-and-hold | Frequent, daily/weekly trades |
| **Holding period** | Long-term (months/years) | Short-term (hours/days) |
| **Knowledge** | Casual investor | Deep market knowledge, technical analysis |
| **Time spent** | Minimal | Significant — full or part-time |
| **Intention** | Long-term appreciation | Quick profit from market movements |
| **Leverage/margin** | None or minimal | Uses leverage, margin |
| **Infrastructure** | Simple brokerage account | Trading algorithms, multiple screens, bots |
| **Similarity to business** | Passive | Resembles a business operation |

**CC's situation assessment:**
- Automated trading bot with 12 strategies → **business indicator**
- Multiple trades per day → **business indicator**
- Short holding periods → **business indicator**
- Deep technical knowledge → **business indicator**
- Documented trading algorithm → **business indicator**
- Small account size → somewhat neutral
- No formal trading business registration → **capital indicator** (weak)

**CC likely falls on the business income side** — which means 100% of gains taxable (not 50% inclusion rate), but also 100% of losses deductible against other income and business expense deductions available.

**Defensive strategy:**
- If CRA challenges the characterization, you want to be **consistent**. Pick a position (capital or business) and file consistently every year.
- If you report as capital gains and CRA reclassifies as business income, the additional tax is significant (effective rate doubles on trading gains)
- Consider proactively reporting as business income — it's the defensible position given CC's trading profile, and losses are more useful

### 6.8 Third-Party Accountant Review

**The cheapest audit insurance available: $300-$500/year.**

What a CPA pre-filing review catches:
- Mathematical errors (the most common audit trigger after matching)
- Missing T-slip income (CRA's matching system catches this every time)
- Deductions that are unreasonable for your income bracket
- Missing schedules (T2125, Schedule 3, T1135)
- HST/GST filing gaps
- Capital gains calculation errors (crypto ACB is complex)

**When to hire a CPA:**
- First year of self-employment (T2125 setup)
- Any year with crypto dispositions over $1,000
- Any year with business income over $30,000
- Any year with a major life event (incorporation, property purchase, marriage)
- **Every year** if budget allows — $300-$500 prevents $3,000-$50,000 in audit costs

**What to look for in a CPA:**
- Experience with self-employed tech workers
- Crypto tax experience (not all CPAs understand ACB)
- CPA, CGA, or CA designation (not just a "tax preparer")
- Will sign the return as preparer (they have skin in the game)
