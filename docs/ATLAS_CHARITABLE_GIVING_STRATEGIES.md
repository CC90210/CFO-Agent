## The Core Principle: CRA Is Co-Funding Your Charitable Giving

Here is the mindset shift before anything else.

When you donate to charity in Canada, the government pays a significant portion of the donation FOR you through tax credits. At a $50K income, CRA funds roughly 40 cents of every dollar you donate above $200. At $280K, CRA funds closer to 50 cents per dollar.

This is not a loophole. It is the deliberate policy of the Canadian government — Parliament decided that encouraging Canadians to direct money toward charities was worth the tax expenditure. Wealthy people have always understood this. Most middle-income Canadians donate small amounts without ever structuring their giving to maximize the credit. That is the inefficiency ATLAS is here to fix.

The strategies in this document range from something CC can implement TODAY (batch donations, claim on highest-income year) to long-term architecture (DAFs, foundations, appreciated securities). All of it is legal. All of it is CRA-accepted. Most of it the wealthy have been doing quietly for decades.

## PART 1: How Charitable Tax Credits Actually Work

### 1.1 The Donation Tax Credit — It's NOT a Deduction, It's a Credit `[RISK: LOW]` **[NOW]**

Most people confuse deductions and credits. This distinction matters a lot.

**Plain English:** A tax deduction reduces your taxable income. It saves you tax at your marginal rate. If you are in the 26% federal bracket and get a $1,000 deduction, you save $260. A tax credit directly reduces your tax bill, dollar for dollar — it does not matter what bracket you are in. Donations in Canada give you a CREDIT, which is generally more valuable than a deduction at lower income levels.

Here is exactly how the credit works in 2025:

**Federal Charitable Donation Tax Credit (ITA s.118.1):**

| Amount of Donation | Federal Credit Rate |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| First $200 | 15% |
| Everything above $200 | 29% |
| Everything above $200 (if income > $235,675) | 33% |

**Ontario Charitable Donation Tax Credit (Ontario Taxation Act, 2007):**

| Amount of Donation | Ontario Credit Rate |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| First $200 | 5.05% |
| Everything above $200 | 11.16% |

**Combined Federal + Ontario Credit Rate:**

| Amount of Donation | Combined Credit Rate |
|--------------------|----------------------|
| First $200 | ~20.05% |
| Everything above $200 | ~40.16% |
| Everything above $200 (income > $235,675) | ~44.16% |

**What this means in plain dollars:**

- Donate $1,000 → credit on first $200 = $40 + credit on $800 = $321 → total credit = **$361**
- You gave $1,000 to charity and got $361 back on your taxes. Your actual out-of-pocket cost: **$639**.
- CRA covered 36% of your donation.

- Donate $10,000 → credit on first $200 = $40 + credit on $9,800 = $3,936 → total credit = **$3,976**
- You gave $10,000 and got $3,976 back. Your actual cost: **$6,024**.
- CRA covered nearly 40% of your donation.

**Annual donation limit:** 75% of net income in any given year. If you earn $50,000 net, you can claim up to $37,500 in charitable donations in that year. Unused donation room carries forward for **5 years** (ITA s.118.1(1), definition of "total charitable gifts"). In the year of death and the year preceding death, the limit increases to **100%** of net income — useful for estate planning (covered in Part 11).

**Technical reference:** The donation tax credit for individuals is found at ITA s.118.1(3). The rates are set at s.118.1(3)(a) (15% on first $200) and s.118.1(3)(b) (29% or 33% on the excess). The provincial credit mirrors this structure under provincial legislation.

### 1.2 The $200 Threshold Strategy — Bunch Your Donations `[RISK: LOW]` **[NOW]**

The credit structure has a built-in inefficiency that most people trigger by accident. The first $200 of donations each year only gets you about 20% back. Everything above $200 gets you about 40% back. If you donate $200 every year, you are always stuck at the low rate.

**Plain English:** Imagine the credit is like a tiered nightclub cover. The first $200 "in the door" charges you a steep cover — you only get 20% back. Once you are past $200, you get 40% back on every dollar. You want to spend as little time as possible below that $200 threshold.

**The bunching strategy:**

- Instead of donating $500/year, save up 2 years of giving and donate $1,000 every other year.
- Year 1: Donate $0. No credit. Save your receipts (or just the cash).
- Year 2: Donate $1,000. Credit = $40 (on $200) + $321 (on $800) = $361.
- Versus: Two years of $500 = two credits of $40 + $120 = $160 each year = $320 total.
- By bunching, you saved $41 extra without changing your total charitable giving. Over 20 years of this, that adds up.

**Better — use the 5-year carry forward:**

You do not have to actually donate the money in the year you want the credit. You can accumulate receipts for up to 5 years and claim them all in the year where your income is highest (and therefore you are most likely above $200).

For CC, the practical play: in years when OASIS revenue is low, do not bother claiming small donations. Bank the receipts. In the year you hit $100K, $150K, or $200K revenue, claim everything at once. You get the 40% rate on all of it, AND it hits in the year you most need the offset.

**Spousal strategy:** CRA allows you to claim your spouse or common-law partner's charitable donation receipts on YOUR return (ITA s.118.1(4)). If one partner earns significantly more than the other, the higher earner should claim ALL donations. The credit rates are the same, but a higher-income filer is more likely to have actual tax owing to absorb the credit.

### 1.3 What Qualifies as a Donation (and What Doesn't) `[RISK: LOW]` **[NOW]**

Not everything you give to a good cause qualifies for the credit.

**Qualifies:**
- Cash or cheque donations to registered Canadian charities (organizations with a CRA charitable registration number)
- Publicly listed securities donated in-kind (Part 2 covers this in detail)
- Certified cultural property (Part 5)
- Ecologically sensitive land (Part 6)
- Life insurance (Part 7)
- Donations to the federal, provincial, or municipal government
- Donations to registered Canadian amateur athletic associations
- Donations to registered journalism organizations (new 2020+)
- Donations to universities outside Canada prescribed by regulation (ITA s.118.1(2)(a))

**Does NOT qualify:**
- Buying a raffle ticket (you receive something in return)
- Paying for a charity dinner where the meal has fair market value
- Donations to foreign charities (with narrow exceptions — US charities qualify under Canada-US treaty if you have US income)
- GoFundMe campaigns to individuals (not registered charities)
- Donations to political parties or candidates (those have a separate federal political contribution credit under ITA s.127(3), capped at $1,575 donation)
- Paying church fees where you receive specific personal services

**Partial gifts:** If you give something to a charity but receive a benefit in return (e.g., buy a $200 charity gala ticket worth $75 in food/entertainment), only the "gift portion" ($125) qualifies. The charity must provide a split receipt. This is called an "advantage" and is governed by ITA s.248(31)-(41).

**International note:** US charities do NOT qualify for the Canadian donation credit unless you have US-source income. Canada-US Tax Treaty, Article XXI(5). The credit is proportional to your US income. For CC — not relevant now, but relevant if OASIS ever earns from US clients structured through a US entity.

## PART 2: Donating Securities — The #1 Wealthy-Person Strategy

### 2.1 The Zero Capital Gains Gift — How It Works `[RISK: LOW]` **[SOON]**

This is the single most powerful strategy for anyone who holds appreciating investments. It is used by every major wealth advisor in Canada. It is 100% legal and explicitly written into the Income Tax Act. And almost no one under 40 knows about it.

**Plain English first:** Normally, when you sell a stock for a profit, you pay capital gains tax. Half of your profit is added to your income and taxed at your marginal rate. If you sell $20,000 of stock you bought for $5,000, you have a $15,000 gain. Half of that — $7,500 — goes on your tax return as income. At a 40% marginal rate, you owe $3,000 in tax.

But here is the rule that changes everything: if you DONATE the stock directly to a registered charity instead of selling it first, the capital gains inclusion rate drops to **ZERO**. Not reduced. Zero. The gain disappears entirely for tax purposes. AND you still get a charitable donation receipt for the full market value of the shares.

You donate the stock. You pay no capital gains tax. You get the full receipt. CRA covers 40% of the donation value. The charity gets the full value.

**The comparison — concrete numbers:**

Scenario: You bought Shopify stock for $5,000 three years ago. It is now worth $20,000. You want to give $20,000 to a charity. Income: $100K (combined federal/Ontario marginal rate ~43%).

**Option A — Sell, then donate cash:**
1. Sell shares: $15,000 capital gain
2. Taxable capital gain: $7,500 (50% inclusion rate)
3. Tax owing on gain: ~$3,225 (at 43% marginal rate)
4. Cash to donate: $20,000
5. Donation credit (on $20,000): $40 + ($19,800 × 40.16%) = ~$8,032
6. **Net cost to you:** $20,000 − $8,032 + $3,225 = **$15,193**

**Option B — Donate shares directly:**
1. Transfer shares in-kind to charity's brokerage account
2. Capital gain: $15,000 — but inclusion rate = **0%** under ITA s.38(a.1)
3. Tax owing on gain: **$0**
4. Donation receipt: $20,000 (full FMV at time of transfer)
5. Donation credit: ~$8,032
6. **Net cost to you:** $20,000 − $8,032 − $0 = **$11,968**

**You save $3,225 just by donating the shares instead of the cash.** The charity gets exactly the same $20,000 either way. The only loser is the tax collector.

**At higher incomes, this gap widens.** At $280K income:
- Capital gains tax on Option A: ~$4,275 (58% tax on $7,500 at top Ontario marginal rate)
- Donation credit on Option B: ~$8,832 (at 44.16% combined rate)
- Additional savings versus Option A: $4,275

**Legal foundation:** ITA s.38(a.1) — when a taxpayer makes a gift of a "listed financial instrument" (publicly listed securities) to a qualified donee, the capital gains inclusion rate is reduced from one-half to nil. This provision was introduced in Budget 2006 and has been in force ever since.

### 2.2 What Securities Qualify for the Zero-Inclusion Donation `[RISK: LOW]`

**Qualifying instruments (ITA s.38(a.1), s.54 definition of "listed financial instrument"):**
- Shares listed on a designated stock exchange (TSX, NYSE, NASDAQ, London Stock Exchange, and 40+ others — full list at ITA s.262 and CRA IC 76-19)
- Bonds and debentures listed on a designated exchange
- Units of a mutual fund trust
- Units of a segregated fund (insurance products)
- Listed ETFs (XEQT, VFV, XIU, etc.)

**Does NOT qualify:**
- Shares of private corporations (CCPC shares not listed on exchange)
- Cryptocurrency directly (BTC, ETH, SOL — not listed securities)
- Stock options prior to exercise (exercise first, then donate shares — see 2.3)
- Shares of a company you control that are not exchange-listed
- Real estate
- Promissory notes

**The crypto exception — important:** If you hold a crypto ETF such as Purpose Bitcoin ETF (BTCC) or 3iQ Ether ETF (QETH) — which ARE listed on the TSX — and you donate those ETF units directly to a charity, you DO get the zero-capital-gains treatment. The ETF is a listed financial instrument even though the underlying asset is crypto. This is a planning opportunity for CC if OASIS crypto exposure is held through ETFs in a taxable account.

For direct crypto (BTC/ETH in a wallet), CRA treats donation as a disposition at FMV — you recognize the full capital gain. The zero-inclusion rule does not apply. Plan accordingly: if you want to donate appreciated crypto, consider whether a crypto ETF conversion first makes sense.

### 2.3 How to Actually Execute a Securities Donation `[RISK: LOW]`

**Step-by-step process:**

1. **Contact the charity** — Call or email the development/finance office. Ask for their brokerage account information for in-kind share transfers (DTC number for US shares, broker name and account number for Canadian shares).

2. **Contact your brokerage** — Tell your broker you want to make an in-kind charitable gift of [X shares] to [Charity Name] at [brokerage details]. They will provide a transfer form. Some brokerages have a standardized "charitable gift of securities" form.

3. **Do NOT sell the shares first.** The moment you sell, you have realized the capital gain and the zero-inclusion benefit disappears permanently. The transfer must go share-to-share, from your account to the charity's account.

4. **FMV is determined as the mean of the high and low price on the transfer date.** Not the closing price — the mean of the daily high and low. This is CRA's method (confirmed in IT-297R2, paragraph 3).

5. **Obtain the receipt** — The charity should issue a donation receipt for the FMV. The receipt must include the charity's registration number, the description of the property, and the date of transfer.

6. **Report on your tax return:**
   - Schedule 3: Report the disposition (disposition proceeds = ACB for capital gains purposes, because inclusion = 0)
   - Schedule 9: Report the donation and calculate the credit
   - The capital gain is reported as disposed, with proceeds equal to ACB — resulting in exactly zero taxable gain

**Timing:** Process can take 2-7 business days depending on the brokerage. Submit before December 31 to count in the current tax year.

**Large donations:** If donating more than $100,000 in securities, get a signed independent appraisal if there is any non-obvious valuation involved. Listed securities do not need appraisals (price is publicly known), but it is good practice to keep trading records.

### 2.4 Donating Employee Stock Options `[RISK: MEDIUM]` **[FUTURE]**

If CC ever receives employee stock options (through a startup, advisory shares, or an employer), there is a powerful donation rule available.

**Plain English:** Normally when you exercise employee stock options and the shares have gone up, you owe income tax on the "option benefit" (the difference between the strike price and the FMV at exercise). But if you donate the shares to charity within 30 days of exercising the options, CRA eliminates half of that option benefit from your income. This is a partial exemption — not zero like the listed securities rule, but significant.

**Technical rule:** ITA s.110(1)(d.01) — deduction equal to one-half of the stock option benefit when shares are donated to a qualified donee within 30 days of exercise. Combined with the donation credit on the FMV, this can result in a net-positive tax outcome where you effectively donate for free or close to it.

**This is relevant if:** OASIS takes on investors or CC takes advisory positions in startups in exchange for options.

## PART 3: Donor-Advised Funds — Your Charitable Foundation Without the Cost

### 3.1 What is a Donor-Advised Fund (DAF)? `[RISK: LOW]` **[SOON]**

**Plain English:** A DAF is like a "charitable bank account" you open with a sponsoring organization (usually a community foundation or financial institution). Here is the deal:

- You put money (or securities) into the account
- You get a tax receipt IMMEDIATELY for the full amount contributed
- The money grows tax-free inside the fund
- Over time, you "recommend" grants from the fund to charities you want to support
- The sponsoring organization makes the actual grants on your behalf

The key word is "recommend" — technically, you do not have legal control over the money once it is in the DAF. You give up ownership. In practice, the sponsoring organization almost always follows your recommendation (that is the whole point of the structure), but they reserve the right to refuse if the recipient is not a qualified donee or if there is a conflict of interest.

**Why this is powerful for tax planning:**

You get the tax credit in the year of contribution — not the year of the actual grants to charities. This means you can engineer the tax credit to hit in your highest-income year, then spread the actual charitable giving across many years.

**Concrete example:** OASIS has a breakthrough year at $280K revenue. You are facing a massive tax bill. You contribute $30,000 to your DAF in December. You get a $30,000 donation receipt immediately — worth about $12,600 in combined federal/Ontario credits (40%+ rate). Then over the next 6 years, you direct $5,000/year in grants to causes you care about. The remaining money inside the DAF grows tax-free in a balanced investment portfolio.

You just turned a tax bill problem into 6 years of structured charitable impact — and paid $30,000 for giving that would have only cost CRA nothing without the structure.

### 3.3 DAF + Appreciated Securities = Maximum Leverage `[RISK: LOW]` **[SOON]**

The most powerful version of the DAF strategy combines the zero-capital-gains securities donation rule with the timing flexibility of the DAF.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Accumulate appreciated ETFs or stocks in a taxable brokerage account over several years
2. In your highest-income year, transfer the appreciated securities directly to your DAF
3. Receive a tax receipt for the full FMV — zero capital gains recognized (ITA s.38(a.1))
4. DAF reinvests the proceeds in a diversified portfolio and they grow tax-free
5. Over the following years, you direct grants to charities you care about

**The math — $100,000 of ETFs (ACB $20,000, unrealized gain $80,000):**

| Option | Capital Gains Tax | Donation Credit | Net Benefit |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Sell, donate cash | ~$17,200 (at 43% on $40K inclusion) | ~$40,000 (40% of $100K) | ~$22,800 |
| Donate ETFs directly to DAF | **$0** | ~$40,000 | **$40,000** |

Donating the securities directly to a DAF instead of selling first effectively **doubles your net tax benefit** in this scenario. That is not an exaggeration — it is the arithmetic.

**CC's implementation path:** Open a brokerage account at Questrade today. Start buying index ETFs (XEQT, VFV, XIU) monthly in a taxable account. Let them appreciate over 3-7 years. When OASIS revenue hits a peak year ($200K+), transfer the most appreciated positions directly to a DAF. Get the receipt, eliminate the embedded capital gains, let the DAF invest, direct grants over time.

### 3.4 DAF Rules and Constraints `[RISK: LOW]`

**What you can and cannot do with a DAF:**

- CAN recommend grants to any registered Canadian charity
- CAN recommend grants to some registered foreign charities (provider-dependent)
- CAN name successor advisors (your kids, a trusted person) when you die
- CAN name your DAF as the beneficiary of your RRSP/RRIF (produces donation receipt that can offset the terminal return — covered in Part 11)
- CANNOT take the money back (it is irrevocable — this is what makes it a genuine gift for tax purposes)
- CANNOT use the fund to pay for personal expenses
- CANNOT require the sponsoring organization to make specific grants (you "recommend")
- CANNOT grant to a non-arm's length party in a way that benefits you personally

**Successor advisor:** When you set up the DAF, name a successor advisor. This person (your spouse, child, or trusted friend) takes over grant recommendations when you die. This is how families maintain multi-generational charitable legacies through a DAF at a fraction of the cost of a private foundation.

**Grant timeline:** There is no legal requirement to distribute funds by a specific date (unlike private foundations, which have a 3.5% disbursement quota). A DAF can theoretically sit invested for decades before making grants. In practice, most sponsoring organizations prefer active grant-making but will not force it.

## PART 4: Private Foundations — The Billionaire Structure

### 4.1 What is a Private Foundation and Why Do the Wealthy Love It? `[RISK: LOW]` **[FUTURE]**

**Plain English:** A private foundation is YOUR OWN registered charity — a corporation or trust that you control, that has obtained charitable status from CRA, and that exists to carry out charitable purposes. You are on the board. You decide where the money goes. You can employ family members. You can invest the foundation's assets. Your name is on the door.

Billionaires love private foundations because:
1. The initial donation gets a full tax receipt (same as any charity)
2. The foundation's investment income is tax-exempt
3. The family controls the board and therefore the capital allocation
4. Family members can be employed (at reasonable salaries for genuine services)
5. The foundation can make grants to causes the family cares about — on the family's timeline
6. The foundation can fund programs, sponsor events, and build a public profile
7. The foundation assets do not go through probate at death — they continue under the board's control

The Gates Foundation, Buffett Foundation, and virtually every billionaire's major charitable vehicle is a private foundation structure (or its US equivalent).

---

### 4.2 The Mechanics and Costs `[RISK: LOW]`

**To set up a private foundation in Canada:**

1. Incorporate the foundation (not-for-profit corporation) — $1,500-$3,000 in legal fees
2. Draft charitable objects and bylaws — $2,000-$5,000 in legal fees
3. Apply to CRA for charitable registration — Form T2050, takes 3-6 months
4. Total setup cost: approximately $5,000-$15,000

**Annual operating costs:**

| Cost | Approximate Amount |
|------|-------------------|
| Annual audit (mandatory for foundations) | $3,000-$8,000 |
| T3010 annual information return (CRA filing) | $1,000-$2,000 (accountant) |
| Legal/compliance (minutes, bylaw updates) | $1,000-$3,000 |
| Director & officer liability insurance | $500-$2,000 |
| **Total annual overhead** | **$5,500-$15,000** |

This overhead is why private foundations only make economic sense above a certain asset threshold. At $200,000 in foundation assets earning 6%, you earn $12,000/year — barely covering costs. At $1,000,000, you earn $60,000/year — comfortable margin above costs.

**Rule of thumb:** Do not set up a private foundation unless you have committed at least $500,000 in total lifetime giving to it, or you have specific non-financial reasons (family legacy, naming rights, control over programming). Otherwise, a DAF delivers 90% of the benefit at 5% of the cost.

---

### 4.3 Private Foundation Rules — The Key Constraints `[RISK: MEDIUM]`

**Disbursement quota (ITA s.149.1(1)):** A private foundation must disburse at least **3.5% of the average value of property NOT used directly in charitable activities** in each fiscal year. In plain terms: if the foundation has $1,000,000 in investments, it must grant out at least $35,000 per year to qualified donees. Failure triggers a 110% disbursement tax on the shortfall (ITA s.188.1).

This was increased from 3.5% to 5% in Budget 2022 — but the 3.5% rate still applies to assets held before the effective date. Check the most current CRA guidance on which rate applies to your specific asset base.

**Non-arm's length transactions (ITA s.188.1):** The foundation cannot enter into transactions with "non-arm's length" persons (you, your family, companies you control) except at fair market value. Cannot pay above-market rent for office space you own. Cannot invest in your own company at preferential terms. CRA watches this closely.

**Prohibited investments (ITA s.149.1(2)):** A private foundation cannot hold more than 2% of any class of shares of a corporation if the foundation and "relevant persons" together hold more than 20%. This prevents using the foundation as a vehicle to maintain voting control of a corporation while getting a tax deduction.

**Family employment — the right way:** You CAN employ family members to do genuine work for the foundation. Grant administration, program coordination, communications, bookkeeping. The salary must be reasonable for the work performed. Document everything. The moment it looks like a personal benefit, CRA will challenge it. `[RISK: MEDIUM]`

## PART 5: Cultural Property Donations — The Art Strategy

### 5.1 Certified Cultural Property — Zero Capital Gains AND No Income Cap `[RISK: MEDIUM]` **[FUTURE]**

**Plain English:** If you own something classified as "cultural property" — think valuable art, rare books, historical objects, Indigenous artifacts, archival material — and you donate it to a designated institution (a museum, gallery, university, archive), you get two exceptional benefits that no other donation category provides:

1. Capital gains inclusion rate = **zero** (same as listed securities, but this applies to ART, not just stocks)
2. The 75%-of-income donation limit does NOT apply — you can offset **100%** of your net income

This means if you have a $500,000 painting and your income is $100,000, you can claim a $500,000 donation receipt, carry $400,000 forward for 5 years, and potentially pay zero income tax for 5-6 years.

The ultra-wealthy have been using art donations to eliminate tax bills for decades.

---

### 5.2 The Rules for Cultural Property Donation `[RISK: MEDIUM]`

**Step 1 — Certification:** The Canadian Cultural Property Export Review Board (CCPERB) must certify that the property is of "outstanding significance and national importance." This is a federal board that evaluates the cultural and heritage value of the item.

**Step 2 — Designated institution:** The recipient must be a "designated institution or public authority" — typically a museum, art gallery, library, archive, or university. CRA maintains a list of designated institutions. Not all charities qualify.

**Step 3 — Valuation:** An independent qualified appraiser must value the property. This is where CRA scrutinizes carefully. If the appraised value seems inflated versus market comparables, expect an audit.

**Tax treatment:**
- ITA s.39(1)(a)(i.1) — capital gain on cultural property donation = nil
- ITA s.118.1(1) — "total cultural gifts" have no 75% income limit
- 5-year carry forward for unclaimed cultural gift credits

**Designated institution examples:** Royal Ontario Museum, Art Gallery of Ontario, National Gallery of Canada, Library and Archives Canada, major university libraries.

**CRA warning:** Beware of "art donation tax shelters" that promised to acquire art cheaply and get inflated appraised values. CRA has shut down dozens of these schemes and reassessed billions in false credits. If someone pitches you an art donation arrangement where the economics seem too good, walk away. The legitimate strategy requires you to genuinely own the property over time, then donate it. `[RISK: HIGH]` for structured tax shelter art schemes.

**ITA references:** s.39(1)(a)(i.1) (nil capital gain), s.118.1(1) definition of "total cultural gifts," Cultural Property Export and Import Act (federal legislation governing CCPERB).

## PART 6: Ecologically Sensitive Land

### 6.1 Donate Land to Preserve Nature — Zero Capital Gains, No Income Cap `[RISK: LOW]` **[FUTURE]**

**Plain English:** If you own land that Environment and Climate Change Canada certifies as ecologically sensitive — wetlands, forests, habitat for Species at Risk, Carolinian forests, shoreline — and you donate it (or a conservation easement on it) to a qualified organization, you get:

1. Zero capital gains on any appreciated value
2. No 75% income limit — offsets up to 100% of net income
3. 10-year carry forward (extended from 5 years in Budget 2022 for ecological gifts — verify current rules)

**This is directly relevant for rural Ontario.** Collingwood area (Georgian Bay shoreline, Niagara Escarpment, Bruce Peninsula) has some of the most ecologically significant private land in Canada. Landowners who hold waterfront property or forest land can use the Ecological Gifts Program to dramatically reduce their estate's tax burden.

**Ecological Gifts Program (EGP):** Administered by Environment and Climate Change Canada. The landowner applies for certification, the property is assessed by federal ecologists, and if approved, the donation to a land trust or government body qualifies for the exceptional tax treatment.

**Qualified recipients:** Provincial land trusts (Ontario Heritage Trust, Nature Conservancy of Canada), municipal governments, federal and provincial Crown.

**Conservation easement (partial donation):** You do not have to donate the whole property. A conservation easement is a legal restriction you place on the land — you keep ownership but permanently give up the right to develop it. The value of the development rights forgone = the donation amount. This is how farmers and cottage owners get the tax benefit while keeping the property in the family.

**Legal authority:** ITA s.38(a.2) (nil capital gain for ecological gifts), ITA s.110.1(1)(d) (corporate deduction for ecological gifts), s.118.1(1) definition of "total ecological gifts."

## PART 7: Life Insurance Donations — The Long Game

### 7.1 Name a Charity as Life Insurance Beneficiary `[RISK: LOW]` **[FUTURE]**

**Plain English:** The simplest version: when you set up a life insurance policy, name a registered charity as the beneficiary (or one of the beneficiaries). When you die, the charity receives the death benefit. Your estate receives a donation receipt equal to the death benefit. That receipt is used on your final tax return to offset income in the year of death (when the 100% income limit applies). This can significantly reduce the estate's final tax bill.

This costs you nothing during your lifetime. The premium you are already paying continues to protect your estate. You are just redirecting the death benefit from your estate (where it would otherwise be subject to probate and potential tax) to a charity (directly, outside your estate, no probate) with a retroactive tax credit.

---

### 7.2 Donate an Existing Life Insurance Policy `[RISK: LOW]` **[FUTURE]**

**More complex version:** Transfer ownership of an existing life insurance policy directly to a charity during your lifetime. Here is what happens:

- At time of transfer: you receive a donation receipt equal to the **cash surrender value (CSV)** of the policy at the time of transfer
- Going forward: all future premium payments you make on behalf of the charity are also deductible as charitable donations (you pay the premiums, the charity is the policy owner)
- At death: the charity receives the full death benefit, entirely outside your estate

**Why this can be powerful:** If you bought a whole life or universal life policy years ago and it has built significant CSV, that CSV sitting inside the policy is essentially trapped equity. By donating the policy, you unlock a tax receipt for that equity NOW, and all ongoing premiums become tax-deductible.

**Mechanics:** Policy must be absolute assignment to the charity (not just a beneficiary designation — that does not qualify for the donation of future premiums). Charity must own the policy and have insurable interest.

**ITA reference:** s.118.1(5.1) — donation receipt for fair market value of property donated (which for a life insurance policy is the CSV), confirmed in CRA IT-244R3.

---

### 7.3 Insured Annuity Donation Strategy `[RISK: MEDIUM]` **[FUTURE]**

**Plain English:** This is a more complex structure used by people 55+ who want a guaranteed income stream, want to preserve their estate, and want to make a major charitable gift. The mechanics:

1. Buy a life annuity from an insurance company — they pay you a guaranteed income for life
2. Donate the annuity payments (or a portion of them) to your chosen charity — you get donation receipts annually
3. Simultaneously buy a life insurance policy to "replace" the estate value that the annuity consumed
4. Net result: guaranteed income stream, annual donation credits, estate preserved through insurance proceeds at death

This is a strategy for ages 60+ with significant capital (typically $500K+ to make the numbers work). Not relevant for CC now, but included for long-range awareness.

**Who structures these:** Chartered Life Underwriters (CLU designation). Not a DIY strategy — requires a specialist.

## PART 8: Corporate Charitable Giving

### 8.1 Corporation Donates vs Individual Donates — Which Wins? `[RISK: LOW]` **[INCORPORATED]**

**Plain English:** Corporations get a tax deduction for charitable donations (reduces taxable income). Individuals get a tax credit (reduces tax payable directly). Which is better?

It depends on the corporate tax rate versus the personal credit rate.

**Ontario CCPC (eligible for Small Business Deduction) tax rates:**
- Federal small business rate: 9%
- Ontario small business rate: 3.2%
- Combined: **12.2%**

If your OASIS corporation donates $10,000, it gets a deduction at 12.2% — saving **$1,220** in corporate tax.

If CC personally donates $10,000, he gets a credit at ~40.16% — saving **$3,976** in personal tax.

**The math is clear: pay yourself a salary from the corporation first, then donate personally.** The personal credit rate (~40%) beats the small business corporate deduction rate (12.2%) by a factor of 3.3x. Always.

**Exception 1 — High passive income:** If OASIS starts earning significant passive income (investment income, royalties, rental), that income is taxed at the general corporate rate (~26.5% in Ontario) rather than the SBD rate. A corporate donation deduction at 26.5% is closer to the personal credit rate. Still slightly worse, but the gap narrows.

**Exception 2 — Corporate capital gains:** If the corporation sells appreciated securities, it can donate them directly and eliminate the capital gain at the corporate level too. ITA s.38(a.1) applies to corporate taxpayers as well. The corporation pays no capital gains tax on the donated securities. The deduction equals FMV. This can be more efficient than distributing proceeds to shareholders (who would then pay personal tax on dividends) and donating personally.

**Exception 3 — Retention of cash:** If your goal is to minimize the cash leaving the corporation, donating from the corporation preserves your personal cash. The corporation gets the deduction; you do not need to draw more salary to fund personal donations. This is a cash flow argument, not a tax rate argument.

---

### 8.2 Corporate Donation Limits and Rules `[RISK: LOW]` **[INCORPORATED]**

**Annual limit:** 75% of the corporation's net income for the year (same as individuals). No 5-year carry forward for corporations — unused room expires. [Important: verify this under current ITA — the 5-year carry forward applies to individuals under s.118.1; corporations deduct under s.110.1 which has its own carry forward rules — 5-year carry forward IS available for corporations under s.110.1(1)(a)].

**Securities donation:** Same zero-capital-gains rule applies to corporations. ITA s.38(a.1) reduces the inclusion rate to zero for corporate donations of listed securities to qualified donees.

**T3010 disclosure:** Your corporation's charitable donations appear on its T2 return and are publicly visible in aggregate. Not a concern for most, but worth knowing.

**Non-arm's length:** CRA will challenge any transaction where the corporation donates to a charity and receives a benefit back (e.g., naming rights that have commercial value, advertising, exclusive access). The "gift" must be a true gift with no advantage to the donor corporation.

### 8.3 The Corporate Giving Structure Post-Incorporation `[RISK: LOW]` **[INCORPORATED]**

Once OASIS incorporates and you have an OpCo/HoldCo structure (see ATLAS_INCORPORATION_TAX_STRATEGIES.md), here is the optimal giving architecture:

1. **Operating income** → Pay salary/dividends to CC personally → Donate personally for maximum credit rate (~40%)
2. **Capital gains in HoldCo** → Donate appreciated securities directly from HoldCo to DAF or charity → Zero capital gains at corporate level + full FMV deduction
3. **Passive investment income in HoldCo** → If approaching $50K AAII grind (reduces SBD room), donate from HoldCo to reduce passive income AND get deduction at ~26.5%
4. **Life insurance held in HoldCo** → Name charity as beneficiary → Donation receipt to estate at death

This is the architecture that wealthy incorporated business owners use to minimize tax at every income level simultaneously.

## PART 9: Flow-Through Shares + Donation — The Super Strategy

### 9.1 How Flow-Through Share Donations Work `[RISK: MEDIUM]` **[FUTURE]**

**Plain English:** This strategy sounds complicated but the concept is simple — you get a tax deduction for buying the shares, and then a donation credit when you give them away. Done right, you can receive MORE in combined tax benefits than you actually spent.

Here is the backstory: Canadian mining, oil & gas, and renewable energy companies are allowed to "flow through" their exploration and development expenses to investors. You buy "flow-through shares" in one of these companies. The company passes its tax-deductible exploration costs to you. You deduct those costs against your own income. This is a federal policy to encourage investment in Canadian resource exploration.

Now here is the twist: if those flow-through shares have an adjusted cost base of zero (which they often do, because all of your investment was deducted as exploration expenses), and if the shares are now worth something (say, they appreciated as the company made a discovery), you can donate them to charity. The shares are listed securities, so the capital gains inclusion rate is zero (ITA s.38(a.1)). You get a donation receipt for the full FMV. Zero capital gains tax. Full donation credit.

**The combined math — example at $150K income (43% combined marginal rate):**

| Step | Amount | Tax Impact |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Buy $10,000 flow-through shares | $10,000 out of pocket | Deduction of $10,000 exploration expenses |
| Tax savings from deduction (43%) | — | **+$4,300 tax saved** |
| Shares still worth $10,000 (no appreciation) | — | Ready to donate |
| Donate $10,000 of shares to charity | $0 capital gains tax (ACB = $0, but inclusion = 0%) | Donation credit of ~$4,016 (40% of $10K) |
| **Net position** | Donated $10,000 to charity | Tax benefit = $4,300 + $4,016 = **$8,316** |
| **Out-of-pocket cost** | **$1,684** | You donated $10,000 for $1,684 |

That is not a typo. You give $10,000 to charity and the net cost to you is $1,684. The rest came from tax savings.

**If the shares appreciated to $15,000:**

| Tax benefit | Amount |
|------------|--------|
| Exploration deduction (43% × $10,000) | $4,300 |
| Donation credit (40% × $15,000) | $6,024 |
| Capital gains tax saved (on $15,000 gain, ACB $0) | $0 (zero inclusion) |
| Total tax benefit | $10,324 |
| Out of pocket | $10,000 invested − $10,324 = **net positive** |

You donated $15,000 to charity and made $324 after all tax impacts. The charity wins. You win. CRA does not.

---

### 9.2 Risks and the CRA Warning `[RISK: HIGH]`

CRA is well aware of the flow-through share donation strategy and watches it carefully.

**Legitimate version:** You genuinely invest in a flow-through LP or flow-through shares of a junior mining company. The investment has real risk. The company spends the money on actual exploration. You hold the shares. They appreciate (or don't — there is genuine investment risk). Then you donate.

**Abusive version:** Promoters set up pre-arranged transactions where you "invest" in flow-through shares, the company incurs fake exploration expenses, you get the deduction, and you immediately donate the shares to a charity that is in on the scheme. No real investment risk. No real exploration. CRA calls this a "gifting tax shelter" and has won these cases in Tax Court repeatedly.

The bright line: if someone approaches you with a flow-through donation arrangement that seems like a guaranteed outcome or promises specific tax results, it is likely a shelter. Legitimate flow-through investing has genuine risk and the decision to donate comes after the investment period, not as part of a pre-arranged scheme.

**Atlas recommendation:** Flow-through shares are a legitimate investment that can be combined with donation planning. But treat the investment decision and the donation decision separately. Invest in flow-through if the underlying exploration thesis makes sense as a speculative position. If the shares appreciate, then consider donation. Do not buy flow-through specifically as a tax shelter donation scheme.

**ITA references:** ITA s.66.1 (Canadian Exploration Expense), s.66.2 (Canadian Development Expense), s.38(a.1) (zero inclusion on donation of listed securities), s.66(12.6)-(12.73) (renunciation to shareholders/partners).

## PART 10: Strategic Giving Calendar

### 10.1 The December Securities Flush `[RISK: LOW]` **[SOON]**

**Plain English:** At the end of a high-income year, look at your taxable investment accounts. Identify your most appreciated positions. Donate a portion directly to your DAF or preferred charity in December. You get the donation credit in the current tax year, eliminating the capital gains you would have eventually triggered. This is especially powerful in years where you have also realized large capital gains from other sources (crypto sales, business asset sales).

**Execution checklist:**
- By December 15: Identify positions to donate and contact brokerage and charity
- By December 20: Initiate the transfer (allow 5-7 business days for settlement)
- By December 31: Transfer must be settled and confirmed to count in current tax year
- January: Obtain official donation receipts from charity
- April-June: Claim on Schedule 9 of T1 return

### 10.2 The Income-Bunching Strategy `[RISK: LOW]` **[SOON]**

**When to apply:** Years when OASIS has an exceptional revenue spike — a major contract, a product launch, an equity sale, or any other event that pushes income significantly above normal.

**Strategy:** In that year:
1. Contribute to RRSP (standard income smoothing — reduces taxable income before applying donation credit)
2. Fund the DAF with appreciated securities (gets the credit + eliminates capital gains)
3. Claim any banked donation receipts (up to 5 years of unused carry-forwards)
4. The combination of RRSP deduction + capital gains elimination + donation credit can dramatically reduce or eliminate the incremental tax on the income spike

**Order of operations matters:**
1. First: RRSP contributions (reduce net income, which affects credit rate calculation)
2. Second: Donation credits (applied to the tax calculated after deductions)
3. Result: Maximum combined benefit

### 10.3 Capital Gains Year Strategy `[RISK: LOW]` **[SOON]**

In years where you realize large capital gains — selling appreciated crypto, selling business assets, selling real estate — the marginal rate on those gains is elevated. Donating appreciated securities in the same year:

1. Eliminates the capital gains on the donated securities themselves
2. Generates a donation credit that directly offsets the capital gains tax on your OTHER capital gains

**Example:** OASIS sells its IP for $500,000, triggering $250,000 taxable capital gain. CC is in the top bracket. In the same year, donate $50,000 of appreciated ETFs to a DAF. The donation credit ($20,000) directly offsets a portion of the capital gains tax bill. The capital gains elimination on the ETFs removes another $X from the tax bill. You reduce the brutal capital gains tax year by combining giving strategies.

### 10.4 Year of Death — The 100% Override `[RISK: LOW]` **[FUTURE]**

In the year of death and the immediately preceding year, the 75% income limit on charitable donations is lifted entirely. A deceased person's estate can claim donation credits equal to **100% of net income** on the terminal return.

This is the most powerful application of charitable giving in estate planning. If structured properly:

1. The estate donates appreciated securities, RRIF proceeds, or insurance to a DAF or charity
2. Donation receipt equals 100% of net income in year of death
3. The entire capital gains exposure on the deemed disposition at death (ITA s.70(5)) can be offset by donation credits
4. Net result: estate pays almost zero income tax in the terminal year

This is how Canadian billionaires structure "deathbed donations" to eliminate tax entirely on their estates. Not morbid planning — proactive planning.

**Important:** The 100% limit applies to the year of death AND the year preceding death. So if terminal illness is diagnosed, a donation in the preceding calendar year can also capture the 100% limit. Do not wait until December 31 of the year of death — act in the preceding year as well.

## PART 11: How the Ultra-Wealthy Actually Do It

### 11.1 The Buffett Play — Donating Your Life's Work `[RISK: LOW]`

Warren Buffett has committed 99% of his Berkshire Hathaway shares to charity. The mechanism: annual gifts of Berkshire B shares to the Gates Foundation and four family foundations. Each gift:

- Triggers zero capital gains (shares held since the 1960s at near-zero ACB — in the US this would be enormous capital gains tax)
- Generates a charitable deduction equal to full FMV (in the US, limited to 30% of AGI for appreciated property to private foundations, carried forward 5 years)
- Effectively transfers billions out of his estate at zero tax

**Canadian parallel:** The equivalent strategy is donating appreciated public company shares or ETFs directly to a DAF or registered charity. The zero-capital-gains rule under ITA s.38(a.1) is the Canadian version of the Buffett play. Same logic, different legislation. Available at any income level.

**The lesson:** Buffett could sell the shares and donate cash. He does not — because selling triggers tax. He donates the shares directly, eliminating the tax entirely. Every single dollar of the capital gain is converted to charitable impact rather than going to the government. CC should apply exactly this logic to any appreciated investment position.

### 11.2 The Zuckerberg Play — Total Control Without Foundation Constraints `[RISK: LOW]` (US only)

The Chan Zuckerberg Initiative is a **limited liability company (LLC)**, not a charity and not a foundation. When Zuckerberg pledged 99% of his Facebook shares to it, the "pledge" did not generate a tax deduction — because an LLC is not a qualified charity. This surprised many people.

Why an LLC? Because an LLC:
- Has no disbursement quota (foundations must pay out 5% annually)
- Can make political donations
- Can invest in for-profit companies
- Has no public disclosure requirements
- Has no CRA/IRS oversight
- Gives total control without any charitable law constraints

The trade-off: no tax deduction on contributions.

**Canadian equivalent:** This exact structure is not available in Canada in the same way, but a similar effect can be achieved through a holding company with philanthropic mandates (not a registered charity, so no tax benefits — but also no regulatory constraints). This is relevant context for understanding why the ultra-wealthy sometimes choose CONTROL over TAX EFFICIENCY. Atlas files this as: know the trade-off and choose deliberately.

### 11.3 The Canadian Billionaire Play — Corporate Control Donation `[RISK: HIGH]`

The most sophisticated Canadian strategy for business owners:

1. Business owner holds controlling interest in an operating company
2. At retirement or succession, donates a portion of shares to a private foundation they control
3. The donation of CCPC shares triggers a capital gains exemption analysis — but if structured properly through an estate freeze (freezing current value, issuing new growth shares), the donated shares can be structured to minimize tax
4. Foundation receives dividends from operating company tax-free (as a registered charity)
5. Foundation board (family) controls grant-making indefinitely
6. Effective outcome: operating company continues, capital gains eliminated or minimized, tax-exempt dividend income flows into charitable purposes controlled by family

**This requires:** An estate freeze, corporate reorganization, legal counsel, and a clear philanthropic purpose. `[RISK: HIGH]` for DIY — requires a specialized tax lawyer and CPA team.

### 11.4 The Sprott Play — Flow-Through Funds at Scale `[RISK: MEDIUM]`

Eric Sprott built significant charitable giving through flow-through share investments at scale. The mechanism: high-income investors (doctors, lawyers, business owners) invest in a flow-through limited partnership. **Category 6 — Universities outside Canada:**
- Specific institutions prescribed in the Income Tax Regulations (typically Commonwealth universities, major US research universities)
- Requires the donor to have attended, or a close family member to have attended, or the donation must be for Canadian student/faculty research

**Category 7 — Registered journalism organizations (new 2020):**
- Qualifying journalism organizations (QJO) registered after 2019 Budget changes

**How to verify:** Canada.ca/charities-registry allows real-time search by organization name or registration number. Always verify before donating for tax purposes.
