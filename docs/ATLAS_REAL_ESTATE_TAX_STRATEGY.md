## 1. Principal Residence Exemption (PRE)

**Applicability: FUTURE** (CC does not currently own a home)

### Legal Framework

- **ITA s.40(2)(b):** Reduces the capital gain on disposition of a principal residence. The exempt portion is calculated by the formula:
  ```
  Exempt gain = Total gain x (1 + # of years designated) / # of years owned
  ```
- **ITA s.54 "principal residence" definition:** A housing unit (house, condo, cottage, mobile home, houseboat) that the taxpayer, their spouse, or dependent child **ordinarily inhabited** at some point during the year.
- **ITA s.54.1:** Extended PRE for job relocation (property still qualifies for up to 4 years if the taxpayer moves for employment and intends to return).

### The "+1 Rule"

The numerator in the PRE formula is `1 + number of designation years`. This means:

- If you own a property for 10 years and designate all 10 years, the entire gain is exempt: `(1 + 10) / 10 = 110%`, capped at 100%.
- The +1 gives a **one-year overlap** — critical when transitioning between two properties. You can designate Property A for year N and Property B for year N+1, with the +1 covering the gap year on whichever property you sell first.
- **Limitation:** Only one property per family unit (you + spouse) can be designated as principal residence for any given year. The +1 does NOT let you claim two properties simultaneously for the same year.

### Designation Years

- You do **not** need to file a designation in the year you acquire or live in the property. Designation is made **on disposition** via Form T2091 (or T1255 for a deceased person's estate).
- Strategic designation: If you own a principal residence AND a cottage, you designate years to whichever property had the higher per-year capital gain.
  - Example: Home gained $200K over 10 years ($20K/yr). Cottage gained $150K over 8 years ($18.75K/yr). Designate the home as PR for all overlapping years — higher per-year gain.

### Multiple Properties

- **Only one designation per year per family unit** (ITA s.54, "principal residence" para (c.1), added in 1982).
- Pre-1982 rule: Each spouse could designate a separate principal residence. This no longer applies.
- **Change of use rules (ITA s.45(1)):** If you stop using a property as your principal residence (e.g., start renting it out), there is a **deemed disposition** at FMV. You can elect under **ITA s.45(2)** to defer this deemed disposition for up to **4 years** (or longer if you move for employment under s.54.1).
- **ITA s.45(3):** If you convert a rental property TO a principal residence, you can elect to deem the conversion not to have occurred, preventing a disposition. However, you cannot claim CCA during the period you want this election to apply.

### CRA Reporting Requirements

- **Form T2091(IND):** Must be filed with the tax return for the year of sale if claiming the PRE. Since 2016, even a **fully exempt** disposition must be reported on Schedule 3 and T2091 must be filed. Failure to file: CRA can deny the exemption (though they may grant a late designation with a penalty of $100/month, max $8,000 — ITA s.220(3.2)).
- **Reporting on Schedule 3:** Report the disposition, calculate the gain, apply PRE formula, resulting taxable gain = $0 (if fully exempt).

### CC-Specific Notes

- CC does not currently own property. When purchasing a first home, the PRE is straightforward — designate all years, full exemption on sale.
- If CC buys a property and later rents it out, file the s.45(2) election to maintain PRE for up to 4 additional years.
- **FHSA angle:** CC should open an FHSA ($8K/year, $40K lifetime) for a future down payment. Contributions are tax-deductible, and withdrawals for a qualifying first home purchase are tax-free. This is the single most powerful registered account for a first-time buyer.

## 2. Rental Property Taxation

**Applicability: FUTURE** (relevant when CC acquires rental property or house-hacks)

### Rental Income Reporting

- Report on **Form T776** (Statement of Real Estate Rentals).
- **Gross rental income** minus **deductible expenses** = net rental income (or loss).
- Net rental income added to T1 as income from property (Line 12600).

### Deductible Expenses (T776)

| Expense | Deductible? | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| Mortgage interest | Yes | Interest portion only, not principal repayment |
| Property taxes | Yes | Municipal taxes |
| Insurance | Yes | Building insurance |
| Repairs & maintenance | Yes | Must maintain, not improve (painting = repair; new deck = capital) |
| Utilities | Yes | If landlord pays |
| Property management fees | Yes | Including software/platforms |
| Advertising | Yes | Tenant acquisition |
| Legal/accounting fees | Yes | Lease preparation, tax filing |
| Travel to rental property | Yes | Reasonable, if you actively manage |
| CCA (depreciation) | Yes* | Subject to rental loss restriction — see below |

### Capital Cost Allowance (CCA)

- **Class 1 (4% declining balance):** Existing residential buildings acquired before 2024 or not meeting the new residential threshold.
- **Class 1 — New Residential Rental Property (10% declining balance):** Available for new eligible apartment buildings with at least 4 dwelling units acquired after 2023, per the Fall Economic Statement 2023. This accelerated rate encourages purpose-built rental construction.
- **Immediate expensing:** The Accelerated Investment Incentive (AIIP) provides an enhanced first-year CCA. For Class 1, the half-year rule is suspended in the first year, effectively giving 1.5x the normal CCA in year one.

### Rental Loss Restriction (ITA s.1100(11))

**Critical rule:** CCA on rental property **cannot be used to create or increase a rental loss**.

- If your rental expenses (excluding CCA) already exceed rental income, you cannot claim any CCA.
- If your rental income exceeds expenses by $5,000, you can claim up to $5,000 in CCA — but no more, even if the CCA pool is larger.
- CCA is **optional** — any unclaimed amount stays in the pool for future years. Strategic deferral: skip CCA in low-income years, claim it in high-income years.

### Terminal Loss vs Recapture on Sale

When you sell a rental property:

- **Recapture (ITA s.13(1)):** If sale price > UCC (undepreciated capital cost) of the class, the difference (up to original cost) is added to income as **recapture**. Taxed at 100% as regular income, NOT as a capital gain.
  - Example: Bought for $400K, claimed $50K CCA (UCC = $350K), sold for $420K. Recapture = $50K (taxed as income). Capital gain = $20K (taxed at 50% inclusion).
- **Terminal loss (ITA s.20(16)):** If you dispose of the **last asset** in a CCA class and UCC > $0, the remaining UCC is deductible as a terminal loss. Fully deductible against income.
  - Exception: Terminal loss is **denied** on buildings (ITA s.13(21.1)) if the land is sold at a gain. The denied terminal loss is added to the cost of the land, reducing the capital gain on the land instead.

### CC-Specific Notes

- House-hacking (buying a duplex/triplex, living in one unit, renting others) is a strong first-property strategy. The owner-occupied portion qualifies for PRE; the rental portion is reported on T776.
- **Mixed-use allocation:** If CC buys a duplex (50/50 split), 50% of mortgage interest, property tax, insurance, etc. is deductible against rental income. The other 50% is personal (non-deductible, but the unit qualifies for PRE).
- CCA strategy: In early years when CC's self-employment income is growing, deferring CCA may be smart. Claim CCA in higher-income years for maximum tax savings.

## 3. Real Estate Flipping Rules (2023+)

**Applicability: NOW** (this rule applies to ALL residential property sales; CC must know it before any purchase)

### The Deemed Business Income Rule

**ITA s.12(12) (effective January 1, 2023):** Any gain on the disposition of a "flipped property" is deemed to be **business income** (100% taxable), NOT a capital gain (50% inclusion).

**"Flipped property" (ITA s.12(13)):** A housing unit (or right to acquire one) located in Canada that was owned for **less than 365 consecutive days** before disposition.

### Impact

| Scenario | Tax Treatment |
|----------|--------------|
| Held 364 days or less | 100% business income — no capital gains treatment, **no PRE available** |
| Held 365+ days | Normal rules — capital gain with PRE potentially available |

### Exceptions (ITA s.12(14))

The deemed business income rule does NOT apply if the sale is due to, or in anticipation of:

1. **Death** of the taxpayer or a related person
2. **Addition to household** — birth, adoption, or person moving in with taxpayer
3. **Separation** — breakdown of marriage or common-law partnership (min 90 days separation)
4. **Threat to personal safety** — domestic violence, criminal threat
5. **Disability or serious illness** of taxpayer or related person
6. **Eligible relocation** — new work location at least 40 km closer (mirrors moving expense rules)
7. **Involuntary disposition** — expropriation, destruction (fire, flood, etc.)
8. **Insolvency** — relating to a deemed disposition under insolvency proceedings
9. **Employment termination** — qualifying employment change

### CRA Administrative Position

- CRA may still assess a property held > 365 days as business income if the facts indicate a flipping intention (i.e., the 365-day rule is a **floor**, not a ceiling). Intent still matters for properties held longer.
- Multiple flips = strong evidence of business intent regardless of holding period.

### CC-Specific Notes

- If CC ever buys a property, holding for a minimum of 365 days is non-negotiable to preserve capital gains treatment and PRE eligibility.
- If CC pursues a house-hack / BRRRR strategy, each property must be held > 365 days minimum.
- The exceptions are narrow — "I found a better deal" is NOT an exception.

## 4. REIT Investing Inside TFSA/RRSP

**Applicability: NOW** (CC has TFSA room and can invest in Canadian REITs immediately)

### What is a REIT Distribution?

Canadian REIT distributions are NOT simple dividends. They are a mix of:

| Component | Tax Treatment (Taxable Account) | Tax Treatment (TFSA/RRSP) |
|-----------|-------------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Other income** (rental income) | 100% taxable at marginal rate | Tax-free (TFSA) / tax-deferred (RRSP) |
| **Capital gains** | 50% inclusion rate (66.67% for gains > $250K after June 25, 2024) | Tax-free / tax-deferred |
| **Return of capital (ROC)** | NOT immediately taxable — reduces ACB. When ACB hits $0, excess ROC = capital gain | Tax-free / tax-deferred |
| **Foreign non-business income** | 100% taxable, may have foreign tax credit | Tax-free / tax-deferred (FTC lost in TFSA) |
| **Eligible dividends** | Grossed-up, dividend tax credit | Tax-free / tax-deferred |

### Optimal Account Placement

**Priority for REIT holdings:**

1. **TFSA (best):** All components tax-free forever. No withholding tax issues for Canadian REITs. ROC is irrelevant inside TFSA. This is the optimal home for REITs.
2. **RRSP (second best):** All components tax-deferred. Taxed as regular income on withdrawal. Good if CC is in a higher bracket now than expected at retirement.
3. **Taxable account (least preferred):** The "other income" component is fully taxable at marginal rate. ROC is a deferral benefit but adds complexity (ACB tracking). High-ROC REITs are more tax-efficient in taxable accounts than low-ROC REITs, but both are still worse than TFSA.

### US REITs in TFSA vs RRSP

- **RRSP:** US REIT distributions are exempt from the 15% US withholding tax under the Canada-US tax treaty (Article XVIII). RRSP is better than TFSA for US REITs.
- **TFSA:** US withholding tax of 15% applies with NO recovery. The treaty does not exempt TFSA.
- **Rule:** Canadian REITs in TFSA. US REITs (if any) in RRSP.

### CC-Specific Notes

- CC should hold Canadian REITs (e.g., RioCan, Canadian Apartment Properties, Granite REIT) inside the TFSA for fully tax-free distributions.
- If CC opens an FHSA, bond-like or REIT holdings could go there for tax-deductible contributions + tax-free growth, with eventual tax-free withdrawal for a home purchase.
- REIT investing is a way to get real estate exposure without the capital, management burden, or LTT costs of direct ownership. Ideal for CC's current stage.

## 5. Mortgage Interest Deductibility

**Applicability: FUTURE** (relevant when CC has a mortgage)

### When Mortgage Interest IS Deductible

1. **Rental property:** Interest on a mortgage used to acquire, improve, or maintain a rental property is deductible against rental income (ITA s.20(1)(c)).
2. **Business property:** Interest on a mortgage for property used in a business (e.g., OASIS office space CC owns) is deductible as a business expense.
3. **Investment loans:** Interest on borrowed money used to earn income from property or business is deductible (ITA s.20(1)(c)). The **use of the funds** determines deductibility, not the security pledged.

### When Mortgage Interest is NOT Deductible

- **Primary residence (personal use):** Mortgage interest on a home you live in is NOT deductible. Canada does not have a US-style mortgage interest deduction.
- **Mixed-use:** If 50% rental / 50% personal, only 50% of interest is deductible.

### The Smith Manoeuvre

A legal strategy to convert non-deductible mortgage interest into deductible interest:

**How it works:**
1. CC has a readvanceable mortgage (HELOC component that increases as mortgage principal is paid down).
2. Each mortgage payment reduces the principal, freeing up HELOC room.
3. CC borrows from the HELOC and invests in income-producing assets (dividend stocks, REITs, bonds).
4. The HELOC interest is now **deductible** under ITA s.20(1)(c) because the borrowed funds are used to earn investment income.
5. Over time, the non-deductible mortgage shrinks and the deductible HELOC grows.

**Requirements:**
- Funds must be used for **income-producing investments** (not TFSA contributions, not personal use).
- Must maintain clear paper trail (separate accounts for investment HELOC vs personal spending).
- CRA has accepted the Smith Manoeuvre — see Singleton v. Canada (2001 SCC 61) and Lipson v. Canada (2009 SCC 1) for the legal foundation on interest deductibility and tracing of borrowed funds.

**Risks:**
- Leveraged investing amplifies losses.
- Rising interest rates increase carrying cost.
- CRA scrutinizes if the investment purpose is not clearly documented.

### CC-Specific Notes

- When CC buys a first home, the Smith Manoeuvre is a long-term play. Requires discipline and a readvanceable mortgage product (Manulife One, Scotia STEP, National Bank All-In-One).
- More relevant once CC has significant equity. Not a day-one strategy.

## 6. Land Transfer Tax (Ontario)

**Applicability: FUTURE** (due on any property purchase in Ontario)

### Ontario Land Transfer Tax Rates

| Value of Consideration | Rate |
|-----------------------|------|
| First $55,000 | 0.5% |
| $55,001 to $250,000 | 1.0% |
| $250,001 to $400,000 | 1.5% |
| $400,001 to $2,000,000 | 2.0% |
| Over $2,000,000 | 2.5% |

**Single family residence surcharge:** An additional 0.5% applies to amounts over $2,000,000 for properties containing one or two single-family residences (effective rate 3.0% on the excess).

### Example Calculation

Property price: $500,000
- $55,000 x 0.5% = $275
- $195,000 x 1.0% = $1,950
- $150,000 x 1.5% = $2,250
- $100,000 x 2.0% = $2,000
- **Total Ontario LTT = $6,475**

### First-Time Homebuyer Rebate

- **Maximum rebate: $4,000** (covers LTT on the first $368,333 of a home's value).
- Eligibility:
  - Must be 18+
  - Canadian citizen or permanent resident
  - Never owned a home (or interest in a home) anywhere in the world
  - Spouse must also never have owned a home while being your spouse
  - Must occupy as principal residence within 9 months
- CC qualifies as a first-time buyer (assuming no prior ownership).

### Toronto Municipal Land Transfer Tax (MLTT)

- **Toronto only** — a second LTT on top of the provincial LTT.
- Rates mirror the provincial rates (0.5% to 2.5%), effectively **doubling** the LTT in Toronto.
- Toronto first-time buyer rebate: up to **$4,475**.
- Combined Toronto + Ontario first-time rebate: up to **$8,475**.
- **CC is in Collingwood** — no municipal LTT. This is a Toronto-only tax. Significant savings vs buying in Toronto.

### Non-Resident Speculation Tax (NRST) / Foreign Buyer Ban

- **NRST:** 25% tax on residential property purchases by foreign nationals or foreign corporations in Ontario (expanded province-wide in 2022, increased from 20% to 25% in 2023).
- **Federal foreign buyer ban (Prohibition on the Purchase of Residential Property by Non-Canadians Act):** Extended through January 1, 2027. Prohibits non-Canadians from purchasing residential property (exceptions for PR applicants, refugees, certain work permit holders).
- **CC is Canadian** — neither NRST nor the foreign buyer ban applies.

### CC-Specific Notes

- Budget $4K-$7K for LTT on a Collingwood-area property ($400K-$600K range). With the first-time buyer rebate, net LTT cost: ~$2K-$3K.
- LTT is due on closing, paid by the buyer. Not financed into the mortgage.

## 7. HST on New Construction

**Applicability: FUTURE** (relevant if CC buys a newly constructed home or pre-construction condo)

### The Basic Rule

- **Resale homes:** No HST. Exempt supply under ETA Schedule V, Part I.
- **New construction / substantially renovated homes:** 13% HST applies (5% federal + 8% Ontario provincial).
- **Assignment sales** (selling a pre-construction contract before closing): HST applies. The profit on assignment is also taxable as business income in most cases.

### New Housing Rebate (Federal — GST Component)

| Fair Market Value | Rebate |
|------------------|--------|
| Up to $350,000 | 36% of GST paid (max $6,300) |
| $350,001 to $450,000 | Rebate phases out linearly |
| Over $450,000 | No federal GST rebate |

- Maximum federal rebate: **$6,300** (36% x $17,500 GST on $350K).

### Ontario New Housing Rebate (PST Component)

| Fair Market Value | Rebate |
|------------------|--------|
| Up to $400,000 | 75% of Ontario PST paid (max $24,000) |
| Over $400,000 | Still $24,000 max (does NOT phase out) |

- Maximum Ontario rebate: **$24,000** (75% x 8% x $400K).
- Unlike the federal rebate, the Ontario rebate does NOT phase out above $400K. You always get up to $24,000.

### Combined Impact Example

New home at $500,000:
- HST = $500,000 x 13% = $65,000
- Federal GST rebate: $0 (FMV > $450K, fully phased out)
- Ontario PST rebate: $24,000
- **Net HST cost: $41,000**

New home at $350,000:
- HST = $350,000 x 13% = $45,500
- Federal GST rebate: $6,300
- Ontario PST rebate: $21,000 (75% x 8% x $350K)
- **Net HST cost: $18,200**

### Rental Property New Housing Rebate

- If the new home is purchased as a rental (not owner-occupied), a **New Residential Rental Property Rebate (NRRP)** is available instead.
- Federal NRRP rebate: same structure as owner-occupied (36% of GST, phases out $350K-$450K).
- Ontario NRRP rebate: 75% of provincial component, max $24,000.
- Must be rented to an individual as their primary residence (not short-term rental / Airbnb in most cases).

### Assignment Sales

- If CC buys pre-construction and assigns the contract before closing, the assignment is taxable:
  - **HST** on the assignment profit (builder typically collects).
  - **Income tax** — CRA treats assignment profits as **business income** (100% taxable), not capital gains, because the property was never used as a residence.
- Assignment sales are a high-tax event. Avoid unless the profit margin justifies it.

### CC-Specific Notes

- If CC buys in Collingwood, resale avoids HST entirely. New construction adds $18K-$41K+ depending on price.
- Budget for HST on new builds. Builders often "include" HST in the sticker price but the rebate may go to the builder — read the APS carefully.

## 8. Corporate-Held Real Estate

**Applicability: FUTURE** (relevant when CC incorporates OASIS and considers holding real estate in the corp)

### Pros of Corporate-Held Real Estate

1. **Lower initial tax rate on rental income:** Small business passive income taxed at ~50.17% in Ontario (not the small business rate of 12.2% — rental income is passive, not active business income). However, a portion is refundable via the RDTOH mechanism when dividends are paid out.
2. **Asset protection:** Creditors of CC personally cannot easily reach corporate-held assets (subject to piercing the corporate veil).
3. **Estate planning:** Shares of a holding company are easier to transfer / estate freeze than direct real estate.
4. **Reinvestment:** Rental income retained in the corp can be reinvested without personal tax until distributed.

### Cons of Corporate-Held Real Estate

1. **No Principal Residence Exemption:** A corporation CANNOT claim the PRE. Any gain on sale is fully taxable.
2. **No $250K capital gains exemption threshold:** The enhanced individual capital gains inclusion rate (66.67% above $250K, effective June 25, 2024) has a $250K annual threshold for individuals — corporations get NO threshold. All corporate capital gains are at 66.67% inclusion (effective rate ~33% in the corp, plus personal tax on distribution).
3. **Passive income grind:** Corporate passive income (including rental income and capital gains) over $50,000 reduces the small business deduction for OASIS's active business income. The grind: for every $1 of passive income over $50K, $5 of active business income loses the small business rate. At $150K passive income, the small business deduction is fully eliminated.
   - **Impact for CC:** If OASIS earns $80K active income and the corp earns $60K rental income, the $10K excess passive income ($60K - $50K) grinds away $50K of the small business deduction. OASIS would pay higher tax on $50K of its active income.
4. **Double taxation on sale:** Corporate capital gain taxed in corp, then taxed again when distributed as a dividend (partially offset by capital dividend account and RDTOH refund, but not fully).
5. **Land transfer tax on transfer:** Transferring personally-owned property into a corp triggers LTT (unless an exemption applies).
6. **Financing difficulty:** Some lenders offer less favorable terms for corporate-held residential property.

### Section 85 Rollover

- **ITA s.85(1):** Allows a taxpayer to transfer property to a corporation on a tax-deferred basis (rollover). The taxpayer elects a transfer price between the property's tax cost (ACB/UCC) and its FMV.
- **Application to real estate:** CC could transfer a personally-owned rental property into a holding company at its tax cost, deferring the capital gain and recapture until the corporation eventually sells.
- **Requirements:** Must file a joint election (Form T2057) with the corporation. The consideration received must include at least one share of the corporation. Boot (non-share consideration like cash or debt assumed) cannot exceed the elected amount without triggering gain.
- **LTT consideration:** Ontario may still assess LTT on the FMV of the property transferred, even if the s.85 rollover defers income tax. An exemption may apply if transferring to a "family business corporation" under certain conditions — consult a real estate lawyer.

### Bare Trust Reporting (2024+)

- **New trust reporting rules (effective 2023, deferred for bare trusts to 2025):** Bare trusts (where one person holds legal title for another's benefit) must file a T3 return.
- **Impact:** If a corporation or individual holds property in a bare trust arrangement (common in real estate), a T3 must be filed annually disclosing all trustees, beneficiaries, and settlors.
- **Penalties:** Up to $2,500 for late filing; gross negligence penalty of 5% of the trust's assets.
- **CC relevance:** If CC holds property in a corp or through any trust-like arrangement, ensure T3 filing compliance.

### CC-Specific Notes

- At CC's current scale, personal ownership is simpler and more tax-efficient (PRE available, no passive income grind).
- Corporate holding becomes worth considering when: (a) CC has multiple rental properties, (b) OASIS is incorporated and generating significant active income, (c) asset protection or estate planning becomes a priority.
- The passive income grind is the biggest trap — a few rental properties in the corp can destroy the small business deduction for OASIS. Model this carefully before transferring anything to a corp.

## 9. Joint Venture vs Partnership vs Co-Ownership

**Applicability: FUTURE** (relevant if CC invests in real estate with partners)

### Co-Ownership (Tenants in Common)

- **Tax treatment:** Each co-owner reports their proportionate share of rental income/expenses on their personal T776. No separate entity filing.
- **Legal structure:** Each owner holds an undivided interest in the property. Can be unequal (e.g., 60/40). Each owner can independently sell, mortgage, or will their share.
- **CRA view:** If co-owners are simply sharing ownership and expenses proportionally, CRA treats this as co-ownership (not a partnership).
- **Pros:** Simplest structure. No partnership return. Each owner claims their own CCA, deductions, and PRE (if applicable to their share).
- **Cons:** Disagreements require legal resolution. Each owner is liable for the entire mortgage (if jointly signed). No separate legal entity for liability protection.

### Partnership

- **Tax treatment:** The partnership itself is NOT taxable. It files an information return (**Form T5013**) allocating income/losses to partners. Each partner reports their allocated share on their personal return.
- **ITA s.96:** Partnership income computed at the partnership level, then allocated to partners per the partnership agreement.
- **Key differences from co-ownership:**
  - Partnership exists when two or more persons carry on a **business** in common with a view to profit (common law test — see Continental Bank Leasing Corp. v. Canada, 1998 SCC).
  - **At-risk rules (ITA s.96(2.2)):** A limited partner cannot deduct losses exceeding their at-risk amount (capital invested + allocated but undistributed income - amounts owing to the partnership).
  - **CCA claimed at partnership level**, then allocated. Co-ownership claims CCA individually.
  - Each partner is **jointly and severally liable** for the partnership's obligations (general partnership). Limited partners have limited liability.
- **When CRA deems a partnership exists:** Active management of multiple properties, joint decision-making on operations, reinvestment of profits into new properties, advertising under a common name. Merely co-owning one rental property is usually NOT a partnership.

### Joint Venture

- **Tax treatment:** Each venturer reports their proportionate share of income, expenses, and CCA directly — similar to co-ownership. No partnership information return required (no T5013).
- **Legal distinction:** A joint venture is a one-off or limited-scope business arrangement, not an ongoing business relationship. Think: "We are pooling resources for this one project" vs. "We are running a real estate business together."
- **CRA view:** CRA distinguishes JVs from partnerships based on the scope, duration, and nature of the arrangement. A JV for a single development project is not a partnership. An ongoing arrangement to buy, manage, and sell multiple properties likely IS a partnership.
- **Pros:** Flexibility. Each venturer controls their own tax position (CCA, expenses). No partnership return.
- **Cons:** If CRA recharacterizes the JV as a partnership, penalties and reassessments follow. Must be genuinely limited in scope.

### Comparison Table

| Feature | Co-Ownership | Partnership | Joint Venture |
|---------|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Filing** | Each owner files T776 | Partnership files T5013, allocates to partners | Each venturer files T776 |
| **CCA** | Claimed individually | Claimed at partnership level | Claimed individually |
| **Liability** | Several (each liable for own share, unless joint mortgage) | Joint and several (general) | Several (typically) |
| **PRE available?** | Yes (on owner's share if occupied) | No (partnership property ≠ personal property) | Potentially (depends on structure) |
| **Loss deductibility** | Full (subject to rental loss CCA restriction) | Subject to at-risk rules for LPs | Full (subject to rental loss CCA restriction) |
| **Best for** | 1-2 properties, passive co-investors | Ongoing real estate business, multiple properties | Single project (development, flip) |

### CC-Specific Notes

- If CC invests in real estate with a partner (e.g., a friend or family member), **co-ownership as tenants in common** is the simplest and most tax-efficient starting structure.
- Avoid inadvertently creating a partnership — if CC and a partner actively manage multiple properties together, CRA may deem it a partnership and require T5013 filings.
- For a single development or flip project, structure as a joint venture with a written JV agreement specifying each party's contributions, responsibilities, and profit split.
- **Always get a written agreement** — even for co-ownership. Cover: ownership percentages, expense allocation, buy-sell provisions, dispute resolution, and what happens if one party wants out.
